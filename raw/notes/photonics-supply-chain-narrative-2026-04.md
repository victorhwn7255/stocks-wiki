## Next architecture: CPO optical engines

### Optics moved close to / onto the switch ASIC package

CPO changes the physics of the electrical path. Instead of driving long PCB traces and faceplate interfaces to a module, the optics are moved next to, or effectively into, the switch package. What CPO does is shift the bottleneck, not remove it. ## Supporting photonics infrastructure

**Lasers / external laser sources.** This is the clearest near-term bottleneck. Conventional pluggables consume EMLs or CW sources at scale, while CPO adds a new requirement for centralized, ultra-high-power external light. LumentumŌĆÖs ELSFP architecture is explicit about the reason: move the continuous-wave laser off the ASIC package so multiple silicon-photonics engines can share a serviceable light source with better thermal behavior. Coherent says its high-power CW DFB lasers for transceivers and CPO are already in full production, with 6-inch indium-phosphide ramping in Texas and very high-volume multi-year orders from a leading AI datacenter customer; Lumentum has separately announced a new 240,000-square-foot U.S. facility for advanced InP optical devices and a multiyear strategic partnership with NVIDIA to expand optics capacity. In practice, that means laser and ELS capacity is a more defensible choke point than generic transceiver assembly. **Modulators: EML, silicon photonics, TFLN, BTO, polymers.** The hierarchy here is important. **Thermal / liquid cooling.** Thermal is not a side issue; it is part of the architecture. CPO exists partly because long electrical paths waste power, but once optics are moved near a hot ASIC, lasers and photonics confront a hostile thermal neighborhood. That is why external laser source architectures explicitly move the heat-generating laser into a more manageable thermal zone, and why NVIDIAŌĆÖs Quantum-X and Spectrum-X photonics switches use liquid cooling for onboard silicon photonics. Even the pluggable road map is being reshaped by thermal density: the newly announced XPO MSA targets 12.8 Tb/s liquid-cooled optics, and Coherent says its XPO concept supports front-panel density up to 204.8 Tb/s per OCP rack unit. The takeaway is straightforward: thermal design is now embedded in optical packaging decisions, which means cooling vendors, TIM/cold-plate suppliers, and serviceable external-light architectures all become part of the photonics supply chain, not an afterthought to it. Putting the tree together, the evidence points to a clear bottleneck hierarchy. In the near term, the most important choke points are high-power InP lasers and external light sources, then advanced-node optical DSP/SerDes, then merchant silicon-photonics foundries and practical modulator platforms. After that come fiber attach, detachable connectors, and packaging/test, because those determine whether CPO can be manufactured and serviced at scale. Optical circuit switching is the next major architectural lever, but it is likely to matter after point-to-point photonic I/O becomes common enough that network topology, not port optics, is the limiting constraint. The unresolved timing question is not whether pluggable 1.6T and CPO can coexistŌĆöthey already doŌĆöbut how quickly CPO escapes anchor ecosystems and how fast alternative modulator materials move from credible demonstrations into true merchant high-volume manufacturing.
