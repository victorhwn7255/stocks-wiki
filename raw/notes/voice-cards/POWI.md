## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Even, mid-length declaratives that never sprint. Runs are hedged and additive, not accelerating: "It's been slow. I think the market's been slow, we've talked a little bit about a pushout." No parabola-close; a thought lands flat and moves on. The register is conversational-careful, not oratorical.
2. **Signature vocabulary & verbal tics** — "You know," is the pervasive filler, several times per answer ("You know, we continue to look at those as, you know, there's opportunity there"). Opens Q&A answers by naming the analyst and thanking them: "Okay, great. Thank you, Chris, for the question." / "Thanks, David. By the way, congrats on the move." / "Sure, Tore. Thanks for that question." Warm sign-off "You're welcome." Understated self-reference to the AI story: "turning to everybody's favorite topic, data center" and "thank you for asking a question about our favorite topic" — mild, dry, self-aware, never breathless.
3. **Metaphor habitat** — Engineering and the design bench, not physics or war. Talks in sockets, topologies, BOM content, dollar content, voltage classes ("our GaN works natively at 800 V, 1,200 V, 1,250 V, 1,700 V"), power levels, days-on-hand. The CFO's habitat is the income statement and the cash cycle ("that is cash, the way I look at it").
4. **Pushback behavior** — Answers bluntly and concedes the weak part without spin. Asked whether small segments are being de-emphasized: "we're not de-emphasizing those areas... yeah, they're not the biggest growth drivers, but they're also not the largest part of our business." Asked about a soft appliance market: "Net-net, flat to slightly up." Grants the point, sizes it honestly, does not reframe it as strength.
5. **Bad-news delivery** — Owned flatly and early, no burying. Consumer decline stated as fact plus cause: "Consumer revenue was down compared to the first quarter of 2025, which had been unusually strong due to tariff-related pull-ins in appliances." Automotive: "That's not huge revenue, right?... You know, it's been slow." The disappointment is named in the same breath as the number.
6. **Hedging style** — Timeline hedges over statistical ones, and always toward patience: "that's longer term for us, right? That's not next year. That's in a couple years, basically. It's a couple years out." Macro caveat carried forward every time: "while visibility is somewhat hampered by the ongoing macro uncertainty" / "cognizant of the macro and geopolitical uncertainty we operate within." Forward figures are dated and framed as sizing-in-progress, not promises: "we're continuing to size it... as we go quarter-to-quarter."
7. **Refusals** — Declines magnitude, offers direction. When an analyst asks for percentage of business by year, the answer stays qualitative: sequencing of wins, "a couple years out," and defers the number ("We're continuing to size it"). Won't give a segment split it hasn't disclosed; won't quantify the near-term AI ramp.
8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Nancy Erba (CFO) speaks figures in a disciplined, ROI-first register: basis points and days ("non-GAAP gross margin was 53.5%... up 20 basis points sequentially"; "days on hand fell by 21 days to 292 days"), targets stated as targets ("Our target is to bring days on hand below 200"), guidance ranges with the mechanism attached ("$47 million ± $500,000... mainly reflects annual merit increases"), and a repeated cost-discipline creed ("the same ROI rigor that we're putting across the company, whether that be in OpEx, inventory, CapEx"; "our intent is for OpEx to grow at a rate of less than half of revenue growth over time"). The account speaks numbers in HER register: bounded, basis-point-precise, discipline-framed, never cheerleading.
