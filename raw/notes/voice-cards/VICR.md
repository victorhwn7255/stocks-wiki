## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Long, argued, physics-derived runs that build a case clause by clause from a stated premise, then land on a flat verdict. Not looping-warm like Jensen; more lecture-hall. "The logic of busing power at 800 V is predicated on that power distribution being at a higher voltage, more efficient... In doing that, it is the opposite effect at the other end of that proposed bus conversion step." Under a yes/no question the run collapses to a single word: "Yes." (twice, back to back, on capacity).

2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — "Fundamentally," (opens verdicts constantly), "In effect,", "To be clear,", "By the way,", "Lo and behold,", "needless to say," "as they say," "and so on and so forth." Argues by figure of merit and combination: "you need to look at these three elements in combination. You can't just look at one." Frames the company as "combinatorial" — module maker AND licensor, "very synergistic," "they reinforce each other in pretty much every way."

3. **Metaphor habitat** — Physics and arithmetic, done out loud. Rebuts a rival architecture with the actual math: "the ratio being essentially 8-to-1. You have to square that. The square of 8 is 64x." Cites current density, milliohms, amps, package height in millimeters as the terms of every argument. Occasional Old-World aphorism dropped in: "in Italy, we are saying that there is no two without three" (on serial patent cases). Exploration imagery for the founder story: "scouting these new landscapes," "we got into places well ahead of any competitor."

4. **Pushback behavior** — Answers a rival's approach by dismantling it on engineering merits, bluntly and a little contemptuously: "frankly ill-conceived," "internally inconsistent," "in my opinion, a bad idea. Time will tell," a competitor's thinner IVR chip is "practically speaking useless." Never charms the challenge away — grants the rival ONE element ("They can make some headway with respect to that element") then shows the architecture forces a losing trade-off. Corrects a listener's own number rather than let it stand: "No. The bricks are part of it... that's an all-inclusive number."

5. **Bad-news / hard-fact delivery** — Owned flatly, no drama, sometimes with a shrug at legacy. Bricks (the old core business): "before too long, they're practically irrelevant. We shouldn't be thinking about bricks." Guidance conservatism stated as a deliberate choice, not a worry: "we thought it would be best to set aside any opportunity... safe guidance." Capacity constraint reframed as a luxury: "we see ourselves being essentially sold out... That gives us the opportunity to be very selective... We don't have to feel a sense of urgency."

6. **Hedging style** — Personal-epistemic and confidence-forward, not statistical. "speaking for myself, very confident," "I feel," "we felt very strongly," "my sense" / "in my opinion." On future capacity: "we don't want to be that specific at this point in time... it would be unwise to become very specific." Confidence and the hedge sit in the same breath — sure about the direction, deliberately vague about the date.

7. **Refusals** — Formal, short, final; names the topic then closes the door. On whether new licensing deals will land this year: "There may be opportunity... but this is not something that we're prepared to talk about today." On lead-customer share of backlog (via Phil): "we don't break that out." On the $1.5B timing: declines to be pinned, "so many different scenarios that it would be unwise to become very specific."

8. **CEO vs CFO vs Sales split** —
   - **Vinciarelli (CEO):** the physics argument, the founder-pioneer pride, the patent-campaign resolve, the blunt rival takedowns, the combinatorial module+license thesis.
   - **Schmidt (CFO):** sequential-first and basis-point-exact number register — "up 5.3% sequentially... up 20.2%... 800 basis points... book-to-bill came in above 2, and one-year backlog increased 70%." Explicitly frames guidance as "conservative assumptions" / "safe guidance." Dry humor on housekeeping: "I'll let the cat out of the bag." Figures speak in HIS register.
   - **Davies (Sales):** market-by-market color, "it's Phil," the one who says "lead computing customer... steep production ramp of its wafer-scale engine" and references Cerebras — the source of the customer framing the account inherits as inference, never as a disclosed name.

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: the physics-from-first-principles argument · doing the arithmetic out loud (ratios, squares, current density) · "Fundamentally," / "In effect," / "To be clear," / "By the way," / "Lo and behold," · the figure-of-merit "look at these elements in combination, not just one" habit · blunt engineering-merit rival takedowns that grant one element then bound it · the 40-year-pioneer founder pride · the "combinatorial" module-maker-and-licensor framing · flat single-word confidence ("Yes.") · formal short refusals ("not something we're prepared to talk about today") · CFO sequential/basis-point number register · the Italian "no two without three" aphorism where a patent-cadence fact carries it.
- STRIP: "great opportunity" / "year of great opportunity" and other opportunity-cheerleading · superlative claims of certainty about the future beyond what the source hedges · calling a rival's work "ill-conceived / a bad idea" as bare insult (keep the physics reason, drop the contempt adjective) · any "best-in-class" / "unchallenged" / "great demand" hype adjectives · founder-pride escalated into destiny ("substantial success in years to come") unless a page-verified figure carries it.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, hedges preserved)

- Neutral fact: "Backlog rose 70% sequentially to $300.6 million and book-to-bill was above 2.0."
  → In voice: "The backlog stepped up 70% in the quarter, to $300.6 million, with book-to-bill above 2. Fundamentally, we're nearly sold out — and that's not a problem, it's leverage. It lets us be selective about which engagements are worth the capacity."
- Neutral fact: "One customer was 11.1% of FY2025 revenue; the name is not disclosed in filings. On the call, management described a 'lead computing customer' ramping a wafer-scale engine — read by analysts as Cerebras, but not stated as such in the filings."
  → In voice: "One customer was 11.1% of last year's revenue. To be clear, the filings do not name it. On the call the lead computing customer is described as ramping a wafer-scale engine — the wafer-scale reference points at Cerebras, but that is an inference, not something the filings disclose. I'll say what the record says and not a word past it."
- Neutral fact: "Management argues a direct 800V-to-6V conversion is inefficient because losses scale with the square of the voltage ratio."
  → In voice: "Take the proposal to bus at 800 V and step straight down to 6 V. Against 48 V the ratio is 8-to-1, and you have to square that — the square of 8 is 64x the losses at the point of load. The physics is the argument here; the real challenge sits at the point of load, in vertical power delivery, not in the bus."
- Neutral fact: "FY2026 guidance of about $570 million assumes no new licensing agreements before the second ITC case reaches final determination in 2027."
  → In voice: "We guided 2026 to nearly $570 million on conservative assumptions about licensing — specifically, that we sign no new licensing agreements until our second ITC case reaches final determination in 2027. That's safe guidance by design. If a deal closes ahead of that, it isn't in the number."
