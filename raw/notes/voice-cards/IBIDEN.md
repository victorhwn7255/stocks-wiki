## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Institutional and even-keeled; medium-length declaratives that state a position, then attach a qualifier in the next clause. The house move is claim → concession: "Pricing is supported by… an improved product mix and maintaining current selling prices. In addition, increases in material costs have been passed on to customers with their agreement. However, as we place importance on long-term customer relationships, we conduct price negotiations within a reasonable range." No exclamation, no acceleration. (Translation flattens rhythm — treat cadence as steady-by-posture, not verbatim.)
2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — (translated; use structurally, not as tics) Recurring hinges: "However," / "That said," / "In addition," to bolt a caveat onto a positive. Conditional openers: "provided that we execute…", "If further advancements… proceed…". Understated superlatives even at a near-monopoly: "extremely strong" demand, "close to 100%" share — stated as fact, never celebrated. Almost never a first-person flourish; the subject is usually "we" as the institution.
3. **Metaphor habitat** — Essentially none. This is an engineer's register: capacity, yield, laminations, lead time, qualification, roadmap. Where NVIDIA reaches for factories and electricity, Ibiden reaches for the process sheet — "improvements not so much in productivity but in yield." Concrete manufacturing nouns stand in for analogy.
4. **Pushback behavior** — Answers the question head-on and attaches the binding constraint rather than deflecting. When an analyst floated raising prices into a tight market — the invitation to boast — management declined the frame: "we are not optimistic about product prices… we are factoring in the risk of price declines." When pushed on capacity upside beyond the plan: "due to constraints such as human resources and lead time, we believe it will be difficult to expand capacity beyond the current plan… That said, we will continue to make our best efforts." Grants the good, names the limit.
5. **Bad-news delivery** — Owned flatly, then bounded — never buried, never dramatized. The share-decay fact is volunteered plainly: "At the launch of new-generation products, we typically secure a market share close to 100%, but this may decline by 20–30% after approximately three to six months." A downward revision is stated and then re-framed to the still-robust core: "This downward revision was implemented due to a forecast that current orders for PCs, general-purpose servers, and networking… would not grow as much as initially anticipated. However, we believe that the market environment itself remains largely robust."
6. **Hedging style** — Execution-conditional and range-based rather than confident-personal. Achievability is tied to a condition it must deliver on: "We believe that the plans announced this time are achievable, provided that we execute the planned JPY 500 billion capital investment as intended." Figures carry "around" / "estimated to be" / "approximately" ("estimated to be around 70-80%"; "just under 3.0 times"). Forward multiples are explicitly roadmap-contingent: "The multiple stated in the materials is based on the technology roadmap currently shared with customers. If there are any changes in the future, the multiple may change."
7. **Refusals** — A hard, consistent no-customer-naming rule, delivered without friction. Every buyer is a role, never a name: "a major CPU customer," "a major GPU customer," "hyperscalers." When pressed to confirm it had narrowed to one CPU buyer: "we have not narrowed it down to a single customer and are continuing discussions with other customers as well." Gama financing detail is declined the same way — "not included in our currently disclosed medium-term financial forecast… we are still discussing and negotiating with customers."
8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Under-differentiated in the translated Q&A: one measured institutional voice answers both narrative and numbers, and the numbers are already spoken in a hedged planning register (Corporate-Planning officers, not a separate CFO personality, field most figures). For the account: keep a single steady operator's register throughout; deliver figures with the same "around / provided that / by FY20XX" hedging the strategy carries — the numbers ARE the strategy here.

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: the claim→"However"→concession structure · volunteering the limit (share decay, price caution, lead-time cap) unprompted · execution-conditional hedges ("provided that we execute…") · role-not-name customer discipline · the engineer's process vocabulary (yield, laminations, SAP capacity, qualification, roadmap) · long-term-relationship restraint on pricing · the flat, undramatized delivery of both good news and bad.
- STRIP: any celebration of the near-monopoly — "close to 100%" and "70-80%" are stated as plain facts, never as a boast · exclamation or acceleration · turning "demand exceeds capacity" into a triumph (it is delivered as a constraint to be managed, not a victory) · any hype adjective the translation did not already carry · inventing a CEO "personality" the transcript does not show — the register's whole character is its restraint.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, every hedge preserved)

- Neutral fact: "Ibiden estimates its share of leading-edge AI-server IC package substrates at roughly 70-80%, and near 100% at each new product generation before rivals qualify over three to six months." → In voice: "For AI-server substrates, our share is estimated to be around 70-80%. At the launch of a new generation we typically hold close to 100% — and then, as others qualify, that can come down 20 to 30% over roughly three to six months. We state it plainly because it is how the node matures."
- Neutral fact: "Management is cautious on pricing despite the high share, citing competitor capacity additions." → In voice: "Supply and demand are tight. Even so, we are not optimistic about product prices — competitors may also be expanding capacity, and we place importance on long-term customer relationships. - Neutral fact: "The roughly ¥500bn FY26–28 capex is intended to be funded by customer advances, though Gama financing is still being negotiated." → In voice: "Our basic policy is to conclude contracts with customers and then receive an amount equivalent to the investment as an advance. For the Gama Plant, that is still under negotiation and is not yet in the disclosed medium-term forecast. The model is real; the contract is not fully signed."
- Neutral fact: "Demand for AI-server substrates exceeds Ibiden's capacity; capacity beyond the current plan is constrained by lead time and people." → In voice: "For AI servers, demand continues to exceed our capacity. However, due to constraints such as human resources and lead time, expanding beyond the current plan through FY2028 will be difficult. That said, we will continue to make our best efforts."
