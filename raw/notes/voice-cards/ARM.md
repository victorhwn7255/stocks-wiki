## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Haas builds an enthusiastic run, then poses his own question and snaps a one-word answer onto it. "In the go forward space, could I see a world of 256 cores, 512 cores? Absolutely." "could I see those core counts doubling or quadrupling over the next number of years? Absolutely." "will you see many, many more CPUs inside a data hall... 100%." The rhetorical-question-then-flat-answer is his most repeated structural tic across the call.
2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — Opens Q&A answers "Yeah, thank you for the question, [first name]." Frame-setter: "The way to think about it is…" / "The way I think about it is…" / "the best way to think about." Emphatic one-word answers: "Absolutely." "100%." "yes." Closer construction "at the end of the day" (×2 in one answer: "at the end of the day, we are responding to customer demand… at the end of the day, customer demand speaks volumes"). Heavy intensifier stacking "very, very" (strong, good, large, high confidence, appreciative) and "huge, huge demand" — a real tic, but a STRIP target.
3. **Metaphor habitat** — Ecosystem / platform / toll. "it lifts all boats" (said of getting the whole ecosystem to back the silicon move). "the beauty of the product." Physical-architecture imagery for demand: "reticle limited," "the size of the chips is already limited by the amount of area that a mask can print," "a data hall," "dedicated racks of CPUs." The worldview is a licensor's: every accelerator "connects to Arm," and "increasingly they are going to be 100% Arm."
4. **Pushback behavior** — Reframes with humor, then pivots to his own strength. Vivek Arya presses on the everyone-claims-50% overlap; Haas: "AMD has 50%, Intel has 50% and we have 50%. So you add up to some crazy number." — grants the absurdity, then redirects to the accelerator attach (NVIDIA/Amazon/Google all "connect to Arm"). On the customer-tension question (does selling silicon anger IP licensees?), he answers with a gratitude narrative rather than a defense: "every single partner we asked said yes."
5. **Bad-news delivery** — Reframed as offset, not owned as loss. Smartphone weakness: "unit growth… actually slip to negative for the mobile market… The lower end of the market is probably going to be where most of that impact is. So it doesn't have too much impact on us. And then… any sort of negative impact we do see, we expect that to be more than offset by demand in cloud AI." An honest self-correction does surface: "we probably have undercalled the CPU demand."
6. **Hedging style** — Converts hedges into enthusiasm ("could I see… Absolutely"). Softeners: "I would say," "in my opinion," "in theory." Honest-limits admission: "Hard to say exactly what that pace of growth will look like."
7. **Refusals** — The licensor's disclosure discipline: will not speak a customer's numbers the customer hasn't spoken. "Hard to say exactly what that pace of growth will look like since we're not going to announce their numbers that they haven't announced." Careful, deliberate framing around who is and isn't named.
8. **The core-count reframe (Haas's signature analytical tic)** — He drags every unit debate back to cores-per-chip, because content-per-chip is what the royalty rides on. On the CPU:GPU ratio: "while the ratios may not go to more CPUs than GPUs from a chip standpoint, they probably will from a core count standpoint." "the Arm AGI CPU… it's 136 cores. Vera, that's 88." "these CPU chips are going to have lots of CPU cores which will drive ASP up." A chip-shipper counts units; Haas counts cores — the tell of a per-chip-royalty business.
9. **CEO vs CFO split** — Jason Child speaks figures in "-ish" hedges ("20-ish percent," "$90-ish million or so," "35-ish percent range") and plus-or-minus ranges ("$1.26 billion, plus or minus $50 million"; "$0.40 plus or minus $0.04"). Disciplined guidance-holding is his signature: "we are maintaining our outlook of $1 billion while we pursue supply chain capacity"; "for right now, let's just assume that's the number until we work through some of the wafer and memory shortage issues." Directs attention to his preferred metric: "we continue to focus on annualized contract value or ACV." Forward humility: "who knows how much longer it goes," "hard to say," the "floor that we can see for right now." Shapes the year: "about 60% probably second half versus 40% front half."

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, hedges preserved)

- Neutral: "About 50% of CPU compute among the top hyperscalers now runs on Arm, and data center royalty more than doubled year over year."
  → In voice: "The way to think about it is this: about half the CPU compute at the top hyperscalers runs on Arm now. Every accelerator sits next to a CPU, and increasingly that CPU is ours - so the data center royalty more than doubled year on year. I don't ship the accelerator. I collect on the chip beside it."
- Neutral: "Committed AGI CPU demand doubled to over $2 billion, but the first-revenue outlook is held flat at about $90 million while supply is secured."
  → In voice: "Demand for the AGI CPU more than doubled - over $2 billion now, from $1 billion at launch. Are we raising the outlook? No. We're holding it at about $90 million until we've secured the memory, the wafers, the packaging and the test. The demand is real; the supply is what I have to go get."
- Neutral: "Arm China is the largest customer at 16% of revenue and is a related party that Arm does not control."
  → In voice: "I'll say this one plainly, because you should hear it from me: my single largest customer, Arm China, is 16% of revenue - and it's a related party neither we nor SoftBank control. It's been coming down as data center royalty grows, 24% a few years back to 16% now. But 16% is 16%."
- Neutral: "Royalty growth is expected to be around 20% for the year, with licensing back-end weighted." (CFO register)
  → In voice: "Royalty growth for the year should land in the 20-ish percent range, plus or minus by quarter. Licensing's a little back-end weighted, like the last three years - call it 60% second half, 40% first. Beyond that, hard to say."
