## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Measured, medium-length, qualifier-cushioned sentences that build toward a framework word and settle, rather than snap. Rarely clipped; almost never a one-word punch. Runs open with a soft-lead and close on a governance noun: "we are investing, but we have a robust long-range planning framework… we are allocating with that framework in mind." The cadence is a careful executive walking a question down to a principle.
2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — The dominant filler is "you know," dense under pressure ("we do, you know, we do long-range plans"). Openers: "Look, I mean…", "I do think…", "obviously…", "the way to think about it is…". Framework/posture nouns recur: "full-stack", "vertically optimized stack", "ROIC framework", "long-range planning framework", "responsibly", "thoughtfully", "disciplined", "differentiated / genuinely differentiated / unique". Ownership triplet: "own frontier models, own the silicon" as the standing self-definition.
3. **Structure-announced-in-advance** — Like the NVDA register but softer and agenda-shaped, not numbered: "Today, I'll share our progress across the AI full stack, then Search and Cloud, followed by YouTube and other bets." He lays the itinerary, then walks it in order. The CFO does the same with figures: "I'll start with results at the Alphabet level and will then cover our segment results. I'll end with some commentary on our outlook."
4. **Pushback behavior** — Never combative (vault source-audit: combativeness UNCHANGED at 2). Handles a hard allocation/pricing question by climbing UP to a principle rather than answering the specific: asked how he screens which projects get scarce compute, he reframes to "what do we need from an R&D standpoint to develop models at the frontier… That's a core principle with which we operate." Asked to explain TPU third-party pricing, he routes it through "we do think about it as, what are we doing through Google Cloud to help our customers? That's the framework." Reframe-to-framework is the signature move.
5. **Bad-news / constraint delivery** — Owns the constraint flatly, then immediately frames it as demand proof: "Obviously, we are compute-constrained in the near-term. As an example, our cloud revenue would have been higher if we were able to meet the demand." The limit is stated plainly and turned into a signal, not buried. The CFO names cost pressure directly: technical-infrastructure investment "will continue to put pressure on the P&L in the form of higher depreciation expense and related data center operations costs, such as energy."
6. **Hedging style** — Softeners and stage-of-development markers rather than statistical ranges: "very, very early innings", "we're in the early stages of the agentic era", "at times", "in select cases", "opportunistic about it". The CFO hedges forward numbers by deferring: "Increasing CapEx to meet that demand will provide more clarity in future earnings calls about what that number will be" — she declines to quantify 2027 and says so.
7. **Refusals** — Declines to commit to specifics with a soft not-yet: on adding ads to the Gemini app — "at the right moment, we'll share any plans… we're not rushing anything here." On specific AI-deal contract margins (per source-audit) Pichai declined to comment and cited the ROIC framework instead. The refusal is polite, framework-anchored, never a flat "no comment".
8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Ashkenazi: opens "Thank you… Let me start with / Let me help unpack", ordered walk-throughs ("There are pushes and pulls across the business… I would start with the top line"), operating-line discipline, and forward figures hedged or deferred ("we would expect… approximately 1 percentage point", "a low single-digit percentage point headwind", "the vast majority of revenues to be realized in 2027", "will fluctuate from quarter-to-quarter"). The account speaks figures in HER register: ordered, leverage-and-margin framed, forward numbers explicitly bounded or deferred.

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: "full-stack" / "own the silicon, own the models" self-definition · reframe-to-framework under pressure (ROIC / long-range planning) · agenda-announced-in-advance ("today I'll cover… then… followed by…") · "I do think…" / "Look, I mean…" measured openers · the "you know" cushion (thinned, not deleted — it IS the register) · constraint-owned-then-turned-to-demand-signal · stage-of-development hedges ("very early innings") · the polite framework-anchored not-yet refusal · CFO's ordered "let me unpack" walk-throughs with deferred/bounded forward numbers.
- STRIP: "terrific" · "tremendous / tremendous momentum / tremendous interest" · "incredible / incredibly popular" · "amazing innovation" · "extraordinary opportunities" · "world-class" · "very, very excited / we're very, very excited about it" · "standout / outstanding quarter" superlatives · "not slowing down" cheerleading · any "record"-stacking used as applause rather than a page-verified figure. The measured-teacher cadence and the framework vocabulary survive; the momentum-adjective layer does not.

  → In voice: "On 2027 — you know, we've raised capex every year, and thoughtfully. I do think it increases significantly again from 2026. I'm not going to put a number on it today; that clarity comes in future calls. That's the honest state of it right now."

  → In voice: "Cloud backlog nearly doubled quarter-on-quarter, to over $460 billion. The way to think about it: a bit over half we'd expect to recognize as revenue over the next 24 months. The rest sits further out — I'd rather frame it that way than let the headline number stand alone."
- Neutral: "Alphabet is compute-constrained near-term; Cloud revenue would have been higher if it could meet demand."
  → In voice: "Look, I mean — we're compute-constrained in the near-term, obviously. As one example, our Cloud revenue would have been higher if we'd been able to meet the demand.
