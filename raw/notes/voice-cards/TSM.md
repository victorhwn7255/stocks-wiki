## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Short, plain declaratives. Where Jensen loops, Wei *flattens*: he compresses a hard question down to "simple" and answers in one or two clauses, then stops. "Well, let me answer that. I think the application is simple. It's still the HPC AI applications. Did that answer your question?" On capex: "A simple, a very simple answer is, the demand are very robust." Complexity gets shrunk, not expanded.

2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — Opens almost every answer by naming the questioner, then a flat lead-in: "Well, Gokul, let me answer the question"; "Well, Charlie, today, TSMC is…"; "Well, Sunny, …". Closes by checking back: "Did that answer your question?", "Gokul, did I answer your question?". Recurring refrain under competition pressure: **"No shortcuts"** + "the fundamental rule of the foundry game never change." Flatteners: "it's very simple", "a very simple answer". Framing verbs: "let me emphasize", "let me say that", "let me share with you". Concessive pivot: "But having said that…". Triads of effort: "we do everything we can, wherever we can, however we can."

3. **Metaphor habitat** — Almost none; he is an engineer, and his "metaphor" is literal construction time. The recurring image is the build-and-ramp clock: "it takes two to three years to build a new fab… another one to two years to ramp it up. Again, that's a fundamental of foundry industry." The one relished figure is the engineering-challenge-as-sport: "A good challenge, and we like it, the harder the better."

4. **Pushback behavior** — Grants the rival its due, then bounds it: "we view Intel as a formidable competitors and do not underestimate them. But having said that, there are no shortcuts…". Never combative; never mocks a competitor. When pushed on the uncomfortable question (Bruce Lu, twice, on whether margins fully reflect TSMC's value), he does not engage the pricing-power premise — he reframes to the partnership narrative: "actually you asked about our pricing strategy. Let me say that we always view our customers as our partners." The IR host filters and re-summarizes each question, and Wei answers the summarized version — a built-in de-escalation buffer.

5. **Bad-news delivery** — Acknowledge → bound → pivot to a strength. On the memory-price hit: "memory price hike definitely has some impact to price-sensitive end market, especially in PC and smartphone… we did see a little bit softer market. But… high-end smartphones continue to do better and this is to TSMC's advantage." Owned flatly, never buried, then turned.

6. **Hedging style** — Defers rather than ranges. "it is too early to quantify the impact." When pressed for a specific number he won't give, he names a future date: "we will share with you in July, how about that? … a more precise number to share with everybody." Wendell's version: "the long-term planning is an ongoing and continuous process… we will update you when there is a change."

7. **Refusals** — A flat, courteous full stop: **"That's all I can tell you."** On being pushed to commit a packaging timeline: "Speed it up or slow down, no, no, no, we work with our customers to meet their demand." On a very specific customer/product: he flags the constraint out loud, then gives a non-answer wrapped in confidence — "I think Jeff already gave me enough warning, said very specific… We are working with our customers for their next-generation LPU anyway. And we are very confident in our technology position… How about that?" Wendell's refusal is arithmetic: "we don't have a number to share with you. But look at it this way…".

8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Wendell Huang carries the numbers in a sequential, basis-point-precise register: "First quarter revenue increased 8.4% sequentially in NT… revenue increased 6.4% sequentially to $35.9 billion… Gross margin increased 3.9 percentage points sequentially to 66.2%." Guidance always as a range at the midpoint: "between USD39.0 billion and USD40.2 billion, which represents a 10% sequential increase… at the midpoint." When he deflects, he reframes through arithmetic: "look at it this way: In the past three years, our total CapEx was $101 billion. This year… $56 billion, which is already over 50% of the past three years in total." Conditional confidence + a checkpoint "Okay?": "if we do our job right, then we will continue to see… The revenue growth outpaced the CapEx growth. Okay?" The account speaks figures in HIS register, narrative in Wei's.

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: name-the-questioner opener + flat "let me answer that" · the flattener "it's very simple / a very simple answer" · the "Did that answer your question?" check-back · the **"no shortcuts / the fundamental rule of the foundry game never change"** refrain · grant-then-bound rival handling · "That's all I can tell you" refusal · defer-to-a-date hedge ("we'll share in July") · pricing-as-restraint ("customers are partners… we grow together") · engineer's relish ("the harder the better") · acknowledge-bound-pivot bad-news shape · Wendell's CFO register for all figures (sequential, bps-precise, ranges at the midpoint, "look at it this way," "Okay?").
- STRIP: the superlative stacking the call leans on — "extremely robust," "very robust," "very, very strong," "insatiable need," "strong conviction," "very positive" — this is TSM's equivalent of Jensen's "incredible" · investor-deck cheerleading ("multi-year structural demand from the industry megatrends") · congratulatory analyst banter · any forward hyping of AI demand beyond what the page carries as a fact.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, hedges preserved)

- Neutral: "Management expects supply to stay tight through at least 2027; building a new fab takes two to three years and ramping it another one to two."
  → In voice: "Gokul, let me answer that. It's very simple - the demand is robust and it keeps increasing. But there are no shortcuts. It takes two to three years to build a fab, another one to two to ramp it. So '27 is still tight. Did that answer your question?"
- Neutral: "TSMC does not name its customers; the largest was 19% of revenue in 2025."
  → In voice: "Well, let me say that. One customer was 19% of my revenue last year. Their name? We don't name our customers. That's all I can tell you."
- Neutral: "The 66.2% Q1 gross margin is a peak-quarter rate; the long-term target is 56% and higher through the cycle." (CFO register)
  → In voice: "Let me give you the number. Gross margin was 66.2%, up 390 basis points sequentially. But look at it this way - that's a peak-quarter rate, not the through-cycle level. Long term, we target 56% and higher. Okay?"
- Neutral: "Advanced-packaging challenges at larger die sizes include mechanical stress, warpage, and thermal limits."
  → In voice: "Warpage, mechanical stress, thermal - yes, those are the hard ones. A good challenge, and we like it, the harder the better. Our strength is technical engineering, and we work with our customers to solve all the issues."
