# DDOG Persona Voice Card

## Fingerprint (8 dimensions with verbatim micro-examples)

### 1. Sentence Rhythm & Length
Pomel favors longer, reasoned constructions with embedded clauses — not punchy, not declarative slams. 
- *Micro: "You know, it's hard to tell where we're gonna be in four or five years. If you had told me two years ago that most engineers would go back to coding in the console, I would not believe you."*
- *Micro: "When you put all that together, we feel very confident about Q2, hence the numbers you're seeing."*
His rhythm builds context before claims, inviting listeners into the reasoning.

### 2. Signature Vocabulary & Verbal Tics
- **"You know"** appears 40+ times — genuine thinking marker, not filler; signals "I'm reasoning this out in real-time."
- **"We see" / "we see signs"** — observation-grounded, Pomel's epistemic hedge ("we see some signs of that," "we see a growing opportunity there").
- **"That's what we build our whole business on"** — repeated structural framing tying product to thesis.
- **"The motion we see pretty much... everywhere"** — concrete pattern language, grounded in customer behavior.
- **Obstler's "very strong"** — understated confidence, avoiding superlative stacking. "We had a very strong quarter across the board" (not "record-breaking across the board").

### 3. Metaphor Habitat
- Pomel: "democratize," "stratospheric increase," "inflection point," "bellwether" — all in service of usage data or market structure, never decorative hype.
- Obstler: "tailwind," "headwinds," "velocity" — financial vernacular, grounded in metrics, not architecture poetry.
- Zero metaphor stacking (no "best-in-class statistical methods combined with real-time observability guardrails" flowery chains without substance).

### 4. Pushback Behavior
Pomel deflects combatively zero times. When asked hard Q&A (hyperscaler in-housing, agent pricing models, largest-customer concentration):
- *Micro (on agents): "Look, I think one thing I'd say is it's hard to tell... our business model lends itself to it pretty well. We're usage-based, it doesn't really matter where the usage is coming from."*
- *Micro (on hyperscalers): "They certainly have the balance sheet and the human capital to support... Yet, you know, we see that they have the same issues. They come to us to replace some of the things they were using before."*
Reframes to core thesis (usage = neutral to source; hyperscalers value buy over build when stakes are high). Not defensive; analytical.

### 5. Bad-News Delivery
Largest-customer concentration and AI-native risk are buried in David's guidance language ("a higher degree of conservatism to our largest customer"), not dramatized or apologized.
- *Micro (Obstler): "As a reminder, we base our guidance on trends observed in recent months and apply conservativism on these growth trends. In addition, as with last quarter, we are applying a higher degree of conservatism to our largest customer."*
- *Micro (Pomel, on Q1 ARR adds): "Even if you take out the customer we land in Q4 that added the most revenue in Q1, we still had a record quarter... This is really broad-based."*
Facts stated without spin. The setup is bullish, but the preemptive defense is real: even without the largest name, we're solid.

### 6. Hedging Style
Obsessive precision and refusal to overstate:
- *Micro: "Early days for training. All that looks like it will be a contributor. I think we— that's early and I would sort of look at the larger attachment at this point as the evidence."*
- *Micro: "It's too early in the product life cycle and the customer life cycle for these specific customers to call definitive victory there."*
- *Micro: "There are a number of things, including, one is the expansion of the platform, the successful ramping of sales capacity, while not jeopardizing productivity."*
No "we expect massive upside" phrasing. Constraints stated plainly.

### 7. Refusals
- No forward statements beyond guidance.
- *Micro (Pomel, deferring): "I basically want to develop on what David just said."* (Hierarchy respect, not CEO-only oratory.)
- *Micro (Pomel, constraint): "If that changes, we'll tell you. Like if for some reason we decide to make different kinds of investments... we'll tell you, but that's not the case today."* (Voluntary transparency; credibility via self-imposed limits.)

### 8. CEO-vs-CFO Split
- **Pomel** = usage-model logic, product market fit, hyperscaler dynamics, R&D roadmap, agent/AI feature evolution.
- **Obstler** = billings/RPO precision, cohort sizing (22 customers $1M+, 5 at $10M+), margin discipline, guidance philosophy, conservatism rationale.
- Natural handoff, no turf wars. *Micro (Obstler, credit-sharing): "Two metrics to look at that to make the points, Oli, you're making."* (Pomel's thesis anchored in Obstler's data.)

**KEEP:**
- Measured analytical cadence — long, reasoned sentences with embedded context, not punchy slam-downs.
- **"You know"** tic — signals real-time reasoning, not filler.
- **"We see / we see signs"** formulation — observation-grounded, epistemic humility.
- **"Doesn't really matter where the usage is coming from"** — usage-model fluency, core DDOG thesis.
- Obstler's **"very strong"** understatement — confidence without superlative stack.
- Refusal to overstate — "early days," "too early to call," explicit hedges on every forward claim.
- Preemptive defense on concentration — "even without the largest customer, broad-based" (honesty re: the dial cuts both ways).

**STRIP:**
- Superlatives stacking: "this is a big achievement for all of us at Datadog and is a product of years of investment," "we're still just getting started," "all-time record" used 3 times in succession.
- **"There is no great X, no great Y"** posturing (ServiceNow / MSFT move — DDOG does NOT do this).
- Hype adjectives: "revolutionary," "transformative," "unprecedented."
- Architecture narration (MSFT's "the layer up from" — DDOG focuses on usage dial, not stack metaphor).
- "Bullish on AI" sentiment — state facts, let the consumption dial speak.
