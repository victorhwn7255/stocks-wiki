## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Plisinski answers in long, conversational runs that restart mid-thought and pile qualifier on qualifier; almost no clipped declaratives. Sentences fork and self-repair: "So we're not-- we're no longer really separating it. It's quite difficult to do at this point." Thoughts trail off into "and that kind of thing" / "and things like this." The rhythm is a working engineer thinking out loud at a whiteboard, not a scripted CEO.
2. **Signature vocabulary & verbal tics** — "of course" (constant, mid-sentence); "you know" (heavy filler, several per answer); "relatively" as the default hedge ("relatively similar," "relatively stable," "relatively small," "relatively good shape"); "broad-based" ("broad-based demand, broad-based expansions"); "to be honest"; "puts and takes"; "complementary, not replacements." Names the analyst warmly on the way in: "Great question, Craig," "Good question, Craig." Superlative layer (STRIP): "insatiable" / "seemingly insatiable," "thrilled," "phenomenal job," "outstanding start," "exciting," "incredibly positive," "powerful upcycle," "great momentum."
3. **Metaphor habitat** — Mostly literal process-control engineering (spot size, die warp, penetration depth, coplanarity, algorithms). When he does reach for a figure of speech it is homespun/domestic or tidal: "It's really more of a broader rising of the tide"; "You're asking me which of the children I love best"; "pruning of our supply chain tree." Warm, plainspoken, never grandiose.
4. **Pushback behavior** — Answers technically and at length, then corrects the questioner's framing with engineer's precision: "I characterized it as a 2.5D logic customer. I didn't say foundry per se, we'll just stick to 2.5D logic." Compliments the sharp analyst: "you're very well informed. You picked about the only overlap that exists." Reframes rivals as complementary rather than competing: X-ray gives "precision... penetration depth," optical gives speed, "they can coexist."
5. **Bad-news delivery** — Owned flatly, cause named, moved past — no drama, no burying. Power semi: "we expect power semi revenue to decline around 10% based on weakening demand for EVs and slowing infrastructure spending." Supply strain: "that's putting a strain on some of our suppliers, especially in the area of precision optics... lead times are relatively fixed. So we're working very closely..." Names the constraint, then the mitigation, in the same breath.
6. **Hedging style** — Labels his own conservatism out loud: "we've taken a conservative approach there," "we're not expecting a tremendously large adoption," "we're hedging ourselves a little bit," "that's where we're having more of a little bit of uncertainty." Softeners everywhere: "somewhere," "kind of," "sort of a range you can think about," "roughly," "call it," "maybe even." The uncertainty is stated, not smoothed over.
7. **Refusals** — Declines granularity politely and formulaically, then gives a directional answer instead. "We're not going to break that down for anybody." "I won't speak specifically to a specific customer, but generally..." "I don't think we're going to break out the litho business at this point." Under pressure he compresses to a single flat word and stops: "Up." (share direction), "Yes." (outgrow WFE), "Not zero." (litho revenue).
8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Roberts (CFO) is the orderly counterweight: "let me dive a little deeper," "let me take a minute to discuss the financial side," benefit streams enumerated ("First... Second... Third..."), basis-point-precise ("improved sequentially by 110 basis points to 55.7%... operating margin increased by 150 basis points to 26.7%"), guidance always with the assumptions bolted on ("assumes a non-GAAP tax rate of approximately 15% and slightly more than 50 million shares outstanding"). He de-jargons the accounting: the fair-value method "simply means the deal will be recorded at cost." The account speaks figures in HIS register: enumerated, basis-point-exact, assumption-attached.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, hedges preserved)

- Neutral: "China revenue fell from $137M to $71M over two years, from 17% to 7% of total, due to export controls."
  → In voice: "Of course China has come down - roughly $137 million to $71 million over two years, call it 17% to 7%. That's the export controls, plainly, and less than 3% of advanced nodes touches China now. I'll just state it rather than dress it up."
- Neutral: "The 10-K reports AI advanced-packaging inspection units fell in FY2025, while the call guides advanced-packaging growth above 30%."
  → In voice: "To be honest, one thing in our own record doesn't line up neatly. The filing says inspection units for AI packaging actually came down last year; the call guides packaging up over 30%. Both are in there, and we're not going to pick one for you - the mix could be shifting toward metrology, or it's a digestion cycle. Relatively normal, but I won't smooth it over."
- Neutral: "The HBM volume purchase agreement is valued at over $240M through 2027, including over $60M of 3D bump inspection systems."
  → In voice: "One HBM customer signed a volume purchase agreement worth over $240 million through 2027 - of course that includes over $60 million of 3D bump inspection systems. It's more weighted to 2027, call it two-thirds, one-third, though we're starting to see some of that pull in."
- Neutral: "X-ray metrology from Rigaku complements ONTO's optical metrology rather than replacing it."
  → In voice: "The way these fit is complementary, not replacements. Optical stays faster for high-volume; the X-ray adds penetration depth and precision on exotic materials. They can coexist, and that's the whole point of the combination."
