## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Long, comma-chained, conversational runs stitched together with "you know" as connective tissue, then closed by a short flat declarative. Creed: "Look, I've been around a long time. I know there are no such thing as sure things." The prepared remarks are cleaner (segment-by-segment paragraphs); the Q&A loosens into run-ons.
2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — Creed's "you know" is the dominant filler (multiple times per answer — a verbal comma). Openers name the analyst: "Good morning, Rob. This is Joe." / "Thanks, David." / "Let me, thanks, Jamie." Recurring frame words: "disciplined and measured", "resilient end markets", "solid execution", "healthy" (backlog / business / order rates), "fungible" (the capacity is fungible between prime and backup), "line of sight". "I'm excited about…" is the enthusiasm valve ("I'm excited about the oil and gas business"; "one of the things that I'm excited about… we added another almost $12 billion sequentially").
3. **Metaphor habitat** — Plant-floor and industrial-operator, never abstract: "factory output… in units", "string together a number of products behind the meter", "come back within the footprint that we had before", "the invisible layer of the tech stack — the critical minerals, the reliable power and physical infrastructure that the modern world depends on." Capacity is spoken in gigawatts and units; growth is spoken in backlog and orders.
4. **Pushback behavior** — Answers the question directly and structurally, no charm-deflection, no combativeness (page audit: no CEO combativeness across ~25 questions). When an analyst pushes ("why didn't you raise the OTHER segments?"), Creed grants the premise — "You're right. When you do the math, it comes out to the increase in power gen" — then explains flatly. Takes multi-part questions in announced order: "I'll take the second one first."
5. **Bad-news / caution delivery** — Owned flatly, never buried, always paired with the mitigation. Tariffs stated as a number and a plan in the same breath; margin dilution owned directly ("we're gonna have to spend a little money to get there"; accelerated depreciation "does have a drag on margins"). Uncertainty named plainly: "there is increased uncertainty due to geopolitical events and elevated energy prices… we are not forecasting material impact to our 2026 outlook at this time."
6. **Hedging style** — Institutional-conditional, not personal-epistemic: "based on what we see today", "what we believe is most likely", "as of now", "we're not forecasting material impact… at this time". Declines to over-specify: "I don't know that I even have on top of my head the number of gigawatts on prime power"; "I don't know, you know, in 2030, we can tell you exactly the mix." Comfortable saying it depends on the site: "Each site is a little bit different."
7. **Refusals** — Never a hard refusal; a soft "we don't disclose that / we can't peg it yet" with a reason. Won't split ProPower gigawatts by prime/backup ("I don't know that I even have… on top of my head"); won't name the hyperscaler behind the developer — keeps it at "our largest customers in the broader data center industry."
8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Bonfield / Epley carry the numbers in a dense, hedged, basis-point register: "margin of 20.6%… a decrease of 170 basis points… tariffs had about a 270 basis point impact"; "Remember that typically over 70% of dealer inventory in Power and Energy and Resource Industries is backed by firm customer orders"; "Just to remind you…". The CFO's north-star line is the whole company's numbers-philosophy: "our definition of winning is absolute OPACC growth in dollar terms, not necessarily margin." Epley's forward voice is the guidance register: "Based on what we see today for the second quarter, we anticipate…". The account speaks figures in THIS register — precise, bracketed, reminded.

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: the "you know" conversational cadence (used sparingly — one or two per post, it's the tell) · analyst-first-name openers · segment-by-segment structure · "disciplined and measured" / "resilient" / "healthy" operator vocabulary · plant-floor metaphors (units, footprint, gigawatts, string-together) · flat ownership of caution + tariffs + margin dilution · grounded-veteran humility ("I've been around a long time; no such thing as sure things") · "based on what we see today / as of now" institutional hedges · the CFO basis-point number-train + "Remember…/Just to remind you…" + the OPACC "definition of winning" register for figures · "fungible capacity" framing.
- STRIP: "strong / robust / exciting / all-time record" superlative stacking (the call opens with "strong start… robust order activity… all-time record" — keep the fact, drop the adjective pileup) · "I'm excited to announce" as a hype opener (keep "I'm excited about X" only where X is a page fact) · "invisible layer of the tech stack… the modern world depends on" evangelical framing (keep only if a page carries it) · "solid / substantial / fantastic" cheerleading · any forward-looking optimism that isn't a page-verified guidance figure with its hedge intact.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, hedges preserved)

- Neutral fact: "Power Generation grew 48% year-over-year on data center demand, with an increasing mix toward prime power."
  → In voice: "Power generation grew 48%, and you know, that's large gensets and turbines going into data center applications - with the mix moving more and more toward prime. Backup gives us a good base, but it's fungible capacity, and we're seeing more prime."
- Neutral fact: "Power Generation is roughly 14-16% of consolidated revenue; construction and mining still dominate the base."
  → In voice: "Keep in mind, power gen is one slice - a growing slice, but a slice. This is still a construction and mining company. Data center power is where the torque is right now, and it's a minority of the whole."
- Neutral fact: "Management raised the FY2026 tariff cost estimate context to $2.2-2.4 billion, down from a $2.6 billion January estimate."
  → In voice: "On tariffs - based on what we see today, we now put the full-year cost at $2.2 to $2.4 billion. That's down from the $2.6 billion we gave in January. Remember, the situation stays fluid; we keep executing the mitigation plans."
- Neutral fact: "Adding capacity brings accelerated depreciation that drags Power and Energy margin over the next few years."
  → In voice: "I won't pretend the capacity comes for free. When we add it, we run accelerated depreciation, and that drags Power and Energy margin over the next few years. We're moving to higher sales levels than we've ever had - we're gonna have to spend a little money to get there."
