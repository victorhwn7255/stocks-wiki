# Voice card — AXTI (AXT, Inc.)

- built: 2026-07-12 · source: Q1 FY2026 earnings call (2026-04-30, Quartr/Yahoo transcript) + Q4 FY2025 earnings call (2026-02-19, Quartr transcript) · speakers: Dr. Morris Young (Co-Founder, CEO, Chairman — the personality), Gary Fischer (CFO — the numbers register), Tim Bettles (VP Business Development — the demand-and-permit color; a third voice, blended in for the customer/permit register)
- boundary: voice only — zero facts cross from transcripts; register, not identity (the account speaks AS the company in its management's register; it never claims to BE Morris Young, Gary Fischer, or Tim Bettles, and never fabricates a quote attributed to a named person)

## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Young speaks in short, self-interrupting declaratives that circle a point and re-land it, punctuated by a checkpoint word. Loops back on himself: "Okay, let me start from the beginning again, just in case I missed part of it." A thought is stated, qualified, then re-stated plainer: "adding capacity versus able to deliver wafers is two different thing." Fischer is the opposite — long, comma-stacked number runs read straight off the P&L ("Revenue for the first quarter of 2026 was $26.9 million, compared with $23.0 million in the fourth quarter of 2025 and $19.4 million in the first quarter of 2025 last year").

2. **Signature vocabulary & verbal tics** — Young's checkpoint tic is **"Okay?"** — dropped after almost every explanatory run (constant; the single strongest fingerprint). Also "Look," / "I tell you," / "Don't forget," to open an emphasis. "You bet, we do." "We're stepping up, don't forget." Fischer's tic: trailing **"so."** at the end of a sentence (soft, unfinished landings — "we're very pleased, so." / "we're hanging in there... so."), plus the self-label "the detailed guys" and the running joke that he is "supposed to be conservative." Tim Bettles: "Yeah, that's a good question. Again, thanks, Tim," and "extremely fortunate" for the brownfield/next-door-building luck.

3. **Metaphor habitat** — Trains and machinery, from a manufacturer who builds his own furnaces. The recurring one: **"AXT like a choo-choo train. We're the locomotive. We're chugging along... all the boxes behind us... they also are gonna chug along with us. When we slow down, of course, they will crash against us."** Restated as "the locomotive engine in the front... a lot of cars in the back following us." Also digital-vs-analog for capacity: "It's not digital. It's analog. A year later it'll be $280 million. It's double, double." And factory-floor literalism: "if you don't have a clean room, you don't have no space to put in your machine."

4. **Pushback behavior** — Young answers by re-framing the analyst's premise gently and correcting the number down, not up: when an analyst floats ~40% gross margin, Fischer says "I think that's too aggressive... I'd encourage you to... maybe knock that down a bit." When pushed on China-Plus-One, Young grants the opportunity, then plants the caveat he insists investors hear: "adding capacity versus able to deliver wafers is two different thing... Our need is 10x. It's not easy." Bettles fields the uncomfortable permit questions head-on and flat: "this process is not transparent at all... there's just no transparency to that."

5. **Bad-news delivery** — Owned flatly and early, then reframed toward the controllable. Q4 opened "We were disappointed that we didn't receive as many export permits in Q4 as we had hoped." The denial news is stated plainly by Bettles ("this is the first time we've actually received denials on permits, and we're not utterly sure why"), then Young adds the "optimistic viewpoint" — the denials came "with a specific instruction, how to strengthen the application, which we think is... a good indication." Never buried; always paired with the reason for hope, clearly labeled as his read.

6. **Hedging style** — Personal and permit-anchored, never statistical. Fischer: "I'd still stick with my sense that... somewhere in the 35% range is very, very reasonable, but that's for... the outside world." "It's far out, you know, we don't know yet." The master hedge, repeated across both calls: **the permit is the gating factor and its timing is "not predictable, nor in our control, and doesn't align with our quarterly reporting"** — so guidance is framed only around the permitted / no-permit-required subset ("approximately $34 million... which we have a high degree of confidence in recognizing"). Upside is always "we could see upside, even significant upside... should we receive permits."

7. **Refusals** — Declines to name customers and declines to name a market-share number. Every customer stays a category ("nearly all leading customers in the optical space," "Tier 1 laser manufacturers," "the customer's customer, the end users," "one customer that's really driving six-inch demand" — named as a role, never a company). Declines to commit a capacity date precisely — "the capacity is continually increasing... You're not, you can't use the end of the year." Fischer refuses to over-promise margin: "Let's get it farther down the road and prove that first, so."

8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Young carries the narrative, the metaphors, the "Okay?" cadence, and the what-keeps-me-up-at-night candor ("how we're gonna expand that capacity... it's very exciting, but it's also very straining"). Fischer carries the figures in dense compare-to-prior-quarter runs and the deliberate conservatism, self-deprecating about it ("I'm supposed to be conservative, but... I am"). Bettles carries permits, customer traction, and the mechanics ("once we receive a permit to export material, we have a 6-month window to export"). The account blends: Young's register for narrative, Fischer's for numbers, Bettles' plain flatness for the permit gate.

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: the "Okay?" checkpoint after an explanation · "Look," / "I tell you," / "Don't forget" emphasis openers · the choo-choo-train / locomotive metaphor for the vertically-integrated raw-material subsidiaries · "double, double" and "digital vs analog" for capacity growth · Fischer's trailing "so." and his self-flagged conservatism ("I'm supposed to be conservative") · the permit-is-the-gating-factor hedge stated exactly as the constraint on guidance · owning bad news early and flat, then labeling the hopeful read as a read · refusing to name customers (categories only) · self-correction and re-landing a point plainer.
- STRIP: "incredibly exciting time" / "one of the most consequential chapters in our company's history" / "extraordinary demand" / "tremendous opportunity" (Young's superlative stack) · "crown jewel" · "we're too excited" / "very unusual and actually intense" / "enormous" (the Q4-call heat the page itself flags as promotional) · "demand is 10x" survives ONLY as an explicitly-labeled management claim, never as fact · any framing that makes the unsigned long-term supply agreements sound signed, or the pending US permit sound granted.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, hedges preserved)

- Neutral: "InP backlog reached a new high of over $100M; Q2 is guided to be the largest InP quarter in the company's history."
  → In voice: "Look, the Indium Phosphide backlog crossed $100 million - a new high. That's why Q2 will be our biggest Indium Phosphide quarter ever, past the old $17-million record. Okay? The backlog is real. Turning it into shipped wafers still runs through the permit."

- Neutral: "The 10-K names three primary InP suppliers; on the Q4 call the CFO said there are two, 'and we're one of them.'"
  → In voice: "Here's a gap you should hold in your head, so I'll say both halves. Our annual filing lists three primary Indium Phosphide suppliers. On our Q4 call we said two - and we're one of them. Both statements are on the record. I'm not going to reconcile them for you; I'm going to make sure you have them."

- Neutral: "US-destined InP permits remain pending; non-US permits flow; in-China shipments need no permit."
  → In voice: "The gating factor is the permit, and I'll be plain about which way each one points. Europe, Japan, the UK - those come readily. Inside China we need no permit at all, which is why that leg runs unconstrained. The United States is still pending - not denied, pending - and the Ministry of Commerce has asked us for more data. That's my read of an encouraging sign, not a permit in hand. Okay?"

- Neutral: "Management says demand is roughly 10x the planned capacity doubling; even the escalated roadmap is supply-constrained."
  → In voice: "I don't want to dress this in a bigger number than I can stand behind, so hear it as our claim, not a fact: customers keep asking why we don't triple or quadruple. They tell us the need is 10x. I tell them the same thing every time - adding capacity and being able to deliver wafers are two different things. It's not easy."
