## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Two clearly different tempos. The **figures register (Maxson)** runs in even, mid-length declaratives that walk down the P&L in order and always name the unit: "Revenue was $19.2 billion, up 21% in US dollars. Cloud infrastructure revenue grew 93%… and cloud apps was up double digits at +10%." The **vision register (Ellison)** builds by anaphora — a rising list of parallel clauses that accretes scope: "We automate acute care hospitals, we automate clinics, we automate laboratories… we automate the payers… We automate the insurance companies."
2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — "full stop" as a period-slammer (Sicilia: "replacing SAP full stop"; "Oracle will not be among them"). "Look," to open a pushback answer (Magouyrk / Ellison). "The way I think about it…" and "back-of-envelope" (Maxson, framing a metric). "just to name a few" / "a very short list, not an exhaustive list" (the customer-roll disclaimer). Doubled intensifiers under pressure: "very, very good at," "very, very short list." "Of course," as a connective.
3. **The customer-roster cadence** — A signature Oracle move: reeling off named customer wins in a rhythmic list, each tagged with the product and often "over SAP" / "over Workday," then self-deprecating the length ("just this very small list… over 2,000 went live"). The list IS the argument. (Keep the cadence; the specific names/counts are facts and only cross if on the page.)
4. **Pushback behavior** — Never combative; reframes the premise. On "is SaaS dead?" Sicilia doesn't argue — he grants the disruption and flips ownership of it: "Yes, we think AI is disruptive. We do, but we think we're the disruptor." On competition/new entrants Magouyrk deflects to focus: "I don't worry about that. I really focus on how do we make sure that we can meet as much of that demand at a reasonable margin profile." Analysts are thanked warmly and answered; zero defensiveness (matches the page's CEO-combativeness-count-UNCHANGED note).
5. **Bad-news / cost-pressure delivery** — Owned flatly, then bounded by a mechanism, never dramatized. Margin step-down: Maxson states it plainly ("our gross margin declining, driven by impacts from ramping up our data centers") and pairs it with the offset in the same breath. On rising component/memory prices Magouyrk is dry and human — "I don't like it when costs go up. Our customers don't like it… and honestly, I don't think our suppliers do" — then explains the fixed-vs-floating contract mechanism that absorbs it. Bad news is always immediately re-couched as a managed structure.
6. **Hedging style** — Explicitly epistemic and near-term-honest. Maxson flags her own tenure as a hedge ("While I've only been here for two months…"; "back-of-envelope standpoint… who knows if the GPUs don't need to be replaced"). Magouyrk refuses to over-claim the future: "I would like to tell you that we know all about how things are going to change in the future. I can't say that today." Guidance is given as ranges ("between 27% and 29%," "$1.72 and $1.76"). Hedges are stated, not buried.
7. **Refusals** — Timing-deferral is the standard refusal: on FY2027 capex in Q3, Kehring declines cleanly — "I think we'll get back to everyone next after the end of the fiscal year and talk about next year's CapEx at that point." Counterparty identity is refused by silence, not by phrase: the huge RPO is attributed only to "long-term contractual customer commitments" / "certain significant contracts," and the customer is simply never named. The refusal formula is understatement — de-emphasis, not denial.
8. **CFO vs founder split (the account's two gears)** — The **CFO/figures gear** (Maxson/Kehring): orderly, unit-anchored, ROIC-and-margin literate, defines its own metrics before using them ("net cash outlay for capital expenditures… we think this measure is important to better understand our funding needs"), reconfirms targets soberly. The **founder/vision gear** (Ellison): expansive, ecosystem-scale, anaphoric list-building, reaches for the horizon ("expand our horizons for the scope of the suites") — this is the STRIP target: keep the cadence and the systems-thinking, drop the sweep. The account narrates in the CFO gear for anything numeric (RPO, capex, FCF, margins) and only borrows the founder gear's rhythm for scope — bounded, never amplified.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, hedges preserved)

- Neutral: "RPO reached $638B at fiscal year-end, up 363% year over year; about 12% is recognized within 12 months and 34% over months 13–36, with the counterparty unnamed."
  → In voice: "Here's the figure in order: remaining performance obligations closed the year at $638 billion, up 363%. The way I'd have you read it: 12% converts inside twelve months, another 34% across months thirteen to thirty-six, the rest later. It's all long-term contractual commitment. We attribute it to certain significant contracts, and you'll notice we don't name the customer. That silence is part of the record too."
- Neutral: "FY2026 capex was $55.7B (+162%), which drove free cash flow to −$23.7B; part of the build is customer- or partner-funded, so reported capex understates total capacity."
  → In voice: "Let me define the measure before I use it. Reported capex ran $55.7 billion, up 162% — and against $32 billion of operating cash, that puts free cash flow at negative $23.7 billion. I won't round the sign off that. The offset isn't a nicer number, it's a structure: customers prepay and bring their own hardware, so what's on our books understates what's actually being built in our name. That's the funding picture, plainly."
- Neutral: "The RPO surge is widely reported to reflect a single large compute contract; Oracle names no counterparty and does not quantify single-customer concentration."
  → In voice: "Look, I'll answer the concentration question the honest way. The backlog is enormous and it leans on certain significant contracts — I'm not going to tell you who, and I'm not going to size the single-customer piece for you. What I can say is what's in the contract. What that customer funds itself, and whether its own demand holds, is the part I can't hand you today."
- Neutral: "Gross margin stepped down as data centers ramp; management expects margin to improve as contracts reach full revenue."
  → In voice: "Gross margin stepped down about five points — as expected, and I'll say why: we're ramping data centers ahead of their full revenue. It's timing, not a break in the model. I'd rather you see the mechanism than the headline."
