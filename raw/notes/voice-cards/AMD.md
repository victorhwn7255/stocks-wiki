## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Long, looping sentences built from comma-spliced qualifiers, carpeted with "you know." Not clipped; she accretes hedges and conditions clause by clause before landing the point. "You know, first of all, when we think about CPU TAM, I mean, we've always said that CPUs are a very critical part of data center infrastructure, and, you know, that's been where we've invested." The rhythm is deliberative, engineer-explaining-to-the-room, never staccato.

2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — **"let's call it"** is the defining Su tic: an approximate-label hedge she uses to name an estimate without over-committing — "let's call it AI demand", "let's call it the 18% CAGR, approximately $60 billion", "let's call it starting with initial volume in Q3", "let's call it general purpose CPU TAM". **"you know"** as an omnipresent softener (multiple per sentence). **"The way to think about this/that is…"** / **"The way we're thinking about it is…"** / **"The best way to think about…"** as her framing device. **"we feel really good about" / "we feel very good about" / "very well positioned"** as measured-confidence markers. **"on track"** for roadmap items. First-name-then-answer opener: "Yeah, sure, Josh"; "Sure, Ross"; "Sure, Vivek"; "Actually, Blaine."

3. **Metaphor habitat** — Almost none; this is the tell. Su does NOT analogize. Her habitat is specs, process nodes, and optimization vectors: "performance per socket and per watt", "throughput optimized, power optimized, cost optimized, AI infrastructure optimized", core counts, CPU-to-GPU ratios ("1-to-4 or 1-to-8 configuration… getting closer to a 1-to-1"). Her one figurative phrase all call is "compute capabilities in our tool chest." She quantifies where a showman would analogize.

4. **Pushback behavior** — Decomposes a multi-part question and works through it in order, flagged out loud: "Yeah. Okay. A lot of pieces of that question, Tom, so let me try to get through it. First of all…" On a rival she **grants then reframes to portfolio breadth** — "No question, Arm is good architecture. It has a place in the data center market… we view it as more point products relative to a portfolio." Measured, never dismissive, never a dunk. She reframes competitive questions into "you need a broad portfolio; not all CPUs are the same."

5. **Bad-news delivery** — Owned flatly and folded into planning, no drama. China: "The data center AI was actually down modestly because of the China transition." Memory-cost hit to H2 PCs/gaming: "we are planning for second half PC shipments to be lower due to higher memory and component costs… we are planning the business accordingly" / "We're taking that into account in our overall model." Bad news becomes a modeled input, not a confession.

6. **Hedging style** — Data-anchored and provisional. Beyond "let's call it": **"It's very hard to call exactly, but…"**; ties confidence explicitly to **visibility** — "When I say good visibility, it's visibility down to which data centers the GPU is going to be installed in"; cites method — "we've looked at it both bottoms up… as well as just doing some clear workload analysis." Ranges over false precision: "55%-58%", "three to five years", "1-to-4 or 1-to-8." Her confidence is evidentiary, not emotional.

7. **Refusals** — Declines to invent a number she doesn't have, pointing to dependency: "I don't have a number that I can tell you in terms of relative ASPs because it really depends on the workload that is being run." "It's very hard to call exactly." (CFO Jean Hu refuses differently — by repetition: pushed by Stacy Rasgon for an ex-China sequential figure, she twice returns "the China revenue in Q1 is not material" rather than give the number.)

8. **CEO vs CFO split** — **Lisa Su** carries the roadmap-and-portfolio narrative: measured, "let's call it", "the way to think about it", three-category factoring, named-product march, confidence-tied-to-visibility. **Jean Hu** carries the figures in her own register: **"puts and takes"** (repeated), **"tailwinds"** (multiple/first-secondly stacking), basis points ("up 170 basis points"), guidance as midpoint-plus-or-minus ("approximately $11.2 billion ± $300 million"), and a slightly halting, ESL-inflected syntax ("That will have different puts and it takes in Q4"). The account speaks figures in HER register: sequential, bounded, tailwind-and-headwind framed.
