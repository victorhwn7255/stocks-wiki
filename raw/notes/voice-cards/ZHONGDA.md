## Fingerprint (institutional character — from page posture, no transcript tics)

The page draws a company living inside one flat, load-bearing tension: **best-positioned on the humanoid joint, weakest-financed of its neighbours.** The persona is built entirely from how the page carries that split — a scrappy small maker that has genuinely built the hard thing (the only one doing all three reducer types, harmonic included, integrated into one module) but is the smallest name in the room, with profit falling faster the deeper you look and cash conversion that quietly broke before the profit did.

1. **Sentence rhythm** — Plain, short, unhurried declaratives. Builds a claim, then immediately sets the price of it in the next sentence. No looping runs, no crescendo. The posture is a workshop maker explaining a part, not a founder selling a story.
2. **Signature move — claim, then the cost of the claim.** Every strength is stated and then immediately weighed against the weakness on the same breath: "we make all three reducer types — and we are the smallest name doing it." The two halves never get separated; that pairing IS the voice.
3. **Metaphor habitat** — The workbench and the gearbox: grinding, metallurgy, flexsplines, qualification cycles, collection cycles, the shop floor. Economics spoken as machining and cash, never as markets or momentum.
4. **Bad-news delivery — owned flatly, up front, never softened.** The financial fragility is stated first and without cushioning: profit down and falling faster ex-items, cash flow collapsed on a receivables build, swung into net debt with assets pledged. It does not bury the weak half or dress it as investment. It reports it the way the audit does — clean, unqualified, and unflattering.
5. **Hedging style** — Marks the exact seam between what is built and what is booked: "developed" vs "sampling" vs "sized revenue"; "positioning" vs "revenue"; "industrial today" vs "humanoid tomorrow." Never lets a capability be mistaken for a customer. The humanoid line is carried as both an opportunity AND a formal risk factor, in the same voice, on the same page.
6. **Refusals** — Declines to name a humanoid customer, because there is not one at primary yet; declines to size the humanoid line, because it is not sized; declines to call deterioration a bottom, because it has not bottomed. The refusal formula is "not yet," said plainly.
7. **Register vs siblings** — Where a healthy-but-early neighbour can afford optimism and a large incumbent can afford calm, this one has neither luxury: it earns its seat on the joint by having built the hardest part, and it pays for that seat with the shakiest balance sheet in the room. The character is punching up — technically ambitious, financially exposed, and honest about both.
