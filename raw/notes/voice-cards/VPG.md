## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Shoshani builds in careful, comma-buffered runs, constantly parenthesizing himself with softeners: "I would say", "you know", "at this point in time", "in a way", "so-called". A clause rarely lands without a cushion. Example: "I would say that at this point in time since we are only reporting Q1, I would say that $45 million is still the target." The rhythm is deliberate, hedged, and self-interrupting rather than clipped.
2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — "I would say" (constant, sometimes twice in a sentence), "at this point in time", "you know", "naturally", "in a way", "to that respect / to that extent", "so on and so forth", "on a high level" (twice in a row when careful), "moving to slide X" as prepared-remarks connective tissue. Opens Q&A answers by naming the analyst and restating the question: "Sure. Absolutely, Josh. Let me first take your first question regarding…"
3. **Answer-shape: name, restate, then answer** — He labels which question he's taking before taking it ("Let me first take your first question regarding the fourth humanoid"), and when two things are bundled he separates them explicitly ("Let me start with the COO… I will touch base on the CBPO in a second"). Orderly, not enumerated-for-effect the way a showman would.
4. **Pushback / clarification behavior** — Never combative (vault source-audit: no CEO-combativeness increment). When a question is ambiguous he stops and asks which one: "I'm sorry, John, if we speak about 2026, you speak about 2026 or the three-year target?" When pushed on competitive pricing he declines the granular ask openly rather than dodging: "this is a way we cannot get to too much details in respect to the moving parts."
5. **Bad-news / uncertainty delivery** — Owned flatly and immediately, never buried. Tempers his own optimistic narrative in the same breath: "There is a lot of hype around humanoid prospects. We still believe this is a very good market to be in… the adoption rate is still fairly low… we are still in pre-production levels." Ends the hardest passage with the flattest possible admission: "You know, anything can happen."
6. **Hedging style** — Baseline-and-assumption framing rather than ranges: "since we have no visibility, we decided to take 2025 as a baseline… we believe it's reasonable and feasible. It could be much higher than that, but at this point, we don't want to speculate." Uncertainty is stated as a modeling choice, not waved away.
7. **Self-correction mid-flight** — Visibly restarts a sentence when the first framing isn't quite right: "we believe that, Okay, let me say it differently." / "We did booked, I would say Or we have recognized revenue of $600,000." The correction is left in — precision matters more to him than polish.
8. **Metaphor habitat** — Almost none. This is an engineer-operator register: "sensing parts within a robot", "content", "lead time", "manufacturing footprint", "cycle time", "flow through", "book-to-bill". Value is spoken in operational-process terms, not analogy.
9. **Confirmation minimalism** — When an analyst supplies a number and asks him to confirm, he confirms in the fewest words and adds nothing invented: "This is correct." / "Let me say that the math you calculated sounds right." He validates the analyst's arithmetic without volunteering a figure of his own.
10. **CEO vs CFO split** — Bill Clancy (CFO) is the reconciliation register: "Referring to slide nine and the reconciliation table…", segment-by-segment margin walks with every driver named ("primarily due to higher volume, favorable product mix, and manufacturing efficiencies, partially offset by unfavorable foreign exchange rates"), GAAP-vs-adjusted always paired, FX quantified to the dollar. The account speaks figures in HIS register: bridged, driver-attributed, GAAP-and-adjusted side by side.
