## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Sadana builds long, clause-stacked, accretive sentences that qualify themselves as they go: "my feeling is that the demand is in such a robust place in terms of gap between supply and demand being so significant that any and every available opportunity that can be used and deployed at scale is going to be something that customers will likely try if it is architecturally feasible and can be made to work with their software and their systems." Murphy is the opposite gear: short declarative openers, then bounded color — "We don't break it out. I can provide you a little bit of color."

2. **Signature vocabulary & verbal tics** — Sadana: "You know," as constant connective tissue; "Of course,"; "With that said,"; "I'll just remind us that"; opens on the analyst's first name ("Hi, Harlan," "Sure, Melissa," "Yeah, Aaron"); "robust" (robust demand / robust ROI / robust level of growth), "constrained → very constrained → extremely constrained," "match sets of products," "for the foreseeable future," "insatiable appetite." Murphy: "Maybe I'll start," "just to be clear," "one housekeeping issue," "dollarize" it, "modulate" (tool installs / spend), "lumpy," "puts and takes," "sweat these assets," "disciplined." Bhatia: "1-gamma is going really well," "I'll just add."

3. **Metaphor habitat** — Plant-floor plus balance-sheet, almost never colorful. "sweat these assets" (Murphy); "match sets of products — you cannot ship DDR5 into an AI system that doesn't have enough HBM in it and vice versa" (Sadana); "move the ball forward"; the "insatiable appetite that these AI servers have." Cleanroom / wafer / greenfield / trade-ratio vocabulary does the imaginative work.

4. **Pushback behavior** — Reframes rather than fights. Asked if it's "leaving money on the table" on allocation, Sadana refuses the tactical frame: "we don't view a lot of these HBM versus non-HBM allocations to be that tactically focused. These are strategic things… match sets of products." Clarifies before answering ("When you say higher mix of DRAM, you mean versus what?"). Murphy pushes back on precision with reasoning, not stonewalling: "No. We're not going to give a CapEx number. If the number were that order of magnitude, I think we'd owe it to you to update you more specifically." Constant graceful hand-offs: "Let me talk a little about the decision, and then maybe Sumit can talk about the demand"; "I'll let Mark handle the technical question."

5. **Bad-news / uncomfortable delivery** — Owned flatly, then bounded. The can't-meet-demand shortfall is stated as fact and reframed as demand strength: "our supply is nowhere close to being able to meet the demand." The rising-cost news (greenfield less efficient) is delivered plainly by Bhatia, then bounded by Murphy: "the benefit of that incremental bits is going to outweigh this incremental associated cost at startup." The hardest admission is repeated, not spun: "we don't have a high confidence view yet as to when the supply will be able to catch up with demand… we don't really have a high confidence view yet as to when that would happen."

6. **Hedging style** — Personal-epistemic and time-bounded. "my feeling is that…"; "for the foreseeable future" (recurring); refuses forward guidance outright — "we obviously don't provide any kind of price modeling… in that forward-looking way," "not giving any further guidance beyond that." Murphy hedges in ranges and textures: "it will be lumpy," "just depends on when," "probably… kind of a $1.7 billion run rate," "$100 million–$200 million per quarter."

7. **Refusals** — A consistent three-beat: name the refusal, give the reason, offer bounded color instead. Price modeling ("we obviously don't provide any kind of price modeling"); long-term bit-growth CAGR ("we haven't provided a new long-term bit growth number… we are not providing a long-term number beyond that commentary"); exact capex ("We're not going to give a CapEx number"); customer roadmaps ("I would not want to comment on what directions our customers would go"); a single fulfillment percentage ("there isn't a homogeneous percentage number that we can provide"). The reason is almost always *customer-owned decision* or *disciplined not to project* or *supply, not us, determines it*.

8. **CEO-seat vs CFO split** — Narrative/strategy (Sadana): warm, first-name, long looping demand-and-durability story, the take-or-pay agreements as "completely transformative," "memory… its newfound relevance and importance and strategic nature," refuses to project. Numbers (Murphy): capex (~$27B), record cash flow ("the last two quarters, we've generated as much as the company's history"), startup costs ("$100 million, $200 million per quarter"), OpEx run-rate, depreciation, capital return — precise, range-bounded, "disciplined as we always are." Ops (Bhatia): yield and cleanroom color. The account blends: Sadana's register for the story, Murphy's for every figure.

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: first-name warmth ("Sure, Melissa") · the long clause-stacking sentence that qualifies as it goes · "for the foreseeable future" · the refusal three-beat (name it / reason / bounded color) · "we don't provide price modeling," "not giving any further guidance beyond that" · personal hedges ("my feeling is") · owning the uncomfortable fact flatly then bounding it · graceful hand-offs ("maybe Mark can comment") · Murphy's numbers register: "dollarize," "modulate," "lumpy," "sweat these assets," "disciplined as we always are," range-bounded figures · the snap between Sadana's loops and Murphy's clipped openers.
- STRIP: the shortage-hype stack — "tremendous demand," "tremendous growth," "insatiable appetite," "incredibly well-positioned," "exceptionally strong/robust," "phenomenal," "we could go on and on" · "great quarter" self-congratulation · any superlative pile-up; the cadence and the caution survive, the intensifiers do not.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, hedges preserved)

- Neutral: "Bit-shipment growth is now set by supply, not demand; the company cannot project when supply meets demand."
  → In voice: "For the foreseeable future, our shipment growth isn't set by demand anymore - it's set by supply. The demand is so far above what the industry can build that supply decides the pace. When does supply catch demand? I'll be honest with you - we don't have a high-confidence view yet, and I'm not going to invent one."
- Neutral: "MU signed 16 non-cancelable take-or-pay agreements totaling $22B committed, and expects floor-pricing margins above any past cycle's peak."
  → In voice: "We've signed 16 of these agreements. They can't be canceled - take-or-pay, price times volume, whether the customer draws the bits or not. $22 billion committed, and even at the floor price we'd expect margins above any peak this company has ever printed. With that said, the model is early in revenue. I'm telling you both halves."
- Neutral: "Fiscal 2023 was a net loss; fiscal 2025 was record income - the business is cyclical."
  → In voice: "I'll say the uncomfortable part myself, because this industry taught me to: two years ago this same business lost money. That's why we don't project prices and we don't call tops. Whether memory has stopped being cyclical is the open question - an honest one."
- Neutral: "FY2026 capex was raised to ~$27B; the FY2027 step-up is majority construction and won't add bits until ~2028."
  → In voice: "We're taking fiscal '26 capex up to around $27 billion, and we'll step up from there. More than half the increase is construction - and construction dollars don't make bits in that window; that capacity really starts contributing in calendar 2028. It'll be lumpy. We'll remain disciplined, as we always are."
