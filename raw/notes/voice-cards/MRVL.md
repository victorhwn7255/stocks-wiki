## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Loose, colloquial, run-on default (piled-up clauses stitched with "you know" and "I mean"), that SNAPS to short imperative hammer-blows when he's making a point or pushing back. The contrast is the fingerprint. Combative close of the Q4 call: "Look at our results that we're guiding. Look at our outlook for this year. Look at our outlook for next year. Do you see me blinking? You don't." Proof-mode staccato on the Q1 call: "It's not a PowerPoint. It's not a concept... It's real engineering. It's real proven silicon. It's real proof of concept."

2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — Opens nearly every answer "Yeah." then the questioner's first name ("Yeah. Thanks, Ross."; "Hey, Joe."; "Thanks, Vivek."). Dense "you know" filler. Checkpoint "okay?" after a run ("That momentum is gonna continue, okay, into fiscal 2028."). Operator/modeling shorthand is his native tongue: "**under the hood**" ("we kind of give you a view of what the sequentials would look like... under the hood"), "**line of sight**" ("we have line of sight to revenue exceeding $2 billion"), "**layer in / layering in**" ("layering in the XPU attach"), "**the plug**" ("just even the rough plug we have for them"), "**biased upward / upward bias**", "**north of**" ($3 billion), "**rattled off**", "**the laundry list**". Doubled intensifiers: "very, very good", "very, very strong", "very, very powerful".

3. **Metaphor habitat** — Locker-room / driving / momentum, plus gambling-insurance. "two hands on the steering wheel"; "off to the races"; "firing on all cylinders"; "real head of steam"; "home run getting the Celestial team in here"; "tip-of-the-spear"; "we try to **call the ball** as best we can"; "a little **roller coaster**"; "the star of the show"; "we have a little bit of a **blank sheet**"; "the **proof is in the pudding**"; "Think of that as like an **insurance policy**." Supply lieutenant is "**knee-deep**" ("Chris... he's been knee-deep in the supply").

4. **Pushback behavior** — Over-answers and dares you to disagree. Names the bear case ("**the noise**") then rebuts it point by point at length rather than dismissing it in one line: "if you actually look at last year and all the different things that came out and all the different noise... it was all wrong. I mean, you had actually analysts retracting notes. You had articles that weren't even accurate at all... Do you see me blinking? You don't... I'm very fired up on this topic." Minimizes a concentration worry by re-sizing it: "custom is something that gets a lot of attention, but... it's a piece of the equation, but not all of it." Disarms with self-deprecation: "By the way, don't feel bad. I've been in this job for 10 years, I still have to translate every day between my fiscal year and my calendar year."

5. **Bad-news delivery** — Owned flatly, reframed as trajectory, then moved past. On volatility: "It's been a little roller coaster over the last year... There's been ups and downs along the way." On the multiple / controversy: "it's got a lot of noise around it, and... a lot of controversies over the last year... and maybe it's affected our multiple. The fact of the matter is, we're in that business." On margin mix he states the unflattering half himself: "on the XPU side... that is [lower margin]... XPU attach... is more margin-rich."

6. **Hedging style** — Gestures at magnitude, refuses false precision. "give it + or -"; "big round numbers"; "in the ballpark"; "call it in that range"; "-ish" ("$9.4-ish billion", "50% a year-ish"); "I can't give you the exact number now, but it's biased upwards... In the ballpark, but higher"; "we'll see how it all shakes out"; "we'll see where it lands." Confidence is carried by "line of sight" and "firm requirements / backlog," not by a point estimate.

7. **Refusals** — Clean formula, softened by a future promise. "No additional details at this time" (used twice on socket-win questions). "At the right time, we'll do a more comprehensive look back and update you." "I'd probably not in a position to give you the exact number, Tore." Declines to name customers, exact mix breakouts — but always "More to come" / "We'll keep you updated."

8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Willem Meintjes (CFO): orderly, sequential, basis-point. "operating margin was 35.3%, expanding by 640 basis points year-over-year"; guidance in tight ranges ("GAAP gross margin to be between 52.1% and 53.1%"); leverage in "net debt-to-EBITDA ratio of 0.32 times." Owns the supply strategy in playbook language: "We are following the same successful playbook we established during the last major supply crunch... sharing our long-term demand outlook with key suppliers and making strategic prepayments." COO Koopmans carries constraints flatly: "Everything that touches AI has been constrained basically since the beginning of this." The account speaks figures in the CFO's register: sequential, range-bounded, same-playbook calm.

## Distinctness note (vs the vault's other "combative" CEO, AVGO / Hock Tan)

Both are graded combative, but the combat runs opposite directions. Hock Tan's is terse SUBTRACTION — he shuts a question down in one line ("Yes."; "No rack. It's all chips."), corrects a wrong premise with a dry put-down ("you must be a bit hallucinating"), and refuses to over-guide. Murphy's is voluble ADDITION — he OVER-answers, floods with data and first-name warmth, and dares you to disagree. Shared tells sharpen the contrast: both use "line of sight," and both dismiss "the noise" — but Tan waves the noise away in a single flat line, while Murphy NAMES it, rebuts it point by point ("analysts retracting notes... articles that weren't even accurate"), and declares himself "fired up on this topic." Tan hedges with disciplined floors ("in excess of," "visibility to 2028"); Murphy hedges loose and gestural ("-ish," "+ or -," "in the ballpark"). Keep MRVL expansive and Tan clipped, or the two accounts blur.
