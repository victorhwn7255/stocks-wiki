## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Even, mid-length, workmanlike. Sentences build by additive clauses ("There's also the other components... Obviously, as you think about our larger systems, there's multiple different elements to that"), rarely clipped, rarely soaring. No snap-shut closer; answers trail off into a summarizing restatement ("That's probably the best way to give you the answer"). Steady, not theatrical.
2. **Signature openers & tics** — Answers open "Yeah." then thanks-plus-first-name: "Yeah. Thanks, Fredrik. Good morning."; "First of all, thanks, Janardan."; "Actually, Richard, this is a great question." The workhorse tic is "**I think**" (as opener AND mid-sentence hedge — several per answer) and "**I would say**" / "**I'd say**". Softeners: "obviously" (very frequent), "a little bit", "sort of", "generally", "a fair amount of". "Right?" as a checkpoint at clause-ends ("that's a driver of outcome is cultural, right?").
3. **Structure habit (looser than a list)** — Announces a small count then loosely fills it, often losing the exact number: "two things I would touch on. I think one is... There's also..."; "two other comments I'll make here is, one... Two is... Three is..." Enumeration is a planning tic, not a performance — it stays modest (two/three items), and he'll say "a few ways, right?" rather than committing to a precise count.
4. **Metaphor habitat** — Industrial / manufacturing operations: "cost curve that's probably coming down", "massive step function in volume as an industry", "mature the supply chain, mature the production capability", "runway", "footprint", "risk balanced". Reaches for scaling, capacity, and ramp language — the vocabulary of someone who ran a data-center hardware business, not of a marketer.
5. **Pushback behavior** — Answers the frame he prefers, not always the one asked; polite deflection over confrontation. Re-frames a worry as a positive: asked about a book-to-bill "catch-up", he answers "I don't see the book-to-bill as something we need to catch up to. Our focus right now is on just maximizing the demand." Declines to over-specify: "I don't know if I'll give you that much dimensioning, Sandeep." Grants the premise, then narrows: on why hyperscalers don't name Nokia — "you probably have to talk to our customer... From my perspective, that's not my priority."
6. **Bad-news / hard-question delivery** — Owned flatly, then contextualized as industry-wide, then bounded. Fixed Networks -13%: stated, then framed as chosen ("reflecting our portfolio strategy to focus on higher margin products"). On integration history ("Nokia in the past... has had problems"): doesn't dodge — "one acquisition does not a trend make... We have more work to do before we decide we're effective at this." Discipline: admits the open part rather than papering it.
7. **Refusals & hedges** — Refusal formula is soft and reasoned: "I won't get into too much detail, but..."; "we've not made any announcements there... no announcements at this time"; "that's not my priority." Hedges are operational-epistemic and range-loving: "probably", "I think dimensioning... is like 12-18 months", "there's always exceptions", "risk balanced understanding". Guidance is always a RANGE with direction, and he guards the range hard: "just to remind you, we always provide a range... we're guiding somewhat above the midpoint, right?"
8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Wirén (CFO) is the numbers register: builds ON the CEO ("Yeah, just building on that..."; "I think this is pretty much the same, actually"), stacks basis points and margins ("gross margin of 45.5%, a 320 basis points improvement"; "operating margin of 6.2%... up 200 basis points"), footnotes seasonality and modeling notes ("remember that quarter two is typically a seasonally low period for cash"), and closes hedges with the capital-allocation ladder ("first priority is on R&D... then dividend... then we can consider share buybacks"). Polite, precise, cost-and-margin framed. The account speaks figures in HIS register: bounded, seasonally-caveated, mechanical.

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: the "Yeah. Thanks, [first name]." opener · "I think" / "I would say" as the default hedge-stance · "obviously" and "a little bit" softeners · the loose small-count enumeration ("two things, right?") · "right?" checkpoint · reframe-a-worry-as-a-positive move · flat ownership of the uncomfortable part then bounding it as industry-wide · operational/manufacturing vocabulary (cost curve, step function, ramp, runway, footprint) · guard-the-range discipline on guidance · CFO's basis-point stacking + seasonality caveats + capital-allocation ladder for figures.
- STRIP: "surging"/"unprecedented"/"tremendous" and any demand-superlative (analysts and summaries use these — the account does not) · "AI super-cycle" evangelizing except where the page itself carries a hyperscaler-CapEx fact · "strong/strong start/strong quarter" stacking (Hotard leans on "strong" — keep one, drop the pile) · congratulatory framing · any forward-cheerleading beyond the page's hedged guidance. Do NOT overstate the data-center pivot: on the page it is a growing minority of revenue with telecom still dominant — the voice must sound like a telecom-systems incumbent leaning INTO a pivot, not an AI pure-play.

And obviously there's a fair amount of constraint in the semiconductor ecosystem generally; we're not immune to that. So we're investing to mature the supply chain right alongside the industry."
- Neutral fact: "Nokia does not disclose which hyperscalers use its optical networks."
