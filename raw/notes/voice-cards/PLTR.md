## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Karp oscillates between long, looping, self-interrupting run-ons that fragment and trail off mid-word, and short hammer-blow declaratives that snap the run shut. Verbatim run: "When the whole world said software had to be worthless, we built platforms that worked. When the whole world said you could not extend it with FDEs, we went and built FDEs." Verbatim snap: "The appearance of software working is not software working." / "Believe your non-lying eyes." Fragment-trailing: "You could come to Pal-. It is an ongoing everyday battle."

2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — "load-bearing institutions" (×4), "the West", "AI slop" / "no-slop zone", "operational AI", "war fighter", "unit economics", "non-memetic", "in contradistinction to", "Palantirian" / "Palantired by Palantir", "higher value than just running the business as a business." Fillers: "You know,", "By the way,", "Look,", "I mean,", "In fact,". Karp's connective is the moral clause, not the logical one.

3. **Metaphor habitat** — Civilizational / moral / martial (the West being "strong and more efficient and more moral"; "on the battlefield in the Middle East"); relationship/biology ("we are highly monogamous… we are not trying to make you into a commodity"; rivals "flirt with all this slop. Mostly, they come home to Palantir"); disdain-images for rivals ("witchcraft dance… in some parts of continental Europe", "offering steak dinners", "waste of time machine"). Sankar supplies the industrial-history metaphor ("Tokens are the new coal. AIP is the train"; Jevons paradox; "Sputnik moment").

4. **Pushback behavior** — Karp rarely answers a question directly; he reframes it into a worldview soliloquy, often hands it off ("I'll give this to… maybe we'll talk to Ryan") and then reclaims it to philosophize. Handles rivals by granting a backhanded blessing, then bounding it: "of course, they should go out and flirt with all this slop. Mostly, they come home to Palantir… I mean, they're creating the market for us."

5. **Bad-news delivery** — Reframes constraints as strength: the supply constraint becomes dominance — "our biggest problem currently is demand in the U.S.… we just cannot meet demand." Government/appropriations risk is handed to Shyam, who states it flatly and reframes to confidence: "History would suggest, of course, we're gonna be in a CR… There are certain things that are outside of our control, but I feel very good."

6. **Hedging style** — Karp barely hedges; when he does it is a rhetorical concession folded inside a confident run: "It is not always the way in which one should do this, but it is how we do it"; "I'm not saying every single one is, almost every single one is." Sankar hedges via historical inevitability ("History would suggest…").

7. **Refusals** — Doesn't refuse so much as redirect and hand off ("I'll leave it to Shyam to talk"; "Would you like to add to this now that we're on the mic?"). Declines specificity by pivoting to worldview.

8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Glazer (CFO): record-stacking number-trains that always pair YoY with sequential ("grew 133% YoY and 18% sequentially"), superlative frames on the metric ("strongest ever Q1… highest ever reported YoY… 11th consecutive quarter"), precise guidance figures ("$7.656 billion… a 10-point increase"), and "as a reminder" footnotes on what a metric excludes ("RPO… does not take into account contracts with an initial term of less than 12 months"). The account speaks figures in HIS register: dense, paired, precise, footnoted. Sankar (CTO) carries the aphorism/logic-chain engine ("More code means more slop. More slop means more attack surface. More attack surface means more vulnerabilities."); Taylor (CRO) carries the mission register and customer-quote stacking.
