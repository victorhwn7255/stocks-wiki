## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length (Brennan)** — Steady, mid-length architecture sentences that lay out a system layer by layer, then close on a plain declarative. Builds from the physical scale-out: "As AI clusters scale from tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of GPUs, connectivity is no longer just about bandwidth." Rarely clipped; rarely nested-clause dense. The rhythm is a briefing, not a sermon.
2. **Signature vocabulary & tics (Brennan)** — Recurring anchor phrases: "reliability has become Credo's **North Star**," "**purpose-built** for this transition," "**bulletproof reliability**," "**vertically integrated**," "system-level," "full spectrum … from die-to-die … to facility-wide optical," "we're in the **early innings**." Framing-verb: "extending both **inward toward the silicon and outward across the data center**." Opens Q&A answers plainly ("Absolutely.", "Sure.", "I think the important message there is…") and closes with a calm confidence marker ("I feel quite good.", "I feel good about our position."). Supply answer tic: "**owning the entire bill of materials**," "leaning in," "clusters don't get built without the small complementary connectivity chips."
3. **Metaphor habitat** — Almost entirely infrastructure/plumbing: the "connectivity fabric," "in-rack and rack-to-rack," "row-scale and facility-wide," "reach" measured in meters, laser count, power envelopes. Not physics-poetry, not sports — the metaphors ARE the product (the network as a fabric that ties clusters together).
4. **Pushback behavior** — Answers directly and without heat. Concentration and supply questions (the two the analysts hammer) get answered straight — the CFO reads the full customer split aloud rather than dodging; the CEO grounds supply confidence in a five-year investment history. Grants a rival's/partner's framing rather than fighting it (on NVIDIA: "they've been really outspoken that where you can use copper, you will use copper … That equation's not gonna change"). Vault source-audit note: **no CEO combativeness** across either call.
5. **Bad-news / limits delivery** — Owned flatly and moved past, not buried. "We don't have any revenue for scale-up. In fact, that market is relatively small in comparison today." On a customer possibly slipping under 10%: "they may not be a 10% customer for the full quarter … if that makes sense." Named limits carried plainly: "I don't think we can point to one where we're fully deployed other than xAI."
6. **Hedging style (Brennan)** — Hedges specifically on **timing he says he doesn't control**: "we don't ultimately control the outcome of when those transceivers go to market"; "the exact timing of the transition will move somewhat as the platforms evolve"; "there's always a rumor about delays." Couples the hedge to a readiness claim ("If the industry is ready, we're ready right now.").
7. **Refusals** — Declines to rank the three optical legs by size ("You're right that there will be one that has the largest revenue, but … all three categories are growing very quickly") — refuses the precise breakout, gives the aggregate instead. Declines precise transition timing. The refusal formula is redirection-to-the-aggregate, never a flat "no comment."
8. **CEO vs CFO split (Fleming)** — Dan Fleming speaks figures in dense sequential trains with basis-point precision: "operating margin improved by 2,144 basis points," "up 7% sequentially and above the high end of our guidance range," "revenue grew 157%." Reads concentration completely and flatly: "34% for the largest, 27% for the second largest, 16% for the third, and notably the fourth customer, which was at 10%." Answers "a question that wasn't asked" preemptively. Bounds guidance with a live caveat: "based on the current tariff regime, which remains fluid." The account speaks numbers in HIS register: complete, sequential, caveated.
