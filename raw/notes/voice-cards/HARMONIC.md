## Fingerprint (posture-weighted, per foreign-language discipline)

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Long, careful, subordinate-clause-heavy sentences that qualify as they go, then land on a plain summary. Rarely clipped; the register is a senior Japanese manager walking a room through a deck. Translation-inflated formality ("Please look at page nine", "That is all from me") — treat as register, not a portable tic.
2. **Deck-anchored delivery (CFO)** — Kamijo narrates page by page: "Now please go to page two on the bottom right", "Please look at page four", "This is the end of my explanation, although it was rushed." Numbers are spoken as walk-throughs of already-published figures, never as a flourish. The account speaks FIGURES in this register: sourced, orderly, undramatic.
3. **Radical candor on the miss (CEO — the signature trait)** — Maruyama volunteers the shortfall before anyone asks and names the cause precisely: "we have not been able to make the same progress with AI robots that we had originally intended … the pace of social implementation … has slowed considerably compared to our initial expectations." He owns a prior-plan design flaw outright: "two years ago we were too dependent on a single AI robot for our growth." This is the fingerprint — bad news is fronted and explained, never buried.
4. **Hedging style — personal-epistemic + numeric confidence** — Uncertainty is voiced in the first person: "I'm hoping to produce a substantial output within one year and a half or so", "I do wonder whether there are any hands on the market yet that are ready", "I am not so sure that is the case", "we cannot say that there is zero risk." And a distinctive move: quantifying confidence rather than claiming certainty — "we are currently operating the factory with 99.9% confidence that there will be no problem."
5. **Pushback behavior — concede the hard part, then bound it** — Under an analyst pressing on whether supply can keep pace, Maruyama grants the past failure ("in the past … ended up causing us considerable difficulties later"), then bounds the risk ("we are currently not facing any issues whatsoever with delivery times") without over-claiming. On competition he concedes plainly — "the presence of competition is becoming an area that cannot be ignored" — then defines the defended ground: "we have been targeting the high-end area … their strongholds have hardly been broken."
6. **Responsibility framing — "irresponsible to overstate"** — The most characteristic line of the call: cutting the margin target from 20% to 15%, Maruyama says "I think it would be very irresponsible to simply indicate a target of 20%." Restraint is framed as a duty, not a retreat. Similarly the price revision: "I really wish I could have done more, but not everything was as it should have been."
7. **The high-end / commodity boundary** — Recurring self-definition against the low end: China has "performance-oriented humanoids, such as robots running marathons"; robots there sold "at unbelievable prices"; HARMONIC declines the low end ("we should not take an aggressive stance in going into the low end of the market") and defines itself by "a highly reliable robot" and "high value-added actuators." The voice knows exactly which market it is and isn't in.
8. **Forward statements carry an explicit conservatism flag** — Projections are labelled as such: "these projections may appear somewhat conservative; however, customer inquiries remain highly dynamic"; "Rather than saying that this is what is sure to happen, we have factored in…"; "I didn't put that much into it" (on price effects in the forecast). Guidance is never sold harder than the evidence.
9. **CFO capex flexibility register** — Kamijo on the five-year investment: "if they fall forward, we may do it early, or we may do it later … at a time that does not inconvenience those customers." Investment is spoken as responsibility to the customer and the balance sheet, not ambition.
10. **CEO vs CFO split** — Maruyama carries narrative, humanoid/defense candor, competition, the plan philosophy (own the miss, protect added value, don't overstate). Kamijo carries the numbers walk-through, segment/subsidiary results, capex and cash flow — orderly, deferential ("as you understand, Mr. Fukuhara"), page-referenced. The account blends: Maruyama's candor for narrative, Kamijo's sourced orderliness for figures.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, every hedge preserved)

- Neutral fact: "Management says the AI-robot ramp has been slower than expected; the prior plan over-relied on a single AI-robot customer."
  → In voice: "We should say it plainly: we have not made the progress on AI robots that we intended. The pace of social implementation among the manufacturers and start-ups we target has slowed considerably against our initial expectations. Part of that is our own doing — the earlier plan leaned too much on a single AI-robot customer, and we have deliberately diversified away from that."
- Neutral fact: "The medium-term operating-margin target was cut from 20% to 15%, held at ¥100B sales."
  → In voice: "We still believe 20% is the right ambition at ¥100 billion of sales, on higher utilization. But with price, labour and the investment we have yet to make, it would be irresponsible to simply put up a 20% target. We set 15%-or-more so it is a number we can stand behind."
- Neutral fact: "Orders have recovered; delivery is currently under control, though supply risk is not zero."
  → In voice: "Orders have become considerably more active, but this is different from the pre-emptive surge of the last two periods that caused us difficulties later. We built ahead and stood the factory by, so we are not facing delivery-time issues at all. We cannot say the risk is zero — but we are running the factory with 99.9% confidence there will be no problem."
- Neutral fact: "Competition in reducers alone cannot be ignored; the company defends the high end."
  → In voice: "Honestly, in reduction gears alone, competition has become something we cannot ignore. There are many wave-gear makers now, in Japan and in China, where robots sell at prices that are hard to believe. We have long targeted the high-end, highly reliable end — and there, the strongholds have hardly been broken. We do not intend to chase the low end."
