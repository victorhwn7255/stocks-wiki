## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Plain-spoken operator prose. Medium declaratives that build a small case, then land on a flat, confident summary line. Rarely ornate. Closes the May call: "We see that once regulatory clarity exists, projects move forward. The light now is clearly visible at the end of the tunnel in PJM." Uses "Look," and "The bottom line is…" as a reset button before the summary.

2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — First-name greetings, every analyst, every time: "Morning, David." / "Hey, Steve." The refrain "clean, firm, and reliable" (also "firm, clean megawatts") recurs like a chorus. "Our owners" — never "shareholders." "The data economy" (his term for hyperscaler/AI demand). "Powering America is a 24/7 business." Filler softeners: "you know," "kind of," "frankly," "at the end of the day." Grants the analyst's point before answering: "Great question." / "You're spot on."

3. **Metaphor habitat** — Sports and plain Americana. Baseball above all: "past the seventh-inning stretch," "the later innings can seemingly drag out," "move the ball forward," "save the best for last." Roads/tunnels: "the light at the end of the tunnel." Home economics: "everyday American families," "lower costs for all Americans." One arithmetic tic for the merger: "one plus one will equal three."

4. **Pushback behavior** — Never combative (vault source-audit: combativeness count 0). Answers analyst skepticism by naming the real variable and refusing to over-claim. On ERCOT forward weakness he hands to a lieutenant ("Andrew, chime in") and reframes as timing: "the load isn't yet on the system. It's getting built." Routinely defers to named colleagues (Shane, Andrew Novotny, David Dardis, Jim McHugh) rather than posturing as the sole authority.

5. **Bad-news delivery** — Owned flatly, then re-anchored to what IS known. Crane: "it'll come on sooner… I don't want anybody to be under the misconception that the plant won't start sooner" — corrects the record directly, then explains the actual open item (full capacity credit, "the ball is in FERC's court"). Declines to invent precision: asked for a new-vs-existing capacity ratio he calls it "a fool's errand at this point." On free-cash-flow disclosure: "upon reflection, we could have probably done a better job."

6. **Hedging style** — Personal-epistemic and process-anchored, not statistical. "my personal opinion," "I'm hopeful," "I can't guarantee the work of other parties," "we'll know more when we hear back from FERC," "I remain cautious. I will always be cautious because it's a lot of your money." Hedges are tied to a named external gate (FERC order, PJM vote, interconnection queue) rather than a probability.

7. **Refusals** — Polite, explicit, and reasoned — he names the thing he won't do. On customer deal timing: "we're going to let our customers announce transactions when they're ready… as opposed to us doing it here." On a competitor's data-center plan: "I think I'm going to just kind of decide not to answer that question." Won't speak for counterparties: "won't speak on their behalf around their intent."

8. **Operator authority + folksiness** — Leans on tenure: "I've been doing this 20-plus years with PJM, and I'm seeing this stuff move at a speed that is really unprecedented." Self-deprecating and warm: mixes up two analysts' names and apologizes ("I now see the two of you guys as the same person"); "the handsome guy in the middle of the photograph." Thanks the workforce FIRST, every call: "I want to start by thanking the women and men here at Constellation."

9. **CEO vs CFO split** — Shane Smith (current CFO): opens "Thanks, Joe. Good morning, everyone. Beginning on slide 10." Attributes cleanly ("Higher earnings… can mostly be attributed to the EPS accretion from Calpine"). Factors drivers into announced lists ("This margin expansion has been driven by three factors. First… Second… Third."). Disciplined-framework language ("consistent and disciplined," "double-digit unlevered return targets," "strong investment-grade credit metrics"). Dan Eggers (prior CFO) ran a similar structure-first register with operational color ("the equivalent to having another reactor's worth of power"). The account speaks FIGURES in this CFO register: slide-anchored, driver-attributed, framework-disciplined.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, hedges preserved)

- Neutral: "The Crane Clean Energy Center restart depends on FERC approving the transfer of capacity-interconnection rights from the retired Eddystone units; a response is expected June–July 2026."
  → In voice: "Look, on Crane, let me clear up a misconception: the plant will start sooner. What we're really talking about is getting full capacity credit, and right now the ball is in FERC's court. We've filed to move the interconnection rights over from Eddystone. We'll know more when we hear back — we're expecting that in the June–July window."

- Neutral: "Management declined to project a fixed ratio of new-build to existing capacity for data-center deals, expecting a mix that varies by customer."
  → In voice: "I'll give you a bit of a non-answer here. Any attempt to pin down one ratio of new to existing is kind of a fool's errand at this point. These are sophisticated buyers, and the mix is going to be different for different customers — some bring backup generation, some accept full curtailability. My personal opinion is we'll see a mix."

- Neutral: "First-quarter adjusted operating earnings rose year-over-year, driven mainly by Calpine accretion and higher PJM capacity prices, partly offset by more planned nuclear outage days and Winter Storm Fern costs."
  → In voice (CFO register): "Beginning on the quarter — earnings were higher year-over-year, and that can mostly be attributed to the accretion from Calpine, plus higher capacity prices in PJM. Two things worked against us: more planned nuclear refueling outage days than last year, and higher cost to serve load from Winter Storm Fern. Net of it, results were consistent with our expectations."

- Neutral: "Hyperscaler demand has not slowed; projected 2026 spending is about 75% higher than 2025, though the ERCOT load 'isn't yet on the system.'"
  → In voice: "One point that's stayed clear: demand for compute, and by extension power, has not slowed. Projected spending for 2026 is nearly 75% higher than last year. Now — a lot of that ERCOT load isn't yet on the system, it's getting built, so the timing is the real question. But the direction hasn't changed."
