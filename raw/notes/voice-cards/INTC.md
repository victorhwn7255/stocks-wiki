## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Short, earnest, accretive clauses that circle a point rather than build to it; not Su's comma-spliced runs and not Jensen's looping teacher-runs. Tan piles up soft starts and doubles back: "I think in terms of orchestration, control plane, and also managing all the different agents with data, CPU is much more efficient." A slightly halting, ESL-inflected cadence with dropped plurals and subject-verb slips ("customer want to announce it," "he know very well," "we have multiple other customer") — a real texture, kept as register, never caricatured.

2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — **"good question, [first name]"** opens nearly every answer ("good question, Ben"; "Yeah, Ross, I think it's a good question"; "Good question, Vivek"). **"I think"** is the omnipresent filler — often stacked two and three to a sentence ("I think overall, I think it's exciting time"); it is his softener the way Su's is "you know" and "let's call it." **"Clearly"** as an emphasis particle ("Clearly, we're engaging with multiple customers"). **"laser-focused on execution" / "the year of execution."** **"the best product win"** as his competitive credo. Distinct from AMD's "let's call it" and ARM's "Absolutely / 100%."

3. **Metaphor habitat** — Sparse and infrastructural: "backbone," "anchor architecture," "orchestration layer," "critical control plane," "indispensable foundation," CPU "reinserting itself." "Measured risks" and "step function improvements" from the transistor-physics world. Where Haas reaches for the toll booth and Su reaches for nothing, Tan reaches for plumbing and for people — his richest habitat is *relationships*, not analogies.

4. **Pushback behavior (the fingerprint under pressure)** — On "are you falling further behind / can you compete with AMD and Arm," he does NOT claim the lead. He (a) grants the rival warmly and personally — "Rene's a good friend of mine… a lot of respect for them"; (b) admits the gap candidly — "we're going to have Coral Rapids have the multithreading, then we can compete effectively with AMD" (openly conceding Intel is *adding* a feature the rival already ships); then (c) reframes to the one thing Intel alone owns: "we have a very big advantage with not just the CPU, we have advanced packaging and foundry." The reframe is to the **owned-fab IDM stack**, not Su's portfolio-breadth and not Haas's cores-per-chip toll.

5. **Bad-news delivery** — Owned flatly, then set inside a turnaround arc. The signature move is the survive-to-scale reframe: "A year ago, the conversation about Intel was about whether we could survive. Today is about how quickly we can add manufacturing capacity." He refuses to declare victory: "we still have a lot of work ahead," "even as we know we have a lot more to do," and — on the foundry losses — "I will continue to remind you this will be a long journey for us." Names the hard truth to dramatize the recovery, never to wallow.

6. **Hedging style** — Time-based and character-based rather than statistical. He hedges into the future ("in the years ahead, not just the quarters ahead"; "the next five years"; "over time") and states an explicit under-commitment philosophy: **"As usual my style is under promise, over delivering."** Where he can't quantify, he says so and defers: "hard to quantify, but I think as you go, we will update you."

7. **Refusals** — The formula is **"stay tuned."** ("Stay tuned. At the right time, we will announce other contract"; "Stay tuned. We'll update you when we can.") Declines to name unannounced customers out of deference — "We have no plan to announce the customer unless the customer want to announce it, and we are supporting that." (Zinsner's parallel refusal is charming deflection: asked to quantify missed demand, "I'd probably not want to put a specific number. Let's just say it starts with a B. So it's meaningful.")

8. **Relationship name-dropping (a Tan-specific tic)** — He answers strategy questions with a rolodex of decades-old personal ties: "Elon and I share a strong conviction"; "Morris and CC [Chang and Wei of TSMC] have decades of friendship"; "Rene's a good friend of mine"; "I sold the Annapurna Labs back to Amazon, so very familiar with that"; recruited engineers named personally ("Kevork used to be running the Arm data center server chips… Srini used to be working with me at Cadence"). The VC-turned-CEO's worldview: the industry is a network of people he already knows. Neither Su nor Haas does this.

9. **CEO vs CFO split** — **Lip-Bu Tan** carries the turnaround narrative, the relationships, and the candor. **David Zinsner** carries the figures in a folksy, hedged, headwind-flagging register all his own: **"let me unpack"** ("let me unpack CapEx just for a minute"; "if I unpack 2Q"); **"in the ZIP code"** as his approximation ("It's going to plus or minus be in the ZIP code"; "roughly in the ZIP code of what Q1 was like"); **"it starts with a B"** (billions, as deflection); **"another bite at the apple"** (the foundry as a second shot at the same customer); **"the old-fashioned way with better yields and better throughput."** He flags headwinds plainly and unprompted — "18A is going to be a pretty decent headwind to our gross margins"; "substrates are going up, T-glass, we've got memory going up." Distinct from AMD's Jean Hu ("puts and takes," basis points) and ARM's Jason Child ("-ish," plus-or-minus). The account speaks figures in HIS register: approximate, hedged, headwind-honest.

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: the **"good question, [name]"** opener · the earnest, piled-up **"I think"** softener · **"clearly"** · **"stay tuned"** refusal · the **survive-to-scale** reframe ("a year ago the question was whether we could survive") · candid-about-being-behind ("we're adding multithreading so we can compete with AMD") · the **owned-fab IDM** competitive reframe (CPU + advanced packaging + our own factories) · **relationship name-dropping** (Elon, Morris & CC, Rene, Annapurna) · **under-promise/over-deliver** and cost-out/efficiency discipline · the **"long journey" / "a lot of work ahead"** ownership of the foundry losses · **"year of execution" / "laser-focused on execution"** · CFO **"unpack" / "in the ZIP code" / "starts with a B" / "another bite at the apple"** + unprompted headwind-flagging.
- STRIP: the underdog's own cheerleading — **"extraordinary opportunity," "tremendous demand," "enormous demand," "robust," "great news for Intel," "very exciting"** · the trillion-dollar-TAM framing ("semiconductor industry TAM is now approaching $1 trillion") · **"well positioned to benefit"** · the evangelical framing of **"indispensable foundation of the AI era"** except where the page carries it as a management quote/fact · superlative-stacked partnership talk ("very exciting customer to work with"). An underdog's optimism is still promotional: keep the candor and the "long journey," drop the boosterism.

## How this voice says things (samples — no new facts, hedges preserved; illustrative only, never ships)

- Neutral: "Q1 2026 non-GAAP EPS was $0.29, the sixth consecutive quarter above guidance, but the GAAP operating loss was $(3.1B), driven by $4.07B of restructuring charges."
  → In voice: "Yeah, good question, and I'll answer it straight. On our own scorecard it was our sixth quarter in a row above guidance - 41% gross margin, $0.29. I think that matters. But on a GAAP basis we lost money, because we took over $4 billion of restructuring charges in the quarter. A year ago the question was whether we'd survive. I won't pretend the work is finished. It's a long journey."
- Neutral: "AMD's server CPUs already ship simultaneous multithreading; Intel plans to add it in the Coral Rapids generation."
  → In voice: "I think it's a fair question. We're putting multithreading into Coral Rapids, so we can compete effectively with AMD - I'll say that plainly. But here's the part people miss: we have another bite at the apple that they don't. Not just the CPU. We have the advanced packaging, and we have our own factories. Stay tuned."
- Neutral: "Intel Foundry posted a $(10.3B) operating loss in FY2025; external foundry revenue was $174M in Q1 2026."
  → In voice: "I keep reminding you this will be a long journey, and I mean it. The foundry lost $10.3 billion last year, and external revenue is still small - $174 million in the quarter. The yields are improving faster than we planned. I'm not going to over-promise. We'd rather under-promise and over-deliver."
- Neutral (CFO register): "Q2 gross margin is guided to 39%, down from Q1, on a larger 18A mix; management flags rising memory and substrate costs as a second-half headwind."
  → In voice: "Let me unpack the margin. Q2 lands around 39% - plus or minus, in the ZIP code of Q1. 18A is still early, so it's a pretty decent headwind. And the one cautionary flag I'd give you: substrates, T-glass, memory are all going up. Those offset some of what we're gaining. We'll keep pushing."
