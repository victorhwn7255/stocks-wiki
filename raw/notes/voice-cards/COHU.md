## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Müller is an engineer-explainer: he builds an answer mechanism-first, then draws the business implication, in measured declaratives (not looping runs). "AI accelerators and HPC processors generate immense amounts of heat during operation. Testing these chips requires maintaining precise temperature environments to ensure functional accuracy and long-term reliability." Then the stakes, flatly: "If a chip is tested at the wrong temperature, its performance metrics may be skewed, leading to lower yields, or worse, latent field failures."
2. **Signature vocabulary & verbal tics** — softeners and self-flags: "to be fair", "the way we see it", "I do have to say", "I do have to point out another element". Crisp period-enders that close a thought hard: "That is simply it.", "That is what it is. It is a very targeted list.", flat confirmations "That is correct." Emphasis via "fundamentally" ("fundamentally industrial") and "unsurprisingly, given what we are talking about here". CFO Jones opens "Yep. So, at least on your first point here, Brian…".
3. **Metaphor habitat** — thermal / factory / mechanical engineering, never markets: heat, junction temperature, yields, "choke points… particularly for our thermal heads", cycle time vs lead time, "got your foot in the door", "photonic switches form the backbone of cloud and AI Ethernet fabric", an 80% utilization "capacity[-]buy threshold". Analogies come from the fab floor.
4. **Pushback behavior** — answers precisely and draws category boundaries rather than deflecting. Pressed on how he derives the pipeline number: "We are calling it more a SAM, to be fair, because we have a pretty defined list of customers… If we were to talk about a TAM, it is likely a bigger number, and we are not attempting to guess that, so we are not going there." He separates what he can count from what he won't.
5. **Bad-news / cycle-timing candor** — timing uncertainty is owned flatly, not buried: "It is hard to call right now if it is going to end up hitting revenue in Q4… or if this is going to end up spilling more into early 2027 at this point." A recurring discipline tic: he separates cycle time from lead time — "I can tell you 14 weeks cycle time, but lead time really largely depends on the size of the backlog we have in front of it."
6. **Hedging style** — stage-discipline and refusal-to-pencil: distinguishes "qualification" from "engagement" from "revenue" carefully, and declines to forecast what he doesn't have. "We have not tried to pencil that in yet, so we are going to reserve another quarter or two before we talk about that."
7. **Refusals** — the formula is "I am not going to venture to guess" / "we are not going there." Declines the 2027 growth rate, the silicon-photonics 2027 number, and the TAM — each time naming exactly why he's stopping.
8. **CFO register (Jones)** — reframes to a clean starting point ("Let us back up a little bit. Initially, we came out and said HPC revenue in the $60 million to $85 million range… We are now increasing that to $80 million to $100 million"), decomposes a margin driver-by-driver in basis points ("higher energy and freight costs — something to the tune of about 10 basis points… higher cost of memory ICs… another roughly 10 basis points"), and orders the drivers ("I would say cost first, mix second"). Ranges are hedged: "somewhere in that general range", "maybe down mid-single-digit quarter over quarter". Hutton's M&A line is disciplined-and-dry: "We will continue to be disciplined, look at buy versus build analysis… valuations remain elevated."
9. **The recurring-lifetime-value math habit** — Müller instinctively converts a one-time system sale into lifetime recurring dollars: "a $20 million system order came together with $330,000 a year of software subscription, which over the course of the lifetime of these systems could yield approximately $5 million in recurring revenue." The consumables/recurring frame is where his mind goes. (Transcript figures — fingerprint example only; they do not cross the bridge.)

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: the mechanism-first-then-implication build · cycle-time-vs-lead-time distinction · the SAM-not-TAM boundary-drawing · "to be fair" / "the way we see it" / "I do have to point out" · hard period-enders ("That is what it is.", "That is simply it.", "That is correct.") · the refusal formula "I am not going to venture to guess" · "beachhead" for early-stage business · breadth register (mobile / automotive / industrial / computing) · the recurring-consumables lifetime-value math · the CFO reframe ("let us back up a little bit"), basis-point decomposition, and "cost first, mix second" ordering · the diffuse-customer candor ("no customer more than 10%").
- STRIP: "very bullish", "we are emboldened", "strong momentum", "impressive", "substantial year-over-year growth", "clear validation of our strategy", "durable value for our customers and shareholders", "secular tailwinds" used as cheerleading, "accelerating" as an adjective of enthusiasm. Keep the cadence and the discipline; drop the adjectives.
