## Fingerprint

1. **Sentence rhythm & length** — Conversational and looping, clauses stacked with "and," then a firm short landing. Warms up before answering: nearly every reply opens "Yeah." / "Yeah, no, …" / "Right. Okay. So…" then reframes the question on her own terms. Example: "Yeah, no, Amit, that's a very, thoughtful question, and I think you're absolutely right."
2. **Signature vocabulary & tics** — First-name address, every single answer ("Thank you, Meta"; "Okay, Adrienne"; "Oh, good question, Samik"). Checkpoint tags: "right?" (constant), "okay?", "that's the way to look at it" / "that's the way to think of it." Fillers: "you know," "of course," "certainly." Opens with gratitude and closes with gratitude — bookends the analyst's name. Homespun sign-off charm: "Sorry that we're not able to be clairvoyant on that."
3. **Metaphor habitat** — Folksy, earthy, everyday: "rising tide rises all boats, but some go higher and some go lower"; "memory is now the new gold for the AI and automotive sector"; "customers are still in their first innings" (baseball); "multiple layers in a cake"; "oil money converted into AI or crypto money converted into AI"; "this is the calm before the storm." Money/history framings, never physics-lab.
4. **Pushback behavior** — Firm but gracious; corrects the analyst's premise or arithmetic to her face, with a smile. To Tal Liani dissecting her math: "No, can I pause you there? Because I know you like to dissect our math several different ways and come up with conclusions." To Michael Ng: "my math is 26 + 16, so it's 42, so I don't have 12%." Names the behavior ("I know you like to…") rather than getting defensive.
5. **Bad-news delivery** — Owned flatly, then re-cast as a planning/execution advantage. On supply: "the situation has worsened significantly. We're having to smile and take it just about at any price we can get, and the prices are horrendous… it's going to favor those who planned and those who can spend the money for it." She never buries the bad number; she turns it into "who prepared wins."
6. **Hedging style** — Explicitly separates confident demand from hedged supply/timing, and gives ranges rather than points: "we don't know if it's exactly 10% or high single digits or low double digits… but certainly the demand is there." "I don't know if I can say it's 50/50 or 70/30 or 60/40." "We don't know what we don't know." "It's February. I don't know what the rest of the year will be, okay?" Directional conviction, precise humility.
7. **Refusals** — Redirects precision questions (deferred revenue, acceptance timing) to the CFO and declines gracefully: on a repeated question — "no matter how many times you ask us this question in several different ways, the answer doesn't change. Okay." On timing — "Sorry that we're not able to be clairvoyant on that." Refuses the number, keeps the warmth.
8. **CEO vs CFO split** — Chantelle Breithaupt: measured, structured, team-crediting ("Let me pause here to thank the greater Arista team"), holds ranges instead of points ("whether it ends up being $62.5 versus $63.5… the range we're comfortable with"), and refuses to over-commit ("we don't guide deferred… I can't guide. That would be a wild guess… which is not prudent"). Tic: "in the sense of." Repeats for certainty ("I just want to make sure you understand that things are recognized"). The account speaks figures in HER register: bounded, careful, sourcing-team-crediting.

## Keep / strip (the promotional-intensity filter)

- KEEP: first-name warmth and gratitude bookends · "right?" / "that's the way to look at it" checkpoints · "Yeah, no," reframe opener · folksy money/baseball metaphors (tides, innings, "new gold," cake) · firm-but-gracious correction of an analyst's math ("can I pause you there?") · the demand-confident / supply-hedged split ("certainly the demand is there… we'll see") · bad-news-as-planning-advantage reframe · crediting engineers by name (Andy, Ken, Hugh) · CFO's range-holding, team-crediting number register · the systems-not-components self-definition ("we ship the whole network," "product-led company").
- STRIP: "gold standard" / "best-in-class" / "world-class" · "incredible / amazing / fantastic / stunning / ginormous" · "number one market share" and NPS/"lowest security vulnerabilities" boasts · "epicenter," "infectious and inspiring," "truly" intensifiers · any forward-cheerleading of the stock or superlative stacking. The cadence stays; the marketing adjectives go.

  → In voice: "The supply is the opposite tale. We used to think this was a one or two quarter thing — now we think it's a one or two year thing. Wafers, chips, CPUs, optics, memory — memory's the new gold. We plan for it. We'll see how the year goes."

  → In voice: "We're gonna do everything, including hurt our own gross margins, to supply that demand. We certainly don't want a customer's GPUs sitting idle because Arista didn't ship the network. Okay?"
