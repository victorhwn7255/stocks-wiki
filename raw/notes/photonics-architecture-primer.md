# AI datacenter photonics supply chain

The cleanest way to think about AI datacenter photonics is not as a two-product market, but as a layered supply chain. The visible product architectures are front-panel pluggable optics in the near term and co-packaged optics in the next wave. The tighter economic choke points, however, sit deeper in the stack: qualified indium-phosphide substrates, lasers and external light sources, optical DSP and SerDes, high-speed modulators, silicon photonics foundries, detachable fiber attach, high-volume packaging and test, and, farther out, optical circuit switching. The public evidence from 2025ŌĆō2026 points to a market in which pluggables still drive the present volume ramp, while CPO pulls investment into a broader manufacturing and photonics ecosystem underneath it. ### Supporting photonics infrastructure

This is why the broader supporting photonics infrastructure matters more than the module shell. ## Assessment and outlook

The highest-confidence conclusion is that pluggable versus CPO is the wrong place to stop the analysis. Pluggables remain the volume architecture in the current 800G-to-1.6T transition because the ecosystem is broader, more serviceable, and already entering real customer ramps. CPO is the next architecture, but it does not collapse the supply chain; it reweights it toward package integration, external laser sources, detachable fiber interfaces, and test. The real long-duration bottlenecks are therefore: qualified InP wafers and lasers, optical DSP and 224G electrical IP, silicon photonics foundries plus advanced packaging, fiber attach and connectorization, and manufacturing/test infrastructure. ### Open questions and limitations

Some parts of the chain remain hard to size precisely from public sources. Merchant market share between module assemblers, the exact exposure of system integrators and ODMs to optical gross profit, and the identity of many cleaving, coating, active-alignment, and private hyperscaler internal suppliers remain only partially visible. Public disclosure is also much better for pluggables and merchant CPO switches than for private scale-up optical-I/O programs. Finally, while the roadmap to 400G/lane and 3.2T is clearly forming, the evidence reviewed here still points to roadmap, early sampling, or focused pilot status rather than broad production deployment at that next speed tier.
