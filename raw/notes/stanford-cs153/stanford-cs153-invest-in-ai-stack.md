# Stanford CS153 and the GAI Factory: Investing in the Frontier Systems Stack

Stanford CS153 Frontier Systems is a Spring 2026 Stanford course on the full stack required to transform generative AI from model capability into deployed economic infrastructure. The course’s organizing premise is that AI is no longer only a model layer or software feature layer; it is an end-to-end production system spanning energy, silicon, data centers, networking, memory, model training, inference, agent products, security, regulation, and company formation. The companion guide describes the course as an effort to understand the “industrialization of intelligence,” with the GAI stack framed as energy → silicon → models → products, and with the investor lens centered on bottlenecks, capex, moats, and deployment risk. The most important investment conclusion from the corpus is that the relevant asset class is not “AI software” or “AI chips” in isolation; it is the AI factory: a balanced, power-secured, highly utilized, reliable, secure, and continuously improving system that converts capital, electricity, data, memory bandwidth, networking, and model architecture into monetized intelligence at scale.

The single highest-conviction implication is that the market is still over-indexed to the wrong observables. GPU shipments, capex budgets, model parameter counts, announced megawatts, and nominal backlog are useful but incomplete metrics. CS153 points to a more discriminating underwriting framework: value per gigawatt, goodput, usable cluster capacity, memory bandwidth per flop, networking balance, tokens per watt, tokens per dollar, latency-adjusted inference throughput, power actually energized, depreciation per monetized AI dollar, and security/resilience as part of the production stack. In that framework, the long-duration winners are the suppliers and operators that control bottlenecked, validated, hard-to-replace parts of the AI factory, while the highest-risk exposures are companies monetizing narrative capacity rather than utilized capacity.

The course implies 10 core infrastructure conclusions. 1. AI is becoming critical infrastructure, not merely software, which increases the strategic value of power-secured data centers, grid equipment, cybersecurity, observability, and sovereign cloud infrastructure. 2. Inference economics, not only training scale, will determine whether the current capex wave is value-accretive. 3. “Value per gigawatt” is the cleanest cross-stack metric because it forces an assessment of utilization, reliability, scheduling, memory bandwidth, network balance, power availability, and monetized output. 4. NVIDIA remains the best-positioned public merchant AI factory supplier because its moat is system-level co-design across GPU, CPU, NVLink, NVSwitch, InfiniBand/Ethernet, software, compilers, rack-scale architectures, and supply-chain execution, but the durability of peak gross margins is less certain than the durability of AI infrastructure demand. 5. HBM and advanced packaging are structural choke points because accelerator supply is only valuable when memory bandwidth and package integration are available at scale. 6. Networking and optics are becoming first-order AI infrastructure, with scale-up fabrics preserving NVIDIA’s moat and scale-out Ethernet creating a broader profit pool for Broadcom, Arista, Marvell, Astera Labs, Credo, and the optical supply chain. 7. Power scarcity is shifting from a second-derivative theme to a primary constraint; the most durable beneficiaries are grid equipment, turbines, electrical distribution, cooling, EPC, and existing firm power. 8. AI-native software creates operating leverage through labor compression, but application gross margins can compress if inference cost is bundled, underpriced, or grows faster than subscription revenue. 9. Multimodal systems, especially video, voice, spatial, robotics, and code agents, raise inference intensity and create demand for memory, networking, storage, low-latency CPUs, and high-throughput serving. 10. Security and resilience become part of the infrastructure product because AI factories concentrate model weights, proprietary data, identity systems, agent permissions, training pipelines, inference endpoints, physical facilities, and national-security-relevant capabilities.

The highest-quality public equity exposures are concentrated in 5 groups. The 1st group is bottlenecked compute and custom silicon: NVIDIA, AMD, Broadcom, Marvell, Arm, and selective hyperscaler custom silicon read-through through Alphabet, Amazon, Microsoft, and Meta. The 2nd group is memory and packaging: SK hynix, Micron, Samsung, TSMC, ASE, Amkor, Ibiden, Unimicron, Shinko, AT&S, and selected advanced-packaging equipment and inspection vendors. The 3rd group is networking, optics, and connectivity: NVIDIA/Mellanox, Broadcom, Marvell, Arista, Cisco, Astera Labs, Credo, Coherent, Lumentum, Fabrinet, Innolight, Eoptolink, Amphenol, TE Connectivity, Molex, Luxshare, Semtech, and MACOM. The 4th group is data-center power, cooling, and grid equipment: GE Vernova, Siemens Energy, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, Eaton, Schneider Electric, ABB, Siemens, Hubbell, nVent, Prysmian, Vertiv, Modine, Munters, Comfort Systems, EMCOR, Quanta Services, MasTec, Fluor, Jacobs, AECOM, and Legrand. The 5th group is AI infrastructure software, security, observability, identity, data governance, and workflow automation: Microsoft, ServiceNow, Salesforce, Adobe, Datadog, Dynatrace, Elastic, Splunk/Cisco, Snowflake, Databricks, MongoDB, Confluent, Cloudflare, Akamai, Palo Alto Networks, CrowdStrike, Zscaler, Fortinet, Okta, SentinelOne, and Wiz.

The most attractive long bias is not a single-stock “AI winner” trade; it is a bottleneck-duration portfolio. The highest-conviction long basket should emphasize NVIDIA, TSMC, HBM leaders, Broadcom, selective Marvell, Arista, Astera Labs, Credo, Eaton, Schneider Electric, Vertiv, GE Vernova, Quanta Services, EMCOR, Comfort Systems, and existing firm-power owners such as Constellation, Vistra, Talen, and NRG where valuation and regulatory risk are acceptable. The most attractive short or avoid basket is concentrated in 4 areas: thin-margin server integrators priced as if they own durable AI scarcity; levered neoclouds with customer-duration, refinancing, and residual-value mismatch; software names whose AI product strategy is mostly bundled inference without pricing power; and narrative power or nuclear names whose timing does not match the 2026-2028 demand window. A credible pairs framework is long HBM leaders vs generic memory beta, long TSMC/advanced packaging vs commodity OSAT exposure, long grid equipment vs speculative generation developers, long hyperscaler balance-sheet AI factories vs levered neocloud capacity, and long workflow/data-moat software vs code/UI moat software.

The biggest overhyped or mispriced narratives are identifiable. 1. AI capex does not equal AI profit. 2. GPU shipments do not equal monetized utilization. 3. Data center megawatts announced do not equal megawatts energized. 4. Power requested is not power secured. 5. HBM demand does not make every memory supplier a winner. 6. CoWoS demand does not make every packaging supplier a winner. 7. Liquid cooling demand does not make every thermal supplier a winner. 8. ASIC adoption can be bullish for total AI infrastructure and bearish for merchant GPU margins. 9. Model efficiency can be bullish through demand elasticity or bearish through lower compute intensity; the sign depends on token growth, pricing, latency, modality mix, and utilization. 10. Sovereign AI can drive capex but produce poor returns if politically allocated rather than utilization-driven.

The 5 numbers that should matter most at the investment committee level are: 1. hyperscaler AI capex, depreciation, and AI revenue run-rate; 2. HBM supply, pricing, customer qualification, sold-out duration, and prepayment terms; 3. CoWoS/SoIC/advanced-package capacity and lead times; 4. GPU-hour/API pricing, GPU lead times, and effective utilization; and 5. power status by project, specifically requested MW, studied MW, contracted MW, permitted MW, under-construction MW, energized MW, and utilized MW. The 5 most uncertain claims are: 1. whether lower inference cost produces demand elasticity large enough to keep infrastructure demand expanding faster than efficiency; 2. whether power remains more binding than silicon after 2028; 3. whether hyperscaler ASICs take enough share to materially reset merchant GPU gross margins; 4. whether neocloud backlog converts into equity value after debt, depreciation, refinancing, and residual-value risk; and 5. whether AI-native software margin expansion from labor compression exceeds inference COGS and competitive price pressure.

CENTRAL THESIS: AI FACTORIES, NOT AI APPLICATIONS

The CS153 worldview is best interpreted as a full-stack capital-allocation framework. Intelligence is being transformed into an industrial output, and the production function includes power, land, cooling, chips, memory, advanced packaging, interconnect, software orchestration, training pipelines, inference-serving systems, product feedback loops, security, and governance. This means the AI infrastructure cycle should not be underwritten as a normal software adoption curve or as a conventional semiconductor cycle. It is closer to an electrification, railroad, telecommunications, cloud, and semiconductor fabrication cycle, with the important difference that the cycle is occurring at software-market speed but with physical-world constraints. CS153 explicitly connects frontier AI to prior general-purpose technology transitions where new infrastructure standards, new capital networks, and new bottlenecks emerge before the economic steady state becomes visible.

The course’s most useful investment construct is “bottleneck migration.” In 2023-2024, the visible bottleneck was access to frontier GPUs and model-training capacity. By 2025-2026, the bottlenecks were more distributed: HBM, CoWoS and advanced packaging, rack-scale liquid cooling, scale-up interconnect, scale-out networking, power delivery, turbine lead times, transformer/switchgear lead times, grid interconnection, and financing. By 2027-2030, the bottleneck is likely to become a more complex blend of inference utilization, power availability, custom silicon share, AI revenue monetization, and capital structure. The implication is that a static “AI winners” list will age poorly; the correct framework is a live bottleneck map.

Amin Vahdat’s “value per gigawatt” framing is the intellectual center of the infrastructure analysis. A gigawatt of AI capacity is not fungible. A 1 GW deployment with balanced HBM bandwidth, high-speed scale-up fabric, reliable power, low cluster failure rates, proper scheduling, sufficient CPU/storage orchestration, low-latency network access, and high utilization can generate materially more economic output than a nominally identical 1 GW deployment with unreliable nodes, scheduling friction, poor memory balance, or insufficient networking. In his CS153 lecture, Vahdat explicitly rejects raw gigawatts and raw spend as sufficient metrics and reframes the key metric as value delivered per dollar, per user, and per gigawatt. That is the same distinction public-market investors must make between capex and return on invested capital.

This shifts the underwriting question for hyperscalers and neoclouds from “How much capex is being spent?” to “How much paid inference, enterprise usage, developer productivity, search monetization, agent workflow automation, or ad improvement is produced per dollar of depreciation and per megawatt?” Microsoft’s disclosure that its AI business surpassed a $37B annual revenue run-rate, Alphabet’s Q1 2026 capex of $35.7B with roughly 60% directed toward servers and 40% toward data centers/networking, Meta’s 2026 capex guidance of
[$125B-](https://x.com/search?q=%24125B-&src=cashtag_click)

$145B, Oracle’s AI contract structure involving $75B of prepaid or customer-supplied hardware portions, and CoreWeave’s $99.4B revenue backlog all validate the scale of the buildout. However, those metrics do not by themselves establish value creation. They require conversion into utilization, pricing, gross profit, depreciation coverage, and free cash flow.

The central long-term industry dynamic is not “GPU scarcity forever.” It is “scarce balanced intelligence production.” NVIDIA can continue to capture outsized value if the relevant scarcity remains full-stack time-to-deploy, software compatibility, rack-scale architecture, NVLink/NVSwitch domains, networking, CUDA/TensorRT/Triton, system validation, and supply-chain execution. Hyperscaler ASICs and merchant alternatives can take share if workloads become stable, high-volume, internally controlled, and cost-per-token optimized. HBM suppliers can retain pricing power if memory bandwidth remains the gating variable. TSMC can retain structural pricing power if leading-edge logic and advanced packaging remain constrained. Power and grid equipment can retain demand even if GPU gross margins normalize, because AI factory deployment cannot scale without energized power.

The course is also explicitly anti-hype when read correctly. It does not support a blanket conclusion that every AI capex recipient is a durable winner. It implies that the market should penalize AI infrastructure stories where capacity is unbalanced, power is speculative, financing is mismatched, utilization is opaque, AI revenue is not disclosed, and gross profit is mostly GPU pass-through. It also implies that software AI winners and semiconductor AI winners may diverge. AI can increase software velocity while pressuring software gross margins if inference COGS are underpriced. AI can increase total compute demand while reducing merchant GPU share if ASICs gain workload-specific traction. AI can expand total power demand while impairing some data center developers if interconnection queues and permitting prevent energization.

COMPUTE AND ACCELERATORS

NVIDIA remains the highest-quality public expression of the AI factory thesis, but the investment case is now more nuanced than simple GPU scarcity. The CS153 Jensen Huang lecture argues that computing is being reinvented for the 1st time in more than 60 years, shifting from prerecorded retrieval and deterministic software toward generated, contextual, continuously running, agentic computing. The relevant claim is architectural: the future system is not just a faster GPU; it is a rack-scale, networked, memory-rich, software-orchestrated computer designed around AI workload structure.

NVIDIA’s current financials support the magnitude of value capture. The company reported Q1 FY2027 revenue of $81.6B, up 85% YoY, with GAAP and non-GAAP gross margins of 74.9% and 75.0%. The FY2026 result was $215.9B of revenue, up 65%, with FY2026 GAAP and non-GAAP gross margins of 71.1% and 71.3%. These are not normal semiconductor-cycle economics; they reflect monopoly-like scarcity rents in a system transition where demand is constrained more by supply, packaging, HBM, power, and validated deployment than by near-term willingness to pay.

The product architecture reinforces the moat. GB200 NVL72 combines 36 Grace CPUs and 72 Blackwell GPUs in a liquid-cooled rack-scale system and is marketed as a 72-GPU NVLink domain acting as a single massive GPU. GB300 NVL72 extends the rack-scale design with 72 Blackwell Ultra GPUs and 36 Grace CPUs, and NVIDIA states that it offers 1.5x dense FP4 FLOPS and 2x attention performance versus Blackwell GPUs. Vera Rubin NVL72, announced for the next platform generation, integrates 72 Rubin GPUs, 36 Vera CPUs, NVLink 6, ConnectX-9, and BlueField-4. The CS153 transcript directly connects this roadmap to inference and agents: Hopper was designed around pre-training, Grace Blackwell NVLink72 around high-memory-bandwidth decode and inference, and Vera Rubin around agentic workloads needing fast memory, storage-to-GPU fabric, and low-latency CPUs for tool use.

The strongest version of the NVIDIA bull case is that AI factory customers are buying time-to-working-cluster and system balance, not only chips. Training clusters require large synchronous systems where 1 failing node can impair the job. Inference systems require high memory bandwidth, low-latency decode, batching, routing, resilient serving, and software compatibility. Agentic systems add CPU tool-call latency, memory retrieval, storage locality, and multi-agent orchestration. NVIDIA’s advantage is therefore not merely CUDA; it is the ability to coordinate silicon, HBM availability, advanced packaging, rack-scale liquid cooling, NVLink/NVSwitch, InfiniBand/Ethernet, BlueField DPUs, software libraries, reference systems, OEM/ODM validation, and customer deployment.

The strongest version of the bear case is not that NVIDIA becomes irrelevant. It is that the market capitalizes current scarcity margins too far into the future. Hyperscaler customers have a clear incentive to use internal ASICs where workloads are stable and utilization is high. Google’s TPU Ironwood is described by Google as its 7th-generation TPU and 1st designed specifically for inference, while Google Cloud describes TPU7x/Ironwood as designed for large-scale AI training and inference, including dense and MoE workloads, pre-training, sampling, and decode-heavy inference. AWS continues to position Trainium and Inferentia around AI cost/performance inside the AWS ecosystem. Meta has expanded its MTIA roadmap and partnership with Broadcom, including multiple generations of MTIA custom silicon through 2029. These internal programs are structurally designed to reduce dependence on merchant GPUs over time.

AMD is the credible merchant alternative but remains a proof-of-software story. AMD reported Q1 2026 Data Center revenue of $5.8B, up 57% YoY, driven by EPYC processors and Instinct GPU shipments. The MI300/MI325/MI350/MI400 roadmap gives hyperscalers a second-source GPU architecture and a negotiating tool against NVIDIA. AMD’s advantages include EPYC server share, high-memory configurations, open-ecosystem customer willingness, Pensando networking assets, and a more attractive customer diversification narrative. The constraints are ROCm maturity, software ecosystem depth, availability of developer tooling, inference-serving maturity, and the ability to match NVIDIA’s validated rack-scale deployment cadence. AMD is therefore best framed as a high-upside second-source beneficiary rather than a like-for-like NVIDIA replacement.

Broadcom and Marvell are the best public read-throughs to hyperscaler custom silicon and AI networking. Broadcom disclosed Q1 FY2026 AI revenue of $8.4B, up 106% YoY, driven by custom AI accelerators and AI networking, and expected Q2 AI semiconductor revenue of . This validates the thesis that ASIC share growth is already a material public-equity profit pool, not a future abstraction. Marvell reported Q2 FY2026 net revenue of $2.006B, with demand tied to data center and AI infrastructure, and remains leveraged to custom silicon, electro-optics, PAM4 DSPs, storage, and high-speed interconnect. Broadcom is the cleaner custom silicon scale asset; Marvell has higher program-specific execution sensitivity and potentially higher optical/custom-silicon torque.

Intel remains a less certain AI infrastructure equity. Xeon remains relevant in CPU orchestration, enterprise infrastructure, and agent tool-call workloads, but Gaudi execution has not established Intel as a central accelerator supplier. Intel Foundry, EMIB/Foveros, advanced packaging, and 18A-related ambitions create strategic optionality, but the burden of proof remains high. Intel can matter if external foundry credibility improves, U.S. strategic sourcing incentives intensify, and CPU/accelerator integration becomes more central to agentic infrastructure. It remains less direct and less de-risked than NVIDIA, AMD, Broadcom, Marvell, TSMC, and HBM suppliers.

Private and emerging accelerator companies should be monitored as disconfirmation signals rather than modeled as near-term public market substitutes. Groq, Cerebras, SambaNova, Tenstorrent, Etched, d-Matrix, EnCharge, Lightmatter, Celestial AI, Rain AI, Positron, and similar companies are all trying to exploit narrower workload structure, lower latency, better power efficiency, analog or in-memory compute, photonic interconnect, or transformer-specific design. The key diligence variables are production customer proof, software stack portability, manufacturing partner, HBM or memory strategy, utilization at real workloads, gross margin at scale, and whether the workload being accelerated remains stable long enough for a specialized chip to earn economic returns. The likely outcome is not a single private accelerator replacing NVIDIA; it is segmentation of the inference market.

HBM, MEMORY, AND STORAGE

HBM remains 1 of the cleanest bottleneck exposures because AI model serving and training are increasingly memory-bandwidth bound rather than pure-FLOPS bound. Vahdat’s CS153 discussion of Amdahl’s law is central: compute without data is useless, and insufficient HBM, SRAM, network bandwidth, storage, or CPU orchestration leaves expensive flops idle. The move toward mixture-of-experts, sparse computation, long context, and decode-heavy inference increases the importance of memory bandwidth relative to raw compute. Jensen’s CS153 explanation of NVLink72 makes the same point from the NVIDIA side: decode and token generation require more memory bandwidth than 1 chip can provide, which drove the architecture toward a 72-GPU rack-scale system.

The public memory data confirm the structural inflection. JEDEC released the JESD270-4 HBM4 standard in April 2025, targeting higher bandwidth, power efficiency, and capacity for AI/HPC systems. Micron’s Q2 FY2026 revenue was $23.86B versus $8.05B YoY, reflecting extraordinary AI-memory demand and the broader memory upcycle. Samsung reported that its Q1 2026 Memory Business surpassed its quarterly sales record by addressing high-value-added AI demand despite limited supply availability. These facts support the view that HBM is not simply another DRAM cycle; it is a structural AI-system bottleneck layered on top of a cyclical memory industry.

The HBM winner set is narrower than the memory universe. SK hynix has been widely viewed as the HBM leader due to qualification timing and high-end stack execution. Micron has strategic value as a U.S.-based HBM supplier and has rapidly scaled AI memory exposure. Samsung has unmatched scale and integration across memory/foundry/packaging, but HBM execution and qualification cadence matter more than headline memory share. The underwriting should focus on HBM3E/HBM4 qualification at NVIDIA, AMD, Broadcom ASIC customers, Google TPU, AWS Trainium, Meta MTIA, and other hyperscaler ASIC programs; stack height; thermal performance; TSV yields; base-die sourcing; known-good-die yield; prepayment terms; and how much commodity DRAM capacity is converted into HBM capacity.

HBM demand is positive for semicap, advanced packaging, inspection, and test. The most direct beneficiaries include DRAM equipment suppliers, TSV and bonding tools, metrology and inspection, advanced packaging tools, wafer-level and panel-level process tools, burn-in/test, and substrate capacity. Applied Materials, Lam Research, Tokyo Electron, ASML, KLA, Onto Innovation, Camtek, Nova, Lasertec, Disco, Advantest, Teradyne, FormFactor, Cohu, BESI, ASMPT, EV Group, SUSS MicroTec, SCREEN, Entegris, Merck/EMD Electronics, DuPont, Shin-Etsu, JSR, and Fujifilm Electronic Materials all have some level of AI infrastructure read-through, but revenue materiality varies significantly. Applied Materials reported Q2 FY2026 revenue of $7.910B, up from $7.100B in the prior year, and KLA’s advanced packaging process-control portfolio is directly relevant to package yield and performance.

NAND and storage are lower-conviction than HBM but still underappreciated. Multimodal workloads require large training sets, video/image/audio corpora, checkpoints, synthetic data, vector databases, retrieval stores, logs, traces, and model observability. Luma’s CS153 lecture disclosed a multimodal training-data footprint on the order of 30 PB of final trainable outputs and described post-training using customer data, user preference data, human annotations, reinforcement learning, and continuous learning. This supports demand for enterprise SSDs, storage controllers, object storage, high-throughput file systems, data lake infrastructure, and retrieval systems. However, NAND is more cyclical, more commodity-like, and less concentrated than HBM, so the margin quality is lower.

CXL and memory pooling remain important medium-term bottleneck relievers. If context length, agent memory, retrieval-augmented workflows, and multimodal data movement continue to expand, memory capacity and memory hierarchy become more important. CXL can help with memory expansion and pooling, but adoption depends on latency tolerance, software support, server architecture, and cloud operator willingness to standardize. The public beneficiaries include Astera Labs, Rambus, Synopsys, Cadence, Alphawave Semi, Marvell, and controller/IP suppliers, but the commercial adoption curve should be treated as multi-year rather than immediate.

FOUNDRY, ADVANCED PACKAGING, SUBSTRATES, OSAT, SEMICAP, EDA, AND IP

TSMC remains the central foundry and advanced-packaging bottleneck in the AI stack. Its 2025 annual report describes robust AI-related demand throughout 2025, Foundry 2.0 industry growth of 16% YoY, and record revenue and EPS. The company’s strategic position is not only leading-edge wafers; it is the combination of N3/N2 and future nodes, CoWoS, SoIC, advanced packaging, HBM integration, customer trust, ecosystem depth, yield learning, and allocation power. The key investment conclusion is that TSMC’s AI pricing power should be analyzed at the system level, not as a generic foundry wafer ASP discussion.

Advanced packaging is a sustainable bottleneck if AI accelerators keep scaling through larger packages, chiplets, HBM stacks, and higher-bandwidth interconnect. CoWoS and 2.5D integration are required to connect leading-edge logic with HBM. Future architectures may increasingly require SoIC, hybrid bonding, co-packaged optics, panel-level packaging, or other high-density integration approaches. The public beneficiaries include TSMC, ASE, Amkor, JCET, Powertech, SPIL, Ibiden, Unimicron, Kinsus, AT&S, Shinko, Toppan, Ajinomoto/ABF, Resonac, Sumitomo Bakelite, BESI, ASMPT, Disco, Advantest, FormFactor, Cohu, KLA, Onto, Camtek, Nova, Applied Materials, Lam Research, Tokyo Electron, EV Group, and SUSS MicroTec. The key differentiation is qualification into high-end AI packages, not generic packaging exposure.

The risk is that market narratives overgeneralize CoWoS scarcity. TSMC’s internal advanced packaging is higher-quality exposure than many commodity OSAT or substrate suppliers. Some OSATs and substrate vendors will capture strong revenue growth but remain subject to customer concentration, capex intensity, mix shifts, and margin cyclicality. Panel-level packaging could reduce cost and expand capacity over time but may also alter the distribution of value capture if it commoditizes parts of the package flow. The best semicap exposures are those tied to process complexity, yield, inspection, metrology, bonding, plating, cleaning, and test rather than pure unit volume.

Samsung Foundry and Intel Foundry matter strategically, but their investment cases require execution proof. Samsung’s integrated memory/foundry/packaging position is strategically valuable if HBM and foundry execution improve simultaneously. Intel’s EMIB, Foveros, 18A roadmap, and U.S. strategic positioning have policy relevance, but external customer credibility and execution remain gating variables. GlobalFoundries, UMC, SMIC, and Rapidus have less direct frontier-AI exposure but remain relevant to the broader supply-chain bifurcation, mature-node support, packaging ecosystems, and geopolitical redundancy.

EDA and IP benefit from AI ASIC proliferation. Synopsys, Cadence, Siemens EDA, Ansys/Synopsys integration, Arm, Rambus, Alphawave Semi, and the UCIe ecosystem are structural beneficiaries of more chip designs, more advanced nodes, more chiplets, more verification complexity, and more custom silicon programs. The risk is not lack of demand; it is valuation and customer concentration. Hyperscaler ASIC programs increase EDA intensity, but some hyperscalers can internalize portions of design expertise and exert procurement pressure. Arm remains a strategic beneficiary of AI data center CPU architecture, edge AI, and custom silicon design, though its public equity sensitivity depends on royalty growth, licensing structure, and valuation.

NETWORKING, FABRICS, OPTICS, AND INTERCONNECT

Networking is moving from peripheral infrastructure to a core determinant of AI factory productivity. Scale-up networking allows GPUs or accelerators to act as a coherent high-bandwidth compute domain. Scale-out networking connects racks, clusters, storage, training jobs, inference endpoints, and multi-data-center deployments. Agentic AI adds tool calls, CPU orchestration, retrieval, memory access, storage-locality requirements, and asynchronous workflows. Multimodal workloads add large data objects and higher bandwidth across storage, compute, and serving systems. A poorly balanced network strands expensive accelerators.

The current architecture is bifurcated between proprietary/high-performance scale-up and more open scale-out. NVIDIA’s NVLink and NVSwitch remain central to high-end rack-scale systems, especially where the 72-GPU domain acts as a single massive accelerator. InfiniBand remains a high-performance training fabric. Ethernet is becoming more credible as AI scale-out expands, particularly through Ultra Ethernet Consortium efforts. UEC Specification 1.0 was launched to provide a high-performance, scalable, interoperable Ethernet-based communications stack for AI and HPC across NICs, switches, optics, and cables. The likely 2026-2030 path is not Ethernet replacing NVIDIA everywhere; it is Ethernet expanding the addressable profit pool in scale-out networking while NVIDIA remains strong in scale-up and vertically validated systems.

Broadcom, Marvell, Arista, NVIDIA/Mellanox, Cisco, Juniper/HPE, Nokia, Astera Labs, Credo, Coherent, Lumentum, Fabrinet, Innolight, Eoptolink, Zhongji Innolight, Accelink, Applied Optoelectronics, Amphenol, TE Connectivity, Molex, Luxshare, Semtech, and MACOM should be analyzed by layer. Switch ASICs, AI NICs, DPUs, retimers, redrivers, PAM4 DSPs, active electrical cables, optical modules, lasers, coherent and short-reach optics, co-packaged optics, connectors, and cable assemblies all benefit from cluster scale. The better margin pools are typically merchant/custom ASICs, high-speed SerDes, high-end connectivity silicon, switching platforms, and validated high-speed interconnect. The more cyclical or commoditizable pools are optics modules and cables where capacity can eventually overshoot, although near-term 800G and 1.6T demand can remain strong.

Arista is a high-quality scale-out beneficiary because cloud titans already use its software-centric networking model and because Ethernet AI fabrics fit its operating model. Broadcom is advantaged in switch ASICs, custom AI accelerators, SerDes, and co-designed hyperscaler relationships. Marvell has credible exposure to custom silicon, optical DSPs, networking, and storage but faces more program-specific ramp risk. Astera Labs and Credo offer more concentrated AI connectivity exposure but also higher valuation and customer-concentration risk. Coherent, Lumentum, and Fabrinet offer optical leverage, but cyclicality and pricing should be monitored closely.

The most important disconfirming evidence for the networking thesis would be a faster-than-expected collapse in optical lead times, a pricing reset in 800G/1.6T modules, NVIDIA retaining more of the AI fabric economics than expected, or hyperscalers internalizing too much connectivity value. The most important confirming evidence would be rising Ethernet AI fabric revenue at Arista, sustained Broadcom AI networking growth, Marvell custom silicon/optical ramps, tight 1.6T lead times, expanding AEC/retimer attach, and evidence that agentic inference materially increases east-west traffic.

SERVER, RACK, OEM, ODM, AND SYSTEM INTEGRATION

Server and rack vendors are necessary to the AI factory but usually weaker value-capture vehicles than semiconductors, memory, networking, packaging, power, and cooling. Supermicro, Dell, HPE, Lenovo, Quanta, Wistron, Wiwynn, Inventec, Foxconn/Hon Hai, Pegatron, Compal, Celestica, Jabil, Flex, and ZT Systems/AMD can report significant AI revenue growth because rack-scale AI systems carry high ASPs. However, much of that revenue is GPU pass-through. Durable profit capture depends on validated rack integration, liquid-cooling competence, supply assurance, time-to-deploy, quality control, warranty risk management, working-capital discipline, and customer-specific engineering.

Supermicro provides the highest-beta public example of the opportunity and risk. Speed, rack-scale customization, liquid-cooling integration, and NVIDIA ecosystem positioning can create strong revenue growth. The offset is lower margin quality, working-capital intensity, customer concentration, quality/warranty risk, and valuation sensitivity to order timing. Dell and HPE are more diversified and may benefit from enterprise AI infrastructure, storage, private cloud, and service attach, but AI server margins must be monitored because high revenue does not automatically imply high gross profit. Lenovo and ODMs capture hyperscale volume but face buyer power. Celestica, Jabil, and Flex may benefit from program-specific manufacturing exposure, though customer concentration and pass-through economics remain important.

The correct underwriting question is whether a systems vendor is earning scarce integration gross profit or merely financing inventory in a volatile supply chain. Strong backlog is not sufficient if GPUs dominate bill-of-materials, customer deposits are low, working capital expands, warranty terms are unfavorable, or cancellations rise when GPU spot markets soften. Rack vendors are most attractive when AI rack complexity is rising faster than procurement standardization; they are least attractive when rack designs become repeatable and buyer power normalizes.

DATA CENTERS, COLOCATION, NEOCLOUDS, AND HYPERSCALERS

The data center market has moved from space scarcity to power scarcity. CBRE’s 2026 data center outlook describes unprecedented AI demand, record low vacancy, mounting power constraints, and limited new deliveries. The implication is that powered shell capacity and real interconnection rights are materially more valuable than land. The spread between “land with a data center narrative” and “energized, permitted, preleased capacity with grid interconnection” should widen.

Hyperscalers are structurally better positioned than neoclouds because they combine balance sheets, procurement scale, customer demand, internal workloads, enterprise relationships, software distribution, and in some cases custom silicon. Microsoft’s $37B AI revenue run-rate disclosure is particularly important because it begins to connect AI infrastructure spend to revenue. Alphabet’s capex disclosure gives a concrete example of the server/data-center/networking split in AI technical infrastructure. Amazon’s AWS Q1 2026 sales growth of 28% to $37.6B shows that cloud demand remains robust, though AWS AI-specific economics are still less transparent than desired. Meta’s capex guidance of
[$125B-](https://x.com/search?q=%24125B-&src=cashtag_click)

$145B illustrates the scale of internal AI infrastructure investment tied to ads, recommendations, Llama, GenAI products, and MTIA.

Oracle is an important special case. OCI appears to have gained share in large-scale AI training and inference due to network architecture, willingness to structure large AI contracts, and customer-specific capacity. Oracle disclosed that certain large AI contracts include $75B of prepaid or customer-supplied hardware portions, reducing the capital it must raise. This is strategically significant: financing structure has become part of the AI factory product. A cloud vendor that can secure customer-funded hardware, prepayments, or long-duration take-or-pay structures has a lower balance-sheet burden than a vendor speculatively building GPU fleets.

CoreWeave is the clearest public test case for neocloud economics. The company disclosed $99.4B of revenue backlog as of March 31, 2026, including RPO and other committed-contract estimates, and it disclosed new Meta agreements including a $21B commitment. That backlog validates extraordinary demand for specialized AI cloud. It does not eliminate equity risk. Neocloud equity value depends on customer concentration, contract duration, take-or-pay structure, GPU residual values, refinancing cost, availability of power, data center execution, utilization, and whether gross profit exceeds depreciation and debt service through multiple GPU generations.

The neocloud bull case is that frontier labs, startups, sovereign customers, and enterprises need faster access to AI capacity than hyperscalers can provide, and specialized operators can run GPU fleets at higher utilization with better customer service. The bear case is that neoclouds become levered spread businesses owning rapidly depreciating assets financed with debt and leased to concentrated customers whose demand and pricing can reset faster than debt amortizes. If GPU spot prices fall, if NVIDIA/AMD generational transitions impair residual values, if refinancing spreads widen, or if key customers move workloads to hyperscalers or internal ASICs, equity value can be impaired even while reported revenue grows.

Equinix, Digital Realty, Vantage, QTS, CyrusOne, Switch, Flexential, DataBank, NTT, GDS, DigitalBridge, and Brookfield Infrastructure should be analyzed by powered capacity, preleasing, tenant quality, geography, grid interconnection, balance-sheet flexibility, and construction cost. Colocation can capture scarcity rents if capacity is power-secured and leased to strong counterparties. The risk is that AI workloads have much higher power density and cooling requirements than traditional enterprise workloads, which can require expensive retrofits and reduce fungibility. Power queue and time-to-energization are now more important than headline MW pipeline.

POWER, GRID, ENERGY, NUCLEAR, GAS, RENEWABLES, AND STORAGE

Power is the most important non-semiconductor bottleneck in the CS153 corpus. Scott Nolan’s lecture states the issue bluntly: a data center without power is not usable AI capacity. He argues that turbines have become scarce with multi-year lead times, that nuclear is strategically important but not an overnight solution, and that the U.S. nuclear fuel chain has a major enrichment bottleneck. Vahdat’s lecture adds that power utilization, redundancy, demand response, reliability trade-offs, and community impact are now core infrastructure design variables.

External data validate the power-scarcity thesis. The IEA estimates data center electricity consumption could roughly double to around 945 TWh by 2030, just under 3% of global electricity consumption, with power generation needed for data centers exceeding 1,000 TWh by 2030 in a related supply framing. The IEA also states that data center electricity demand grows roughly 15% annually from 2024 to 2030, about 4x faster than all other sectors. This supports the conclusion that the power story is not a minor thematic overlay; it is an upstream constraint on AI deployment.

The best public power beneficiaries are equipment and grid-execution companies with direct order conversion. GE Vernova reported Q1 2026 orders of $18.3B, up 71% organically, and stated that its Electrification segment booked $2.4B of equipment orders to support data centers, more than all of the prior year. Eaton reported strong electrical demand and backlog growth, with data-center demand contributing to the electrical cycle. Schneider Electric reported record Q1 2026 revenue of €9.8B, up 11.2% organically, with Energy Management up 12.8% and data centers highlighted as a major driver. Vertiv reported strong Q1 2026 profit and free-cash-flow metrics and remains directly exposed to AI-ready data center power and thermal systems.

The most genuine AI power beneficiaries are GE Vernova, Siemens Energy, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, Eaton, Schneider Electric, ABB, Siemens, Hubbell, nVent, Prysmian, Vertiv, Quanta Services, EMCOR, Comfort Systems, MasTec, and selected engineering/construction names. These companies sell into multiple scenarios: hyperscale buildouts, grid upgrades, electrification, data center power distribution, transformers, switchgear, UPS, substations, transmission, mechanical/electrical construction, and cooling. The risk is execution and valuation, not demand validity. Backlogs can be high but margins can be pressured by labor, supply constraints, fixed-price contracts, and cost inflation.

Existing firm power is higher-quality than speculative future generation. Constellation, Vistra, Talen, NRG, NextEra, Duke Energy, Southern, Entergy, Dominion, Exelon, PG&E, AES, and Brookfield Renewable should be analyzed through asset location, firm capacity, nuclear/gas exposure, PPA terms, interconnection, regulatory treatment, and ratepayer politics. Independent power producers with existing nuclear or gas assets near data center demand can capture scarcity rents or enter attractive PPAs. Regulated utilities can benefit through rate base growth, but public utility commissions may limit returns if ratepayer burden becomes politically contentious. Renewable developers benefit from PPAs and load growth, but intermittency and interconnection queues limit direct replacement of firm power for 24/7 AI workloads.

Nuclear is strategically important but time-mismatched for much of the 2026-2028 bottleneck. CS153’s Scott Nolan frames nuclear as a 5-10-year scaling vector and identifies uranium enrichment as a critical bottleneck. His transcript states that nuclear fuel requires mining, conversion to gas, enrichment, conversion back to solid, and fuel fabrication, and that the U.S. has less than 0.1% market share in enrichment, leaving it reliant on European firms and Russia. This supports long-term interest in Cameco, BWXT, Centrus, Oklo, NuScale, TerraPower, X-energy, Kairos, General Matter, Orano, and Urenco, but the timing is crucial. Existing nuclear plants, uprates, restarts, and PPAs matter sooner. SMRs and advanced nuclear matter more in 2030+ scenarios.

Natural gas is likely the near-to-medium-term bridge. Turbines, pipelines, interconnection, emissions policy, and gas availability become critical. GE Vernova, Siemens Energy, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, Caterpillar, Cummins, and pipeline-adjacent infrastructure can benefit if behind-the-meter or grid-connected gas generation accelerates. The risks are turbine slot scarcity, carbon commitments, local opposition, air permits, gas supply constraints, and the possibility that hyperscalers use demand response, power management, and efficiency to reduce peak load growth.

Cooling is becoming a system-level design issue. Rack-scale AI systems such as GB200/GB300 NVL72 and future Rubin-class platforms increase rack densities and push direct-to-chip liquid cooling, CDUs, pumps, rear-door heat exchangers, facility retrofits, coolant chemistry, leak detection, serviceability, and warranty into the core deployment equation. Vertiv, Schneider Electric, Eaton/Boyd, CoolIT, Asetek, Modine, nVent, Munters, Johnson Controls, Carrier, Trane, Supermicro, Dell, HPE, Lenovo, EMCOR, and Comfort Systems are the relevant exposures. The key distinction is platform-qualified thermal architecture versus generic HVAC exposure. The former can earn durable AI gross profit; the latter can become commodity capacity.

MODEL LABS, APPLICATIONS, AND DEMAND FORMATION

The demand side of the AI factory is shifting from chat to agents and multimodal production. CS153’s Sam Altman lecture argues that affordable token spend can let a startup do the work of a 100-person engineering team, while also suggesting that frontier labs have underinvested in delivering cheap intelligence at inference scale. That is a direct demand signal for inference compute, but it also implies application-level margin pressure if lower token prices become expected.

Current API pricing is a critical observable. OpenAI’s GPT-5.5 pricing is listed at $5 per 1M input tokens and $30 per 1M output tokens, with higher pricing for pro models and discounts for batch/flex usage. API price cuts can be read 2 ways. They can signal improving cost curves and expanding demand elasticity, which is bullish for total usage. They can also signal competitive pressure and model-provider gross margin compression, which is bearish for model-lab standalone economics. The relevant question is whether lower price per token drives enough growth in output tokens, agent steps, multimodal generation, and enterprise workflows to keep total infrastructure demand expanding.

Code agents are probably the cleanest near-term inference demand category because willingness to pay is tied to expensive labor and measurable productivity. A coding agent that raises engineering productivity can support high inference spend if it substitutes for engineering labor or accelerates software output. This supports Microsoft/GitHub, OpenAI, Anthropic, Cursor, Replit, Cognition, GitLab, Atlassian, Datadog, observability vendors, and cloud infrastructure providers. The risk is that coding-agent usage becomes highly competitive and price-per-task declines faster than usage expands.

Video and visual intelligence are among the most infrastructure-intensive application categories. Luma’s lecture emphasizes unified models combining language, image, video, audio, time, causality, memory, and world understanding. It also describes large multimodal data reserves, post-training, user preferences, human annotations, and continuous learning. This supports the view that video generation, creative workflows, world models, robotics simulation, advertising production, design tools, and visual agents can materially increase storage, GPU, HBM, networking, and serving requirements. The public beneficiaries include NVIDIA, AMD, Google, Amazon, Adobe, Microsoft, TSMC, HBM suppliers, storage vendors, and cloud providers; the private watchlist includes Luma, Runway, Pika, Black Forest Labs, Midjourney, Stability AI, and related creative model labs.

Voice systems create a different infrastructure profile: lower-latency inference, audio generation, streaming, safety, identity, localization, watermarking, fraud prevention, and real-time agent orchestration. ElevenLabs’ CS153 role reinforces voice as a human interface layer for agents. The relevant public equities include Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Apple, Cloudflare, Akamai, Twilio, Zoom, Salesforce, ServiceNow, security vendors, identity vendors, and potentially edge/cloud providers. The primary risks are trust, voice fraud, consent, identity rights, content policy, and commoditization of basic text-to-speech.

Enterprise AI demand is likely to bifurcate. High-intensity workflows include code agents, customer-service voice agents, sales/revenue operations agents, legal document automation, financial analysis, scientific research, design/creative workflows, cybersecurity automation, and robotics/simulation. Lower-intensity or commoditizing workflows include summarization, simple classification, static marketing copy, simple chatbots, and wrapper apps without proprietary data or workflow control. Infrastructure demand will be pulled by the former; software margin compression will be concentrated in the latter.

AI-NATIVE SOFTWARE AND OPERATING MODELS

The Garry Tan/Diana Hu lecture is best understood as an operating-model thesis rather than a startup hype lecture. AI-native companies are described as closed-loop systems with agents, memory, evals, permissions, feedback loops, and read access to company artifacts. The key investment implication is that productivity gains do not come from generic AI adoption; they come from redesigning the company as an agentic system where context, workflow, evaluation, and memory are native infrastructure. This is directly relevant to public SaaS because it shifts the moat from code and UI toward workflow control, proprietary data, distribution, integrations, security, compliance, and customer trust.

AI-native software economics are likely to diverge by category. Software companies that can price AI features explicitly, route intelligently across models, use proprietary data, automate high-value workflows, and reduce labor intensity can expand margins. Software companies that bundle expensive inference into flat-rate subscriptions, lack workflow ownership, lack proprietary data, or face low switching costs can see gross margin compression. This explains why a blanket “SaaS apocalypse” framework is too crude. Ben Horowitz’s CS153 lecture makes a similar point by distinguishing weak code/UI moats from stronger supply-chain, channel, workflow, data, and domain-specific moats.

The most advantaged public software names are those with distribution, identity, workflow, data gravity, observability, or security posture. Microsoft has the strongest combined position through Azure, GitHub, Microsoft 365, Copilot, OpenAI exposure, enterprise distribution, identity, security, and internal silicon efforts. ServiceNow has workflow depth and enterprise process control. Salesforce has distribution and customer data but must prove AI attach, pricing, and margin discipline. Adobe has creative workflow control and Firefly distribution but faces intense model competition. Atlassian and GitLab face both opportunity and disruption from code agents. Snowflake and Databricks benefit from data gravity and AI data pipelines, but consumption growth must be monitored. Datadog, Dynatrace, Elastic, Splunk/Cisco, and Cloudflare can benefit from AI observability and infrastructure complexity, though cloud-native bundling is a risk.

Nikhyl Singhal’s CS153 product lecture adds a labor-market and organizational lens. Product, design, and engineering roles are merging as builders use AI tools directly. Information-moving middle management is structurally pressured, while high-judgment builders, product architects, and AI-native operators gain value. For public software, this implies lower long-term headcount intensity in some categories, but it also implies that incumbents with slow product loops and heavy management layers are vulnerable to smaller teams moving faster. The analysis should therefore focus not only on product functionality but on organizational speed, AI-native development cadence, and the ability to convert usage data into product improvements.

SECURITY, RESILIENCE, SOVEREIGNTY, AND CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE

CS153’s security lecture reframes AI infrastructure as critical infrastructure. Joe Sullivan’s lecture argues that cybersecurity has shifted from primarily protecting data from leaving the building to preserving operational resilience against ransomware and disruption. This matters because AI factories combine valuable model weights, training data, inference endpoints, identity systems, tool permissions, code-generation pipelines, physical data centers, firmware, GPU clusters, and cloud orchestration layers. A breach or outage can impair not only confidentiality but production capability.

AI increases the attack surface in 7 major areas. 1. Model weights become high-value intellectual property and potentially strategic assets. 2. Training data can be poisoned, stolen, or litigated. 3. Inference endpoints can be abused through prompt injection, data exfiltration, automated fraud, or denial-of-service. 4. Agents create new permissioning and identity risks because they can act on behalf of humans. 5. Code-generation tools increase software velocity but also increase vulnerability velocity. 6. Data centers and power systems become operational targets. 7. Open-source and private model release decisions become security-policy variables.

The public beneficiaries include Palo Alto Networks, CrowdStrike, Zscaler, Fortinet, Cloudflare, Akamai, Fastly, Okta, SentinelOne, Wiz, CyberArk, Datadog, Dynatrace, Elastic, Splunk/Cisco, MongoDB, Snowflake, Databricks, Informatica, Collibra, and confidential-computing/security hardware vendors. The highest-quality exposure is not generic endpoint protection; it is identity, cloud security, data security posture management, runtime agent monitoring, AI application firewalls, model-security tooling, observability, incident response, governance, and secure access to enterprise data.

The main risk is platform bundling. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Cloudflare, Palo Alto Networks, CrowdStrike, Zscaler, and Cisco/Splunk will all try to own overlapping AI security and observability budgets. The category will grow, but pricing power may concentrate in platforms rather than point products. AI security startups will proliferate, but only those with deployment depth, data access, governance integration, and runtime visibility should command durable economics.

Sovereign AI and regulated enterprise AI increase demand for local infrastructure, hybrid cloud, on-prem systems, data residency, confidential computing, auditability, and secure model serving. Oracle, Microsoft, Google, AWS, HPE, Dell, Lenovo, NVIDIA, AMD, Intel, sovereign cloud operators, cybersecurity vendors, and data governance providers all benefit. However, sovereign AI can be economically inefficient if capacity is built for political reasons rather than utilization. The correct distinction is sovereign AI capex vs sovereign AI return on capital.

GEOPOLITICS, EXPORT CONTROLS, CHINA, AND SUPPLY CHAIN

AI infrastructure is now a geopolitical asset. U.S. export controls have expanded from advanced GPUs to semiconductor manufacturing equipment, software tools, HBM, and related supply-chain items. BIS’s December 2024 rule added controls on semiconductor manufacturing equipment, software tools, and HBM, and BIS’s January 2026 policy shifted certain advanced chip exports to China and Macau toward case-by-case review under security conditions. This matters because HBM, advanced accelerators, advanced packaging, and manufacturing tools are all chokepoints in the AI factory.

The U.S.-China split creates 2 simultaneous effects. It reduces addressable market for U.S.-linked accelerator, HBM, EDA, and equipment suppliers in restricted scenarios, but it also increases strategic value and pricing power for compliant non-China supply chains. NVIDIA, AMD, Broadcom, Marvell, Micron, TSMC, Samsung, SK hynix, ASML, Applied Materials, Lam Research, KLA, Tokyo Electron, Synopsys, Cadence, Arm, and advanced packaging vendors all have some exposure to policy volatility. China will continue to invest in domestic accelerators, foundry, memory, networking, and model labs, but frontier parity is constrained by leading-edge foundry, HBM, EUV, advanced packaging, EDA, and tool access.

Taiwan concentration remains the largest single geopolitical risk in the AI factory. TSMC’s leading-edge and packaging dominance is strategically beneficial in normal conditions and a systemic risk in stress conditions. Korea is central through HBM. Japan is central through materials, equipment, substrates, and components. The Netherlands is central through EUV. The U.S. is central through GPU/ASIC design, cloud platforms, EDA, and AI labs. Europe and the Middle East are increasingly important through sovereign AI, power, and capital allocation. This global interdependence means the AI factory is structurally exposed to export controls, sanctions, Taiwan risk, shipping lanes, rare materials, power policy, and sovereign capital allocation.

VALUE POOLS AND UNIT ECONOMICS

The AI infrastructure value pool should be modeled bottom-up from token and multimodal demand rather than top-down from TAM headlines. A useful model starts with users, sessions per user, agent steps per session, tokens per step, output-token ratio, multimodal multiplier, latency target, model mix, and cost per token. That drives required inference throughput, which drives accelerator units, HBM attach, networking attach, power, rack count, cooling, and data center MW. Training demand should be modeled separately through model size, tokens trained, training runs per year, experimental run intensity, synthetic data loops, and frontier lab cadence.

The most relevant unit equations are straightforward. Cost per token equals accelerator depreciation, HBM/package premium amortization, power, networking, storage, CPU, operations, and financing costs divided by tokens produced at the target quality and latency multiplied by utilization. Gross profit per token equals realized price per token minus cost per token. Revenue per MW equals tokens per MW multiplied by average realized price per token. GPU-hour gross profit equals price per GPU-hour minus depreciation, power, networking, storage, operations, and financing per hour. Neocloud equity value is the present value of that spread after interest, debt amortization, and residual-value changes.

The 2026-2030 revenue pools can be grouped qualitatively. Accelerators and custom AI silicon are the largest and most visible near-term pool. HBM is smaller but higher-scarcity. Foundry and advanced packaging are high-quality structural pools, especially where TSMC dominates. Networking and optics are increasingly large and more competitive. Servers and racks have large revenue but lower gross-profit quality. Data center construction and power equipment are large, multi-year pools with execution constraints. Cooling is a high-growth sub-pool tied to rack density. Cloud/neocloud AI services may become very large but are subject to utilization, pricing, and depreciation. Model/API revenue is high growth but price competitive. Enterprise AI software can be high margin if priced and differentiated, but inference COGS can compress margins. Security, observability, and data governance are durable second-order beneficiaries.

The most important sensitivity is model efficiency. If inference cost falls 10x and usage grows 50x, infrastructure demand rises. If inference cost falls 100x and usage grows 5x, infrastructure demand falls. If cost falls 100x and agentic/multimodal usage grows 1,000x, the entire stack remains supply constrained. The evidence from CS153 leans toward high elasticity because speakers repeatedly describe cheap intelligence as demand-expanding, with agents, coding, voice, video, and creative workflows as new demand categories. However, this remains the highest-uncertainty variable.

SCENARIOS FOR 2026-2030

In the bull scenario, token and multimodal usage grow 50x-100x+, cost per token falls 30x-100x, and demand elasticity dominates efficiency. Agentic workflows, code generation, video, voice, enterprise automation, search, robotics, and scientific workloads all scale. Power remains tight but buildable through gas, grid equipment, demand response, renewables, storage, and early nuclear-related contracts. NVIDIA remains dominant but loses some share to ASICs; AMD gains as a second source; Broadcom and Marvell benefit from custom silicon; HBM remains tight; TSMC and advanced packaging retain pricing power; power/cooling/grid equipment remain durable winners; hyperscalers convert capex into AI revenue; neoclouds with strong contracts survive. Winners include NVIDIA, TSMC, HBM leaders, Broadcom, Marvell, Arista, Astera Labs, Credo, Eaton, Schneider Electric, Vertiv, GE Vernova, Quanta Services, EMCOR, Comfort Systems, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Oracle, and selected AI workflow software names.

In the base scenario, token growth is strong but uneven, cost declines remain meaningful, and infrastructure bottlenecks rotate. Training remains strategic but inference becomes the primary economic battleground. NVIDIA remains the largest merchant platform, but ASIC share rises. HBM remains tight through 2027 and more balanced by 2028. CoWoS and advanced packaging improve but remain strategically important. Ethernet gains share in scale-out networking but does not displace NVIDIA scale-up. Power and grid constraints delay some projects, raising the value of already energized capacity. Hyperscaler capex remains high but more disciplined, and AI revenue disclosure improves. The correct portfolio is balanced across semis, memory, packaging, networking, power, cooling, and software infrastructure.

In the bear scenario, model efficiency improves faster than monetized demand, enterprise adoption remains pilot-heavy, API prices fall faster than usage expands, and hyperscalers slow capex for more than 1 quarter. GPU spot pricing weakens, neocloud refinancing costs rise, HBM pricing normalizes, CoWoS lead times fall, server/rack backlogs are revised lower, and optics capacity overshoots. NVIDIA remains strategically strong but gross margins reset; AMD and ASIC vendors still gain some share but at lower overall accelerator TAM; power equipment backlogs hold up longer than semis because grid cycles are multi-year, but valuation multiples compress. The worst exposures are levered neoclouds, AI server pass-through names, high-multiple optics without defensible content, and software companies with underpriced inference.

In the stress scenario, an external shock dominates: Taiwan conflict, export-control escalation, power permitting freeze, cyber incident affecting AI infrastructure, major data center backlash, or financing-market dislocation. The portfolio reaction would favor defense-like supply-chain assets, cybersecurity, grid resilience, existing firm power, U.S.-based memory and advanced manufacturing, and balance-sheet-rich hyperscalers, while pressuring levered neoclouds, Taiwan-heavy single-point exposures, speculative power developers, and highly valued single-product AI infrastructure companies.

INVESTABLE CONCLUSIONS

NVIDIA remains a core AI infrastructure long bias but should be risk-managed as a crowded position with asymmetric gross-margin expectations. The company’s system moat is real, and CS153’s architecture discussion supports its transition from chips to rack-scale AI factories. The key disconfirming evidence would be rapid hyperscaler ASIC share gain in high-value inference, declining Blackwell/Rubin pricing, falling networking attach, shortening lead times, or gross margins moving toward conventional semiconductor levels faster than expected.

TSMC remains a structural AI factory bottleneck. Leading-edge logic, CoWoS, SoIC, advanced packaging, and ecosystem execution are not easily replicated. The principal risk is geopolitical, followed by customer concentration and possible packaging capacity normalization. TSMC is higher-quality AI infrastructure exposure than most OSATs because it controls the scarce integration point.

HBM leaders remain high-conviction but should be monitored for capacity overbuild. SK hynix, Micron, and Samsung can all benefit, but qualification, yield, stack height, customer mix, and HBM4 timing determine relative winners. Commodity DRAM/NAND beta should not be treated as equivalent to AI HBM exposure.

Broadcom is a high-quality ASIC/networking beneficiary. Its AI revenue disclosure provides rare visibility into the custom accelerator and AI networking value pool. The risk is customer concentration and the possibility that hyperscalers push economics lower as programs scale. Marvell offers more optical/custom-silicon torque but with greater program execution risk.

Arista, Astera Labs, Credo, Coherent, Lumentum, Fabrinet, Innolight, Eoptolink, Amphenol, and TE Connectivity benefit from scale-out AI networking and optics, but dispersion will be high. The best names will have durable content, low-power high-speed connectivity, validated hyperscaler qualification, and pricing resilience. The weakest will be exposed to module oversupply or commoditized hardware.

Power and cooling remain among the most attractive second-derivative beneficiaries because the power bottleneck is validated by both CS153 and external data. GE Vernova, Siemens Energy, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, Eaton, Schneider Electric, ABB, Siemens, Hubbell, nVent, Prysmian, Vertiv, Quanta Services, EMCOR, and Comfort Systems all have genuine AI read-through. The primary risk is valuation after a large rerating, not the reality of demand.

Existing firm-power owners are more investable than speculative future power concepts. Constellation, Vistra, Talen, and NRG have real assets that can serve data center demand. Regulated utilities can benefit through load growth but face ratepayer and regulatory risk. SMR and advanced nuclear names provide long-dated optionality, but most are poorly matched to the 2026-2028 bottleneck.

Hyperscalers should be evaluated on AI return on capital, not capex scale. Microsoft has the clearest AI revenue disclosure; Alphabet has a strong internal TPU and AI product stack; Amazon has AWS scale and Trainium/Inferentia; Meta has internal AI demand, ads monetization, Llama, and MTIA; Oracle has differentiated AI contract structuring; CoreWeave has high backlog but higher leverage and residual-value risk. The ranking depends on AI revenue transparency, depreciation burden, custom silicon success, and power-secured capacity.

Public SaaS should be split by moat type. Workflow/data/channel/security moats can survive and improve with AI. Code/UI-only moats are structurally impaired. AI features that are bundled without usage-based monetization can dilute gross margins. AI-native labor compression is real, but inference COGS must be measured. ServiceNow, Microsoft, Adobe, Salesforce, Datadog, Snowflake, Databricks, Cloudflare, Palo Alto Networks, CrowdStrike, Zscaler, and Okta should be evaluated through the lens of pricing power, proprietary data, workflow control, distribution, and AI cost pass-through.

Cybersecurity, observability, identity, and data governance are durable beneficiaries because AI factories increase operational complexity and attack surface. The risk is not demand; it is platform consolidation and customer budget overlap. Best-positioned vendors are those with deep runtime visibility, identity context, cloud-native telemetry, data access governance, and AI-specific controls.

KPI DASHBOARD AND DISCONFIRMATION TESTS

The most important quarterly KPIs are hyperscaler capex growth, depreciation growth, AI revenue disclosure, cloud gross margin, free cash flow, data center lease commitments, internal silicon adoption, and AI product attach. If capex continues to rise while AI revenue, cloud gross margin, or user engagement fails to improve, the AI factory thesis weakens at the hyperscaler equity level even if supplier revenue remains strong.

The most important semiconductor KPIs are Blackwell/GB300/Rubin lead times, GPU ASPs, networking attach, HBM sold-out duration, HBM4 qualification, CoWoS capacity, advanced packaging lead times, substrate pricing, AI switch/optics demand, and 800G/1.6T module pricing. The thesis weakens if HBM capacity becomes abundant, CoWoS lead times normalize, GPU spot prices fall persistently, or hyperscaler ASIC programs materially reduce merchant GPU share.

The most important power/data center KPIs are MW requested vs contracted vs permitted vs under construction vs energized; data center vacancy; preleasing; power pricing; transformer lead times; switchgear backlog; turbine slot availability; utility load forecasts; interconnection queue movement; local permitting outcomes; and demand-response adoption. The thesis weakens if announced MW fails to become energized, regulators block cost recovery, or hyperscalers slow projects due to power or community opposition.

The most important AI demand KPIs are API pricing, output-token pricing, model usage, paid seats, coding-agent adoption, enterprise deployment conversion, multimodal generation volume, inference latency targets, customer willingness to pay, and gross margin of AI products. The thesis weakens if token growth decelerates despite price cuts, enterprise usage remains pilot-level, or AI software gross margins compress materially.

The most important financing KPIs are neocloud debt spreads, GPU-backed financing terms, contract duration, take-or-pay percentage, customer concentration, collateral advance rates, residual-value assumptions, customer prepayments, and refinancing maturity walls. The thesis weakens if neoclouds require equity issuance or expensive refinancing despite strong revenue growth.

What utilization is required to justify the next tranche of capex? How much power is requested, contracted, permitted, under construction, energized, and utilized?

What is the HBM3E and HBM4 qualification status by major customer? What yield assumptions are embedded in capacity guidance? How much commodity DRAM capacity is being converted? What pricing mechanism applies to HBM4? What is the margin difference between HBM and commodity DRAM at normalized supply?

For foundry and packaging suppliers, the critical questions are: What is CoWoS and SoIC capacity by quarter? What portion is allocated to GPUs versus ASICs? For software and AI application companies, the critical questions are: What is gross margin by AI product? How much inference cost is passed through versus bundled? What AI security capabilities are native versus partner-integrated? How much of the market is at risk of cloud-platform bundling?

FINAL ASSESSMENT

CS153’s investment message is that generative AI infrastructure is entering the hard part of the cycle. The initial market phase rewarded visible scarcity in NVIDIA GPUs and frontier model narratives. The next phase will reward balanced AI factory deployment: high-utilization compute, qualified HBM, advanced packaging, low-latency high-bandwidth fabrics, liquid-cooled rack-scale systems, energized power, resilient data centers, secure inference endpoints, and applications with enough economic value to pay for the factory. The best investments will be those where scarcity is physical, validated, and monetized. The worst will be those where scarcity is narrated, financed with leverage, or measured using the wrong metric.

The most robust conclusion is that AI infrastructure demand can remain strong even if parts of the current AI equity trade disappoint. A world with faster ASIC adoption can still be bullish for HBM, TSMC, packaging, networking, power, cooling, and hyperscaler margin efficiency. A world with falling inference cost can still be bullish if usage elasticity is high. A world with open-source model adoption can still increase infrastructure demand while pressuring model-provider pricing. A world with sovereign AI capex can help suppliers while producing poor returns for local operators. A world with rapid AI-native software adoption can reduce labor intensity while increasing inference COGS. These are not contradictions; they are the core complexity of the AI factory cycle.

The committee-level positioning implication is to maintain exposure to bottlenecked, validated AI infrastructure while reducing exposure to pass-through, levered, or narrative-only beneficiaries. The preferred exposure stack is: 1. NVIDIA and selected accelerator/custom-silicon leaders, with margin-risk awareness; 2. HBM and advanced packaging; 3. TSMC and process-control/advanced-packaging semicap; 4. AI networking and high-speed interconnect; 5. power, grid, and cooling equipment; 6. hyperscalers with disclosed AI monetization and balance-sheet capacity; 7. security, observability, identity, and data-governance platforms; and 8. AI-native software with workflow/data moats and clear AI pricing. The avoid/short-risk stack is: 1. levered neoclouds without contract-duration and financing protection; 2. server/rack pass-through names priced as scarce IP; 3. AI app wrappers with no workflow/data moat; 4. speculative power narratives without interconnection or near-term generation; and 5. software companies bundling expensive inference without pricing power.

The 2026-2030 AI infrastructure cycle should therefore be underwritten less like a generic technology adoption curve and more like a compressed industrial buildout with software-like demand volatility and utility-like physical constraints. The winning analytical framework is not “AI demand is high.” It is: which bottleneck is binding, who owns it, how long it lasts, what margin it earns, what capex is required to relieve it, what customer concentration exists, how much utilization is visible, and what evidence would prove the thesis wrong.
