## TL;DR

1. **GS baseline (Briggs): a MODEST 15% productivity uplift after full AI adoption** → applying historical productivity→displacement elasticity → **~9% of US workers (~15M) reallocated over ~10 years** (<1pp unemployment increase in any given year, spread out) — with **net job creation reabsorbing them** (85% of 80-yr job growth = tech-created new roles; ~30M created / ~29M destroyed annually). NOT a jobs apocalypse.
2. **Acemoglu (the skeptic): small NET job loss of <2-4% within 5 years** — visible ~2027 (more layoffs/slower hiring where AI can replace tasks). Nowhere near the tech-commentator doom.
3. **★ Acemoglu's key thesis (the on-thesis part): the bottleneck is the MISSING reliable, easy-to-use APPLICATION layer on top of the foundation models — not capability.** Prompt-engineering by individuals/managers is "inconsistent, time-consuming, unreliable." Companies need **"the Microsoft Office version of AI"** — turnkey apps they can adopt — **"and we're not seeing those applications yet."** Coding/software is the exception (models already capable + engineers are AI-expert). Agentic AI opens the path *only if* app/model developers ship reliable turnkey tools (not every company DIY-ing).
4. **Investment is skewed to REPLACING workers, not COMPLEMENTING them** (Acemoglu's decade-long argument) — the complementary path is more productive but under-invested → **bigger net job losses in 10-15 years if this continues.** Labor-income inequality likely rises.
5. **The big wild card: AI + robotics** — physical tasks are **~50% of US work**; a robotics breakthrough would "open up the number of jobs AI impacts hugely." (Efforts more outside the US.)
6. **Thompson: "rising tides, not crashing waves"** — capability ≠ adoption; you also need data access (privacy/records friction) + cost-effectiveness (often not worth it). Slow, uneven, *visible* — large firms before small, high-value tasks before the long tail. Skeptical AI "destroys work without creating it."

**Neil Thompson — MIT (capability ≠ adoption, ~09:00–17:00):**

- AI is **broader but less reliable** than past tech (Excel/DB = narrow, 100% reliable; AI = broad, hallucination-prone) → "a tool that helps," not a modular replace. **"Rising tides, not crashing waves"** — gradual, visible, manageable. `[opinion]`

**Daron Acemoglu — MIT/Nobel (the skeptic, ~17:00–27:00):**

Coding is the exception. **Agentic AI helps only if app/model developers ship reliable turnkey tools**, not every company building its own. `[forward — the app-layer-bottleneck thesis]`

[Briggs] Today AI's imprint shows in tech, management consulting, graphic design — combined, ~10,000-15,000 drag on month-over-month job growth; still a narrow shock. Under our baseline **15% productivity uplift after full AI adoption**, combined with the historical productivity→displacement elasticity, we estimate **~9% of US workers reallocated during the transition — ~15M workers over ~10 years** — but <1pp unemployment rise in any given year. We expect **more than enough new jobs created to reabsorb them**: over 80 years ~85% of job growth was tech-created new positions; ~30M jobs created / ~29M destroyed annually; even a 5% acceleration in job creation offsets AI displacement. I don't subscribe to permanent mass job loss — the doom view focuses on job loss and ignores job creation.

[Thompson] AI is transformative and improving fast, but capability is only one step to job change. You also need to give it all the right information (privacy/records access — hard) and it must be cost-effective (my lab: often it isn't — exacting systems cost a lot to run). So capability improves fast, but **adoption takes much longer**; large firms before small, high-value tasks before a long tail. Expert vs inexpert (with David Autor): if your *inexpert* tasks get automated you become more valuable (wages up, fewer of you — proofreaders after spellcheck); if your *expert* tasks get automated, wages fall but more enter (GPS → more Uber drivers). AI is broader than Excel/databases but less reliable (hallucinations) — a tool that helps, not a modular replace. **"Rising tides, not crashing waves"** — gradual, visible, manageable; we should be skeptical it destroys work without creating it.

[Acemoglu] Very hard to predict, but I'd imagine ~2027 we see a bit more layoffs / slower hiring where AI can replace some tasks. **I do not think current models are good at complementing workers yet** — most use is small (checking text, reference checks). **Net job losses <2-4% within 5 years** — nowhere near the apocalypse. What makes me cautious: **we don't have easy-to-use applications on the foundation models that large employers can adopt** — prompt-engineering by managers is inconsistent, unreliable; we need the **"Microsoft Office version of AI," and we're not seeing those apps yet.** Coding is the exception (models capable + engineers are AI-expert). Agentic AI helps *if* developers ship reliable turnkey tools, not every firm DIY-ing. Most vulnerable: cognitive-routine (customer service, back office) ≈ 8-9M US workers. Over 10-15 years it depends where investment goes — **the complementary path is productive but under-invested; investment is skewed to replacing workers → bigger net losses if it continues.** The **big wild card is AI + robotics** — physical is ~50% of US work; a breakthrough expands the impact hugely. There's **no economic law that job creation must match destruction** — since ~1980 it's fallen short for non-college workers; I expect **labor-income inequality to rise** (routine cognitive jobs aren't highly paid; well-paid workers find other jobs, so the next layer bears the burden).
