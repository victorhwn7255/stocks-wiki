## TL;DR

A growth-equity veteran argues the AI-kills-software narrative is overdone and the software derating is a buying window: software's moat was never R&D — it is **distribution, sales/marketing, and customer relationships**, so AI-era software is "the incumbent's game to lose" (Workday's 98–99% gross dollar retention as exhibit A; e-commerce's incumbent-won history as the analogy). On the macro he is a named bear: the **"AI CapEx bubble will end badly — telecom all over again"** because the earnings math and the power math don't work ("where are the nuclear power plants coming from?"), and **models will commoditize** (hyperscalers' data + cost advantage; DeepSeek-class models running locally at a fraction of the cost). The one AI lane he validates from his own portfolio: **consumption-billed infrastructure software** (ClickHouse, Grafana, Datadog ~high-20s/30% growth at scale) because "agents appear to consume more resources than people."

## Key claims + numbers (timestamps from chapter markers; tags: [verifiable] / [opinion] / [forward])

**The AI exit-multiple trap (~17:24–19:24)**
- 2020–21's error is repeating in AI: underwriting 20–25x revenue exit multiples. "You can pay 20–25x revenues and if you're right it's fantastic… but when your maximum multiple collapses, you look like an idiot." `[opinion]`
- Shorthand test: if the company grows for 18 months and you're still not in the money, you overpaid. `[opinion]`

**Software as a game of distribution (~19:24–23:20) — the moat thesis**
- "The competitive advantage of software has never been about R&D… software companies are really about distribution, sales and marketing, customer success, client services. It is the incumbent's game to lose." `[opinion — his single moat discriminator]`
- Workday: 98–99% gross dollar retention; ~$10B revenue growing 10–15%; ~$3B FCF; Exxon/KKR/P&G spent 3–5 years implementing it; enterprises will not DIY-rebuild HR software, though "the GUI and how you access it is going to be far different." `[verifiable at WDAY 10-K — figures roughly consistent with public data]`
- Thousands of Workday engineers improving the product beats "my cousin vibe-coding his way to building Workday." `[opinion]`
- The disruption-ripe cohort is **over-levered PE-owned software** (Coupa post-Thoma Bravo) where R&D/sales/product got cut to hit margin targets — not independent growth-focused incumbents. `[opinion — a kill-zone refinement]`
- E-commerce analogy: in 1999–2000 everyone thought big-box retail was dead; the top-10 US e-commerce list today is Amazon + Walmart, Home Depot, Lowe's, Macy's, Target… incumbents won; the losers (Sears, Kmart, Montgomery Ward) were over-levered or didn't innovate. `[opinion/analogy]`
- "The best risk-adjusted returns right now are in public software names… people hate software." Constellation Software's chart as the skepticism indicator. `[opinion + book-talking; price observation = signal only, not weighted]`

**The AI capex bear case (~42:05–46:00) — the demand-side challenge**
- "This AI CapEx bubble will end badly… it's like the telecom bubble all over again." Apple may look like the smart one. `[opinion/forward]`
- The narrative-incentive meta-claim: "VCs investing in all these AI companies *have to* portray the view that software is going to be dead, because they have to justify how much money they're spending." `[opinion — cuts both ways; see red flags]`
- The math: money going in → earnings required → **power required** — "where are the nuclear power plants coming up? This doesn't work." `[opinion — note he treats the vault's power-constraint thesis as bubble evidence]`
- Host's counter (carried honestly): telecom overbuilt dark fiber that sat idle; AI GPUs are "burning" and everything still feels mega under-supplied. Green's concession: "I have no clue when this will stop. It will probably go longer than people think." `[opinion both sides]`
- **Models will commoditize**: Google/Meta/Amazon/Apple have more data and a structural cost advantage; Chinese/European models (DeepSeek named) cost a fraction and run locally — token-price deflation undermines the model companies' economics. `[forward]`
- Anthropic's recent round "was kind of like an IPO" — i.e., the cycle-top liquidity event candidate. `[opinion]`

**The consumption-infra validation (~46:00–47:00)**
- "Agents appear to consume more resources than actually people" → consumption-based infra software is the AI lane he finds investable: ClickHouse, Grafana Labs; Datadog "growing high 20s, 30% a year at scale." `[verifiable — DDOG ~28% per its FY2025; consistent]`
- ClickHouse's burn-to-revenue ratio "is like nothing" — capital-efficient AI-era growth exists. `[opinion/verifiable at Tier 4]`

**AI readiness scoring of portfolio companies (~42:30–44:00)**
- Lead Edge scores every portfolio company on AI readiness: data structured for AI leverage; AI product iteration rate; **AI revenues on new products** — explicitly *not* whether engineering headcount shrank. "If your 2026 budget was 150 engineers, you should still have 150 — they're exponentially more productive and create more products to sell." `[opinion — anti-headcount-cut framing]`

**Off-thesis but flagged for completeness:** the Lead Edge 8 criteria (~15:24; revenue >$10M, growth >25%, gross margin >70%, recurring, capital-efficient revenue>cumulative-burn, profitable, no customer concentration) — interesting investor-craft, no vault hook; the criteria notably do NOT predict deal outcomes by his own admission ("no correlation"), they are a focus filter.
