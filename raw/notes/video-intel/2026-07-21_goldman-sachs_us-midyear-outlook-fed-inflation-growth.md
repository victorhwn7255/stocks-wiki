**Allison Nathan:** With a newly installed Fed chairman, a surprisingly soft inflation print, and persistent geopolitical tensions, what are the implications for US inflation, growth, and Fed policy ahead? I'm Allison Nathan and this is Goldman Sachs Exchanges. Here to break it down is my colleague from Goldman Sachs Research, David Mericle, our chief US economist. David, welcome back. — Thanks, Allison.

Let's start wide. Thinking about the many twists and turns this year, what about the US economy has gone just as you expected, and more interestingly what has surprised you the most? — I don't know that anything has gone just as we expected. On the surprises — a good one and a not-so-good one. On the good side: after we barely created any jobs for the better part of a year, all of a sudden, in the aftermath (somewhat puzzlingly) of a war breaking out and oil prices spiking, job growth has picked up a lot over the last four months. The labor market now looks fairly different than it did at the beginning of the year — that was something we'd been a little worried about, and now that concern, at least for now, is off the table. On the negative side, the biggest surprise: whereas we started the year thinking inflation would get better for the simple reason that the tariffs are behind us and would drop out of the year-on-year calculation, we've unfortunately seen — because of the effects of the war and the effects of AI demand, which have been amplified in the price statistics by mismeasurement — that inflation will go more sideways, stay closer to 3% than 2%.

Let's stick with inflation. We did have a pretty weak print last week — June CPI came in weaker than expected. What do you make of that? An inflection point, or a mechanical shift that reverses (gasoline went down, now headed up again)? — I think that was probably a bit of an outlier print, and I wouldn't say it greatly changed our view on the path. But for a number of reasons we think it's the start of a run of softer inflation data than we saw over the prior six months. One: tariff effects now seem pretty negligible on a go-forward monthly basis. Two: while there's been re-escalation recently in the war with Iran and higher oil prices, they're still down quite a bit from those late-April/May peaks — so the biggest effects of the war on prices probably occurred in Q2, and the sequential effects in Q3/Q4 would be smaller. And the third big theme — these mismeasured, exaggerated effects of AI demand in the PCE index — there too, not entirely behind us, but probably sequentially softer in the coming months. Coupled with the fact that the economy itself is not creating an inflation problem — it's these other factors that kept inflation higher for longer — we think we'll see softer monthly inflation going forward. June is the start of that, but probably exaggerates the good news a bit.

Dig in on the re-escalation of the war — you're not concerned about the inflation risks? — If oil prices stay where they are, we'd continue to expect softer sequential inflation. But absolutely, that's the key upside risk. Net of the effects of tariffs, the war, and these AI effects, inflation has actually been reasonably close to 2% for a while. Core CPI, if you take out even just tariff effects, is running close to 2%; these effects on PCE are worth a little more than a percentage point. So if it weren't for these factors we'd be reasonably close to 2%. The biggest wild card going forward is absolutely the war.

We have a Fed meeting next week — the second for new chairman Kevin Warsh. Despite softer inflation data and your expectation of continued softening, he has a cautious tone, and other Fed members have come out somewhat hawkish relative to expectations. What should investors make of it? — The June CPI data and their implications for PCE are probably soft enough to keep the Fed on hold at the July meeting — that seems pretty clear. On subsequent meetings, the message from the FOMC minutes is that from here they're kind of done litigating what exactly is causing inflation — whether it's appropriate to look through the different factors. If we continue to see high inflation, many feel we really need to respond, because this has gone on too long. Everyone agrees that at some point, even if this is a long series of one-time supply shocks, it becomes dangerous — it risks making people too accustomed to high inflation, and it could take on a life of its own. Normally you'd look through a one-time tariff price-level increase, or an oil spike from a war, or inflation from mismeasurement in one price index — that's how I'd think about it too. But in this context the message is that they've downplayed reasons for high inflation for too long, and whatever the source, they won't have much further patience. So the inflation path we expect for the rest of the year — core PCE at about 20 basis points a month or a touch higher — would be soft enough to keep the Fed comfortable looking through this and staying on hold. But there's not a lot of margin for error.

Just to clarify — your mainline expectation is Fed on hold this year, the next move a cut in 2027? — That's right, that's our forecast. The debate about 2027, when these shocks are behind us and inflation's back to 2%, is an entirely separate discussion. For this year we have the Fed on hold on the thought that the inflation problem is not one of macroeconomic overheating — there's not a strong case for cutting in response — and we think the data improves enough that they look through it.

Market expectations have swung — right now they expect the next move to be a hike, later this year. Does that look mispriced? — A little less black-and-white than that. I'd view market pricing of a "hike and a half" as maybe a 50-50 chance of two or three hikes. Our own view is that the probability of hikes is less than 50%, more like 25%. But it's not quite as black-and-white as hike versus no-hike.

One moment on the balance sheet — Chairman Warsh has different views than his predecessor. How does that factor in? — Warsh has commissioned a task force. Two issues: the size of the balance sheet (bound up with the ample-reserves framework) and the composition of assets. On size — no interest within the Fed in reverting to the old system, so not much room to shrink, maybe a little through regulatory/supervisory changes. On composition — more of an open question; people could be talked into either approach. One argument: it's not the Fed's job to choose the composition of government debt (that's Treasury's role) — buy in proportion to what Treasury issues. The other: hold mostly bills to match asset/liability duration and reduce profit volatility, to avoid becoming politicized if Congress noticed profits one year and losses the next. Both coherent — but I'd say it doesn't matter much, because whatever the Fed chooses, Treasury would adapt, so it wouldn't affect the debt available to the public or, ultimately, interest rates.

The other change in focus — communication. What are you hearing? — A few issues. One is Warsh's long-standing opposition to forward guidance. There's a distinction between commitment-based forward guidance — "this is what we're definitely going to do" — which nobody at the Fed thinks they should use outside the zero lower bound, versus a softer form: "this is our best forecast for the economy, with implications for policy." As soon as you talk about your view on the economy — whether you're more worried about inflation or employment — you hint at a lean; and as soon as you talk about your best guess of the neutral rate, you convey where you're trying to get to. So some soft, non-committal forward guidance is inevitable if you talk about the economy. What's up for grabs is to what degree the FOMC as a committee should give forward guidance. They've already taken the soft guidance out of the statement. One thing they could do that I don't think would bother people much is adopt former vice chairman Don Kohn's proposal to stop publishing the medians in the SEP — the median projections. Most investors and your Bloomberg terminal would go on publishing the median anyway, but at least it wouldn't seem like the committee is blessing a particular forecast and policy path. I'd view that as a relatively minor change. Dispensing with the SEP entirely would be a big step away from transparency, and I'd be surprised if there was comfort with that. — And that's the market concern, right? Without guidance, it's more vulnerable to surprises and volatility. — I think that's right. If you leave it to the market to form its own story, it might infer too much from policy moves, run ahead of what the Fed intends. As long as the Fed's not moving, that's probably not a huge deal; but at a meeting where they make a change — especially a rate hike — without giving a sense of how they're thinking, markets could move more than they'd want. Perhaps we saw a bit of that at Warsh's first [June] meeting.

Back to the labor-market resilience — is it a sign growth is holding up better? — I think both are true. I don't know exactly what to make of job growth abruptly picking up at a seemingly unlikely time. Historically, when oil prices go up, employment tends to decline in discretionary sectors — if I'm spending more on gasoline, I spend less at a restaurant, so restaurants hire fewer waiters. Instead we've seen the opposite — a pretty impressive pickup in job growth, to a point now somewhat above the break-even rate. In an environment of diminished immigration, we only need 50 to 60,000 jobs a month, and we've been running above that.

So does growth hold up? — Our growth forecast is a hair below potential, but pretty close. Coming into the year we were a bit above our estimate of potential at 2.5%. We haven't seen much impact of higher oil prices on consumer spending yet; you're looking at mediocre real income growth, an already-low saving rate, and tax refund payments that have run their course. So in the back half we expect somewhat softer consumption, coupled with weakness in housing and government spending, but outsized strength in business investment — all of which adds up to GDP growth of around 2%. Not bad, but somewhat worse than we'd have had without the oil/gasoline increase. — And the stock market's been up — the wealth effect? — The increase in the stock market boosts consumer spending through the wealth effect — worth about 3 to 4 tenths of a percentage point over the last year, and about the same over the coming year. Not the main driver, but a positive.

Put it all together — what are the risks you're most focused on? — The biggest risk, at least for what we focus on, is probably still the war — its potential impact on inflation, the impact of somewhat higher inflation on monetary policy, and the risk all of that would pose to financial markets. — Thanks so much, David. — Thank you, Allison.
