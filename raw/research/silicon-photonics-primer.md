# Silicon Photonics and Co-Packaged Optics Technology Primer

## What silicon photonics actually is

Silicon photonics is best understood not as ŌĆ£a silicon laser technology,ŌĆØ but as a **silicon-based photonic integrated circuit platform** that uses the manufacturing logic of CMOS to pattern optical functions into silicon wafers. In commercial datacenter practice, that usually means a silicon-on-insulator platform in which silicon waveguides route light, interferometers and resonators modulate it, multiplexers and splitters distribute it, and germanium or silicon-germanium devices detect it, while optical gain is still imported from IIIŌĆōV materials rather than generated efficiently in elemental silicon itself. The reason the platform mattered commercially is straightforward: silicon fabrication already had the scale, lithographic fidelity, process control, and wafer economics needed to make dense passive and electro-optic plumbing repeatably. That is the core advantage, not some uniquely superior optical property of silicon as a light source. (Shekhar et al., roadmap review; Wan et al., Review of silicon photonics with CMOS technologies). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn24search21Ņłéturn32search12Ņłü

The basic physics starts with **light confinement**. Silicon has a much higher refractive index than silicon dioxide, so an SOI stack forms a very high-index-contrast waveguide system. That lets light be confined in submicron cross-sections, routed through tight bends, split, filtered, and wavelength-multiplexed on a very small footprint. The same property that makes silicon photonics dense also makes it unforgiving: sidewall roughness scatters light, polarization handling becomes more delicate, and coupling to standard single-mode fiber becomes intrinsically hard because a conventional fiber mode is large while an SOI waveguide mode is tiny. Public packaging guidance for CPO still describes a typical SOI waveguide cross-section on the order of hundreds of nanometers, and packaging specialists explicitly highlight the mode-size mismatch between an ~8ŌĆō10 ╬╝m fiber mode and a silicon waveguide roughly ~500 nm ├Ś 220 nm as a central coupling problem. That coupling mismatch is not a packaging footnote; it is the direct consequence of the very confinement that gives silicon photonics its density. (High-index-contrast silicon waveguide literature; Semiconductor Engineering, June 2025). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn2search18Ņłéturn33view2Ņłü

The most common way silicon photonics **modulates** light is also a consequence of siliconŌĆÖs limitations. Silicon lacks a strong native Pockels effect, so mainstream modulators historically leaned on the **carrier plasma dispersion effect**: by injecting or depleting free carriers near a waveguide, the refractive index and absorption of silicon shift enough to encode data. That is why many silicon-photonic modulators are implemented as pŌĆōn or pŌĆōiŌĆōn junctions wrapped around MachŌĆōZehnder interferometers or ring resonators. The advantage is CMOS compatibility. The disadvantage is that one is not using a giant electro-optic coefficient the way one would in lithium niobate; instead one is leaning on carrier effects, with all the associated compromises in drive voltage, thermal sensitivity, linearity, and footprint. The entire modern silicon-photonics modulator literature can be read as a long optimization around those tradeoffs. (Reed et al., *Silicon Optical Modulators* review; Van Thourhout et al., modulator review). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn2search5Ņłéturn2search9Ņłéturn2search17Ņłü

The **detector story** is different and much more favorable. At the 1.3ŌĆō1.6 ╬╝m communication bands used in datacenter and telecom links, germanium absorbs light well where silicon does not. That made **Ge-on-Si photodetectors** one of the decisive enabling pieces of the platform: grow or integrate germanium on silicon, align it to a silicon waveguide, and the platform now owns both the routing and the receive side. The Ge-on-Si literature long ago established that such detectors can be monolithically integrated with silicon electronics, and more recent work continues to push bandwidth and responsivity deep into regimes that are entirely adequate for very high-speed interconnects. The practical result is that ŌĆ£silicon photonicsŌĆØ in datacenter hardware almost always means **silicon for passive routing and modulation plus germanium for detection**. (Michel et al., *High-performance Ge-on-Si photodetectors*; Lischke et al., Nature Photonics 2021). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn23search17Ņłéturn23search16Ņłü

The hardest conceptual point for non-specialists is the **external laser problem**. Silicon is an **indirect-bandgap semiconductor**, so electron-hole recombination in pure silicon does not efficiently emit photons. In plain language: silicon is very good at guiding light and acceptable at modulating it, but it is not naturally a good gain medium for practical integrated lasers. That is why the commercial silicon-photonics stack historically separated the problem into two materials systems: silicon for the PIC, IIIŌĆōV materials for gain. There are now serious heterogeneous and monolithic research efforts to put IIIŌĆōV gain on silicon, including quantum-dot and InP-based approaches, but those are precisely attempts to work around siliconŌĆÖs native inability to lase efficiently. For datacenter silicon photonics deployed today, the cleanest mental model is still: **silicon PIC plus external or heterogeneously integrated IIIŌĆōV laser**. (Nature Photonics 2010 review; lasers-on-silicon reviews; Wan et al. review). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn2search0Ņłéturn2search4Ņłéturn2search8Ņłéturn32search12Ņłü

This also explains why the silicon-photonics platform exists **alongside**, not instead of, alternative photonic integration approaches. Monolithic **InP PICs** can integrate gain, modulation, and detection on the same material system, which is a real advantage when an architecture wants native lasers, semiconductor optical amplifiers, electro-absorption modulators, and photodiodes in one active platform. The tradeoff is that silicon still generally wins on wafer scale, lithographic maturity, and passive routing density economics. **Thin-film lithium niobate** is different again: it is structurally attractive where one wants very strong electro-optic response, high linearity, and excellent modulator performance, but it does not solve the whole datacenter transceiver bill of materials by itself because lasers and detectors still come from elsewhere. **Polymer and silicon-organic-hybrid photonics** can produce very efficient modulation with low drive voltage and compact footprints, but the literature still treats aging and temperature stability as central commercialization constraints rather than solved footnotes. So silicon photonics is not ŌĆ£the best optics platformŌĆØ in the abstract. It is the platform whose **manufacturing economics and passive-function density** best align with short-reach interconnect at very high volume, provided the architecture accepts external gain. (Yan et al., monolithic InP/SOI platform; Zhu et al., TFLN review; Taghavi et al., polymer modulator review). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn1search7Ņłéturn1search13Ņłéturn1search5Ņłéturn1search14Ņłéturn1search2Ņłü

## The co-packaged optics architecture and what makes it different

Co-packaged optics means that the **optical engine is moved from a removable front-panel transceiver into the package neighborhood of the switch ASIC or compute die**. That sounds like a modest packaging shift, but architecturally it is a change in where the electrical-to-optical boundary sits. In a pluggable design, high-speed electrical signals leave the ASIC package, cross package escape routing, board traces, connectors, and module interfaces, and only then meet the optical engine in a QSFP-DD or OSFP class module. In a CPO design, the optical engine sits adjacent to the ASIC on the same first-level package, interposer, or package substrate, so the electrical path is collapsed from board-scale to package-scale. The purpose is not to make fiber magically lower loss than before. Fiber was already low loss. The purpose is to eliminate the **short-reach electrical bottleneck** between the ASIC and the optics. (Tan et al., CPO review; Semiconductor Engineering, June 2025). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn31search2Ņłéturn31search7Ņłéturn33view2Ņłü

A simple comparison is more useful than prose alone:

```text
Conventional pluggable architecture
-----------------------------------
[ASIC package] --electrical PCB traces--> [QSFP/OSFP module]
                                          | DSP/retimer
                                          | laser + modulator + Rx
                                          +--> fiber out front panel

Co-packaged optics
------------------
            +--> [optical engine] --> fiber attach array
[ASIC package/interposer]
            +--> [optical engine] --> fiber attach array
            +--> [optical engine] --> fiber attach array

External laser version of CPO
-----------------------------
[Faceplate ELSFP / mid-board laser] --> fiber --> [package optical engines]
```

What changes in the CPO case is the **escape path**. Public packaging commentary from multiple vendors now describes electrical distances collapsing down to package-scale implementations, in some examples to around 100 ╬╝m from the compute/switch node to the optical I/O chiplet, with corresponding electrical loss dropping from ŌĆ£more than 20 dBŌĆØ in traditional designs to around ŌĆ£1ŌĆō2 dBŌĆØ in package-level implementations. Those are not just pretty metrics: they determine how much equalization, retiming, SerDes power, and thermal headroom the system must burn just to get a clean signal into the optics. (Semiconductor Engineering, June 2025; OIF COI white paper). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn33view2Ņłéturn29view0Ņłü

That is why CPO is structurally different from pluggable modules such as OSFP and QSFP-DD. Those form factors are front-panel, serviceable, and standardized around host-module management models such as CMIS. They keep lasers, DSPs, and optics in a removable can with their own thermal envelope. They are very good at serviceability and interoperability, and the standards are still evolving to support higher powers; for example, the OSFP MSA revision history explicitly lifted module current limits to support roughly 30 W class modules. CPO, by contrast, moves the optical engine inside the package architecture and often removes or relocates at least one of the historically separate subsystems, especially the laser. Put differently: pluggables optimize the module boundary, while CPO redesigns the **system boundary**. (OSFP MSA Rev. 5.1; OIF CMIS and smart-module documentation). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35search4Ņłéturn35search9Ņłéturn35search2Ņłü

The engineering case for CPO is therefore a package-level equation involving **power efficiency, bandwidth density, and cabling density**. Industry packaging experts now talk about package-level optical I/O achieving aerial bandwidth densities beyond 17 Tbps/mm┬▓ and shoreline densities beyond 4 Tbps/mm in research prototypes, while production-oriented commentary cites CPO ambitions around ~1 Tbps/mm front-edge bandwidth density and material reductions in pJ/bit relative to pluggables. Those numbers should be handled carefully because some are research results and others are vendor system estimates, but the direction is clear: the closer the optics move to the die, the less electrical overhead the system pays for every transmitted bit. (Columbia chip-I/O paper; Semiconductor Engineering, June 2025; Broadcom OFC 2026 announcement). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn24search17Ņłéturn33view2Ņłéturn34view3Ņłü

What **does not** disappear in CPO is equally important. Lasers remain the least convenient component to bury next to a hot ASIC; ring-based silicon photonics remains thermally sensitive; manufacturing yield now depends on electronics, photonics, fiber attach, and package assembly all working at once; and serviceability becomes much worse if the optical engine is non-removable. That is why so much of the practical CPO literature immediately reintroduces the **external laser** as a way of preserving laser serviceability and keeping the hottest, shortest-lifetime optical source away from the hottest digital package. CPO is therefore not ŌĆ£pluggables but closer.ŌĆØ It is a different system partition that fixes one bottleneck by making thermal, manufacturing, and reliability problems newly central. (Tan et al., CPO review; OIF Co-Packaging Framework; Lumentum ELSFP description). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn24search13Ņłéturn39view0Ņłéturn25view2Ņłü

## External light sources and UHP lasers

Because commercial silicon photonics still does not rely on pure-silicon lasers, the practical system question becomes: **where does the light come from?** In co-packaged systems, the increasingly standard answer is an **external light source**. The Optical Internetworking ForumŌĆÖs framework explicitly distinguishes ŌĆ£laser sources for CPOŌĆØ that are external to the optical engine from those internal to it, and names several placements for the external case: a mid-board laser source, a front-panel pluggable laser source, or a shelf laser source. OIFŌĆÖs own examples then make the central systems point: a single form factor may contain many lasers; each may feed one or multiple modulators; and power splitting can happen in the ELS, inside the optical engine, or between the two. This is the physical meaning of ELS architecture. (OIF Co-Packaging Framework, Figure 9; OIF ELSFP 2.0). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn39view0Ņłéturn35search6Ņłü

The faceplate-pluggable implementation has now been formalized as **ELSFP**, and vendor product pages map directly onto that standard. Lumentum describes its ELSFP as a centralized, serviceable CW source for CPO systems that removes the CW laser from the switch or ASIC package so that multiple silicon-photonic optical engines can share one high-power source. That description is consistent with OIFŌĆÖs architecture: the laser is physically decoupled from the hot package but still logically part of the co-packaged optical system. The crucial point is that ŌĆ£externalŌĆØ does **not** mean remote in the telecom sense; it means external to the optical engine or package, often still very much inside the box. (OIF ELSFP 2.0; Lumentum ELSFP overview). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35search6Ņłéturn25view2Ņłü

Why does the industry suddenly care about **ultra-high-power** CW lasers at 1311 nm? Because co-packaged systems spend laser power on many things before the receiver ever gets a clean eye: fiber attach loss, engine coupling loss, splitter loss, WDM mux/demux loss, routing loss, resonant-device margin, and receiver sensitivity budget. OIFŌĆÖs co-packaging framework explicitly notes that external-light architectures may involve one laser supplying light to multiple modulators and that power splitting can occur at several points in the chain. Vendor disclosures say the same thing more bluntly. LumentumŌĆÖs 2022 CLEO paper states that for CPO-based 51T switches, **each laser would need roughly 22ŌĆō25 dBm** of optical power, while AOIŌĆÖs 2025 release explicitly says a 400 mW-class laser helps close 800G/1.6T power budgets by overcoming **coupling, splitting, and routing losses** without pushing too much heat near the switch ASIC. That is the engineering logic behind ŌĆ£UHPŌĆØ: in CPO, optical source power becomes a shared system budget, not just a transmitter component spec. (Lumentum CLEO 2022; OIF Co-Packaging Framework; AOI UHP laser release). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn39view2Ņłéturn39view0Ņłéturn27view1Ņłü

The most useful way to interpret the celebrated **400 mW** threshold is in decibel budget, not in marketing adjectives. Roughly speaking, 100 mW is 20 dBm, 200 mW is 23 dBm, and 400 mW is about 26 dBm. So moving from 200 mW to 400 mW buys about **3 dB** of extra optical budget; moving from 100 mW to 400 mW buys about **6 dB**. In ideal optics, 3 dB is the penalty of an extra 1:2 split, and 6 dB is the penalty of an ideal 1:4 split. Real systems are worse because splitters, couplers, connectors, muxes, and waveguides all add excess loss, but the core intuition survives: a 400 mW-class laser can support either **more sharing** or **more margin** than a 200 mW or 100 mW source. That is why LumentumŌĆÖs earlier statement that 22ŌĆō25 dBm is needed for a 51T class switch lines up so directly with todayŌĆÖs 350ŌĆō400+ mW product announcements. A 400 mW laser is not magic; it is a practical way of reaching roughly 26 dBm while still leaving room for packaging and power-conversion efficiency targets. (Lumentum CLEO 2022; Lumentum UHP product page; Coherent 400 mW release; AOI UHP release). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn39view2Ņłéturn25view1Ņłéturn25view3Ņłéturn27view1Ņłü

The other reason ŌĆ£high powerŌĆØ is not sufficient by itself is that **source quality matters**. CoherentŌĆÖs 2025 sampling announcement paired >400 mW at 55┬░C with linewidth below 200 kHz and relative intensity noise below ŌĆō145 dB/Hz; LumentumŌĆÖs current product page pairs up to 350 mW at 50┬░C with linewidth below 500 kHz, low RIN, and power-conversion efficiency above 20%; LumentumŌĆÖs earlier CLEO paper explicitly set a preliminary **PCE target around 10%** as necessary for system-level power reduction. ## InP wafer manufacturing and what six-inch monopoly means

Indium phosphide matters because it sits at the center of the **active-device side** of datacenter optics. Unlike silicon, InP and its lattice-matched alloy systems are suitable for efficient radiative devices in the 1.2ŌĆō1.7 ╬╝m optical-communications window. SumitomoŌĆÖs technical review literature is explicit that the material systems formed on InP substrates for optical-fiber communication include GaInAsP and AlGaInAs quantum well structures in the relevant wavelengths. In practical terms, that is why InP underlies distributed-feedback lasers, EMLs, semiconductor optical amplifiers, and many high-speed photodiodes. When CPO discussions talk about ŌĆ£InP wafers,ŌĆØ they are not talking about passive photonic plumbing; they are talking about the substrate base for **the devices that create and amplify light**. (Sumitomo technical review on quantum devices; Coherent 6-inch InP platform announcement). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn36search9Ņłéturn37view0Ņłü

The substrate itself is not trivial to make. SumitomoŌĆÖs substrate materials overview describes InP and GaAs substrates as products of **melt growth**, and its earlier review notes transitions across methods such as liquid-encapsulated Czochralski and vertical Bridgman-type processes in compound-semiconductor substrate manufacturing. A later Sumitomo technical paper specifically states that the company succeeded in developing **6-inch Fe-doped InP substrates** at competitive cost using **VB** technique. That matters because the manufacturing problem is not merely to grow a crystal; it is to grow a sufficiently large, uniform, low-defect single crystal, slice it into wafers, and then lap and polish it to flatness, thickness, and defect specs that downstream epitaxy and device fabs can live with. InP is brittle, thermally tricky, and less forgiving than silicon. Scaling diameter raises stress, bow, warp, dislocation management, and equipment-automation requirements simultaneously. (Sumitomo 2010 substrate review; Sumitomo 2016 6-inch InP paper). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn36search5Ņłéturn36search1Ņłü

This is why **wafer diameter** matters so much. The easy geometry is often left unstated. A 6-inch wafer has roughly **4├Ś** the area of a 3-inch wafer and **2.25├Ś** the area of a 4-inch wafer. So when Coherent says 6-inch InP enables ŌĆ£4├Ś more devices per wafer,ŌĆØ that claim is geometrically coherent *if the baseline being displaced is a 3-inch-era production configuration*, not if the comparison is against a mature 4-inch process. ## Scale-out and scale-up CPO phasing

The distinction between **scale-out** and **scale-up** is not merely marketing language. The ŅłĆentityŅłé["organization","OIF","optical internetworking forum"]Ņłü defines **scale-up** as connectivity within a single system, typically interconnecting compute elements and switches, and **scale-out** as connectivity between a large number of single systems. In its AI-compute white paper, OIF further distinguishes ŌĆ£link type 4ŌĆØ scale-out linksŌĆötypically Ethernet or Ethernet variants interconnecting local pods and storageŌĆöfrom ŌĆ£link type 3ŌĆØ compute-optics-interface links that support I/O coherency and may carry shared-memory load/store traffic. That is a profound difference. Scale-out is network traffic. Scale-up is often fabric traffic whose latency and error behaviors directly affect the computation itself. (OIF COI white paper). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28view1Ņłéturn29view0Ņłü

That difference shows up immediately in requirements. OIF notes that COI/scale-up links in some common architectures require about **9├Ś more bandwidth capacity** than scale-out links and are tail-latency sensitive because corrupted requests can trigger retransmissions that stall new transfers from the same buffer. The same white paper then argues that standard Ethernet optics are usually not optimal for scale-up because scale-up links want much shorter reach, lower latency, and solutions tuned to shorter distances rather than 500-meter Ethernet assumptions. This is why scale-up optical proposals talk about coherent-ish memory semantics, flow control, very low error rates, and per-fiber bandwidth tradeoffs in ways that ordinary switch CPO discussions often do not. (OIF COI white paper). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn29view0Ņłéturn28view0Ņłü

From there, two different deployment narratives emerge. The **scale-out-first** narrative is the one most naturally associated with switch CPO. It says: datacenter networks already use optics at the rack and cluster level, the board-level electrical escape from the switch is the bottleneck, and moving the optical engines into the switch package solves a power and front-panel density problem without changing the fundamental network model. That is why BroadcomŌĆÖs public CPO roadmap centers on 102.4T Ethernet-class switch platforms such as Tomahawk 6ŌĆōDavisson CPO and describes them as part of a portfolio for AI scale-out, scale-up, and scale-across connectivity. In this framing, switch CPO is the nearest-term industrialization step because it lives adjacent to an already optical networking domain. (Broadcom OFC 2026 release). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view3Ņłü

The **scale-up-later-but-bigger** narrative starts from a very different bottleneck. MarvellŌĆÖs public AI-interconnect framing argues that scale-up domains begin as copper inside trays and racks because copper is cheap, low power, and low latency at short reach, but as systems push from tray-scale toward rack-scale and row-scale, copperŌĆÖs reach limit becomes the problem. The same article describes a path from LPO to NPO/OBO to CPO for scale-up and argues that larger scale-up domains can network hundreds or thousands of XPUs for a single compute task. OIFŌĆÖs own example of naively replacing todayŌĆÖs local copper fabric with standard retimed optical modules in a 72-compute-element rack leads to a ridiculous resultŌĆö1296 retimed 800G modules and roughly 16 kW of additional powerŌĆöwhich is precisely why scale-up optics cannot simply import existing pluggables unchanged. In that sense, scale-up may deploy **later** than switch CPO because its requirements are harder, but it may become **larger** in bandwidth demand because the local compute fabric is so much hungrier. (Marvell AI interconnect blog; OIF COI white paper). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn27view3Ņłéturn39view1Ņłü

The phrase **ŌĆ£scale-up is orders of magnitude largerŌĆØ** therefore needs careful unpacking. There is no single universal multiplier that applies across all AI cluster topologies. What can be said rigorously is that some OIF-modeled scale-up architectures require on the order of **9├Ś** the bandwidth capacity of scale-out, that scale-up networks are much more latency-sensitive, and that optical scale-up can drive even higher fiber density than scale-out because the locality of the traffic does not reduce the bandwidth requirementŌĆöit only shortens the reach. In other words, the ŌĆ£largerŌĆØ claim is most defensible in **aggregate fabric bandwidth and port count**, not necessarily in the number of lasers per endpoint in every topology. If a design keeps copper inside trays and only uses optics for row-scale expansion, the optical content per accelerator may look very different than in a fully optical package-to-package system. If it goes all the way to package-level optical I/O, then component demand rises dramatically because every compute endpoint becomes an optical endpoint. (OIF COI white paper; Marvell integrated optics framing). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28view1Ņłéturn28view0Ņłéturn27view3Ņłü

The contested nature of the phasing question should therefore be preserved. One vendor framing emphasizes **switch CPO for scale-out** because that is closest to todayŌĆÖs datacenter optics. Another emphasizes **compute optics for scale-up** because that is where the local fabric bandwidth problem becomes impossible for copper. Both are technically coherent framings. The deciding variables are not slogans but the pace at which copper fails at 200G/lane and beyond, the maturity of low-latency optical link standards for coherent/credit-based fabrics, and whether integrated optics can meet power and reliability targets without giving away the latency and serviceability advantages copper still has at very short reach. (OIF COI white paper; Broadcom and Marvell public technical framing). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn29view0Ņłéturn34view3Ņłéturn27view3Ņłü

## The competitive alternatives to CPO

CPO does not compete against ŌĆ£doing nothing.ŌĆØ It competes against a ladder of intermediate architectures that each remove part of the electrical problem without accepting all of CPOŌĆÖs manufacturing and service burdens. The first of those is **linear pluggable optics**. OIFŌĆÖs retiming taxonomy is useful here: fully retimed modules are the most robust but least energy-efficient; half-retimed and non-retimed architectures reduce power by delegating more equalization burden to the host SerDes and by removing retimers from the module. Marvell then maps that logic into product language by describing LPO as pluggable optics that retain the modular form factor but lower power and latency by offloading more signal processing to the endpoints. That gets some of the CPO energy benefit while preserving the field-serviceable pluggable model. The cost is tighter system co-design, less margin, fewer diagnostics, and usually less robustness. (OIF COI white paper; Marvell AI interconnect blog). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn29view0Ņłéturn27view3Ņłü

The next rung is **near-packaged optics** or **on-board optics**. Here the optical engine is moved closer to the ASIC than in a pluggable but not all the way into full co-packaging. MarvellŌĆÖs public framing describes electrical path lengths dropping from roughly 300 mm in LPO toward 100 mm or less in NPO/OBO, with CPO then taking that down further to package-level dimensions. ŅłĆentityŅłé["company","Broadcom","semiconductor company"]Ņłü has publicly disclosed a VCSEL-based NPO approach as well, pitching it on efficiency, reliability, and escape-bandwidth density. NPO is attractive because it captures some of the signal-integrity and power gains of shorter electrical channels while keeping the module boundary looser than full CPO. Its weakness is equally obvious: it removes some channel loss, but not as much as CPO, and it still leaves the system managing more off-package electrical structure than the ŌĆ£optics at the package edgeŌĆØ vision does. (Marvell AI interconnect blog; Broadcom OFC 2026 release). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn27view3Ņłéturn34view3Ņłü

Then there is **co-packaged copper**, which looks less glamorous but is technically important. OIF explicitly defines CPC as co-packaging the ASIC with the copper connector so the high-speed electrical channel does not have to traverse the host PCB. CPC is a reminder that the real near-term bottleneck is often the electrical host channel, not optics per se. CPC can therefore extend copper by cleaning up the shortest, ugliest electrical losses without introducing photonic packaging into the critical path. But copper is still copper. The reach objective for OIFŌĆÖs CEI-224G long-reach family is only about 1 meter for copper cable. That is enough for some local scale-up, not enough for everything the AI fabric roadmap wants. So CPC can be a powerful bridge architecture, but it does not fundamentally change the reach ceiling that eventually pushes systems toward optics. (OIF COI white paper). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn29view0Ņłü

Why might CPO **fail to displace pluggables**? The first reason is operational, not theoretical: pluggables are serviceable, standardized, and deeply embedded in host-management and qualification workflows. OIFŌĆÖs CMIS ecosystem exists precisely because pluggables are a mature multi-vendor module world. Second, pluggables continue to evolve; OSFP power capability is increasing, and linear/half-retimed variants can claw back some of the power that once made DSP-heavy modules look intolerable. Third, CPOŌĆÖs hardest problemsŌĆöfiber attach, optical test, rework, integration yield, redundancy design, and package serviceabilityŌĆöare not the kind of problems that disappear because a conference demo worked. If LPO and NPO solutions prove ŌĆ£good enoughŌĆØ at 200G/lane and even some 400G/lane regimes, they can delay the need for CPO substantially. (OSFP MSA Rev. 5.1; OIF CMIS; OIF COI white paper). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35search4Ņłéturn35search9Ņłéturn29view0Ņłü

Why might CPO **succeed faster than expected**? Because at some point the host electrical channel simply becomes too expensive in power and too unpredictable in margin. Package-level electrical routing is more deterministic than board-level routing, and CPO eliminates a huge amount of connector, trace, and retiming overhead. External laser standards such as ELSFP reduce one of the biggest objectionsŌĆöburying the least reliable optical source inside the package. Vendors are also now showing credible system building blocks: Broadcom is publicly tying CPO to 102.4T switch platforms, and the leading laser vendors are no longer talking about generic CW sources but about dedicated CPO-class UHP lasers and pluggable external laser modules. That is much closer to a supply chain than a science fair. (Broadcom OFC 2026 release; OIF ELSFP 2.0; Lumentum and Coherent CPO laser disclosures). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view3Ņłéturn35search6Ņłéturn25view2Ņłéturn25view3Ņłü

The right **technical milestones** are therefore specific. CPO is validated, not by a slogan, but by a set of converging proofs: repeatable low-loss fiber attach at package scale; reliable external-laser operation above 22ŌĆō26 dBm source power with acceptable PCE; multi-vendor interoperability around ELSFP, management, and optical compute interconnect standards; package-level optical test and burn-in good enough for expensive compute packages; and shipping systems that demonstrate durable pJ/bit advantage over pluggables without unacceptable rework or field-failure penalties. CPO is falsified, or at least delayed, if LPO/NPO keep hitting the required lane rates with acceptable power and latency, if fiber-attach yield remains stubbornly poor, or if serviceability costs erase the power advantage at system scale. (OIF ELSFP and COI documents; laser reliability literature; packaging expert commentary). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35search6Ņłéturn29view0Ņłéturn32search19Ņłéturn33view2Ņłü

## The packaging integration problem

CPO is, at bottom, a **heterogeneous integration problem**. The photonic engine needs modulators, photodetectors, waveguides, couplers, driver and TIA electronics, clocking and control, and a physically stable fiber-attach structure, all while sharing a package neighborhood with a hot high-power ASIC and whatever interposer, substrate, or fan-out technology the compute platform uses. That is why the industrial actors are not just optics vendors. Public roadmaps involve advanced-packaging foundries and OSATs as much as photonics suppliers. ŅłĆentityŅłé["company","TSMC","semiconductor foundry"]Ņłü has publicly described a full-stack silicon-photonics and co-packaged-optics offering built around its photonic engine and optical-attach technologies; ŅłĆentityŅłé["company","Marvell Technology","semiconductor company"]Ņłü has demonstrated a compact 3D silicon-photonics light engine for package-level integrated optics; and Broadcom has tied Davisson CPO to its 102.4T switch roadmap. The common theme is that no one is treating the optical engine as a free-standing chip anymore. It is a **package citizen**. (TSMC silicon photonics/CPO announcements; Marvell 3D light engine disclosure; Broadcom OFC 2026 release). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn8search16Ņłéturn27view3Ņłéturn34view3Ņłü

That produces a packaging stack unlike ordinary logic packaging. Fiber attaches or fiber-array units must couple reliably into very small silicon modes; edge couplers or grating couplers must be chosen; external laser feeds may need polarization-maintaining fiber; turning mirrors, silicon lenses, V-grooves, and alignment features appear in what used to be an all-electrical package discussion; and test strategies must cover both IC behaviors and optical-path behaviors. TSMCŌĆÖs and other foundriesŌĆÖ public language around silicon lenses, optical fiber attachment units, and photonic engines is telling: the package now has a real optical front end, not just a faster SerDes escape. (TSMC COUPE-related public disclosures; OIF Co-Packaging Framework). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn8search16Ņłéturn39view0Ņłü

The most notorious failure mode is **fiber-to-PIC alignment**. SemiengŌĆÖs 2025 packaging report, drawing on comments from multiple industry practitioners, describes package-level optical alignment tolerances on the order of a few tenths of a micron, with <50 nm transverse tolerance sometimes cited for fiber arrays. That sensitivity is easy to explain physically: one is aligning a large fiber mode to a tiny high-contrast silicon waveguide mode. Detachable fiber interfaces may add roughly 1 dB of loss per fiber interface, while permanent V-groove-like approaches reduce loss but hurt repairability. The long-term reliability problem is not just finding the alignment once; it is **holding it** against epoxy creep, thermal cycling, and mechanical stress for years. In photonics packaging, yield and reliability are often the same problem seen at different timescales. (Semiconductor Engineering, June 2025; scalable fiber-to-PIC packaging literature). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn33view2Ņłéturn32search11Ņłü

The second major failure mode is **thermal drift**. Silicon photonics devices near switch and compute dies sit in a much noisier thermal environment than front-panel optics. Industry packaging commentary now routinely cites a wavelength shift around ~0.1 nm per 1┬░C in photonic systems, which is especially uncomfortable for microring-based architectures because resonance alignment can move out from under the signal path. That is why thermal interface-material stacks, local heaters, wavelength-control loops, and externalized lasers all appear so prominently in CPO literature. The laser does not get externalized only because it fails more often. It also gets externalized because hot digital packages are a poor home for temperature-sensitive compound-semiconductor sources. (Semiconductor Engineering, June 2025; Advanced Photonics Nexus thermal-management review). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn33view2Ņłéturn32search0Ņłü

The third problem is **test and qualification**. A compute package with co-packaged optics is too valuable to treat like a disposable module, which means the supply chain needs known-good-die strategies for both electronics and photonics, wafer-level and package-level optical test, and reliability screening that catches low-level random failures in lasers and photonic devices before they are attached to expensive logic packages. Recent laser-reliability work discussed for silicon-photonic systems emphasizes larger qualification sample sizes and added wafer qualification to catch excursions and low-level random failures. That sounds mundane, but it is exactly the kind of process discipline that separates ŌĆ£optical demoŌĆØ from ŌĆ£platform shipped at scale.ŌĆØ (Semiconductor laser reliability paper; Tan et al., CPO review). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn32search19Ņłéturn24search13Ņłü

The final integration problem is organizational as much as physical. In ordinary advanced packaging, the dominant interfaces are electrical and thermomechanical. In CPO, the package must simultaneously meet electrical signaling, optical coupling, thermal control, materials-compatibility, and serviceability constraints. That is why commentary from foundries and packaging houses increasingly focuses on **built-in monitoring, redundancy, and self-correction** rather than treating repairability as a given. A pluggable can be yanked and replaced. A co-packaged optical engine next to a premium switch or compute die usually cannot. The package therefore has to be designed to fail less, not merely to fail gracefully. (Semiconductor Engineering, June 2025; OIF co-packaging and COI materials). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn33view2Ņłéturn39view0Ņłéturn29view0Ņłü

## Synthesis and caveats

### What this means for the AI chokepoint thesis

The clearest implication for a laser-supply chokepoint framework is that **silicon photonics does not remove the laser bottleneck; it sharpens it**. The platform deliberately outsources optical gain because silicon remains a poor native laser material. As photonic functions move onto silicon PICs and into packages, the systemŌĆÖs dependency on a smaller number of external or heterogeneously integrated IIIŌĆōV light sources becomes more explicit, not less. The optical engine can be silicon. The light still usually comes from InP-based sources somewhere else in the system. (Nature Photonics reviews; OIF co-packaging framework; Lumentum ELSFP architecture). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn2search0Ņłéturn39view0Ņłéturn25view2Ņłü

The second implication is that the chokepoint is not just **ŌĆ£number of lasersŌĆØ**. It is the much narrower problem of **qualified UHP CW lasers with acceptable linewidth, RIN, efficiency, temperature behavior, and lifetime**. A 400 mW CPO-class laser is not simply a brighter transceiver laser. It is a shared optical budget device expected to tolerate splitting and routing losses across multiple lanes or wavelengths while staying stable enough for dense silicon-photonic engines. That points to a bottleneck in the intersection of InP device design, packaging, thermal engineering, and production qualification. (Lumentum CLEO 2022; Coherent 400 mW release; OFC 2025 >500 mW SOA-DFB work). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn39view2Ņłéturn25view3Ņłéturn24search14Ņłü

The third implication is that scale-up versus scale-out changes the **intensity** of the chokepoint. Switch CPO for scale-out can raise demand for external lasers and optical engines, but the AI-compute literature suggests that the truly explosive optical content may emerge when scale-up fabrics move beyond copper and insist on low-latency optical links within racks or rows. OIFŌĆÖs own estimate that compute-optics links can demand ~9├Ś scale-out bandwidth in common architectures is the right caution against thinking only in terms of Ethernet switch ports. If the industry really moves from package escape to optical local fabrics, the bottleneck migrates from ŌĆ£optical transceivers at the network edgeŌĆØ to ŌĆ£optical engines and shared laser plants inside the compute fabric.ŌĆØ (OIF COI white paper; Marvell AI interconnect framing). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28view1Ņłéturn29view0Ņłéturn27view3Ņłü

The fourth implication is that a datacenter laser chokepoint is inseparable from a **packaging and test chokepoint**. A system is not capacity-ready just because wafers and lasers exist. The package must align fiber arrays, qualify photonic engines next to hot ASICs, manage ring drift, and screen latent failures before attaching photonics to very expensive compute packages. If package-scale yields remain difficult, the effective supply chokepoint may sit one step downstream of the laser die itself. In other words, the technically correct bottleneck map is: **InP substrate and epi capability ŌåÆ high-power CW laser device capability ŌåÆ laser/ELS packaging and qualification ŌåÆ CPO package integration and optical test**. (TSMC CPO stack disclosures; Semiconductor Engineering, June 2025; laser reliability screening literature). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn8search16Ņłéturn33view2Ņłéturn32search19Ņłü

### Vendor claims vs established engineering

A recurring vendor claim is that **ŌĆ£400 mW is a breakthrough moat.ŌĆØ** Established engineering says something narrower. A 400 mW source is important because it reaches roughly 26 dBm and therefore buys several decibels of shared optical budget relative to 100ŌĆō200 mW sources, helping compensate splitter, coupling, and routing losses in ELS/CPO architectures. What it does **not** mean is that 400 mW is a magical threshold at which CPO suddenly becomes easy. The relevant questions remain PCE, linewidth, RIN, coupling efficiency, thermal rollover, and qualification. Vendor claims that treat power alone as the differentiator understate the system problem. (Lumentum CLEO 2022; Coherent 400 mW release; AOI 400 mW release). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn39view2Ņłéturn25view3Ņłéturn27view1Ņłü

Another recurring claim is some form of **ŌĆ£six-inch monopoly.ŌĆØ** Established engineering supports a weaker but still meaningful statement. Coherent appears to have a serious position in *qualified 6-inch InP device fabs* for active photonic products, and that can be a real structural manufacturing advantage if yield, automation, and product transfers hold. But the broader ecosystem includes other firms with 6-inch substrate or epitaxy claims, and long-standing suppliers such as Sumitomo publicly offer 6-inch InP substrates. So the strongest supportable wording is not ŌĆ£monopoly,ŌĆØ but rather **ŌĆ£an unusually advanced multi-site 6-inch InP device-fabrication position.ŌĆØ** (Coherent 2024/2026 disclosures; Sumitomo materials disclosures; AXT and IQE disclosures). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn37view0Ņłéturn27view2Ņłéturn36search11Ņłéturn38search1Ņłéturn38search6Ņłü

Vendors also increasingly frame **scale-up optical I/O** as the inevitable next step and sometimes imply that it will instantly dwarf scale-out. Established engineering again says: directionally plausible, quantitatively topology-dependent. OIF validates that scale-up fabrics can require much more bandwidth than scale-out and that pluggables are not a naive drop-in replacement for todayŌĆÖs local copper fabrics. But whether that turns into ŌĆ£orders of magnitudeŌĆØ more laser demand depends on architecture: how much copper remains inside trays, how many wavelengths ride each fiber, what radix the local switches use, and whether the package adopts CPO, NPO, or something in between. (OIF COI white paper; Marvell and Broadcom public framing). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28view1Ņłéturn29view0Ņłéturn27view3Ņłéturn34view3Ņłü

Finally, some vendor and industry narratives imply that once optics move into the package, energy falls cleanly toward **sub-pJ/bit** and the rest is execution. The better reading is that such numbers are partly **research-demonstration targets** and partly **architecture-level aspirations**. They indicate the direction of travel and the value of shortening electrical channels, but they do not by themselves prove manufacturable, serviceable, field-hardened systems. In CPO, the gap between a beautiful optical engine and a production-worthy package is unusually consequential. (Columbia co-designed chip I/O work; Tan et al., CPO review; Semiconductor Engineering, June 2025). ŅłĆciteŅłéturn24search17Ņłéturn24search13Ņłéturn33view2Ņłü
