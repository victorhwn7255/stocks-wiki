# The HALEU Bottleneck: Capturing the SMR Trade Through the Fuel Chain

**A Production-Ready Investment Research Note**
*Sector: Nuclear Fuel Cycle & Advanced Reactors | Mandate: Long-Only Concentrated Fundamental | Horizon: 3–5 Years (2026–2030) with Structural Commentary Through Mid-2030s*

---

## Executive Summary and Variant View

The consensus trade in the nuclear renaissance is to own the reactor developer logos — Oklo, NuScale, X-energy, TerraPower — because they are the most easily understood proxies for "AI needs nuclear." Generalist money has crowded into these names because the narrative is clean, the headlines are constant, and the link to hyperscaler offtake is direct. We think the consensus is materially mis-pricing where the value actually accrues over the 2026–2030 deployment window.

**Our variant view, in one sentence:** the binding constraint on advanced-reactor deployment is not reactor design certification or even site permitting — it is the upstream nuclear fuel chain, and within that chain it is the ability to enrich, deconvert, and fabricate High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium (HALEU) at commercial scale. Until that bottleneck clears, the upstream owners (Centrus, BWXT, and Cameco/Westinghouse) capture a disproportionate share of the economics, while the developer cohort burns cash, dilutes equity, and waits in line for fuel.

The math underscores why. Centrus Energy's American Centrifuge Plant in Piketon, Ohio is currently the only NRC-licensed Western producer of HALEU, and its installed cascade delivers approximately 900 kg per year — slightly over one metric ton of uranium (MTU). Centrus disclosed that it enriched "over 1 metric ton" of HALEU UF₆ cumulatively as of full-year 2025. The DOE has projected domestic HALEU demand reaching ~40 MTU/year by 2030 and ~50 MTU/year by 2035, with TerraPower Natrium's first core alone requiring ~15 MT, X-energy's Xe-100 fleet at ~1.5 MT per reactor across 11+ planned units, and Oklo's first deployment at ~5 MT (sourced from down-blended EBR-II inventory). Centrus' own Phase III scale-up plan and the DOE's January 2026 $900 million task order to American Centrifuge Operating contemplate a path to 12 MTU/year — still under 25% of 2035 projected need, and the only Western funded path to that scale that is currently under construction.

The names with the strongest theses, in order of conviction:

1. **Centrus Energy (LEU)** — *Highest conviction.* Sole licensed Western HALEU enricher; $2.0B cash; $900M January 2026 DOE task order; NNSA sole-source intent for national-security uranium; $2.3B contingent LEU backlog; pricing power converging with Western-only supply.
2. **BWX Technologies (BWXT)** — *Highest conviction.* Naval reactor sole-source franchise funding aggressive expansion into TRISO fabrication, HALEU deconversion, and microreactors; $7.3B backlog (+50% YoY); Project Pele full core delivery December 2025; the only diversified, profitable, defense-anchored way to play the fuel chain.
3. **Cameco (CCJ)** — *High conviction.* Uranium leader with the world's most strategic Western mining portfolio plus 49% Westinghouse, which is now embedded in the October 2025 $80B U.S. Government strategic partnership for AP1000/AP300 deployment.
4. **GE Vernova (GEV)** — *Tier 2.* BWRX-300 traction (TVA Clinch River, OPG Darlington 2030, four-unit pipeline) plus the broader Prolec/electrification story; LEU-fueled, so direct fuel-chain leverage is muted but reactor offtake is catalyzed.
5. **Constellation (CEG), Vistra (VST), Talen (TLN)** — *Tier 2.* Existing nuclear fleets monetizing scarcity through 20-year hyperscaler PPAs; not the variant view but a credible adjacent leg.
6. **Oklo (OKLO), NuScale (SMR)** — *Tier 3, with skepticism.* Pre-revenue, dilutive, and pricing in near-perfect execution. We are explicitly on the wrong side of consensus here. At ~$8.5B enterprise value for Oklo and a NuScale story that has already slipped its first-of-kind FID to early 2027, the risk-reward for fundamental long-only capital is unattractive at current valuations relative to the upstream chain.
7. **Lightbridge (LTBR), Silex (SLX.AX)** — *Speculative optionality.* Not buyable at scale for a concentrated fund.

The trade is not "short the developers" — that is the wrong framing for a long-only mandate. The trade is **substitute the upstream fuel chain (Centrus / BWXT / Cameco) for the developer book (Oklo / NuScale / X-energy proxies) wherever a fund has nuclear exposure**, on the thesis that the upstream layer captures more of the SMR economics during 2026–2028 because the developers must transit through that layer before they can deploy.

The remainder of this note maps the chokepoint chain step-by-step, lays out the investable universe, presents the framework for sizing conviction, scenario-tests the thesis, and identifies the bear cases that would invalidate it.

## PART 1 — FUEL CHAIN MAP AND CHOKEPOINT ANALYSIS

The nuclear fuel cycle is a sequential industrial process: mine → mill → convert (UF₆) → enrich → deconvert → fabricate → load. The Western chain has been hollowed out over four decades while Russia rebuilt a vertically integrated competitor (Rosatom/TENEX). Sanctions and the May 2024 Prohibiting Russian Uranium Imports Act have torn open structural gaps in conversion and enrichment that cannot be closed in less than a 5–7 year timeframe given physical lead times. We grade each layer for bottleneck severity on a 1–5 scale, where 5 is the hardest binding constraint over the investment horizon.

### 1.1 Uranium Mining — Bottleneck Severity: 3/5 (Moderate; tightening)

Global primary mine supply runs around 155–160 million pounds U₃O₈ per year (the 2024 figure was approximately 158 Mlb against reactor consumption of approximately 180 Mlb, with the gap filled by secondary supply). Cameco and Sprott both forecast that mine supply will increasingly fall short of reactor demand as advanced reactors layer on top of a global fleet that is itself growing through life extensions, restarts, and new builds. The World Nuclear Association's base case envisions installed capacity reaching 746 GWe by 2040 from approximately 395 GWe today.

**Concentration is the critical risk.** Kazatomprom alone is responsible for roughly 40% of global supply, predominantly through ISR (in-situ recovery) operations subject to sulphuric acid availability constraints and increasingly tilted toward Russian and Chinese offtake. Kazatomprom revised its 2026 production target down from ~32,800 tU₃O₈ to ~29,700 tU₃O₈ in August 2025, citing "value over volume." Cameco, the only major Western miner with a Tier 1 portfolio, delivered 21.0 Mlb (Cameco share) in 2025 — itself a downward revision from prior 23.4 Mlb 2024 production, reflecting development delays at McArthur River. The two largest single mines that Western utilities depend on, Cigar Lake and McArthur River, have known finite lives (10 and 15 years respectively at current rates).

The mid-cap miner cohort — NexGen Energy (NXE), Denison Mines (DNN), Energy Fuels (UUUU), Uranium Energy Corp (UEC), and Paladin (Langer Heinrich) — collectively represents the West's restart and greenfield optionality. NexGen's Arrow project (Saskatchewan) is the largest new high-grade asset in the pipeline but remains pre-production; Denison's Wheeler River (Phoenix) is targeting ISR production in the late 2020s; UUUU and UEC have ramped U.S. ISR but at modest scale.

Africa is a wildcard. Niger uranium output collapsed in 2024–2025 under the post-coup junta, with Orano's SOMAÏR producing zero in 2025. France's Orano fuel-cycle business has scrambled to substitute supply.

**Pricing context (year-end 2025/early 2026):** Spot uranium ended 2025 averaging US$73.54/lb; long-term price reached US$86.50/lb in December 2025, a 14-year high in real terms. Conversion term prices rose ~27% year-on-year and enrichment term prices ~6%. Producers are demanding market-reference contracts with ceilings of $130–140/lb. Long-term contracted volume in 2025 was ~116 Mlb — below replacement rate, implying a cumulative uncovered requirements problem that compounds going into the 2027–2030 reactor restart wave.

**Bottom line:** Mine supply is tightening but not the binding constraint over our 3–5 year horizon. Geopolitical concentration is the tail risk. Cameco is the primary Western operator with both the asset base and the integrated downstream position to capture the full chain. Mid-caps offer torque but require beta tolerance.

### 1.2 Conversion to UF₆ — Bottleneck Severity: 4/5 (Severe)

Conversion is the most acute Western bottleneck after HALEU, and it has been hiding in plain sight. There are only four commercial Western conversion facilities globally: Cameco's Port Hope (Canada), Orano's Tricastin (France), Solstice Advanced Materials' Metropolis Works (Illinois, the only U.S. converter, sold through ConverDyn), and the much smaller capacity at Springfields (UK, currently being rebuilt). Russia's TENEX conversion historically represented ~30% of global capacity.

Metropolis Works is sold out through 2028 and is now ramping toward 10,000 tU/year of UF₆ output for 2026 (vs. 7,000 tU prior steady-state), a ~20% increase, with expansion announcements in February 2025. Solstice (spun out of Honeywell in October 2025) and General Atomics have retained engineering firms to evaluate "Metropolis 2.0" — a new greenfield plant. Conversion term pricing rose ~27% in 2025, the largest move across the fuel cycle, validating the pricing-power thesis.

**Investable angles:**
- **Cameco Port Hope** (already in Cameco's integrated operations) — the most direct Western conversion exposure for public market investors; not separately reported but a meaningful EBITDA contributor that has expanded margins materially in 2024–2025.
- **Solstice Advanced Materials** (post-Honeywell spin) — limited public liquidity and conversion is one of several segments; not a clean play.
- Conversion is structurally undersupplied through at least 2028. Pricing power should sustain.

### 1.3 Enrichment to LEU (3–5% U-235) — Bottleneck Severity: 4/5 (Severe)

Western LEU enrichment runs at three commercial facilities: URENCO (Eunice, NM; Capenhurst, UK; Almelo, Netherlands; Gronau, Germany), Orano (Tricastin, France), and Centrus' developing LEU/HALEU operations in Piketon, Ohio. URENCO USA's Eunice plant is the only commercial enrichment capacity operating in North America; current capacity ~4,900 tSWU/year, with a 700,000 SWU (15%) expansion underway, half-installed by Q1 2026 and complete by 2027. URENCO has stated it intends to expand Eunice further by up to 2.1 million SWU based on customer contracts.

**Russia's exit changes the math.** TENEX/Rosatom historically supplied 44% of global enrichment services and roughly 24–27% of U.S. utility demand. The Prohibiting Russian Uranium Imports Act, signed May 13, 2024, prohibits imports as of August 11, 2024 with DOE waiver authority through January 1, 2028. Spot SWU prices doubled from approximately $90/SWU in early 2022 to $152–165/SWU in 2024–2025; UxC analysts have flagged $200/SWU as plausible if Russia retaliates with an export ban. At full ban implementation in 2028, the SWU demand-supply gap is on the order of 5–10 million SWU/year unless Western capacity additions accelerate.

**The October 2024 / January 2026 DOE LEU+ contract architecture is the most consequential development.** Under the $2.7 billion appropriation, DOE selected six companies in December 2024 to compete for LEU enrichment work (Centrus' American Centrifuge Operating, General Matter, Global Laser Enrichment, Louisiana Energy Services [URENCO USA], Laser Isotope Separation Technologies, Orano Federal Services). On January 5, 2026, DOE issued first production-scale task orders totaling $2.7 billion, awarding $900 million each to Centrus, General Matter, and Orano Federal Services. URENCO USA was authorized by the NRC in September 2025 to enrich up to 10% U-235 (LEU+), making Eunice the first U.S. commercial facility licensed for LEU+ production — a meaningful intermediate fuel for accident-tolerant fuels and a feedstock for downstream HALEU enrichment.

**Bottom line:** LEU enrichment is structurally tight through at least 2030. Centrus, URENCO, and Orano are the only credible scaled providers. URENCO USA's expansion is real, funded, and largely on schedule. Orano's Project IKE in Oak Ridge is funded ($900M task order) but the facility itself is not yet under construction; Orano is targeting "delivering production at the beginning of the next decade" — i.e., 2030+. Centrus' commercial-scale Piketon expansion will take 3+ years from FID. The structural deficit through 2028–2029 is largely already locked in.

### 1.4 Enrichment to HALEU (5–19.75% U-235) — *THE CRITICAL CHOKEPOINT*

**Bottleneck Severity: 5/5 (Acute and Binding)**

This is the analytical anchor for the entire thesis. HALEU is required by virtually every Western advanced reactor design under development — TerraPower Natrium, X-energy Xe-100, Oklo Aurora, Kairos KP-FHR, BWXT BANR/Pele/mTERA, Last Energy 20MWe — because the smaller, longer-cycle, fast-spectrum, or pebble-bed cores cannot achieve their economics or longevity using 5%-LEU.

**The supply position today is brutally simple.** Centrus' American Centrifuge Plant Phase II cascade (16 AC100M centrifuges) produces 900 kg HALEU UF₆ per year. Centrus completed Phase II — 900 kg cumulative delivery to DOE — in June 2025, with full-year 2025 deliveries cumulatively crossing 1,000 kg ("over 1 metric ton" per Centrus' Q4 2025 press release). Phase III (extended through June 30, 2026 under the DOE option exercised in June 2025) continues at the 900 kg/year cadence. **That is the entirety of operational Western HALEU enrichment capacity — approximately 1 MTU/year.**

**Demand reality:** DOE has formally projected ≥40 MTU/year by 2030 and ~50 MTU/year by 2035; INL's 2020 high-deployment scenario projected 50 MTU/year as early as 2026 (now slipped to 2030–2032). The deployment math:

| Reactor | First Core HALEU Need | Deployed Fleet Need | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| TerraPower Natrium (Kemmerer Unit 1) | ~15 MT | 8 units (Meta MOU) | Fuel load 2030, COD 2031 |
| X-energy Xe-100 (Dow Seadrift, AWS) | ~6 MT (4-pack) | ~16+ MT/year for 11-reactor TX-1 capacity | First fuel 2027–2028 |
| Oklo Aurora-INL | 5 MT (down-blended EBR-II) | ~75 MWe per unit, 1,350 MWe pipeline | COD late 2027–2028 |
| Kairos Hermes / Hermes 2 | <1 MT each (TRISO) | TVA/Google 50 MWe Hermes 2 | 2030+ |
| BWXT Project Pele | <1 MT (DOE HEU down-blend) | 1.5 MWe transportable | 2028 |
| Last Energy | <1 MT each | 20 MWe | TBD |

In aggregate the demand stack runs from ~3–6 MTU/year in 2027 (first cores) to potentially 30–50 MTU/year by 2032–2035 if even a fraction of announced deployments materialize. The supply stack at Western producers — Centrus only at present — runs at 1 MTU/year and would, under fully funded scale-up plans, reach 6 MTU/year (60-machine cascade) and 12 MTU/year (120-machine cascade) over a multi-year buildout, with first commercial-scale incremental capacity not online until 2027–2028 at the earliest.

**The DOE allocation process is the de facto rationing mechanism.** In April 2025, DOE selected five reactor developers — TRISO-X (X-energy), Kairos Power, Radiant Industries, Westinghouse, and TerraPower — to receive initial HALEU allocations, with three needing fuel in 2025. Of the 15 companies that requested HALEU, only those five made the cut. **This is, in effect, government-allocated commodity rationing for a fuel commodity — a striking signal of how binding the constraint is.**

**Centrus' contract economics (where consensus is materially under-pricing):**
- HALEU Operation Contract Phase II value increased to $152.3 million as of November 2024
- Phase III: DOE in June 2025 split the first three-year extension into 1+2 year options, exercising the first; Phase III total value $110M for the year through June 2026, with options for up to eight years of extensions at the Department's discretion
- January 5, 2026: DOE selected Centrus' American Centrifuge Operating (ACO) for a **$900 million task order**, subject to negotiations, to expand the Piketon facility for commercial-scale HALEU production
- $1.5 billion DOE uranium processing contract (mentioned in BWXT context but originally Centrus-related) — and the NNSA's October 2025 stated intent to **sole-source** certain uranium enrichment activities to Centrus for national security purposes (Centrus' AC100M is the only deployment-ready U.S.-origin enrichment technology)
- $2.3 billion in contingent LEU sales contracts/commitments ($2.1B definitive + $0.2B subject to definitive) backing the proposed Piketon expansion
- $2.0 billion unrestricted cash as of December 31, 2025

**Centrus' competitive position is stronger than consensus appreciates.** The DOE LEU+ Task Order 2 selections (Centrus, General Matter, Orano) each got ~$900M, but this needs to be unpacked. General Matter is a recently formed startup with no operational facility; Orano's Project IKE is greenfield and won't produce until "the beginning of the next decade." Centrus is the **only** awardee with a licensed, operating facility, an existing supplier base, U.S.-origin technology (AC100M), and an FOAK demonstration completed. The path from $900M task order to commercial production is shortest at Piketon by years.

**URENCO USA HALEU plans:** URENCO has publicly explored HALEU production but has not yet committed to a dedicated HALEU unit; LEU+ authorization (10%) is the precursor. **Orano HALEU plans:** integrated within Project IKE Oak Ridge. **General Matter:** a stealth-mode startup; capability and timeline are unproven. **Global Laser Enrichment / Silex Systems:** SILEX achieved Technology Readiness Level (TRL)-6 in October 2025; commercial deployment timeline is post-2030 absent a step change.

**Bottom line on HALEU:** Centrus sits inside a regulatory and technological moat that is wider, deeper, and longer-duration than the equity market has fully priced. The closest Western competitor is at least 3–5 years behind. Centrus is the purest expression of the thesis.

### 1.5 Deconversion (UF₆ to U-metal or Oxide) — Bottleneck Severity: 4/5

Deconversion is the conversion of HALEU UF₆ gas into the physical forms needed by reactor cores: metal (for Oklo, TerraPower), oxide kernels (for TRISO pebbles in X-energy, Kairos, BWXT Pele), or carbide. **BWXT is the dominant U.S. player.** In October 2024, DOE selected six companies for HALEU deconversion: BWXT's Nuclear Fuel Services, Centrus' ACO, Framatome, GE Vernova, Orano, and Westinghouse, each with $2 million minimum and a $2.7 billion ceiling for the program.

**BWXT's competitive moat in deconversion stems from three structural advantages:**
1. **Naval reactors heritage.** BWXT is the sole-source supplier of nuclear cores for the U.S. Navy's submarines and aircraft carriers. The Lynchburg, Virginia operations have decades of high-assay enriched uranium handling experience that no commercial competitor can replicate without a multi-year qualification process.
2. **Operational specialty fuels franchise.** BWXT's Specialty Fuels Fabrication Group has 20+ years of TRISO design and manufacturing experience for government applications. In December 2025, BWXT delivered the **full TRISO core for Project Pele** (the DoD's first transportable microreactor) to INL — 40,000 fuel compacts shipped on November 5, 2025. This is the first delivery of operational TRISO fuel for a U.S. military microreactor.
3. **DOE/DoD relationship density.** Project Pele itself is a $300M cost-type contract; BWXT also won a $1.5 billion DOE uranium processing award (announced as part of the Q3 2025 backlog). The AOT (Aerojet Ordnance Tennessee) acquisition from L3Harris closed January 2025, expanding special materials processing capabilities. The Kinectrics acquisition closed mid-2025, adding nuclear technical services.

**BWXT also serves as the Kairos Power TRISO partner** (announced September 2025). Kairos selected BWXT explicitly for "industry-leading TRISO development and manufacturing" capabilities and to "accelerate innovation in TRISO fuel manufacturing processes." This positions BWXT as the dual-source counterparty to TRISO-X (X-energy's captive subsidiary) — meaning that any reactor design requiring TRISO fuel without an X-energy relationship effectively must go through BWXT.

**TX-1 (X-energy/TRISO-X).** Construction began November 2025 in Oak Ridge, Tennessee on a 215,000 sq ft Category II fuel fabrication facility. NRC approved a 40-year Part 70 Special Nuclear Material license in October 2025 (the first commercial-scale facility licensed for HALEU TRISO fuel). Initial output target: 5 MTU/year (~700,000 pebbles/year), enough for 11 Xe-100 reactors. NRC license review approval anticipated May 2026; operations start 2027. **This is real infrastructure but with a captive-customer profile** — its primary purpose is to feed X-energy's Dow Seadrift project; third-party HALEU pebble fabrication remains a stated possibility but secondary to internal needs.

**Oklo Aurora Fuel Fabrication Facility (A3F).** DOE approved the Conceptual Safety Design Report in October 2024 and the Preliminary Documented Safety Analysis in 2025; assembly is underway at INL. Capacity is small (5 MT initial core for Aurora-INL, sourced from down-blended EBR-II HEU spent fuel). Oklo has also signed an MOU with Lightbridge to evaluate co-location of fuel fabrication. This is a vertically integrated, single-customer footprint — not a commercial third-party deconversion service.

**Bottom line:** Deconversion is the second-tightest layer after enrichment, and BWXT is the only diversified, profitable, listed pure-play with a structural moat. TRISO-X is captive; A3F is captive. BWXT is the only commercial open-market deconversion provider with operating capacity at scale.

### 1.6 Fuel Fabrication — Bottleneck Severity: 3/5

Fabrication is the most commoditized layer of the chain for traditional LWR fuel but tightens substantially for HALEU and TRISO formats. The Western majors:

- **Westinghouse** (Cameco 49% / Brookfield 51%): Columbia, SC fabrication for AP1000, AP300; Springfields, UK rebuild for European utilities; eVinci microreactor in development; Westinghouse received conditional HALEU allocation in April 2025 alongside TRISO-X, Kairos, Radiant, and TerraPower. Westinghouse's BWRX-300 and AP300 fuel are LEU-based and fit comfortably in URENCO/Orano supply.
- **Framatome** (EDF subsidiary, not directly listed): Manufactures fuel for the EPR fleet, BWRX-300, and signed an expanded fuel manufacturing agreement with NuScale covering both U.S. and European projects. Framatome also won a deconversion contract under the October 2024 DOE selection. EDF (EDF.PA) is closely tied to the French government and offers indirect exposure but is not investable for U.S. concentrated funds.
- **GE-Hitachi Nuclear Fuel** (Wilmington, NC): BWRX-300, current BWR fleet. GE Vernova (GEV) holds the public market exposure; nuclear is one of its lower-disclosed segments but emerging as a meaningful secondary growth engine.
- **BWXT Specialty Fuels** + **TRISO-X** + **Orano CERCA** (research reactor TRISO components): the TRISO-specific fabrication universe.
- **Lightbridge (LTBR)** — pre-revenue metallic fuel innovation; co-extrusion demonstration completed February 2025 at INL; irradiation testing began November 2025 in INL ATR. Lightbridge has framework agreements with Battelle Energy Alliance (the INL operator) and an MOU with Oklo on co-location of fuel fabrication. Commercial deployment is post-2030.

**Bottom line:** Standard LEU fabrication is not a binding constraint. HALEU/TRISO-format fabrication is, and is dominated by BWXT and TRISO-X. The fabrication chokepoint is effectively a sub-segment of the deconversion chokepoint.

### 1.7 Geopolitical Context — Cross-Cutting Risk Severity: 4/5

The Western fuel chain's geopolitical risk profile is asymmetric and tilted negatively over the investment horizon:

- **Russia.** The Prohibiting Russian Uranium Imports Act, signed May 13, 2024, with annual quota waivers through 2027 and full prohibition January 1, 2028, terminates Russia as a source of LEU and natural uranium. Russia has retaliated by requiring TENEX-specific export licenses on each shipment, creating ongoing supply uncertainty. The DOE Russian Uranium Ban Waiver process is administered case-by-case.
- **Kazakhstan.** Production cuts (29,700 tU₃O₈ vs. prior 32,800 tU target for 2026) and legislative changes weighting JV ownership toward Kazatomprom (up to 90% at JV contract extension, up to 75% on subsoil rights transfers) are tightening Western access. Rosatom has joint ventures producing >60% of its 7,000 tU/year through Kazakhstan, creating ongoing Russian backdoor exposure for Kazakh uranium.
- **Niger.** Orano's SOMAÏR has produced no uranium since the 2023 coup; the Niger junta has been strategically aligning with Russia. France's African uranium strategy is severely compromised.
- **Western alliance cohesion.** The October 2025 $80 billion U.S. Government strategic partnership with Westinghouse/Cameco/Brookfield, Trump's May 2025 Executive Orders on nuclear (EO 14299, 14300, 14301, 14302), and the ADVANCE Act of 2024 collectively constitute the most coordinated nuclear industrial policy in the West since the 1970s. This is bullish for U.S. listed names with strong domestic positioning (Centrus, BWXT, Cameco/Westinghouse) and somewhat ambiguous for European-headquartered counterparts that need to compete with the U.S.-Japan-Canada-Korea industrial bloc.
- **Australia (AUKUS).** BHP Olympic Dam and Cameco's Australian JVs increasingly important as a politically aligned, geopolitically stable supply source.

The combination of NRC license, operating facility, and indigenous technology is virtually impossible for any Western competitor to replicate inside our investment horizon.

### 3.2 Scenario Analysis

We stress-test the variant view across three scenarios.

**Base case (probability 50%): SMR deployment broadly on schedule; HALEU demand peaks 2030–2032.**
- Centrus: hit Phase III scale-up; commercial-scale 6–12 MTU/year capacity online 2028–2030; revenue ~$700–900M by 2028; analyst price targets ($240–280) plausible to upside.
- BWXT: commercial nuclear segment continues 25%+ growth; consolidated revenue ~$4.5–5.0B by 2028; backlog $9–10B; multiple expands to 28–32x earnings on growth recognition.
- Cameco: Westinghouse partnership delivers FID on first AP1000s by 2027–2028; uranium prices $90–110/lb sustained; share of Westinghouse adjusted EBITDA $700M+; equity participates in Westinghouse IPO optionality.
- Oklo, NuScale, X-energy proxies: deliver but with execution noise; Oklo first power 2028 confirms thesis but valuation already priced in.

**Slippage case (probability 35%): SMR delays of 2–3 years; demand stretches to 2032–2035.**
- Centrus: HALEU demand peak shifts but Russia ban still bites; LEU+ pricing stays strong; revenue trajectory delayed but contracted backlog still drives EBITDA growth; the equity case becomes about LEU+ scarcity rather than HALEU.
- BWXT: defense/naval cushion absorbs delay; commercial nuclear growth slows but doesn't reverse; backlog supports execution.
- Cameco: uranium prices sustained or rise on supply-side disappointments; Westinghouse delays not fatal because $80B partnership has multi-year horizon.
- **Oklo, NuScale: equity hammered.** A 2-year slippage on a pre-revenue developer with priced-in 2028 commercial operation is the most punishing outcome.

**Failure case (probability 15%): major SMR program failures; demand softens; Russia ban partially reversed.**
- Centrus: government revenue floor (HALEU Operation Contract through 2034 if extended, NNSA sole-source) cushions; equity de-rates 30–50%.
- BWXT: defense business is the bedrock; nuclear segment slows but base business unaffected; equity de-rates 15–25%.
- Cameco: uranium price falls but supply-side discipline (Kazatomprom cuts already in progress) cushions; Westinghouse value impaired.
- **Oklo, NuScale: capital impairment; equity declines 60–80%.** Highly asymmetric downside.

**The asymmetry is decisive.** In the base and slippage cases, Tier 1 names compound. In the failure case, Tier 1 names de-rate but retain operating businesses; Tier 3 developers face capital impairment.

### 3.5 Bear Cases — What Kills the Thesis

A rigorous fundamental analyst must steel-man the disconfirming arguments.

**1. DOE budget cuts in next administration.** The thesis depends on continued DOE funding for HALEU programs ($2.7B LEU+ program; $900M individual task orders; HALEU Operation Contract extensions). A future administration could de-prioritize advanced nuclear and let appropriations lapse. **Counter:** the bipartisan support for the ADVANCE Act (Senate 88–2; House 393–14), the May 2025 Trump executive orders, and the strategic linkage to AI/data center power needs make full reversal unlikely. But a slowdown is plausible, particularly under a Democratic administration prioritizing renewables.

**2. Faster-than-expected capacity expansion at Orano/URENCO.** Orano Project IKE could come online by 2028 (vs. our 2030+ assumption); URENCO USA could commit to a dedicated HALEU unit. **Counter:** physical lead times are 4–6 years for centrifuge cascades; Orano has not yet begun construction; URENCO has not announced HALEU FID. The Centrus head start is structurally hard to compress.

**3. SMR designs migrating to LEU.** If BWRX-300, AP300, and similar LEU-fueled designs win share against HALEU-fueled designs (Natrium, Xe-100, Aurora), the HALEU demand pull weakens. **Counter:** the December 2025 DOE selection of TVA and Holtec for $800M cost-share for advanced LWR SMRs supports this risk vector somewhat. **However**, the variant view's primary upstream names are not zero-sum to BWRX-300 wins. Cameco/Westinghouse benefits directly from AP1000/AP300 deployment under the $80B partnership. BWXT has been awarded the BWRX-300 reactor pressure valve manufacturing for OPG and steam generators for Rolls-Royce SMR. **Centrus is the most exposed to this risk** but URENCO's LEU+ buildout (now authorized to 10%) actually competes with Centrus on the LEU side, while Centrus retains the HALEU monopoly. Net effect: the LEU+ migration risk reduces but does not eliminate the HALEU thesis.

**4. Fusion progress (Helion, Commonwealth Fusion Systems, TAE).** Pure timing risk; commercially relevant fusion is post-2035 in even optimistic forecasts. Tail risk only.

**5. Russia sanctions easing.** A geopolitical reversal that allows Russian SWU back into U.S. and European markets at scale would compress LEU/SWU pricing and reduce HALEU substitution rationale. **Counter:** the Prohibiting Russian Uranium Imports Act expires 2040 and is statutory; reversal requires Congressional action. Trump administration nuclear EOs explicitly target Russia displacement. Reversal is low probability over our horizon.

**6. Hyperscaler nuclear capex moderation.** If the AI capex cycle peaks earlier than expected (e.g., 2027 as some bears suggest), the demand-pull for new nuclear weakens. **Counter:** existing hyperscaler PPAs are 20-year contracts; even a moderation in *new* capex doesn't unwind contracted demand. The 80+ GW of corporate clean energy contracts already signed by U.S. data centers (per S&P Global) creates a ~3-year buffer.

**7. Kazakhstan/Niger geopolitical disruption.** A meaningful Kazakh export disruption would tighten uranium markets sharply (positive for Cameco, mid-cap miners) but could pressure conversion/enrichment if utilities pivot to alternative supply chains. **Net effect: ambiguous, probably bullish for upstream.**

**8. Fuel-chain capacity additions outside of the listed names.** General Matter (DOE LEU/HALEU awardee) is a stealth-mode startup that could surprise; Nusano has announced a 350 MTU/year ambition with proprietary technology; LIS Technologies adds laser optionality. **Counter:** all of these are pre-operational and most are pre-licensed. Centrus' time-to-market lead is at minimum 3–5 years.

The single most important bear-case scenario to monitor is **#3 (LEU migration)**, because it directly undermines the HALEU-specific thesis. Mitigation: balance the Tier 1 trade across Centrus (HALEU exposure), BWXT (TRISO + naval defense ballast + LEU-agnostic), and Cameco (uranium + Westinghouse, somewhat LEU-agnostic). The combined trio works under both HALEU-led and LEU-led deployment scenarios.

## Closing Note: The Trade in One Page

Generalist money owns the SMR developers because they are the most legible nuclear renaissance equities. The mistake is treating reactor design as the binding constraint when the binding constraint is fuel supply. Through 2026–2030, the fuel chain — and within it, HALEU enrichment and TRISO/HALEU deconversion — will price like a constrained commodity with sole-source pricing power. Centrus owns the HALEU monopoly. BWXT owns TRISO and HALEU deconversion, with naval defense paying the bills while it scales. Cameco owns the upstream (uranium, conversion) and 49% of Westinghouse, which has just been declared a U.S. industrial policy partner with $80B of contemplated reactor deployments and an embedded equity participation by the U.S. Government.

The right concentration for a long-only concentrated fundamental fund is to own this trio in size, complement with GE Vernova for diversified electrification + BWRX-300 leverage and one or two regulated nuclear utilities (Constellation, Vistra) for fleet incumbency and PPA pricing power. The developer cohort — Oklo, NuScale, X-energy proxies — is best left to specialist venture capital and momentum/thematic ETFs. The variant view is not contrarian for its own sake; it is a fundamental view about where in the value chain economic rents accrue when capacity is scarce relative to demand.

Catalysts over the next 12 months — Centrus' $900M task order finalization, BWXT's Project Pele criticality, the Westinghouse $80B partnership FID, TVA Clinch River construction milestones, the Russian uranium ban hardening into 2028 — are sequenced to compound the upstream thesis. The dominant risk is a faster-than-expected Western enrichment capacity expansion (Orano IKE, URENCO HALEU) or a major SMR designed migration to LEU (BWRX-300 winning share). Both risks are tractable and partially offset within the recommended portfolio construction. The structural thesis — that the fuel chain is the binding constraint and Centrus/BWXT/Cameco are the highest-quality publicly listed expressions of it — should hold across the base, slippage, and even most failure scenarios.
