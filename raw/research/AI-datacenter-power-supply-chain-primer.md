# AI Datacenter Power and Energy Supply Chain

## Core conclusion

The market is finally repricing a fact that had been hiding in plain sight: AI infrastructure is no longer primarily constrained by GPUs, packaging, or memory. It is increasingly constrained by **firm power, grid access, transformer availability, and thermal management**. That shifts economic value away from the most obvious compute names and toward assets that control **licensed baseload generation, regulated interconnection rights, grid equipment throughput, and liquid-cooling deployment capacity**. The most durable scarcity rents today sit in three places: existing nuclear fleets and their interconnection rights, regulated or contracted power-delivery infrastructure in data-center-heavy regions, and electrical/thermal balance-of-plant suppliers that can actually ship into a supply-constrained market. The highest-confidence mispricing, in my view, is **not** in the loudest narrative names. It is in businesses where the market still treats power as a commodity when it has become a **time-critical, site-specific, permit-constrained scarce asset**. That favors owners of existing nuclear megawatts, certain regulated utilities with signed large-load economics, selected grid-equipment names, and the thermal stack around liquid cooling. By contrast, the parts of the market most obviously running ahead of fundamentals are the **pre-revenue SMR equities** and the **hydrogen or demonstration-stage distributed-power stories** that have far less permitting, fuel, and commercial evidence than their market narratives imply. That inference follows directly from the current gap between signed contracts and actual commercial deployment milestones. The March 2026 White House **Ratepayer Protection Pledge** is best understood not as a generalized restriction on hyperscaler buildout, but as a change in who pays. ## Investment framework and where the asymmetry is largest

The best way to think about exposure quality is to rank the stack by **scarcity, substitutability, and timeline risk**. The highest-quality moats are assets with existing licenses, interconnections, or installed manufacturing: existing nuclear fleets; regulated utilities with signed large-load cost recovery; transformer/switchgear/UPS suppliers; and cooling leaders with liquid-cooling manufacturing depth. Those businesses already have revenue engines and do not need a science project to work. The next tier is integrated developers like Brookfield, NextEra, or AES that can combine power, land, and interconnection. The bottom tier, despite the excitement, is first-of-a-kind SMR equity and fuel-cell or hydrogen stories without large signed deployments. That is not because the technologies are unimportant. It is because the market has to discount **time**, **fuel**, **NRC throughput**, and **construction execution**, and many equity prices currently do not do that adequately. This is an inference from the underlying contracting and licensing evidence, not a statement that the technologies will fail. ## Open questions and public-data limits

Several of the most important variables are still not fully disclosed in public materials. Many 2025ŌĆō2026 power agreements do **not** disclose strike prices, escalation terms, curtailment obligations, or exact physical-versus-financial delivery mechanics. Private-company valuations for names like Kairos and TerraPower are also harder to benchmark cleanly than the public peers. Oracle and OpenAI remain especially opaque on standalone power procurement relative to Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta. That means the highest-confidence conclusions are about **who controls bottlenecks and who has signed real contracts**, not about precise project IRRs or undisclosed power prices.
