## The pattern, documented

The historical record supports the existence of a real NVIDIA pattern, but it is narrower and more conditional than the phrase ŌĆ£absorb and extendŌĆØ can imply. The pattern fires most clearly when NVIDIA acquires infrastructure technologies that sit directly on a control boundary in its platform stack: interconnects in Mellanox, network operating systems in Cumulus, cluster-management software in Bright, workload orchestration in Run:ai, and model optimization in OmniML. In those cases, NVIDIA does not merely hold them; it operationally folds them into a wider stack, often retaining the product name while eliminating the company as an independent actor. The clearest evidence is Mellanox, where NVIDIA transformed a fast-growing networking vendor into a core architectural layer of its Data Center franchise and later exposed networking as a distinct multibillion-dollar revenue line. *(NVIDIA and target-company announcements and product documentation, 2019ŌĆō2026).* ŅłĆciteŅłéturn24search7Ņłéturn27search1Ņłéturn27search3Ņłéturn26search1Ņłéturn26search4Ņłéturn25search6Ņłéturn26search11Ņłéturn26search5Ņłéturn21search1Ņłéturn20search3Ņłéturn42search0Ņłéturn39view1Ņłéturn40view0Ņłü

The same record also shows when the pattern does **not** fire. Minority investments usually remain minority investments. CoreWeave, Applied Digital, Nebius, Recursion, SoundHound, and Ayar Labs all gave NVIDIA some mix of supply assurance, ecosystem leverage, product alignment, or financial exposure. But those relationships remained collaborations rather than full integrations. Structurally, the dividing line appears to be control over execution versus coordination with suppliers and partners. When NVIDIA needs tighter product integration, software control, and architectural coherence, it has repeatedly bought the capability outright. When NVIDIA needs capacity, ecosystem expansion, geographic manufacturing, or market-development leverage, it has more often used minority financing and commercial agreements. Mellanox, Cumulus, Run:ai, and OmniML fit the first category. CoreWeave, Nebius, Ayar, Lumentum, and Coherent fit the second. Bright sits in the middle: it was acquired outright, but the integration was quieter, more utilitarian, and brand-transitional rather than immediate and market-facing. *(Primary announcements and product materials across the case set).* ŅłĆciteŅłéturn24search7Ņłéturn21search3Ņłéturn35view0Ņłéturn40view0Ņłéturn42search0Ņłéturn14search0Ņłéturn12search0Ņłéturn13search2Ņłéturn33search2Ņłéturn33search3Ņłü

Arm sharpens the analysis because it shows that NVIDIA does at times try to acquire foundational dependencies, not merely invest in them. But Arm also shows the limit: when the dependency is too systemically important and too regulatorily sensitive, the strategy can be blocked. That matters for interpreting later deals. The historical record does not show a company reflexively buying every strategic partner. It shows a company willing to buy when integration is decisive and feasible, willing to invest when coordination is the better instrument, and willing to stop when external constraints make control unattainable. *(NVIDIA/SoftBank materials; CMA; FTC).* ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19search4Ņłéturn19search1Ņłéturn19search14Ņłéturn19search9Ņłü

The best condensed formulation of the pattern is therefore this: NVIDIA tends to **fully absorb software and networking control points that can materially extend the performance or programmability of its platform**, while it tends to **take minority positions in suppliers, cloud partners, and ecosystem builders when capital, capacity, and roadmap influence matter more than legal control**. That is a real historical pattern. It is not universal, but it is documented.

### Implications for LITE and COHR

On the public record as of April 2026, the Lumentum and Coherent transactions look more like the Ayar Labs/CoreWeave/Nebius pattern than the Mellanox pattern. The deal structures are minority financings, not merger agreements. The commercial terms emphasize nonexclusive supply, multibillion-dollar purchase commitments, future capacity rights, U.S.-based manufacturing expansion, and joint R&D. Those are the hallmarks of ecosystem financing used to secure throughput and influence roadmaps in bottlenecked supply chains. *(Lumentum and Coherent filings and joint releases, Mar. 2, 2026).* ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view0Ņłéturn34view1Ņłéturn33search2Ņłéturn33search3Ņłü

The historical pattern therefore suggests that the default expectation should be **continued strategic collaboration rather than near-term absorption**. NVIDIAŌĆÖs record shows many minority investments that deepened into richer commercial dependencies without becoming acquisitions. For Lumentum and Coherent, the relevant historical analog is not ŌĆ£Mellanox eventually got boughtŌĆØ but ŌĆ£NVIDIA funds the bottleneck, secures capacity, and keeps the supplier independent while integrating the technology at the platform level.ŌĆØ That interpretation is especially strong because both agreements are explicitly nonexclusive. *(CoreWeave, Nebius, Ayar, Lumentum, and Coherent primary materials).* ŅłĆciteŅłéturn9search4Ņłéturn12search9Ņłéturn13search2Ņłéturn13search3Ņłéturn33search2Ņłéturn33search3Ņłü

The caveat is structural, not speculative. If optics were to become as central to NVIDIAŌĆÖs differentiation as high-performance fabric became in 2019, and if one of these suppliers became both strategically indispensable and practically acquirable, the Arm/Mellanox side of the pattern says NVIDIA has the organizational disposition to seek deeper control. But that is not what the March 2026 documentation shows. What it shows is supplier financing with roadmap and capacity alignment.
