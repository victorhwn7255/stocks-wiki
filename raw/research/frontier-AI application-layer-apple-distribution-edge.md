# The frontier AI application layer and Apple’s distribution edge

## Executive judgment

The strongest current reading is that the frontier AI **application** layer is becoming the next wave of growth, but not in the simple sense that “value always migrates up” and leaves infrastructure behind. What looks more likely is a **selective migration**: durable value is starting to accumulate where applications control a workflow, hold user context, and can price on usage or outcomes, while broad, undifferentiated “model wrappers” remain vulnerable to price compression and bundling by vertically integrated labs and hyperscalers. That is why the evidence looks strongest today in coding, regulated workflow software, AI-enhanced search, and OS-level assistants, rather than in every consumer chatbot or every “agent” startup. citeturn10search1turn10search12turn24search1turn13search2turn29search1

The present data also argues that the application layer is already **real**, not hypothetical. OpenAI says ChatGPT has more than **900 million weekly active users**, more than **50 million consumer subscribers**, and more than **9 million paying business users**; Google said the Gemini app passed **750 million monthly active users**, AI Overviews reached **2.5 billion monthly active users**, and AI Mode passed **1 billion monthly users**; Microsoft says its first-party Copilots passed **150 million monthly active users** and Microsoft 365 Copilot now has **more than 20 million paid seats**; Meta says more than **1 billion people use Meta AI every month**; Amazon says AWS’s AI revenue run rate is **over $15 billion**, and Alexa+ is now broadly available in the U.S. and bundled into Prime. Those are not novelty-scale footprints. citeturn38view0turn37view1turn28search2turn28search8turn37view2turn37view3turn37view4turn37view5

At the same time, the bear case is real. Reuters reported that OpenAI’s adjusted gross margin fell from **40% in 2024 to 33% in 2025** as inference costs quadrupled, and Microsoft has now explicitly moved to help customers choose among mixed and open-source models rather than anchoring everything to one lab. GitHub Copilot has shifted toward usage-based billing, Cursor’s enterprise pricing includes usage allotments and overages, and Google is actively making it easier to import chat history from rival AI apps. Those are all signs that inference is a real variable cost and that switching costs are not yet naturally SaaS-like. citeturn24search1turn15news32turn11search11turn15search5turn9search19

On Apple specifically, the best hedged verdict is: **Apple is an underappreciated consumer AI winner as a distribution, orchestration, and toll layer — not yet as a frontier-intelligence owner.** Its edge is its device base, default OS position, App Store economics, on-device and Private Cloud Compute privacy architecture, and now its system-level agent surface through Siri AI, App Intents, Shortcuts, and Apple Intelligence. Its weakness is that it is visibly relying on outside frontier intelligence: Apple’s ChatGPT extension is live, and Apple and Google have now publicly announced that the next generation of Apple Foundation Models will be based on Google’s Gemini models and cloud technology. That makes Apple powerful, but it also means Apple may capture rent primarily as the **default interface and routing layer**, not as the owner of the core intelligence stack. citeturn34view0turn35view1turn8search0turn8search3turn21search2turn37view0turn35view0

## The base rate from prior platform shifts

The useful historical lesson is not that value **must** rise to the top layer, but that major platform shifts usually create a new bargaining problem between infrastructure owners, platform owners, and complements. Academic platform literature is clear that applications and complements increase the value of the base platform, and that value capture depends on governance, bottlenecks, and control of demand rather than on a universal law that “the highest layer wins.” In other words, “value migrates up” is a **base rate with many exceptions**, not a law of nature. citeturn10search1turn10search12turn10search18

What is common across the PC, internet, mobile, and cloud eras is that once the lower layer becomes more standardized and cheaper, the scarce asset shifts toward the layer that organizes the user experience, demand, and workflow. In mobile, for example, cross-platform apps such as WhatsApp and Instagram scaled to roughly **3 billion** monthly users each, while Uber built a **199 million** monthly active platform consumer base; in cloud-era software, companies like ServiceNow still report software-like recurring economics even while embedding AI into the product. The common pattern is not “apps always win,” but “the layer that controls the habit, workflow, or transaction often wins.” citeturn22search0turn22search4turn22search5turn13search2

What is idiosyncratic differs by era. The PC era monetized through software licenses and operating-system standards; the internet era monetized through search, ads, and marketplaces; mobile monetized through consumer engagement, app stores, and transactions; cloud monetized through recurring workflow software. That matters for AI, because AI is not just another app category. It is both a capability sold directly to users and a capability embedded throughout existing products. OpenAI is already describing the stack as a compute layer topped by a product platform, then agents and workflow automation, with subscriptions, ads, commerce, APIs, and eventually outcome-based pricing all coexisting. citeturn38view1turn11search5turn16search0

That is why the AI cycle partly rhymes with history and partly breaks it. The bull case is that cheaper intelligence expands the total set of tasks software can perform, so the largest long-run pools should again sit near the user workflow and experience. The bear case is that this time many of the infra owners are also the model owners, the cloud hosts, and the distribution owners, so they can keep much of the app rent inside one P&L. Google can move Gemini into Search, Android, and Workspace; Microsoft can push Copilot into Windows, GitHub, and Microsoft 365; Amazon can combine Bedrock, AWS, Prime, commerce, and Alexa; Meta can distribute Meta AI into Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp. That vertical integration is the clearest reason the old “value migrates up” script may be weaker this cycle. citeturn28search2turn9search0turn37view1turn37view2turn37view4turn37view5turn37view3

## Where application-layer monetization is real today

The cleanest evidence that the application layer is monetizing is in the **frontier labs’ own products** and in a few enterprise categories. OpenAI has the strongest direct proof: more than **900 million weekly active users**, more than **50 million consumer subscribers**, and more than **9 million paying business users**, with a business model spanning subscriptions, ads/commerce-supported free usage, and usage-based APIs. Google’s consumer reach is even broader if Search is counted as AI application surface: AI Overviews now have **2.5 billion monthly active users**, AI Mode passed **1 billion**, and Gemini app usage passed **750 million** monthly active users, while Google is already testing and expanding ad formats for the AI era of Search and selling AI subscriptions through Google AI plans. Microsoft similarly shows paid enterprise traction through more than **20 million** Microsoft 365 Copilot seats. citeturn38view0turn38view1turn28search2turn28search8turn37view1turn11search4turn11search5turn37view2

The frontier-lab consumer layer is therefore **economically real**, but it is not uniform. OpenAI and Google have clear direct monetization engines. Microsoft has highly visible enterprise seat monetization. Meta clearly has scale, but its monetization remains more indirect: the company says more than **1 billion** people use Meta AI monthly and that AI interactions will increasingly improve recommendations and ads across its platforms. Amazon’s Alexa+ also looks more like a bundled distribution play than a standalone software-margin story: it is free for Prime members, available at **$19.99 per month** otherwise, and has reached “tens of millions” in early access requests. Those are real products, but the monetization logic differs sharply by company. citeturn37view3turn37view4

In **enterprise AI-native verticals**, coding is the clearest proof point. GitHub Copilot now has explicit seat pricing plus AI credits and usage-based billing; Microsoft says more than **90% of the Fortune 500** use Microsoft AI and that Microsoft 365 Copilot has more than **20 million** paid seats. Cursor’s official pricing also mixes seats with included usage allotments and prepaid additional usage, which is exactly what one would expect in a category where inference is a meaningful direct cost. Reuters reported Cursor’s scaling was extraordinary, but those revenue figures are still private-company disclosures rather than audited primary-source numbers, so they should be treated as **Tier 3** evidence requiring verification. citeturn11search11turn14search12turn37view2turn15search0turn15search5turn15search2

Legal and regulated-professional AI looks more durable than the average “AI wrapper” because it pairs model output with proprietary content, domain workflow, and trust requirements. Thomson Reuters reported in Q1 2026 that **98%** of its revenue was recurring and that organic growth was driven by **Westlaw and CoCounsel**; it also said one million professionals now use CoCounsel, and its public materials emphasize proprietary content, domain expertise, and zero-retention arrangements with model providers. That is a very different structure from a generic chatbot app. It suggests that vertical AI with proprietary knowledge assets can preserve much more of classic software economics. citeturn29search1turn29search7turn29search6turn29search13

The weakest public evidence today is in the more heavily hyped parts of the stack: **support-agent startups, search challengers, healthcare, finance, and pure orchestration tools**. There are strong product signals and some private-company revenue reports, but public primary-source economics remain thin. Reuters covered Decagon’s fundraise, but not robust audited unit economics; Sierra’s public site shows customer-experience positioning, but the best-reported revenue figures are still private and secondary. In other words, those segments may be real, but the public proof is still much thinner than in coding, search, or regulated workflow software. citeturn30search0turn30search2turn30news35

The **agentic orchestration layer** is also more ambiguous than the hype suggests. Anthropic launched the Model Context Protocol as an open standard; OpenAI has upgraded its Agents SDK with sandboxed execution for long-horizon tasks; Microsoft is steadily deepening Copilot Studio’s orchestration runtime. But precisely because the standards and frameworks are becoming more open and provider-flexible, the standalone moat at the protocol layer may prove thinner than many expect. The orchestration layer is important, but it may capture more value as part of a broader platform than as an independent toll booth. citeturn23search0turn23search1turn23search12

## Economics and moat durability

The core economic question is whether frontier AI apps can become **software businesses** or whether they remain **resold compute businesses**. Current evidence says the answer depends heavily on the segment. Reuters reported that OpenAI’s adjusted gross margin fell to **33%** in 2025 from **40%** in 2024 as inference costs quadrupled; other Reuters reporting on OpenAI’s first half of 2025 implied gross margin around **42%** before other complications, still far below classic software levels. Those figures are valuable but are not primary audited disclosures, so they should be treated as **Tier 3** and verified if used in investment work. citeturn24search1turn24search7

In contrast, established workflow software still looks much more like classic SaaS. ServiceNow reported a **77.5% GAAP subscription gross margin** and **81.5% non-GAAP subscription gross margin** in Q1 2026 even while it pushed AI into the product. Thomson Reuters reported **98% recurring revenues** and a **48.3% adjusted EBITDA margin** in Q1 2026, with CoCounsel and Westlaw as growth drivers. That gap is the crucial structural point: when AI is embedded inside a broader proprietary workflow, margins can remain software-like; when the product is close to raw frontier-model consumption, variable inference costs matter much more. citeturn13search2turn29search1

The pricing models are also changing in ways that support this conclusion. GitHub Copilot has moved to usage-based billing with included AI credits; Cursor explicitly prices enterprise plans per seat with included usage and configurable limits; OpenAI says it now operates a mix of per-user subscriptions, a free ad- and commerce-supported tier, and usage-based APIs; Google sells AI subscriptions while using the AI search surface to create ad inventory; Apple’s own developer stack is moving in the opposite direction for some use cases, offering access to Apple Foundation Models and Private Cloud Compute with **no cloud API cost** for eligible small developers. This is not the pricing profile of old flat-fee SaaS. It is a hybrid of seat, consumption, and outcome pricing. citeturn11search11turn15search5turn38view1turn11search0turn11search5turn8search3turn35view1

Moat durability therefore looks strongest where three conditions stack together: **proprietary workflow or required context**, **consumption or outcome pricing**, and **owned distribution**. Thomson Reuters scores well on proprietary content and workflow. Microsoft scores well on distribution and workflow because of Microsoft 365, Windows, GitHub, and the Microsoft Graph context. Google scores well on distribution, direct model ownership, and monetization through Search and Workspace. Apple scores extremely well on distribution and private on-device context, but more weakly on model ownership. Pure standalone consumer assistants without proprietary workflow or owned distribution look weakest. citeturn29search6turn37view2turn28search2turn28search8turn37view1turn35view1turn37view0

The strongest disconfirming evidence to the moat thesis is how quickly the market is becoming **multi-model and provider-flexible**. Microsoft’s new Frontier Company is explicitly premised on customers choosing among models instead of staying captive to one. GitHub is integrating rival agents such as Claude and Codex into Copilot. Google now offers tools to import chat history from competing AI apps into Gemini. Those are strong signs that simple model access alone is not a moat; durable rents will likely require workflow embedding, context, or privileged distribution. citeturn15news32turn14news28turn9search19

## Distribution, model quality, and default position

The current cycle already shows that **model quality can defeat distribution in the early phase**. OpenAI built ChatGPT into a global habit without any OS default, telco carriage deal, or preloaded handset footprint; the company now says ChatGPT has more than **900 million weekly active users**. That is extremely strong evidence against a simplistic “distribution always wins” thesis. In frontier AI, step-changes in product quality can still pull users directly into a new app. citeturn38view0

But the same cycle also shows how powerful default distribution becomes once the model is “good enough.” Google has inserted Gemini into Search, AI Overviews, AI Mode, Android, and Workspace, and it now says AI Overviews exceed **2.5 billion** monthly active users and AI Mode exceeds **1 billion**. Microsoft says it has **150 million** monthly active users across first-party Copilots and **900 million** monthly active users of AI features across its products. Meta AI crossed **1 billion** monthly users largely by being embedded in Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp. In other words, model pull created the category, but defaults and installed surfaces are now massively expanding it. citeturn28search2turn28search8turn37view2turn37view3

The mobile-era lesson cuts both ways. Cross-platform apps such as WhatsApp and Instagram each scaled to about **3 billion** monthly users, and Uber reached **199 million** monthly active platform consumers, which is cautionary evidence against overestimating handset-maker control. Great apps can cross device boundaries. But AI assistants are not just ordinary apps: they sit closer to permissions, personal context, on-screen awareness, and tool invocation. Apple’s App Intents framework, for example, is designed to make app actions and content discoverable to Siri AI and Apple Intelligence; Google is replacing Google Assistant with Gemini across more Android surfaces; Microsoft is deepening Copilot Studio orchestration across enterprise data and apps. Those are privileged positions ordinary apps do not automatically get. citeturn22search0turn22search4turn22search5turn35view1turn9search0turn9search2turn23search12

The practical conclusion is that **distribution and model quality are not substitutes; they are sequential advantages**. Model quality matters most when a new category is being created. Distribution matters most once enough users accept the category and the battle moves to default surfaces, permissions, memory, commerce, and workflow routing. That is exactly why ChatGPT can still be a destination app while Google, Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, and Apple can simultaneously be powerful application-layer winners. citeturn38view0turn28search2turn37view2turn37view3turn37view4turn35view1

## Apple and the distribution-rich incumbents

The Apple bull case is straightforward and stronger than many surface takes admit. Apple said its active installed base reached a new all-time high and had earlier said it surpassed **2.5 billion** active devices; Services revenue hit another all-time high; Apple Intelligence is integrated into the OS and app surfaces; App Intents and Shortcuts position Apple as the system-level routing layer for app actions; Apple’s Private Cloud Compute architecture extends its privacy model into server-side AI; and developers can integrate into Apple Intelligence while, in some cases, using Apple Foundation Models on-device or on Private Cloud Compute at effectively no API cost. That is an unusually strong combination of premium hardware distribution, system permissions, developer control, and privacy positioning. citeturn0search7turn34view0turn35view1turn8search0turn8search3

Apple also has a direct toll position. Its App Store Small Business Program confirms a **15%** commission on paid apps and in-app purchases for qualifying small developers, implying the broader App Store still matters economically as AI apps scale on iOS. If consumer AI remains app-centric, Apple participates. If consumer AI becomes assistant-centric, Apple may still participate through default placement and OS routing. Either way, Apple has a credible claim on distribution economics. citeturn16search0turn16search5

The Apple bear case is equally real. Apple’s own public pages make clear that **Siri AI is still coming later this year**, initially in English, and not initially available in the EU on iOS, iPadOS, and watchOS. Apple Intelligence is compatible only with newer devices, not with the entire installed base. More importantly, Apple and Google have publicly announced a multi-year collaboration under which the next generation of Apple Foundation Models will be based on **Google’s Gemini models and cloud technology**, while Apple’s ChatGPT extension is already part of the Apple Intelligence experience. That means Apple is presently depending on others for a material part of frontier model quality. citeturn35view0turn36view1turn36view3turn21search2turn37view0

There is also a Services-risk angle. Reuters, citing court records and related analysis, put Apple’s 2022 default-search payment from Google at about **$20 billion**; that figure is important, but it is **Tier 3** and should be verified directly from court materials rather than treated as primary-source fact. The strategic point is sound even if the exact number changes: if AI-era search reduces the value of default Safari search placement faster than Apple creates new AI monetization channels, then Apple’s Services mix could come under pressure. citeturn17search2turn17search7

The cleanest verdict is therefore this: **Apple is an underappreciated AI application-layer winner if the winning layer is the consumer-device distribution and orchestration layer.** It is not yet an underappreciated winner if the winning layer must mean “frontier-model owner with direct software-margin control over the intelligence.” Apple’s upside condition is that Siri AI, App Intents, and Apple Intelligence become the default agentic shell through which users invoke apps, commerce, and personal context on their devices. Apple’s downside condition is that users increasingly bypass Siri and go straight to ChatGPT, Gemini, or other destination apps while Apple merely hosts them and rents their intelligence. citeturn35view1turn37view0turn38view0

Against the other distribution-rich incumbents, Apple looks **strong but not uniquely advantaged**. Google currently has the best combination of frontier model ownership, Search monetization, Android distribution, and Workspace workflow; Microsoft has the strongest enterprise workflow capture, with Copilot, Windows, GitHub, Azure, and a disclosed **$37 billion** AI annual revenue run rate; Meta has the broadest free consumer attention surfaces and a proven ad machine tied to AI recommendations; Amazon has the most obvious commerce-action loop and Bedrock/AWS distribution; Apple has the cleanest premium-device distribution and privacy story, but the weakest direct control of frontier models among that top group. On balance, Google appears best positioned if model + distribution + monetization all matter at once; Apple appears most interesting where the key question is **who owns the default on-device agent entry point**. citeturn37view1turn25search0turn37view3turn37view4turn37view5turn35view1turn37view0

## Timing, infrastructure feedback, and what to verify

The timing question remains crucial because the infrastructure build-out is already extraordinary. Reuters said Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft are on track to invest about **$650 billion** in AI-related spend in 2026, while other Reuters reporting put the broader AI capex boom closer to **$800 billion**. Microsoft’s AI business is now at a **$37 billion** run rate, AWS says its AI revenue run rate is **over $15 billion**, OpenAI says ChatGPT sits above **900 million** weekly active users and more than **50 million** subscribers, and Reuters reported run-rate revenues of roughly **$20 billion** for OpenAI and **$9 billion** for Anthropic in early 2026. The application layer is therefore no longer empty; it is generating real demand. But the revenue ramp is still uneven relative to how aggressively capex is being deployed. The “revenue gap” argument has not been disproven yet. citeturn27search18turn27search0turn25search0turn37view5turn38view0turn24search0

The best real-time **signposts** that value is truly migrating into the application layer would be these: continued acceleration in direct AI-app revenue; improving gross margins for scaled application businesses after inference costs; more conversion from seat pricing into usage- and outcome-based pricing; and evidence that AI applications are becoming budget-line items rather than experimental tools. There are early hints of each: OpenAI openly describes multi-tier monetization across subscriptions, ads/commerce, and APIs; GitHub and Cursor have both moved toward usage-linked pricing; Thomson Reuters and ServiceNow show that AI can drive growth inside recurring workflow software; and Microsoft’s Copilot seat counts suggest AI is already becoming a standard enterprise software purchase. citeturn38view1turn11search11turn15search5turn29search1turn13search2turn37view2

The strongest **disconfirmers** would be the opposite pattern: token-price deflation flowing straight through to end users; frontier apps failing to reach software-like gross margins; consumer usage staying high but paid conversion weakening; vertical integration by Google, Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, and Apple keeping most of the rent inside bundled platforms; and growing model/provider portability reducing any standalone app’s moat. The public evidence already shows pieces of that risk. OpenAI’s reported margins are still well below classic SaaS, Microsoft is encouraging mixed-model adoption, GitHub is integrating rival agents, and Google is making switching easier. citeturn24search1turn15news32turn14news28turn9search19

The single clearest **confirmer** that the frontier AI application layer is the next durable value wave would be this: **a scaled AI application business proving multi-billion recurring revenue with expanding gross margins after inference costs, while retention and usage deepen rather than fade.** That would show that the category has crossed from “resold compute” into “software-plus-workflow.” The single clearest **falsifier** would be this: **even the category leaders remain stuck in sub-software margins, constant price compression, and weak switching costs, while most monetizable AI usage gets bundled into hyperscaler or device ecosystems.** citeturn24search1turn13search2turn29search1turn15news32

The Apple verdict, finally, is best stated as: **underappreciated winner, with conditions**. Apple is very plausibly a winner in consumer AI distribution, permissions, privacy, and orchestration. It is not yet proven as a winner in frontier-model ownership or direct AI software monetization. If Siri AI becomes the default agent shell that routes actions across apps and personal context, Apple could capture a very meaningful share of AI-era value without owning the best model. If users keep opening ChatGPT, Gemini, or Meta AI directly, and if search-placement economics erode faster than Apple builds new AI revenues, then Apple risks owning premium pipes while renting the intelligence. citeturn35view1turn37view0turn21search2turn17search7

### Open questions and limitations

The biggest limitation is that many of the most important application-layer companies are private, and some of the most interesting numbers in circulation — including OpenAI and Anthropic run-rate revenues, OpenAI adjusted gross margin, Cursor ARR, and the estimated Apple-Google search payment — come from Reuters or other secondary reporting rather than audited company filings. Those figures are useful directional evidence, but they are not substitutes for primary-source verification. citeturn24search0turn24search1turn15search2turn17search2

A second limitation is that public incumbents often do **not** separate AI application revenue cleanly from broader Search, Cloud, Services, or productivity revenue. Alphabet’s earnings materials disclose Gemini and AI user figures and the company’s segment revenues, but not a pure standalone AI-application revenue line; Apple and Meta similarly disclose AI adoption and broad segment results without isolating a pure AI-app P&L. That makes cross-company comparability imperfect. citeturn37view1turn18search9turn34view0turn37view3
