# ASML, EUV Lithography, and the Foundry Dependency Chain

## ASML's monopoly position in EUV

ASML remains the only company with a commercial extreme ultraviolet lithography platform in high-volume semiconductor manufacturing. Its own product materials state that its 0.33 numerical aperture NXE scanners are used in high-volume logic manufacturing at 7 nm, 5 nm, and 3 nm, and the company’s 2024 and 2025 reported results show that EUV is not a lab-only technology but a scaled commercial franchise: ASML recognized revenue on 44 EUV systems in 2024 and 48 EUV systems in 2025, including the first High NA deliveries. That is the operative meaning of monopoly here: not merely technical leadership, but sole commercially proven supply at the frontier. (ASML EUV products page; ASML 2024 Annual Report, p. 57; ASML Q4 2025 results). citeturn20search2turn37view0turn49search0turn49search2

The durability of that monopoly rests on a stack of barriers that are individually formidable and jointly prohibitive. EUV requires a synchronized system of source power, reflective optics, masks, metrology, contamination control, resist/process integration, and fab-side learning. A recent major review describes EUV as the culmination of a long technical progression in source technology, optics, masks, and resists rather than a single machine breakthrough. ASML’s own disclosures describe the company less as a conventional equipment OEM than as a system architect and integrator embedded in a long-cycle innovation ecosystem with customers, suppliers, research organizations, and academia. In practice, the frontier moat is not just an IP portfolio; it is accumulated process know-how distributed across ASML, its customers, and a small set of critical partners. (Nature Reviews Primer on EUV; ASML 2024 Annual Report, “How we innovate”; ASML 2024 Annual Report, supplier section). citeturn34search4turn42view1turn30view0

The most credible named alternative in public view is Canon’s nanoimprint lithography. Canon’s 2025 industrial presentation says its nanoimprint semiconductor strategy is aimed at “installation and process verification at customer sites” to realize mass production for advanced devices, and it claims patterning around 10 nm through a stamp-based method. That is noteworthy. It is not, however, the same thing as displacing ASML EUV in leading-edge logic. Reuters reported in early 2024 that market interest exceeded Canon’s expectations, but also that it was still too early to say when those tools would materially contribute to sales, while specialist coverage remained skeptical that nanoimprint was close to mainstream semiconductor manufacturing adoption. The realistic interpretation is that Canon has a live advanced-patterning alternative with possible niche or selective use cases, but not yet a demonstrated, volume-proven replacement for EUV at the leading-edge foundry nodes that matter for AI logic. (Canon Industrial Group 2025 presentation; Reuters, January 30, 2024; Bits&Chips, November 2023). citeturn52search0turn52search3turn52search2

China’s domestic effort is more serious than casual commentary often admits, but still does not amount to a commercial alternative. Reuters reported in December 2025 that a Shenzhen team completed a crude EUV prototype in early 2025, that it could generate EUV light, and that Beijing was targeting working chips by 2028, while sources judged 2030 more realistic. The same reporting emphasized that the decisive obstacles were not merely generating EUV light but replicating the precision optics and system-level integration that ASML and its ecosystem built over decades. That makes the timeline highly asymmetric: China may continue narrowing subcomponent gaps over the next several years, but a volume-ready domestic platform for leading-edge foundry production still appears to be an end-of-decade question rather than a near-term competitive threat. (Reuters, December 17, 2025). citeturn52search1turn52news30

Huawei and SMIC’s “alternative approach” is therefore better understood as a workaround path based on deep ultraviolet multipatterning, not as a substitute platform equivalent to EUV. TechInsights wrote that 7 nm features can be fabricated without EUV using 193 nm immersion DUV, but with materially higher process complexity and likely lower yields. That is consistent with Reuters’ 2025 reporting, citing TechInsights, that Huawei’s MateBook Fold used a chip made on SMIC’s older 7 nm-class N+2 process rather than the more ambitious 5 nm-equivalent N+3 process many observers expected. In other words, China has demonstrated that it can reach into the 7 nm class without EUV, but the public evidence still points to a severe yield/cost penalty below that level and no demonstrated path to the volume economics of TSMC, Samsung, or Intel on EUV-based nodes. (TechInsights on SMIC 7 nm and next-generation process; Reuters, June 23, 2025). citeturn33search1turn33search4turn33news32

The realistic bottom line is therefore narrow and strong. No public evidence supports the view that any non-ASML alternative will reach leading-edge, high-volume foundry production inside the next 12-18 months. Canon nanoimprint is a real technology program but is still in customer-site verification rather than broad leading-edge production use. China’s prototype EUV work is strategically important, but by Reuters’ reporting remains pre-commercial and likely years from yielding working chips at scale. DUV multipatterning can partially substitute at 7 nm-class geometries, but it does so by trading away cost, cycle time, and yield. For the purposes of an AI supply-chain chokepoint framework, ASML’s EUV monopoly remains structurally intact. (Canon Industrial Group 2025 presentation; Reuters, December 17, 2025; TechInsights). citeturn52search0turn52search1turn33search1

## The downstream dependency chain

The dependency chain starts from a simple fact: ASML’s own EUV product page says NXE systems are used in high-volume manufacturing of advanced logic and memory chips at 7 nm, 5 nm, and 3 nm. That means the relevant frontier foundries are choosing among nodes, flows, and timing differences, but not among EUV vendors. Their practical choice set is “ASML EUV now,” “ASML High NA later,” or “DUV workaround with penalties.” (ASML EUV products page). citeturn20search2

For TSMC, the EUV dependency is highest and best documented. TSMC’s 2019 annual-report materials described N7+ as the first high-volume foundry process using EUV and said N5 would enter volume production with “extensive EUV adoption” in the first half of 2020. TSMC’s current 5 nm technology page further states that N5 was its “second technology to use EUV lithography,” and its HPC/mobile technology pages describe N5 as using “full-fledged EUV adoption.” By 2024, TSMC said 3 nm represented 18 percent of total wafer revenue, N2 was on track for volume production in the second half of 2025, and A16 was scheduled for volume production in the second half of 2026. Public tool-count disclosure is still thin, but in a statement quoted by *The Register*, TSMC said its EUV tool count in 2023 was ten times 2019 and represented 56 percent of the global installed base; *The Register* estimated the 2019 base at roughly 13 tools, implying something on the order of 130 or more EUV scanners by 2023. Because that reconstruction is not a formal filing disclosure, it should be treated as an informed external estimate rather than a hard company number. Even so, it aligns with TSMC’s visible node cadence and makes TSMC the largest downstream concentration point of ASML EUV demand. Sensitivity to EUV supply constraints is therefore extremely high, though TSMC’s 4Q23 call also showed some flexibility, noting that certain N3 capacity could be supported by N5 tools through conversion. (TSMC 2019 Annual Report, Letter to Shareholders; TSMC 5 nm/HPC/mobile technology pages; TSMC 2024 Annual Report; The Register, November 2024; TSMC 4Q23 earnings-call transcript). citeturn19search12turn19search1turn19search4turn19search6turn18view1turn18view3turn18view4turn20search13turn20search10turn21search6

Samsung Foundry is deeply EUV-dependent from 7 nm onward, but the dependency is mediated by weaker customer traction and more variable execution. Samsung’s logic-node page says SF7 introduced EUV in 2019, while SF5, SF4, and SF3 are all explicitly tagged as EUV nodes, with SF3 described as GAA plus EUV. Samsung’s official foundry-forum roadmap puts first-generation SF2 mass production for mobile in 2025, then HPC in 2026 and automotive in 2027, with SF2Z following in 2027. What is missing is publicly disclosed scanner count. Samsung does not appear to publish a TSMC-style “share of installed base” indicator, and no reliable official figure for total installed EUV tools by node was identified. Sensitivity to EUV supply exists, but recent evidence suggests Samsung’s more immediate bottleneck has often been customer demand, ramp confidence, and yield rather than absolute tool availability: Reuters reported in October 2024 that Samsung had delayed accepting some ASML EUV equipment for its Taylor, Texas fab because the fab lacked major customers. That episode matters analytically because it shows that “EUV dependency” and “EUV currently binding” are not the same thing. Samsung depends on ASML EUV for SF4/SF3/SF2, but in 2024-2025 that dependence did not always translate into an immediate tool-shortage constraint. (Samsung Foundry logic-node page; Samsung Foundry Forum 2023 and 2024 announcements; Reuters, October 18, 2024). citeturn25view0turn23view0turn26search2turn26search1turn22news30

Intel Foundry is similarly dependent on ASML, but with a different timing profile between low-NA and High NA EUV. Intel’s foundry process page says Intel 18A is ready for full product design starts and that Intel 14A will use “Industry First High Numerical Aperture (High NA) EUV.” Intel’s April 2024 press materials confirm that it received and assembled the industry’s first High NA EUV system, and a later Intel community post reported acceptance testing on the EXE:5200B in December 2025. Intel’s product announcements further show that Intel 18A had moved from roadmap to product reality by January 2026 with the launch of the first AI PC platform built on 18A. Public low-NA EUV tool counts, however, remain undisclosed. The hard disclosed count is at least one EXE:5000 and one EXE:5200B High NA system for development/industrialization, while the broader 18A low-NA fleet remains opaque. Intel’s sensitivity to ASML is therefore straightforward: Intel 18A requires low-NA EUV; Intel 14A is explicitly tied to High NA EUV; and Intel’s attempt to differentiate at 14A depends on being earlier to High NA in production than TSMC or Samsung. (Intel foundry process page; Intel High NA press kit; Intel community post, December 2025; Intel CES 2026 and Panther Lake announcements). citeturn29view0turn30view0turn31search9turn32search3turn32search2

SMIC sits outside the ASML EUV chain altogether, which is precisely why it sits in a different competitive bucket. TechInsights’ analysis of SMIC’s 7 nm work noted that 7 nm is possible without EUV but at the cost of much higher lithographic complexity, and its follow-on analysis made the same point about next-generation nodes: DUV can keep the door open, but not on the same economic terms. Reuters’ June 2025 report that Huawei’s new laptop used a chip on SMIC’s older 7 nm-class N+2 process rather than the expected N+3 5 nm-equivalent process is important because it suggests the absence of EUV remains a meaningful brake on commercial scaling below 7 nm. Installed EUV tools at SMIC are therefore effectively zero, and its sensitivity to EUV supply constraints is absolute in the sense that it cannot buy its way into the frontier under current controls. (TechInsights; Reuters, June 23, 2025). citeturn33search1turn33search4turn33news32

## ASML's own upstream dependencies

ASML’s monopoly does not mean self-sufficiency. The single most important upstream chokepoint is entity["company","Carl Zeiss SMT","optics supplier oberkochen germany"]. ASML’s SEC-filed disclosures describe Carl Zeiss SMT as its single supplier of optical columns, and older filings state directly that ASML is Zeiss’s single customer for those columns. A later filing note states that ASML owns a 24.9 percent equity interest in Carl Zeiss SMT Holding, and a 2026 search snippet from ASML’s 2025 annual report describes Carl Zeiss SMT as the sole supplier of lenses, mirrors, illuminators, collectors, and other critical optical components. In practical terms, the ASML chokepoint rests on a smaller optics chokepoint beneath it. (ASML 2020 Annual Report; ASML 2022 Annual Report note on investments in associates; ASML 2025 annual report search snippet). citeturn43search17turn43search7turn44search0

ASML’s broader supplier network is large in count but concentrated in spend. Its 2024 annual report says the company had 5,150 suppliers, distributed roughly as 1,600 in the Netherlands, 750 in the rest of EMEA, 1,400 in North America, and 1,400 in Asia. Yet the same report says the top 35 suppliers account for 80 percent of total sourcing spend. That combination of broad vendor count and narrow spend concentration is exactly what a chokepoint analyst should expect in a hyper-complex capital-equipment system: supplier diversification at the long tail, but strategic dependence at the core. (ASML 2024 Annual Report, p. 7 and supplier section). citeturn42view0turn42view1

The source side is somewhat different. Because the EUV source capability sits inside ASML through the former Cymer organization, ASML’s source exposure is less a problem of merchant-vendor control than one of continuing power, yield, uptime, and subsystem industrialization. Older Cymer technical literature underscores how source power, conversion efficiency, collection efficiency, and control systems remain highly specialized engineering problems. Put differently, optics are the obvious external choke, while source performance is the more internalized but still deeply nontrivial one. That is why ASML’s disclosures repeatedly present the company not as a vertically complete manufacturer of everything that matters, but as an integrator sitting atop a narrow band of strategic modules and technology partners. (Cymer SPIE paper on LPP EUV source; ASML 2024 Annual Report, “How we innovate”). citeturn34search8turn42view1

Geographically, the supply chain is concentrated in allied industrial regions. The Netherlands and Germany matter disproportionately because of system integration and optics; the United States matters because of the source, key modules, and a large supplier base; and East Asia matters because that is where many customers, service bases, and manufacturing interfaces sit. ASML’s own customer-support footprint spans China, Japan, Malaysia, Singapore, South Korea, Taiwan, the U.S., and multiple European locations. The result is not a single-country dependency but a tightly coupled transatlantic-plus-allied-Asia network. That lowers the probability of a single-point geopolitical failure, but it does not eliminate chokepoints; it relocates them into a handful of firms and jurisdictions. (ASML 2024 Annual Report, customer support and supplier facts). citeturn39view0turn39view1turn42view0

## Capacity and constraint dynamics

The current capacity picture is tight but improving. ASML closed 2025 with €32.7 billion of revenue, 48 EUV systems sold including High NA, and a backlog of €38.8 billion, of which a Q4 2025 management transcript identified €25.5 billion as EUV backlog. In Q1 2026, ASML raised its full-year outlook to €36-40 billion after customers increased their short- and medium-term demand expectations, and Reuters reported that the company aimed to ship 60 low-NA EUV tools in 2026 and had capacity for 80 in 2027. This does not describe unconstrained abundance; it describes a supplier scaling output into still-strong demand. (ASML Q4 2025 results and investor-call transcript; ASML Q1 2026 press release; Reuters, April 15, 2026). citeturn49search0turn49search2turn49search3turn47view0turn48news27

The immediate cycle has shifted sharply across time. In 2024, ASML recognized revenue on 44 EUV systems, down from 53 in 2023, and its annual report described 2024 as a transition year in which lower demand from key customers was offset by China shipments from an earlier backlog. The same report also said “several fab push-outs” had occurred because AI was not benefiting all customers equally in the short term, and that competitive foundry dynamics were influencing lithography timing. That is a materially different state of the world from the “everyone wants every frontier tool immediately” narrative often imposed on the sector. In 2024, EUV remained strategically scarce, but it was not uniformly the active bottleneck for every customer. (ASML 2024 Annual Report, pp. 54-57 and CEO/CFO commentary). citeturn35view4turn35view5turn40view1turn37view0

By 2025-2026, the balance swung back toward AI-led tightening. ASML’s Q1 2026 statement said demand for chips was outpacing supply and that customers were accelerating capacity-expansion plans for 2026 and beyond. Reuters separately linked the step-up in ASML’s outlook to surging AI demand. In parallel, TSMC’s April 2026 results were read by Reuters as evidence that constrained advanced AI-chip capacity was supporting the firm’s pricing and growth. The implication is not that EUV is the only bottleneck in the AI stack; advanced packaging and HBM remain critical. But by 2026, EUV tool supply again looks like a live limiting factor for how quickly frontier logic capacity can expand. (ASML Q1 2026 press release; Reuters, April 15-16, 2026). citeturn47view0turn50news28turn41search9

The best way to answer whether EUV is a “binding constraint” is therefore conditional. For TSMC’s N3/N2/A16 path and Intel’s 18A/14A path, yes: the frontier node roadmap is inseparable from ASML allocation, installation, and performance. For Samsung in 2024-2025, the Reuters reporting on delayed Taylor-fab EUV deliveries shows that customer demand and fab timing could temporarily dominate the supply equation. For SMIC, the binding constraint is even blunter: the absence of EUV access forces DUV workarounds that appear to cap economics and scale. The broad trend from 2020 through 2026 is thus a move from pandemic-era equipment scarcity, to uneven digestion and push-outs, and then back to AI-driven tightening, rather than a single monotonic shortage story. (Reuters, October 18, 2024; ASML 2024 Annual Report; Reuters, April 15, 2026; TechInsights). citeturn22news30turn40view1turn50news28turn33search1

## Geopolitical and export control dynamics

On EUV exports to China, the policy regime is already close to maximal restriction. ASML stated in 2023 that its EUV systems were already controlled by the Dutch government, and the Dutch government’s September 6, 2024 notice expanded national authorization requirements for additional advanced semiconductor-manufacturing equipment, especially immersion DUV. ASML’s 2024 annual report states that EUV sales are subject to license requirements and details how Dutch controls were extended to additional immersion DUV models in September 2024. In other words, there is no realistic legal pathway today for unrestricted EUV deliveries into China. (ASML statement, June 30, 2023; Government of the Netherlands, September 6, 2024; ASML 2024 Annual Report). citeturn50search4turn50search3turn38view0

The impact on ASML’s revenue mix has nevertheless been more nuanced than a simple “China disappears” narrative suggests. ASML’s 2024 report says China shipments were supported by a backlog built over previous years and that the company expected more normalized China sales thereafter. Reuters reported in April 2026 that China accounted for 33 percent of ASML’s sales in 2025 and that ASML expected roughly 20 percent of 2026 sales from China. That seemingly paradoxical outcome reflects the fact that older systems and some DUV tools remained sellable under licenses, and that China was buying heavily where it still could. The relevant point for the chokepoint thesis is that China’s strong revenue contribution did not mean it had broken the EUV barrier; it meant China was stockpiling around the barrier. (ASML 2024 Annual Report; Reuters, April 3 and April 15, 2026). citeturn35view4turn50search2turn50news28

The “gray zone” in 2026 is therefore less about covert EUV shipments than about DUV immersion and servicing of already-installed equipment. Reuters reported in April 2026 that proposed U.S. legislation would broaden pressure for countrywide restrictions on ASML immersion DUV exports and could introduce new licensing requirements around servicing restricted Chinese fabs; a later Reuters update said a scaled-back version of that bill still contained a blanket ban on ASML DUV immersion machines to China and tighter service provisions for named firms such as SMIC. No public evidence was identified of lawful EUV tool deliveries to Chinese fabs after controls tightened. The active policy frontier is instead whether the West will move from “no EUV” to “less DUV and less service.” (Reuters, April 3, April 7, and April 16, 2026). citeturn50search2turn50search5turn50news27

Over the next 12-18 months, the most plausible evolution is incremental tightening around DUV immersion and service rather than any dramatic new change in EUV policy, because EUV is already effectively blocked. That creates a two-speed geopolitical dynamic. The first speed is static: China still cannot buy the frontier scanner it needs. The second speed is dynamic: the West is still debating how far to squeeze the workaround path that runs through immersion DUV, spare parts, and field service. That matters because SMIC’s present 7 nm-class capability appears to depend precisely on the workaround path. (ASML 2024 Annual Report; Reuters, April 2026; TechInsights). citeturn38view0turn50news27turn33search1

## Relationship to TSMC's CoWoS and COUPE packaging roadmap

EUV constrains advanced packaging primarily upstream, not directly inside the package process itself. TSMC’s annual report says demand was driven by both leading-edge logic and advanced packaging, and its CoWoS materials describe the platform as a package technology that integrates logic chiplets and HBM on a silicon interposer. The public descriptions emphasize interposers, TSVs, deep trench capacitors, and package-level integration; they do not describe EUV as a defining element of CoWoS fabrication. The most defensible reading is therefore that the ASML dependency enters CoWoS through the advanced logic die being packaged, while the CoWoS platform creates a separate packaging bottleneck of its own. (TSMC 2024 Annual Report; TSMC CoWoS page). citeturn18view1turn18view2turn51search1turn51search6

That distinction is crucial for AI systems. A frontier AI accelerator can be blocked either because the compute die cannot be produced at N5/N3/N2-class nodes without ASML EUV, or because the package/interposer/HBM stack cannot be assembled fast enough even after the die exists. TSMC’s own language in 2024 that it was expanding 3 nm, 2 nm, and CoWoS capacity across several locations makes clear that logic and packaging must both scale together. The chokepoint does not disappear when the wafer leaves the lithography bay; it changes form. (TSMC 2024 Annual Report; Reuters on TSMC’s constrained advanced AI capacity, April 2026). citeturn18view2turn41search9

That same logic carries into COUPE. Public TSMC-related materials describe COUPE as an integration approach using SoIC-X and CoWoS/3D Fabric to bring electrical and photonic dies together at wafer/package level. TrendForce described the roadmap as moving from pluggables in 2025 to CoWoS-based CPO integration in 2026, while TSMC research materials say the COUPE scheme is intended for wafer-level system integration in HPC. That implies a layered dependency chain for co-packaged optics: the package architecture itself is a packaging-and-photonics problem, but the electrical interface die, switch die, or accelerator die inside the module still inherits the same ASML exposure as the rest of advanced logic. (TrendForce, April 1, 2026; TSMC Research on COUPE; SEMI slide summarizing TSMC COUPE integration). citeturn51search0turn51search14turn51search5

It is therefore inaccurate to say that “EUV constrains CoWoS” in the same direct sense that EUV constrains N3 or N2. CoWoS and COUPE are better treated as parallel bottlenecks stacked atop EUV-dependent logic. They amplify ASML’s importance indirectly because the package can only create value if the packaged dies exist and are competitive. Reuters’ March 2026 report that ASML is exploring advanced-packaging lithography beyond its traditional EUV domain is revealing in this regard: ASML itself appears to recognize that more value is migrating downstream into package-level patterning and integration. (Reuters, March 2, 2026). citeturn52search16

## Financial structure

ASML’s financial structure shows a business still dominated by system sales but with a rising, strategically important installed-base annuity. In 2024, ASML reported €28.3 billion of total net sales, of which €21.8 billion was net system sales and €6.5 billion was net service and field-option sales; gross margin was 51.3 percent and R&D spending was €4.3 billion. In 2025, sales rose to €32.7 billion, gross margin improved to 52.8 percent, and installed-base-management sales rose to €8.2 billion. That is significant because it means the monopoly is not just selling irreplaceable frontier boxes; it is also monetizing the lifetime service, upgrade, and productivity path of the installed fleet. (ASML 2024 Annual Report, pp. 56-57; ASML Q4 2025 results and investor-call transcript). citeturn37view0turn42view0turn49search0turn49search2

The product mix is still heavily tied to EUV and DUV systems, with clear signs that EUV carries disproportionate strategic weight. Management said in the 2025 investor-call transcript that EUV system sales, including High NA, were €11.6 billion in 2025, up 39 percent from 2024, while DUV system sales were €12.0 billion. Metrology and inspection remained much smaller at €825 million. In other words, DUV still matters as a large business line, but EUV is the technology franchise that increasingly drives the long-duration growth narrative, while service monetizes both. (ASML Q4 2025 investor-call transcript). citeturn49search2

Margin behavior also fits the chokepoint interpretation. ASML said 2024 gross margin was held back by the dilutive impact of first High NA deliveries, though offset by installed-base growth; by 2025, gross margin had risen to 52.8 percent. In Q1 2026, installed-base-management sales reached €2.488 billion on €8.767 billion of total sales, reinforcing that the service mix is becoming more important as the fleet scales. This is the financial expression of an infrastructure monopoly: heavy R&D and industrialization costs up front, followed by growing recurring economics as the customer base becomes locked into uptime, upgrades, and performance tuning. (ASML 2024 Annual Report; ASML Q1 2026 press release; ASML Q4 2025 results). citeturn40view1turn47view0turn49search0

Customer concentration is real, but named percentages are not publicly disclosed by ASML. Fitch identified ASML’s three largest customers as TSMC, Intel, and Samsung. ASML’s own 2024 geographic data show €22.4 billion of its €28.3 billion revenue came from Asia, which is not a customer breakdown but does indicate where the system intensity remains centered. Combining that with TSMC’s own statement that it represented 56 percent of global EUV installed base in 2023 strongly suggests that TSMC is the single largest downstream economic counterparty in the EUV ecosystem, though an exact annual revenue percentage could not be validated from public primary disclosure. China’s temporarily large sales contribution in 2025 should not be misread as diversified frontier-customer concentration; it mostly reflects permitted non-EUV shipments into a closed market trying to pre-buy what remained accessible. (Fitch, April 5, 2023; ASML 2024 Annual Report; The Register/TSMC statement; Reuters, April 3, 2026). citeturn41search10turn40view1turn20search13turn50search2

R&D intensity remains unusually high for a capital-equipment company, which is precisely what monopoly maintenance at the frontier requires. ASML spent €4.3 billion on R&D in 2024 and €4.7 billion in 2025, roughly 14-15 percent of sales. At the same time, 2024 free cash flow was €9.1 billion and 2025 free cash flow was about €11.0 billion. That combination of high R&D burden and strong cash generation is what allows ASML to co-invest with suppliers, absorb long product cycles such as High NA, and still return cash to shareholders without compromising the technology roadmap. ## Synthesis and documentation

### What this means for the AI chokepoint thesis

The evidence supports a layered chokepoint view rather than a single-firm story. ASML sits beneath the foundries as an upstream gate on frontier scaling, but the power of that gate is multiplied by customer-side integration, learning curves, and packaging ecosystems. That means TSMC’s Layer 2 position is not reducible to “TSMC owns the node.” TSMC’s advantage is partly derivative of being the largest and most experienced downstream absorber of ASML EUV capacity, process learning, and service support. The scanner monopoly and the foundry execution moat reinforce one another rather than substituting for one another. (ASML EUV products page; TSMC 2024 Annual Report; The Register/TSMC statement). citeturn20search2turn18view1turn20search13

This also clarifies why foundry competition has been so asymmetric. Samsung and Intel can access ASML, but they do not inherit TSMC’s downstream advantages automatically. Samsung’s recent history shows that EUV access without customer pull and confident yields is not enough. Intel’s 18A/14A path is strategically important precisely because it tries to combine access to ASML EUV, earlier High NA adoption, and a stronger geographic proposition in the United States. But until those pieces are validated in sustained external foundry volume, TSMC’s installed-base lead and customer entrenchment still matter more than nominal node branding. (Reuters, October 18, 2024; Intel foundry process page; Intel High NA press kit). citeturn22news30turn29view0turn30view0

For AI specifically, the most important implication is that the bottleneck is now compound. Frontier accelerators require leading-edge EUV-patterned logic, advanced packaging such as CoWoS, and often scarce memory and power infrastructure. ASML therefore remains foundational, but it is no longer the only live bottleneck in system output. What makes TSMC especially powerful is that it sits at the junction of two high-value constraints at once: leadership-node wafer supply and advanced packaging capacity, with COUPE extending that logic into the silicon-photonics era. That combination makes TSMC more than a foundry and makes ASML more than an equipment vendor; together they form a coupled infrastructure layer for AI compute. (TSMC 2024 Annual Report; TSMC CoWoS/COUPE materials; ASML Q1 2026 press release). citeturn18view2turn51search1turn51search14turn47view0

China’s progress does not yet break this thesis. The public record still shows that China’s most credible path in the near term is DUV-based workaround and accelerated domestic substitution in adjacent equipment categories, not a genuine alternative to ASML EUV. That matters because it means the Western chokepoint remains intact at the exact segment most relevant to cutting-edge AI logic. The contest is not over, and the end-of-decade trajectory is uncertain. But on a 12-18 month horizon, the AI chokepoint thesis remains strengthened, not weakened: ASML still controls the frontier scanner, TSMC still controls the largest downstream installed base and packaging nexus, and competitors remain constrained either by policy, customer confidence, or both. ### Known gaps and contested claims

The largest unresolved gap is precise customer-level scanner allocation. TSMC has hinted at installed-base share, but ASML does not publish named customer percentages of revenue or exact scanner counts by customer, and Samsung and Intel are far less transparent than TSMC on fleet size. The report therefore distinguishes between hard disclosures and external reconstructions, especially when discussing TSMC’s likely 2023 tool count and current customer concentration. citeturn20search13turn20search10turn41search10

A second uncertainty is the practical competitiveness of Canon nanoimprint in advanced logic. Canon’s own materials are clearly more ambitious than the skeptical tone of specialist coverage, but public evidence of broad-volume adoption at leading-edge logic nodes remains weak. The technology should therefore be treated as credible in development, not yet credible as a near-term replacement for ASML EUV in the foundry mainstream. citeturn52search0turn52search2turn52search3

A third contested area is China’s EUV timetable. Reuters’ December 2025 reporting is the most detailed public account identified, but it is still based substantially on sourcing rather than public technical validation, and it explicitly says the prototype had not produced working chips. Any projection beyond “important progress, still pre-commercial” would therefore overstate the evidence. citeturn52search1turn52news30

Finally, the exact boundary between EUV constraints and advanced-packaging constraints remains fluid in AI systems. Public materials strongly support the conclusion that CoWoS and COUPE are primarily packaging/integration bottlenecks layered on top of EUV-dependent logic, but public disclosures are not detailed enough to map every lithography step in interposer, package-RDL, and photonics flows. The report therefore treats the “no meaningful EUV content in packaging itself” point as the most likely reading of current public evidence, not as an absolute settled fact. citeturn51search1turn51search6turn51search14turn52search16
