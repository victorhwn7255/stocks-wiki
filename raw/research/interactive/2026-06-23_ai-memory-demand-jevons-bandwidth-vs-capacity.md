# AI Memory Demand & the Jevons Question — Bandwidth vs. Capacity, 2026 vs. 2027-28

## 1. Verdict

The Jevons question does not have one answer — it has four, and the bull/bear contradiction (Reiner-Pope "memory demand is relentless" vs. Basis Points "designed to use less memory") dissolves the moment you split the noun. **On the HBM-*bandwidth* axis in the near term (2026), Jevons wins decisively and at primary-source grade**: efficiency gains (KV-cache compression, FP4, MoE) are *reinvested* into longer context, agentic loops, and test-time reasoning rather than banked — and all three makers are sold out through 2026 on multi-year contracts, with hyperscalers *raising* capex *because* of memory prices, not cutting it. The verdict weakens as you move along two axes: **commodity DRAM/NAND capacity** is the more elastic, more contestable leg (China catch-up + efficiency bite hardest here), and the **2027-2028 new-capacity wave** is where the honest bear case lives — the makers are funding the very fabs that could end their own shortage, and the historical base rate ("every memory cycle ends the same") has not been repealed, only higher-floored by the HBM oligopoly. The cleanest framing for the vault: it is not "less memory," it is plausibly *"less of the oligopoly-controlled bandwidth memory, more cheap capacity memory."* MU sits on the protected bandwidth axis; SNDK rides the elastic capacity swing — but both reads are now genuinely two-sided (HBF can route bandwidth-adjacent value back to NAND; MU's commodity-DRAM leg shares SNDK's China exposure). Treat the durable-chokepoint call as **near-term high confidence / 2027-28 medium-and-contested**, not a flat secular win.

## 2. The value chain (downstream → upstream)

- **Demand backbone (downstream):** Hyperscalers — GOOGL / MSFT / AMZN / META — ~$725B combined 2026 capex, +77% YoY [confirmed]. MSFT attributed ~$25B of 2026 capex to component/hardware pricing. Capex is rising *on* memory prices, not falling on efficiency.
- **Token-volume multiplier:** Reasoning + agentic workloads. NVIDIA output-tokens/question >5×/yr since H2 2024 [confirmed]; Goldman 24× token consumption by 2030 [confirmed]; agentic coding ~1,000× a code-chat query (Microsoft Research, input-token-driven) [confirmed, re-attributed from Fortune to MSR].
- **System/accelerator layer:** NVIDIA (Rubin/Rubin CPX/Vera, disaggregated prefill-decode, NVLink pooling, Groq SRAM license), AMD/ARM/INTC as HBM-*buyers*. Per-rack HBM rises (NVL144 CPX: 100TB / 1.7 PB/s [confirmed]); disaggregation *relocates* memory tiers, does not eliminate silicon.
- **The chokepoint (upstream):** HBM oligopoly — SK Hynix (~62% share), Micron, Samsung. HBM4 base die fabbed by **TSMC**. Sold out through 2026; ~50-66% of key-customer demand met [confirmed, Micron].
- **Commodity tier:** DRAM/NAND capacity — MU (HBM + DRAM), SNDK (pure NAND). The elastic axis. China entrants CXMT (DRAM) + YMTC (NAND, ~13% share, level with MU/SNDK) ramping into 2027-28.
- **Substitution tail (new):** HBF (High-Bandwidth Flash, SanDisk + SK Hynix, launched Feb 25 2026) + SRAM-only inference (Groq) + CXL/NVMe tiered KV-offload.

## 3. Analysis by angle

**The physics that make the bandwidth axis inelastic (Angles 1, 11).** One HBM bit consumes ~3-4× the wafer area of DDR5 per GB [confirmed; sources split 3× Tom's Hardware / 4× TrendForce — use ~3-4×]. The load-bearing datapoint is the wafer-vs-bit gap: HBM is **~18/22/30% of DRAM wafer input but only ~8/9/13% of bit supply** across end-2025/26/27 [confirmed verbatim, TrendForce 2026-06-02] — that gap *is* the cannibalization, and it tightens commodity DRAM independent of any efficiency gain. *[unverified: the "~93% of output shifted to HBM," the "5%→6%→9% widening shortfall," and "one-third of DRAM wafer by 2027" figures are NOT in their cited sources — direction holds, specific numbers do not.]*

**KV-cache compression is real but a step-down, not a recurring deflation (Angle 2).** Per-token KV footprint fell ~100× from 2017 (MHA) to 2025 (MLA + quantization) [verified, Alderson]. DeepSeek-V3 MLA stores ~70.3 KB/token vs 327.7 (Qwen) / 516.1 (LLaMA-3.1-405B) GQA peers — **4.66-7.28× over GQA** [verified, arXiv 2505.09343 Table 1; the original "7-14× over MHA" had no basis — retired]. Crucially, compression eases **both** bandwidth and capacity (decode is bandwidth-bound; shrinking the cache cuts re-reads) — but the gains are largely already banked (GQA is default), while context × agents × reasoning-tokens keep growing linearly. *Tiered KV-offload (Mooncake: 525% throughput, 59-498% capacity [verified]) is the cleaner disconfirmer — it routes demand off HBM onto DRAM/NAND.* *[unverified/retired: Mooncake "87% GPU reduction."]*

**FP4/FP8 quantization cuts bytes but the bandwidth wall stays binding (Angle 3).** NVFP4 cuts model footprint ~3.5× vs FP16 / ~1.8× vs FP8; KV-cache ~50% vs FP8 with <1% accuracy loss, doubling context/batch [all verified, NVIDIA]. Decode trends *more* bandwidth-bound (XQuant, verified). **But [contested]:** the prefill / high-batch / long-sequence regime IS compute-bound (FlattenQuant, verified) — and that is exactly where 2027-28 volume lives. Honest read: FlattenQuant (prefill, compute-bound) and XQuant (decode, bandwidth-bound) describe different phases, not a contradiction.

**MoE sparsity — capacity up, bandwidth conditional on batch (Angle 4).** MoE decouples compute (active params, e.g. DeepSeek-V3 5.5%) from footprint (total params, all resident in HBM → 4-14× dense memory). Batch-1 cuts bandwidth ~18× (DeepSeek-R1 18,901→1,040 GB/s [verified]), **but at production batch all experts must be loaded** — "batching forces activation of all experts" (Lynx [verified]) — and SSD offload carries a verified **~12× energy penalty**, capping it to edge/latency-tolerant serving. *[unverified: "batch-8 fully activates 64 experts" and "Mixtral 7.63/8 at batch ~57" — not in cited sources; the nearest measured figure (Qwen1.5-MoE 46.79% at batch 8) runs the other way.]*

**System-design memory-leaning relocates, doesn't reduce (Angles 5, 7).** Rubin CPX targeted prefill at GDDR7 (128GB, ~1.8-2.0 TB/s) not HBM — the genuine bear datapoint — but per-rack aggregate HBM rises (NVL144 CPX 100TB / 1.7 PB/s [confirmed]). *[contested/stale: the GDDR7→HBM "reversion" is market speculation, not a confirmed channel-check; CPX may even be cancelled — single Tier-3 source NVIDIA's own marketing contradicts. The Jukan/X reversion claim is Tier-5, signal-only.]* **SRAM-only inference (Groq LPU ~80 TB/s on-chip vs ~8 TB/s off-chip HBM, ~10× [corroborated]) is a real architectural threat** — and the "reportedly developing SRAM chip for GTC 2026" rumor appears to have **resolved into an announced ~$20B NVIDIA-Groq licensing deal** *[unverified, Tier 3/4 secondary, no NVIDIA primary]*. Counter: SRAM is capacity-starved (hundreds of chips per model) → multi-tier SRAM+HBM+DRAM hierarchy that *expands* TAM, not replaces HBM.

**The demand-side volume math overwhelms efficiency — on bandwidth, near-term (Angles 6, 9).** No demand destruction in any current primary datapoint: 1Q26 DRAM revenue surged, contract prices rising, HBM sold-out. **The strongest counter-thread: DDR5 now surpasses HBM in per-wafer revenue AND profitability (TrendForce Q1 2026; Micron CEO Mehrotra on record "non-HBM margins currently higher than HBM") [confirmed]** — capacity-bound inference may route to *commodity* DRAM, not the oligopoly bandwidth. *[unverified: OpenRouter "27T / 68×" — primary disclosures put March 2026 at ~12T; "30-100× reasoning" unsourced; several RamExchange attributions ("−50% in a few quarters," "20% vs 40%+") not in cited source.]*

**HBM-maker guidance is unusually unanimous on sold-out (Angle 8).** Micron: entire CY2026 HBM booked incl. HBM4, multi-year price/volume agreements, capex guided "above $25B" [the widely-cited "$20B" is a stale Dec-2025 figure]. SK Hynix: demand "exceeds 3-year capacity," ~72% op margin, tight to 2028 (chairman flagged 2030). Samsung: "demand outpaced supply" (Oct 2025, *predating* HBM4 qualification — strengthens the read). **HBM4 re-prices UP ~60-70% over HBM3E [confirmed; original "~50%" was the floor]** — the rent re-prices generationally, it does not commoditize.

**Bit-supply vs bit-demand — the supply response is structurally late (Angle 10).** Micron HBM TAM CAGR ~40% through 2028 ($35B→~$100B) [verified verbatim]; 2026 capex going into tech/hybrid-bonding not wafers ("minimal 2026 bit-supply impact") [verified qualitatively]. *[Four figures mis-cited and corrected: the TrendForce "16% vs 35%" spread, DRAM "+58-63% 2Q26" (actually +55-60% 1Q26), the "18→30% wafer-input series" attribution, and the "10-15% capacity growth" — none in their cited articles; one falsifier (KV-cache thesis) was mis-attributed to Blocks & Files.]* The bull verdict rests on one unproven assumption: that bit-demand CAGR stays near 40% into 2028.

**China catch-up relieves capacity first, bandwidth last (Angle 13).** CXMT ~300k WSPM by end-2026, #4 DRAM maker (~7.6%); YMTC ~13% NAND, targeting 15%. **But [contested]: CXMT competes on *supply availability, not price*** (Digitimes 2026-06-08) — only ~7-10% consumer discount, not a price war — and the **April-2026 reporting shows CXMT's HBM3 timeline *slipped* ("mass production unlikely in 2026")**, pushing the China-HBM threat to ~2028+ and *strengthening* near-term HBM durability. The "as early as 2026 HBM3E" claim is the optimistic outlier, since walked back.

**HBF — the first named substitution tail on the bandwidth axis (Angles 1, 12).** SanDisk + SK Hynix launched HBF Feb 25 2026 (512GB/16-high, ~1.6 TB/s, 8-16× HBM capacity [verified]). GlobalSemiResearch: once HBF commercializes "HBM demand-growth slope is expected to flatten" [verified; re-attributed from SemiAnalysis, which does NOT contain this claim]. Latency floor (~10µs vs HBM 10-100ns) caps it to cold KV / bulk weights — complement, not replacement — but it routes bandwidth-adjacent value back to **NAND/SNDK**, partly inverting the "SNDK = pure loser" read. Samples 2H26, device early 2027 — no near-term relief.

**MU vs SNDK value-capture (Angle 15).** MU owns the durable bandwidth rent (HBM 5-6× DDR5 premium, ~60-70% HBM4 step-up, packaging-capacity-bound). SNDK rides NAND capacity; NBM contracts ($42B backlog, >$11B guarantees, "over a third of FY2027 bits" [verified]) blunt near-term downside but carry variable elements exposed to the 2027-28 China wave. *[unverified: SNDK "~32% full-year FY2026 GM" conflicts with Q3 78.4% + Q4 79-81% guidance — traces only to a Tier-3 secondary; Micron "$33.5B / ~81%" is a Tier-4 preview, not a reported result.]* **Honest caveat the original under-weighted:** MU's >80% GM is a *peak-of-cycle* print, not a through-cycle floor.
