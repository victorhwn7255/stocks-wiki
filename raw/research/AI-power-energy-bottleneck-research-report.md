# The AI Datacenter Power Stack: A Fundamental Long-Only Map of Bottlenecks, High-Conviction Names, and Variant Views

*Buy-side research note — prepared from the perspective of a concentrated, long-biased fundamental analyst covering the AI power-and-energy supply chain. Geographic focus: United States, with select European and Japanese names where exposure is structurally differentiated. Position sizing and trade construction excluded by design.*

---

## 1. Research Methodology Overview: How a Top-Tier Fundamental Fund Builds This Mosaic

Building proprietary conviction on the AI datacenter power chain is fundamentally a *physics-meets-policy-meets-permitting* exercise — far closer in style to how Lone Pine or D1 cover semis than how a generalist analyst covers utilities. The mosaic is not assembled from sell-side decks; it is assembled from primary regulatory filings, queue data, and channel work. A serious process integrates eight overlapping data streams.

**First, regulatory and reliability filings.** NERC's annual Long-Term Reliability Assessment (LTRA) and biannual Seasonal Assessments are the foundational document — the January 2026 LTRA flagged 13 of 23 North American assessment areas as elevated- or high-risk over five years, with summer peak demand growth revised up 69% to 224 GW over a decade. The State of Reliability report supplements this with backward-looking event data. PJM's Base Residual Auction parameters, Monitoring Analytics' quarterly market reports, MISO's accredited capacity studies, and ERCOT's seasonal CDR/SARA reports give specific quantitative anchors. FERC Form 1 (utility financials), Form 715 (transmission planning), and EIA Form 860/861/923 (generator inventory and operations) provide the underlying generator and load-serving-entity data.

**Second, interconnection queue forensics.** The PJM, MISO, ERCOT, CAISO, SPP, and SERC queues are public but require structured cleaning to extract genuine signal — withdrawal rates, study-cycle slippage, network-upgrade cost surprises, and queue-position vs. expected commercial operation date. As of mid-2025, PJM had processed 60% of its transition backlog under FERC Order 2023; ERCOT and MISO are running on different timetables. Tracking these queues by zone reveals the *real* cost of interconnection, often two to four times what hyperscaler corporate-development teams initially model.

**Third, utility Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) filings.** Dominion's 2024 IRP, Duke's 2025 Carolinas Resource Plan, Georgia Power's 2025 Triennial IRP Update, and AEP's resource plans are where utilities commit specific MW of gas, nuclear, and renewable adds against specific load forecasts. Cross-referencing IRP load forecasts against hyperscaler 10-K and CDP-disclosed power commitments is where a discerning analyst tests the *quality* of forecasted load — Microsoft's filed objection in the Georgia Power proceeding, arguing GPC includes data centers that have only "considered" Georgia rather than committed financially, is a textbook example of how forecast inflation can be tested.

**Fourth, hyperscaler power-commitment disclosures.** Microsoft's, Google's (Alphabet's), Meta's, Amazon's, and Oracle's 10-Ks and sustainability reports disclose contracted PPA capacity. Combining these with lease and project-level disclosures (e.g., Amazon's AWS-Talen Susquehanna campus, Microsoft's Crane/Three Mile Island PPA, Meta's Clinton PPA, Alphabet's Intersect Power equity acquisition, xAI's Memphis TVA arrangement, Oracle's 2.8 GW Bloom Energy fuel cell offtake) lets you back into how much of the implied "AI power demand" is contractually locked vs. forecasted.

**Fifth, FERC orders and rate cases.** FERC Order 2023 (interconnection reform, July 2023) and Order 1920 (long-term transmission planning, May 2024) reshape the rules of the road — but the fight over their implementation in compliance filings and the November 2024 FERC rejection of the AWS-Talen ISA expansion to 480 MW reveal where political and legal frictions actually sit. Equally important are state PUC rate cases — Virginia SCC's data-center large-load tariff, Texas PUCT's SB6 implementation, Georgia PSC, Indiana IURC, Ohio PUCO — where the rubber meets the road on cost allocation.

**Sixth, expert network calls.** A serious process aggressively uses GLG, Tegus, AlphaSights, and direct cold outreach. Highest-value targets: utility T&D engineering directors (lead-time signal on transformers, switchgear, breakers); EPC project managers at Quanta, Bechtel, Burns & McDonnell, Kiewit, MasTec, EMCOR, Comfort Systems' modular subsidiary; transformer manufacturer regional sales reps (Hitachi Energy, Siemens Energy, Prolec GE, Hyundai Electric, Hyosung); ex-NRC licensing staff for SMR throughput reality-checks; PJM/MISO/ERCOT market-design and load-forecasting staff; ex-FERC commissioners and staff; hyperscaler power procurement leads; switchgear distributor channel checks (Sonepar, Rexel, Wesco, Graybar inventory and lead-time movement).

**Seventh, channel and equipment-distributor checks.** Lead-time movement at distributors is a leading indicator that often precedes earnings prints by one to two quarters — Wesco/Anixter, Rexel, Graybar, and Sonepar give visibility on medium-voltage switchgear, busway, and conductor inventory positions. Pricing on grain-oriented electrical steel (Cleveland-Cliffs is the only US producer) and copper cathode are the upstream commodity checks for transformer and conductor cost inflation.

**Eighth, third-party data subscriptions.** Wood Mackenzie's quarterly T&D Equipment Tracker (where the 128-week power transformer / 144-week GSU lead-time figures originate), S&P Global Market Intelligence's RTO data, EnerKnol for state-level regulatory tracking, Aurora Energy Research and Brattle scenario models, and Yes Energy / Modo Energy for nodal LMP and curtailment analytics.

**Case study: Situational Awareness LP's Bloom Energy thesis.** Leopold Aschenbrenner's fund — which scaled from roughly $225 million at launch in late 2024 to a reported $5.5 billion in disclosed US equity by Q1 2026 — illustrates the methodology. Aschenbrenner's "Situational Awareness" essay argued AGI scaling would saturate compute budgets, making *power* (not chips, not models) the binding constraint. The fund's Bloom Energy position — built through 2025 in the mid-teens per share, before the April 2026 announcement that Oracle would expand its master services agreement to up to 2.8 GW (1.2 GW already contracted, on top of an already-announced framework) — captured the asymmetric payoff from a non-obvious thesis: behind-the-meter solid-oxide fuel cells could *circumvent* the grid bottleneck entirely. Bloom's reported backlog of roughly $20 billion (including a separate $2.65 billion AEP agreement and a $5 billion Brookfield financing framework) makes it the cleanest pure-play on hyperscalers giving up on grid timelines. The methodology lesson is *not* that fuel cells are the answer; it is that the highest-conviction calls in this theme come from rigorously mapping the *binding* physical and regulatory constraint and identifying the cheapest, fastest workaround. Notably, Situational Awareness exited Vistra during Q4 2025 — a contrarian signal that the merchant-IPP rerate may have already priced in the bull case.

## 2. Critical Path Mapping: Generation to Meter

The AI datacenter power critical path has five layers, each with its own bottleneck profile, lead-time vs. baseline gap, and oligopoly structure.

### 2.1 Generation

The binding physical constraint at the generation layer is *firm, dispatchable, near-term* megawatts. Wind/solar additions are not the constraint — interconnection of variable resources is the constraint. Firm generation comes from four pools, each with its own throttle:

- **Existing nuclear fleet (~95 GW US, ~90% capacity factor):** essentially zero new units; bottleneck is output-allocation contracts. The Crane (Three Mile Island Unit 1, 835 MW, Microsoft 20-year PPA), Clinton (~1.1 GW, Meta 20-year PPA), Palisades (Holtec restart), and Duane Arnold restart conversations exhaust the available restart pool. Capacity factor is structurally already high — there is no "more juice" to extract. This is why Constellation, Vistra, Talen, and PSEG trade as scarcity assets.
- **Small Modular Reactors (SMRs):** physical first-of-a-kind delivery is late this decade at earliest. NuScale received NRC approval for its 462 MW US460 design in May 2025; Oklo is targeting first Aurora deployment at Idaho National Lab in late 2027 (with COLA submitted in 2025); BWXT, X-energy, Kairos, and TerraPower's Natrium are 2028–2030+. Lead time vs. historical baseline: not directly comparable — there is no historical baseline for advanced reactors. Oligopoly: NuScale, Oklo, BWXT (TRISO fuel + small reactors for defense), GE-Hitachi BWRX-300, X-energy, TerraPower.
- **Gas turbines:** the most acute equipment bottleneck in the entire stack. GE Vernova ended 2025 with an ~80 GW backlog stretching into 2029 (booking 18 GW in Q4 2025 alone), Siemens Energy reported a record €131 billion (~US$148 billion) backlog with FY25 turbine units doubling to 194 from 100 the prior year, and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries is doubling capacity but quoting 2028–2030 delivery. Heavy-duty turbine lead times have stretched from ~2.5–3 years pre-2023 to 5–7 years today. The Big 3 control >70% of frame supply; Solar Turbines (a Caterpillar subsidiary) handles the small/aero-derivative tier and is itself capacity-constrained.
- **Behind-the-meter alternatives:** reciprocating engines (Caterpillar, Cummins, Wärtsilä), aero-derivative turbines (GE Vernova LM2500/6000, Pratt & Whitney FT8/FT4000, Mitsubishi Power MFT-8), solid oxide fuel cells (Bloom Energy), and gas-fired peakers built off pipeline laterals. Lead times for reciprocating engines are 12–24 months; Bloom can deploy fuel cells in roughly 90 days from manufacturing release. This is *the* near-term workaround for hyperscalers.

How is the market pricing it? Sell-side has caught up to gas turbine OEM scarcity (GEV has rerated dramatically), is *underpricing* SMR fuel-chain torque (HALEU constraint is more binding than design constraint), and is *overpricing* near-term SMR developer optionality (deployments are late-decade at best).

### 2.2 Transmission

The binding constraint on transmission is *time*: high-voltage transformer manufacturing slots, line permitting and right-of-way, and labor (linemen are essentially fully utilized). A new 500 kV or 765 kV line takes 8–12 years from initial planning to energization in most US jurisdictions; FERC Order 1920 and Order 2023 attempt to compress this but cannot override state siting authority.

- **Large power transformers and GSUs:** Wood Mackenzie's Q2 2025 survey put power transformer lead times at ~128 weeks (~2.5 years) and GSUs at 144 weeks (~2.8 years), with some custom orders quoted at over four years. This is up from 50–60-week historical baselines. Demand for substation-class power transformers is up ~116% since 2019; GSU demand is up 274%. The US is roughly 80% import-dependent, and announced domestic capacity expansions (Hitachi Energy's $457 million South Boston, VA plant by 2028; Siemens Energy's $150 million Charlotte, NC plant by early 2027; Eaton's $340 million Jonesville, SC plant by 2027; Hyosung's doubled US output; Pennsylvania Transformer Technology's $102.5 million Raeford expansion) total roughly $2 billion but are insufficient — Wood Mackenzie projects a 30% power transformer shortfall in 2025 narrowing to ~5% only by 2030. Oligopoly: Hitachi Energy (ABB), Siemens Energy, GE Vernova/Prolec GE, Eaton, Mitsubishi Electric, Toshiba, Hyundai Electric, Hyosung, WEG.
- **Transmission lines and right-of-way:** the binding constraint is permitting and state siting. FERC's narrow ROFR/backstop authority under Order 1977 helps but doesn't supersede state law. Oligopoly: line construction is concentrated in Quanta Services, MasTec, MYR Group, Primoris, and a handful of regional EPCs — all of which are book-to-bill positive and labor-constrained.
- **Substations and switchgear:** medium-voltage switchgear (15 kV–38 kV) lead times have moved from 12–20 weeks pre-2022 to 44+ weeks per Wood Mackenzie's Q2 2025 read. Eaton, Schneider Electric, ABB, Siemens, Hubbell, and Powell Industries dominate.

### 2.3 Distribution

Distribution is where data centers physically meter. The constraints are pad-mount transformers (three-phase pad-mount lead times have stretched from 8–16 weeks to 50+ weeks at peak), medium-voltage switchgear, and skilled electrical labor.

### 2.4 Behind-the-Meter

Behind-the-meter is the asymmetric workaround: it bypasses the interconnection queue. Hyperscalers are increasingly accepting higher levelized cost in exchange for *time-to-energization*. Bloom Energy projects 38% of data center facilities will use some form of on-site generation for primary power by 2030 (vs. 13% one year prior), with 27% running entirely on self-generated electricity. The competitive set: Bloom Energy (solid oxide fuel cells), Caterpillar (reciprocating engines and Solar Turbines aero-derivatives), Cummins (HSPI engines, hydrogen fuel cells via Accelera), Generac (industrial gensets), and a long tail of microgrid integrators.

### 2.5 Datacenter-Side: UPS, Busway, Liquid Cooling

Inside the datacenter, the constraint is *thermal density*. NVIDIA Blackwell GB200 NVL72 racks at 100+ kW require liquid cooling; future Vera Rubin racks push this to 600 kW+ and require direct-liquid or immersion cooling. Vertiv has emerged as the standard: Q4 2025 organic orders rose ~252% YoY, backlog reached $15 billion (book-to-bill of ~2.9x), and the company has co-developed reference architectures with NVIDIA. Eaton's Electrical sector is similarly capacity-constrained on busway and switchgear; Schneider Electric leads in white-space power but trails Vertiv in liquid cooling agility. Modine, Trane Technologies, Munters, and Johnson Controls round out the thermal cohort.

## 3. Bottleneck Severity Triangulation

Quantifying severity across the chain (severity = lead-time gap × concentration × demand growth × substitutability):

| Bottleneck | Lead Time Today | Historical Baseline | Key Players | Severity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Heavy-duty gas turbines (>200 MW) | 5–7 years; some reservations to 2030+ | 2.5–3 years | GE Vernova, Siemens Energy, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries (>70% global share) | **Acute** |
| Large power transformers (>100 MVA) | ~128 weeks | ~50 weeks | Hitachi Energy, Siemens Energy, GEV/Prolec GE, Eaton, Hyundai, Hyosung, WEG | **Acute** |
| Generator step-up transformers | ~144 weeks | ~50 weeks | Same as above | **Acute** |
| Medium-voltage switchgear | ~44 weeks | 12–20 weeks | Eaton, Schneider, ABB, Siemens, Hubbell, Powell | **High** |
| HALEU (advanced reactor fuel) | ~1 MTU/yr current US output (Centrus Piketon); demand ~50 MTU/yr by mid-2030s | n/a (no commercial baseline) | Centrus Energy, BWXT (deconversion), Orano, URENCO, Westinghouse, Framatome (DOE-funded) | **Severe** |
| Skilled lineworkers / nuclear operators | National lineman shortage estimated 25k–35k by 2030 | Roughly balanced pre-2020 | Quanta, MasTec, MYR, Primoris, EMCOR, Comfort Systems | **High** |
| Heavy-haul logistics for LPTs | Up to 18 months for permits; few rail cars rated for 400-ton cargo | Routine | Specialized carriers | **Moderate-to-High** |
| Liquid cooling capacity | 12–18 months for AI-optimized white-space | n/a | Vertiv, Schneider, Modine, Trane | **High but resolving** |

**Transmission permitting** is best treated as a *system* constraint rather than a single bottleneck: FERC Order 2023 is reducing interconnection-cycle slippage, FERC Order 1920 is forcing 20-year long-term planning in compliance filings due in 2025–2026, but state-level NIMBY and PUC siting authority still gates 8–12-year line construction timelines. PJM's Board approved an $11.8 billion transmission expansion plan in early 2026, which is constructive but not transformational.

**HALEU deserves extended treatment because it is the single most underappreciated chokepoint.** Centrus Energy's Piketon, Ohio American Centrifuge Plant is the only NRC-licensed US HALEU facility. As of mid-2025, it had cumulatively delivered ~920 kg to DOE under its demonstration contract — roughly one metric ton over two years, at modest demonstration scale. The DOE in January 2026 committed $2.7 billion over ten years to expand domestic enrichment, and Centrus added 12 MTU of HALEU capacity to its planned build-out following a $900 million DOE enrichment award. But TerraPower has already pushed Natrium startup from 2028 to 2030 explicitly because of HALEU. If even a fraction of announced advanced-reactor designs (Natrium, Xe-100, Aurora, Hermes, Kairos) hit their 2028–2032 deployment schedules, demand will reach tens of metric tons annually — orders of magnitude above current capability. Centrus, BWXT (TRISO fuel and HALEU deconversion), and Cameco (uranium mining) sit at the chokepoint; SMR developers do not.

## 4. Regulatory and Policy Bottlenecks

The regulatory layer is where this theme separates analytical sophisticates from generalists. Five threads to track:

**FERC Order 2023 (interconnection reform).** Final rule effective November 2023, with compliance filings extending through 2024–2025. Cluster-study replacement of serial first-come-first-served processing, study deadlines with penalty backstops, withdrawal-deposit increases, and co-location at single points of interconnection. Beneficiary: anything currently stuck in queue (renewable IPPs disproportionately), and anything with co-location — directly relevant to the behind-the-meter and on-site nuclear theses.

**FERC Order 1920 (long-term regional transmission planning).** Issued May 2024, requiring 20-year planning horizons, seven categories of long-term scenarios, and modified cost-allocation frameworks. Compliance filings began June 2025 with the first full long-term cycles starting June 2026. Order 1977 sets FERC's narrow backstop transmission siting authority. Beneficiary: transmission-focused EPCs (Quanta, MYR, MasTec) and grid OEMs (GE Vernova, Eaton, Hitachi Energy/ABB, Siemens Energy).

**PJM capacity auction price spikes.** The 2025/26 BRA cleared at $269.92/MW-day (record at the time), the 2026/27 BRA cleared at the FERC-approved $329.17/MW-day cap (with the BGE zone at $466.35 and Dominion zone at $444.26), and the 2027/28 BRA cleared at $333.44/MW-day — also at the cap, and below PJM's 20% installed reserve margin target by ~6,625 MW. Total auction cost: $14.7B → $16.1B → $16.4B in three sequential auctions. Monitoring Analytics attributes 40% of the most recent auction's cost to data centers. This created the political backlash. The PA Governor Shapiro–PJM settlement that produced the price cap is set to expire after the 2028/29 auction, and absent reinstatement Monitoring Analytics estimated the 2027/28 auction would have cleared at ~$530/MW-day (~60% higher).

**The March 4, 2026 White House Ratepayer Protection Pledge.** Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, OpenAI, Oracle, and xAI signed a non-binding pledge to "build, bring, or buy" all power needed for new data centers and to pay all power-delivery infrastructure upgrades, including paying contracted rates whether or not power is consumed. The pledge is voluntary and lacks enforcement mechanisms; critics (Abe Silverman, Latitude Media, Brian Janous) note that many state PUCs already have large-load tariffs achieving the same result, and the pledge is silent on existing data centers. Texas SB6 (signed 2025) is the harder-edged state analogue, mandating curtailment of >75 MW loads interconnected after Dec 31, 2025 during firm load shed events. Ohio, Indiana, Kansas, Michigan, and Virginia have analogous rules in flight. The investment implication is *bifurcated*: the pledge is a *positive* for behind-the-meter players (Bloom, Caterpillar, Cummins, Generac), neutral-to-positive for hyperscalers' direct-PPA suppliers (Constellation, Vistra, Talen, NextEra), and *negative* for regulated utilities that had been using socialized cost-allocation to fund hyperscaler-driven transmission rate base.

**State PUC dynamics.** Virginia SCC (Dominion's data-center large-load tariff), Texas PUCT (SB6 implementation), Georgia PSC (the December 2025 approval of Georgia Power's massive grid expansion despite Microsoft's contested forecast methodology), Indiana IURC (large-load interconnection rules), Ohio PUCO, North Carolina Utilities Commission, and Arizona ACC are the active venues. The differentiated regulated-utility opportunity is in jurisdictions that *constructively* allow rate-base growth while protecting residential ratepayers — historically Southeastern jurisdictions (GA, NC, SC, AL) score better than Mid-Atlantic.

**NRC throughput.** The ADVANCE Act (signed 2024) directs the NRC to reduce hourly fees for advanced-reactor applicants (~55% reduction effective October 2025) and consider novel licensing methods. NuScale's US600 took 3.5 years; the larger US460 took under two years. Oklo plans subsequent COLAs to reuse the first-application's safety basis. These are constructive but do not change the reality that first-of-a-kind designs still take 18–36 months of NRC review *plus* construction, putting commercial operation for AP1000s, BWRX-300s, and Aurora units in the 2030+ window for most projects.

**ERCOT curtailment economics.** Approximately 8 TWh of wind and solar were curtailed in 2024, with reports of ~9.2 TWh in 2025, concentrated in West Texas and the Panhandle where transmission to load centers is constrained. The most-curtailed solo solar and wind units lose 60% of theoretical output. The economic implication: as-produced PPAs do not pay for curtailed energy, and capture prices have fallen well below LCOE, eroding merchant renewable IPP returns. This is why co-located battery storage (charging during curtailment, discharging during peak) is structurally outperforming standalone solar (72% capture vs. 57% in H1 2025) and why the Permian Basin Reliability Plan's proposed 765 kV backbone is so important for ERCOT IPP economics.

**Merchant vs. contracted revenue stacks.** Merchant generators in PJM stack capacity payments (now $329–$333/MW-day) + energy market revenues (LMPs) + ancillary services, and have benefited from reserve scarcity. Contracted PPA generators (NextEra-Alphabet, Constellation-Microsoft, Talen-AWS) trade capacity-market upside for contracted certainty, typically at price points that index to long-dated wholesale curves. The variant to monitor is *re-contracting* dynamics: as initial AI-era PPAs expire in the 2030s, will incumbent counterparties extract real-options value, or will SMR/new-build supply have re-balanced the market?

## 5. High-Conviction Stock Categories and Names

Names below are organized by exposure type. For each, I summarize: (a) what bottleneck/asset it controls; (b) variant view vs. sell-side consensus; (c) key risks; (d) differentiation within category.

### 5.1 Existing Nuclear Fleet Operators

**Constellation Energy (CEG).** Largest pure-play US nuclear operator (~22 GW of output across 21 reactors). Controls: (a) the Three Mile Island Unit 1 / Crane restart (835 MW, 20-year Microsoft PPA, restart pulled forward to 2027), (b) the 1.1 GW Clinton plant (20-year Meta PPA), (c) operational fleet capacity factor ~94%. Variant view: sell-side has rerated the stock on the announced PPAs but underprices (i) the scarcity premium of *re-pricing* existing PPAs as they roll off — Constellation has captive negotiating leverage no SMR developer will have until at least the 2030s; (ii) the optionality of co-located SMR additions at existing nuclear sites where licensing pathways are accelerated. Risks: PJM/FERC co-location rulings (the FERC denial of the AWS-Talen ISA expansion to 480 MW in November 2024 is a precedent), hyperscaler capex moderation, and political risk to "double-counted" capacity. Differentiation: the only US name with direct 20-year contracted exposure to two of the seven hyperscalers signing the Ratepayer Protection Pledge.

**Vistra (VST).** Nuclear (Comanche Peak, ~2.4 GW) + a 25 GW gas/coal/renewable fleet, primarily merchant in ERCOT and PJM. Controls a 20-year nuclear PPA with an undisclosed hyperscaler at Comanche Peak. Variant: the merchant exposure is *more* differentiated than CEG given ERCOT/PJM scarcity-pricing. However, Situational Awareness LP exited the position in Q4 2025 — a useful contrarian signal that the easy money may be made. Risks: ERCOT market design changes under SB6, capacity-price normalization. Differentiation: highest non-CEG nuclear scarcity exposure, with merchant optionality.

**Talen Energy (TLN).** Direct AWS Susquehanna campus exposure (the original 960 MW co-location, plus the contested expansion). Variant: stock has rerated dramatically; key downside catalyst is the FERC posture on co-location ISA amendments. Risks: regulatory tail. Differentiation: cleanest co-location case study.

**Public Service Enterprise Group (PSEG, ticker PEG).** Operates Salem and Hope Creek nuclear in NJ; CEO LaRossa has publicly discussed both front-of-meter and behind-the-meter inquiries from 50–100 MW data centers. Variant: PEG trades closer to a regulated utility multiple while offering meaningful nuclear optionality — cheapest entry on the basket. Risks: NJ regulatory environment is more constrained than PA. Differentiation: regulated-utility cash flow stability with nuclear option value.

**Dominion Energy (D).** Operates four nuclear units in VA + the data-center-alley load. Variant: Dominion is *both* a generator and the regulated wires utility serving Loudoun County — a structural advantage as data-center buildout continues, but exposed to Ratepayer Protection Pledge cost-shifting. Risks: VA SCC large-load tariff design. Differentiation: only large-cap that captures *both* generation and T&D rate-base growth in the largest data-center market.

### 5.2 SMR Developers and Nuclear Fuel Chain

**Centrus Energy (LEU).** The only operational US HALEU producer. The variant view (and one of the strongest in this report): sell-side flow is concentrated in SMR-developer narratives (Oklo, NuScale), but *deployments cannot happen without fuel*. Centrus has structural pricing power as a near-monopoly Western HALEU supplier, with DOE backstop financing and a 12 MTU/year planned expansion under the recent $900 million DOE award. Risks: DOE appropriations, competition from URENCO/Orano facilities funded under DOE's October 2024 selections. Differentiation: highest revenue-per-deployed-SMR-megawatt economics in the basket, lowest competing supply.

**BWX Technologies (BWXT).** TRISO fuel fabrication, HALEU deconversion, Naval Reactors fuel, microreactor (Project Pele). Variant: BWXT is the highest-quality compounder in the SMR fuel chain — defense Navy revenue is a stable anchor, and TRISO fuel is required for X-energy's Xe-100 (Amazon-funded), Kairos (Google-aligned), and microreactor designs. Risks: Naval contract concentration, HALEU pace-of-build risk. Differentiation: only public US TRISO fuel producer; defense moat.

**Oklo (OKLO).** Aurora 75 MWe fast reactor, Idaho National Lab first deployment late 2027 target, COLA submitted 2025. Variant: stock has high option value but limited quantitative anchor — the variant call is to *underweight* relative to sell-side enthusiasm, not because Oklo's tech is wrong but because (i) NRC review remains the gating risk, and (ii) the fuel-chain bottleneck (HALEU) is not solved. Risks: licensing timeline slippage, dilution. Differentiation: Equinix data-center commercial agreement; vertically integrated build-own-operate model.

**NuScale Power (SMR).** Only US company with NRC-approved SMR design (US600 in 2020, US460 May 2025). Variant: NRC approval is necessary but not sufficient — a customer must apply for combined construction-and-operating license. NuScale's relationship with ENTRA1 Energy and recent commercial announcements need to translate into financed projects. Risks: capital intensity, FOAK execution. Differentiation: regulatory moat (the only NRC-stamped design at scale).

**Cameco (CCJ).** Tier-1 uranium mining, 49% Westinghouse stake (with Brookfield Renewable). Variant: Cameco is the *only* way to play the upstream physical uranium tightening and the Westinghouse AP1000 / AP300 deployment cycle. Sprott Physical Uranium Trust gives more direct yellowcake exposure, but Cameco offers operational leverage plus the Westinghouse fuel-cycle franchise. Risks: project execution at McArthur River/Cigar Lake, geopolitics. Differentiation: integrated mine-to-fuel-to-reactor exposure.

### 5.3 Regulated Utilities with Hyperscaler Load Growth

**Duke Energy (DUK).** Carolinas data-center alley (Microsoft, Google, Apple in NC/SC), 2025 Carolinas Resource Plan, large-load tariffs. Variant: Duke is the *quality* regulated utility in the Southeast rotation thesis, with constructive PSC regimes and visible rate-base growth. Risks: Carolinas Resource Plan cost-recovery contestation. Differentiation: Southeast (lower NIMBY, faster siting) vs. PJM peers.

**Southern Company (SO).** Georgia Power = Atlanta data-center hub; Plant Vogtle Units 3&4 (2.2 GW combined) in commercial operation. Variant: Vogtle is *uniquely* delivered new nuclear baseload; despite IEEFA and Microsoft's contested critique that GPC may overestimate near-term load growth, the Georgia PSC has approved expansion. Risks: forecast inflation backlash, allowed ROE compression. Differentiation: only US utility with newly commissioned AP1000s plus first-mover status on next AP1000/AP300 builds.

**American Electric Power (AEP).** Largest US transmission system; 11-state footprint covering Ohio, Indiana, Texas, Virginia, Kentucky, Tennessee — exactly the rotation geography. AEP's announced KKR/PSP $2.82 billion 19.9% transmission-Transcos transaction (Q4 2024) supports a 5-year, $54 billion capex plan. Variant: AEP's *transmission* exposure is what differentiates it — it earns FERC-jurisdictional ROEs across the Ohio/Indiana data-center growth path, plus regulated generation in TX/KY/WV. ## 6. Variant Views vs. Consensus

Ten variant views where rigorous fundamental work yields a differentiated thesis from sell-side initiation reports:

**6.1 Transformer-OEM backlog quality is underestimated.** Sell-side models Hitachi Energy (ABB), Siemens Energy, and GEV transformer/electrification segments with single-digit price/cost expansion through 2027. The reality on the ground — Wood Mackenzie's confirmed 30% LPT shortfall, $2 billion of announced US capacity expansions only fully online 2027–2028, GOES steel volatility, and 60–70% LPT unit-cost inflation since 2020 — supports a multi-year price/cost expansion of 15–25% with no demand destruction. The market will be late to capitalize this into multiples.

**6.2 HALEU bottleneck means Centrus and BWXT have torque underappreciated by SMR-developer-focused investors.** Generalist investors funnel into Oklo, NuScale, and SMR developer narratives — but a deployed SMR fleet *requires* HALEU at scale. The DOE's $2.7B commitment is real but Centrus has structural pricing power as a near-monopoly Western enricher with NRC license, fully built-out cascade pad in Piketon, and DOE backstop. BWXT's TRISO franchise is similarly underappreciated.

**6.3 Behind-the-meter gas (CAT, CMI, Bloom) is more underpriced than nuclear given near-term deployment reality.** SMRs cannot meaningfully deploy before 2028–2032; gas turbines from the Big 3 OEMs are sold out into 2029–2030; transformers run multi-year. The only generation source that can be deployed in 2026 at scale is BTM gas + fuel cells. This is exactly Aschenbrenner's bet on Bloom and the unappreciated reason Caterpillar's E&T segment is structurally rerating. The Ratepayer Protection Pledge accelerates this further.

**6.4 PJM capacity-price normalization or political intervention is underpriced as a risk for merchant IPPs.** The PA Gov. Shapiro–PJM cap is set to expire post-2028/29 auction. Without it, Monitoring Analytics calculated the 2027/28 BRA would have cleared at ~$530/MW-day. But the political backlash — nine PJM-state governors' July 2025 letter, IL CUB and DC OPC complaints, and state-level flight risk (NJ exit conversations) — implies a meaningful probability of cap reinstatement or rule reform. Merchant generators (NRG, Vistra, AES) have priced in the high case; the variant is to discount the durability of $300+/MW-day capacity revenue.

**6.5 Northern Virginia / Texas → Southeast / Midwest rotation creates asymmetric regulated-utility opportunities.** Loudoun County water/permitting/transmission constraints and ERCOT West Texas curtailment economics are pushing data-center development into the Southeast (DUK, SO, NextEra Florida footprint) and MISO/Indiana–Ohio (AEP, AES). The under-valued names are *regulated utilities* in the receiving regions; the over-valued geographies are northern Virginia transmission infrastructure plays that are already capitalized. ConstructConnect's March 2026 data shows 56% of January 2026 data-center investment concentrated in the South.

**6.6 Liquid-cooling commoditization is later-cycle than feared.** Sell-side bear cases on Vertiv assume liquid-cooling commoditizes by 2027–2028 as Chinese ODMs and Schneider Electric scale. The variant: NVIDIA's reference-architecture lock-in for GB200 / Vera Rubin / Rubin Ultra is structurally tighter than the bears credit, and Vertiv's binding POs with advance payments through 2027 imply pricing discipline persists. Q4 2025's 252% organic order growth and 2.9x book-to-bill confirm the demand curve is steepening, not flattening.

**6.7 Gas turbine and transformer order books represent forward demand visibility that creates downside exposure if AI capex moderates.** The flip side of bullish backlog narratives: GEV, Siemens Energy, MHI, and the LPT OEMs have effectively pulled forward 5–7 years of demand. If AI training-cluster capex moderates 20–30% in 2026–2027 (a non-trivial scenario given GPU utilization and LLM economics debates), backlog cancellation/deferral risk is real. Hyperscaler "build, bring, or buy" pledges have legal weight; CFOs will not cancel turbine slots casually. But the cancellation tail is materially larger than the market currently discounts.

**6.8 Hyperscaler-vs-hyperscaler competition for the same scarce nuclear/transmission assets.** Microsoft secured TMI; Meta secured Clinton; Amazon secured Susquehanna; Alphabet secured the NextEra Duane Arnold restart; xAI is at TVA Memphis; Oracle is going Bloom Energy. The remaining restartable nuclear pool is shrinking. The variant: this scarcity is what makes existing nuclear operators (CEG, VST, TLN, PSEG, D, NextEra) trade at premium multiples — not just to current contracts, but to *re-pricing optionality* on contract roll-off.

**6.9 Stranded-capacity risk from the Ratepayer Protection Pledge.** If hyperscalers pay for power "whether they use it or not," they will rationally curtail or right-size capacity rather than continue paying for unused MW. This stranded-capacity risk is concentrated in regulated utilities that built rate-base on optimistic load forecasts (Microsoft's contestation of Georgia Power's forecast methodology is the canary). The variant: regulated utilities in jurisdictions where forecast-overrun cost-recovery is contestable face asymmetric downside.

**6.10 Curtailment economics are eroding merchant renewable IPP returns more than acknowledged.** ERCOT curtailed >8 TWh in 2024 and reportedly higher in 2025, with the most-curtailed sites losing 60% of theoretical output. As-produced PPA economics are damaged. The variant: standalone merchant solar/wind in West Texas and the SPP have negative real returns at current capture-price discounts; co-located battery (Vistra's solar+storage portfolio, NextEra's hybrid pipeline) and contracted-with-data-center co-located projects are the only economic models. Sell-side LCOE models systematically under-discount curtailment risk.

## 7. What Most Analysts Miss

The dominant sell-side narrative reduces this theme to "picks and shovels": buy the data-center REITs, the cooling OEMs, and the merchant nukes. A more rigorous analytical view surfaces several layers most generalists miss:

**Curtailment economics in ERCOT (and increasingly SPP/PJM)** systematically destroy merchant renewable IPP returns. Annual ERCOT curtailment averaged ~1.2 GW per hour in 2024; the West and Panhandle zones are structurally export-constrained. Standalone solar in West Texas captures ~57% of average locational price (vs. 72% for co-located storage). The Permian Basin Reliability Plan's 765 kV backbone is the most important under-discussed transmission project in the US — ERCOT IPP economics depend on it.

**Grid congestion pricing and locational marginal price (LMP) divergence** matters enormously for project siting and behind-the-meter economics. PJM's LMP map shows persistent congestion premia in the Dominion zone; ERCOT's nodal LMPs show 200%+ price divergence between West Texas generation and East Texas load. Behind-the-meter gas + Bloom fuel cells on-site at customer load eliminate this risk entirely — which is precisely why hyperscalers will pay 1.5–2x grid LCOE for behind-the-meter capacity.

**Merchant power revenue stack vs. contracted PPA revenue stack** is structurally misunderstood. Merchant generators stack capacity ($300+/MW-day), energy ($25–$45/MWh average), and ancillary services ($3–$10/MWh) — but bear weather and demand risk. Contracted PPA generators (Talen-AWS, CEG-MSFT, NEE-GOOGL) trade some of that upside for certainty. As capacity prices normalize (or are politically capped), merchant relative value compresses; as long-dated PPAs are signed at 2024–2025 strike prices, their economics will be *embedded* assets that re-rate.

**Political risk of capacity-price spikes triggering rate-base reform** is real and underpriced. The PJM CUB statement, the Shapiro settlement, and the Ratepayer Protection Pledge all point in the same direction: cost-allocation reform shifts cost from residential ratepayers to data centers. This is *positive* for behind-the-meter, *neutral-to-negative* for regulated utilities that had been capturing socialized rate-base growth.

**Hyperscaler-vs-hyperscaler competition** for the same scarce nuclear restarts, gas turbine slots, and Tier-1 EPC capacity creates winner-take-most dynamics. Microsoft, Amazon, and Alphabet are not symmetric competitors — they have different balance sheets, different power-procurement teams, different willingness-to-pay. Watching which hyperscalers secure scarce assets reveals procurement-team quality and is leading-indicator for AI capex effectiveness.

**Stranded-capacity risk from the Ratepayer Protection Pledge.** "Pay whether you use it or not" creates a sunk-cost incentive to keep building even when AI capex moderates — but also creates downside if specific hyperscalers consolidate or pull back, leaving stranded assets.

**Forward-demand visibility = forward downside exposure.** GEV's 80 GW backlog stretching into 2029, Siemens Energy's $148B backlog, Vertiv's $15B backlog, Comfort Systems' $11.94B backlog — these are simultaneously the bull case and the source of asymmetric downside if AI capex moderates. The market currently extrapolates linear-growth-from-here; the variant is to discount cancellation/deferral tail-risk explicitly.

## Concluding Framework

The AI datacenter power chain is structurally short on three things: firm dispatchable megawatts, manufactured-equipment slots, and skilled labor. Each is fixed in supply for 24–60 months; the demand curve is steepening, not flattening. The Ratepayer Protection Pledge formalizes hyperscaler accountability for self-funding generation and grid upgrades, which accelerates the bifurcation of investable opportunities into (a) behind-the-meter circumvention of grid timelines (Bloom, CAT, CMI), (b) scarce existing nuclear (CEG, VST, TLN, PSEG, D), (c) gas-turbine and transformer OEM scarcity rents (GEV, Siemens Energy, Hitachi Energy/ABB, Eaton), (d) the labor-and-execution oligopoly in T&D EPC (Quanta, EMCOR, Comfort Systems), and (e) the upstream fuel and metals chokepoints (Centrus, BWXT, Cameco, Freeport-McMoRan).

The investable variant view, in one sentence: *the market has rerated headline names but persistently underprices (i) the duration of the gas-turbine and transformer cycle, (ii) the fuel-chain torque relative to SMR-developer optionality, (iii) the speed and economics of behind-the-meter substitution, and (iv) the political risk to capacity-price normalization in PJM*.
