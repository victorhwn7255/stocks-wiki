# Overseas Fab Expansion

## Thesis relevance

Geographic diversification of semiconductor manufacturing has two effects on the thesis:

1. **Margin impact:** Overseas fabs dilute [[TSM]]'s gross margin by 2-4%, creating a structural headwind to margin expansion even as pricing power strengthens. This affects the Layer 2 value capture assessment.

2. **Geopolitical risk mitigation:** Expansion outside Taiwan reduces concentration risk — one of the disconfirming signals in `_thesis.md` ("macro or geopolitics dominate structural analysis"). The fact that TSMC and its customers are accepting meaningful margin dilution to diversify geography signals that geopolitical risk is taken seriously enough to price into business decisions.

## TSMC overseas footprint

### Arizona

**Ownership:** 100% TSMC-owned (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 5).

**Tier 1 status (TSM 20-F FY2025):**

- First facility entered high-volume manufacturing end of 2024 (TSM 20-F FY2025)
- Second facility under construction (TSM 20-F FY2025)
- Third facility commenced 2025 (TSM 20-F FY2025)
- Additional land parcel acquired 2026 (TSM 20-F FY2025)
- **CHIPS Act funding:** US$6.6B direct funding + up to US$5B in loans. TSMC Arizona is also eligible for a 25% advanced manufacturing investment credit on qualified investments (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 30).
- US noncurrent assets: NT$540,057M (14% of total noncurrent assets), nearly flat YoY from NT$541,836M in 2024 (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 38). The near-flat figure despite active construction likely reflects asset-in-progress timing.
- North America employees: 5,290 (up from 4,497 in 2024, from 2,668 in 2023) (TSM 20-F FY2025).

**Tier 2 updates (TSM Q1 2026 call):**

- 2nd fab: 3nm volume production scheduled H2 2027
- Jim Fontanelli (Arete) noted the Arizona site "is becoming more and more strategic" with leading-edge commitment
- C.C. Wei expressed "much more confidence than last year" in improving the Arizona cost structure, citing accumulated operational experience

Arizona is evolving from a geopolitical hedge into a strategic leading-edge site. The acquisition of a second land parcel and commencement of a third facility suggest TSMC anticipates sustained U.S. demand for domestically manufactured leading-edge chips.

### Japan (JASM)

**Ownership:** 72.6% TSMC, Sony Semiconductor Solutions <20%, DENSO >10%, Toyota (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 5). This is the most diversified JV structure among TSMC's overseas fabs — three industrial partners alongside TSMC, each bringing customer demand and automotive/sensor domain expertise.

**Tier 1 status (TSM 20-F FY2025):**

- Volume production started December 2024 (TSM 20-F FY2025)
- Japan noncurrent assets: NT$117,403M (3% of total), down from NT$126,601M in 2024 (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 38)
- Japan employees: 2,447 (up from 1,791 in 2024, from 804 in 2023) (TSM 20-F FY2025)

**Tier 2 updates (TSM Q1 2026 call):**

- Fab 1 operational, focused on CMOS image sensor applications (mature node capacity)
- Fab 2 planned for 3nm technology, volume production scheduled 2028

Japan expansion serves both the mature node strategy (Fab 1) and leading-edge diversification (Fab 2).

### Germany (ESMC)

**Ownership:** 70% TSMC, Bosch/Infineon/NXP 10% each (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 5). All three JV partners are European semiconductor companies with automotive and industrial end markets — the customer base aligns with the fab's mature node focus.

**Tier 1 status (TSM 20-F FY2025):**

- EUR5B in state aid (TSM 20-F FY2025)
- Europe noncurrent assets: NT$51,515M, up sharply from NT$19,150M in 2024 (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 38) — ESMC construction costs beginning to materialize on the balance sheet
- Europe employees: 209 (up from 78 in 2024, from 60 in 2023) (TSM 20-F FY2025)

**Tier 2 updates (TSM Q1 2026 call):**

- Focused on automotive and industrial applications (mature node capacity)
- No timeline specifics provided in the Q1 2026 call

## Margin impact

Overseas fabs are expected to dilute TSMC's gross margin (TSM Q1 2026 call):

- **Early stages:** 2-3% gross margin dilution
- **Later stages:** Widening to 3-4% as the scale of overseas expansion grows

This dilution is structural and ongoing — it does not diminish over time as overseas fabs mature. Rather, it widens as more overseas capacity comes online. The dilution reflects higher operating costs (labor, construction, utilities) in the U.S., Japan, and Europe compared to Taiwan.

For context, TSMC's Q1 2026 gross margin was 66.2% (TSM Q1 2026 call). Even with 3-4% overseas dilution fully phased in, gross margins would remain above 62% — well above the Layer 2 framework range of 40-60% and above management's long-term target of 56%+ through the cycle.

**Interaction with other margin factors:**

- N2 ramp dilution (2-3% in 2026) is temporary and diminishes as the node matures
- N3 margin improvement (crossing corporate average H2 2026) is a tailwind
- Overseas dilution is persistent and additive to any temporary ramp dilution

## Geopolitical context

The overseas expansion program exists because TSMC's customers and their governments view Taiwan concentration risk as material enough to justify margin-dilutive geographic diversification. This is a market signal about geopolitical risk that is worth more than any analyst's risk assessment — it is priced into actual business decisions.

### Revenue geography vs. production geography

The 20-F quantifies the structural divergence between where TSMC's revenue originates and where its production capacity sits. North America accounts for 75% of net revenue (NT$2,834,692M) but only 14% of noncurrent assets (NT$540,057M). Taiwan holds 80% of noncurrent assets (NT$3,102,343M) while generating only 8% of revenue (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 38 and Note 22a). This is the precise dimension on which geopolitical risk operates — any disruption to Taiwan-based production directly impacts the revenue base of TSMC's dominant market, with limited domestic manufacturing capacity in that market to absorb the shortfall. The CHIPS Act-funded Arizona expansion addresses this gap, but at current scale the production-revenue asymmetry remains structurally significant.

### Physical risk evidence

Earthquake losses provide concrete quantification of Taiwan concentration costs: April 2024 ~NT$3B and January 2025 ~NT$5.3B, both net of insurance, primarily affecting inventories, plant facilities, and machinery/equipment (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 37). These are not hypothetical risk factors — they are realized losses from Taiwan's seismic environment, reinforcing the economic rationale for geographic diversification beyond the geopolitical argument.

The Middle East situation, discussed separately in the Q1 2026 call, reinforced supply chain vulnerability concerns. TSMC noted working with the Taiwan government on energy security (LNG supply) and maintaining safety stock on specialty chemicals and gases (TSM Q1 2026 call). These are operational mitigations for Taiwan-based risks, complementary to the strategic geographic diversification.

**Mature node strategy in the geopolitical context:** TSMC is winding down Fab 2 (6-inch) and Fab 5 (8-inch, GaN focus) in Taiwan and reallocating space to leading-edge capacity (TSM Q1 2026 call). Mature node production is being partially shifted to overseas sites (JASM Fab 1 in Japan, ESMC in Germany). This suggests TSMC is concentrating its Taiwan footprint on the highest-value leading-edge production while distributing mature capacity geographically.
