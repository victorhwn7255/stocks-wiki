# Hyperscaler Custom ASIC Programs

## Thesis relevance

**Page primary scope: custom-ASIC strategic positioning across hyperscaler ecosystem.** Hyperscaler custom silicon programs are reshaping the competitive landscape across Layers 1-5 of the AI supply chain. The programs create deep, multi-year structural dependencies between hyperscalers and their silicon design partners — dependencies that are visible in the vault through an unusual financial instrument: revenue-milestone-vesting warrants.

The warrant pattern is thesis-relevant because it reveals the economic structure of custom ASIC relationships: hyperscalers exchange equity dilution for supply assurance and pricing leverage, while suppliers accept margin compression (contra-revenue from warrant amortization) in exchange for multi-year volume commitments. The warrants are structurally analogous to the [[NVDA]]-[[LITE]]/[[COHR]] equity investments ($2B each, March 2026) — revealed-preference signals of relationship depth that formal disclosures often understate.

The customer-owned tooling (COT) risk is the structural counterweight: as hyperscalers accumulate design expertise across generations, the relationship moat may erode. Both [[AVGO]] and [[MRVL]] Tier 1 filings explicitly flag this risk; both CEOs dismiss it on earnings calls — a Tier 1/Tier 2 framing gap documented on both company pages.

**Amazon warrant pattern as cross-thread observation.** Amazon warrants across [[ALAB]] / [[AAOI]] / [[FN]] are documented within this page as a cross-category observation given Amazon warrant holder consistency (Amazon NV Investment Holdings LLC is the sole named warrant holder across vault). The Amazon warrant pattern straddles custom-ASIC ([[ALAB]] Scorpio P-Series) and non-custom-ASIC ([[AAOI]] transceiver supply, [[FN]] optical contract manufacturing) categories. Warrant-pattern-only content does not warrant page renaming until 4th instance OR first non-Amazon warrant emerges (Microsoft / Google / Meta) — at which point reframing decision warranted at next codification.

**Structural observations:**

- **Amazon is the only named warrant holder.** [[ALAB]]'s warrant holder is "Amazon NV Investment Holdings LLC" (ALAB 10-K FY2025). [[AAOI]]'s is Amazon (AAOI 10-K FY2025). [[FN]]'s is "Amazon NV Investment Holdings LLC" (FN 10-Q Q2 FY2026). [[MRVL]]'s warrant holders are unnamed. The naming pattern itself is signal: Amazon's warrants are associated with product procurement and contract-manufacturing payments (transceiver supply, connectivity, optical contract manufacturing), while MRVL's are associated with custom ASIC programs where customer anonymity is standard.

- **Revenue-milestone vesting is universal.** All five warrants vest on cumulative purchase thresholds, not time. This locks in multi-year procurement volume — the hyperscaler cannot walk away without forfeiting unvested shares, and the supplier cannot lose the customer without losing the contra-revenue burden. The $6.5B vesting threshold on ALAB's new warrant implies multi-billion-dollar procurement over 7 years from a single customer.

- **Contra-revenue as margin compression.** Warrant amortization reduces recognized revenue, compressing reported gross margins. ALAB: ~2 points/quarter. AAOI: ~2.5% of warrant-customer revenue. This is the price of supply lock-in — reported margins understate the underlying economics of the product itself but overstate the margins available to the supplier after customer equity participation.

- **[[AVGO]] is the structural outlier.** AVGO discloses six custom XPU customers (four named: Google, Anthropic, Meta, OpenAI) with aggregate ~10 GW deployment by 2027 but discloses no warrant structures in either Tier 1 filing. AVGO's custom ASIC relationships are structured differently — potentially through long-term supply agreements, rack leasing, or other mechanisms rather than equity warrants. Alternatively, AVGO's market position (200G SerDes leadership, "we are the lead in CPOs," supply secured through 2028) may give it sufficient leverage to avoid granting equity. The absence of warrants at AVGO is itself analytically significant: it may indicate stronger bargaining position, different relationship structure, or simply different disclosure conventions.

## Custom ASIC competitive landscape by layer

| Layer | Company | Custom ASIC role | Key metric | Warrant/equity mechanism |
|-------|---------|-----------------|------------|-------------------------|
| Layer 1 | [[AVGO]] | XPU designer (6 customers, 4 named) | $8.4B Q1 FY2026 AI semi (+106% YoY) | None disclosed |
| Layer 3 | [[MRVL]] | XPU designer (20+ design wins) | $1.5B FY2026 custom, doubled YoY | Two warrants ($283M combined FV) |
| Layer 3 | [[ALAB]] | Connectivity/fabric for XPU clusters | $852.5M FY2025 total (+115% YoY) | Three Amazon warrants ($6.5B + prior) |
| Layer 5 | [[AAOI]] | Module supplier to XPU ecosystem | $455.7M FY2025 (+83% YoY) | Amazon warrant ($4B vesting) |

The warrant structure correlates inversely with layer position: Layer 1 (AVGO) has no warrants, Layer 3 (MRVL, ALAB) has moderate warrants, and Layer 5 (AAOI) has aggressive warrants relative to company size. This is consistent with bargaining power declining down the supply chain — lower-layer suppliers must offer more equity to secure the same relationships that higher-layer platform definers can secure through competitive advantage alone.

**AVGO vs. MRVL competitive dynamic.** The two primary custom ASIC designers compete directly at multiple hyperscalers. Murphy's emotional defensiveness ("Do you see me blinking?" — MRVL Q4 FY2026 call) and Hock Tan's combativeness ("you must be a bit hallucinating" — AVGO Q1 FY2026 call) both surface on structurally reasonable margin/competition questions, suggesting live competitive pressure at overlapping accounts. AVGO's advantage: SerDes leadership, six-customer breadth, supply secured through 2028. MRVL's advantage: Photonic Fabric (CPO for scale-up), UALink switching, broader interconnect portfolio (AEC, retimers, DCI).

**[[CSCO]] Silicon One — the "silicon diversity" alternative (S108, Q3 FY2026).** Distinct from the merchant-XPU-designer model above, Cisco's Silicon One is a vertically-integrated networking-silicon line sold *inside Cisco systems* (not as standalone merchant ASICs). Q3 FY2026 disclosed CSCO's **first named hyperscaler Silicon One design wins** — two P200-powered systems (scale-across, two different hyperscalers) + one G200-powered system (scale-out), plus a 3rd P200 win in early Q4. Robbins frames it as a "silicon diversity" play (a hyperscaler-desired second source to incumbent merchant switch silicon, i.e. [[AVGO]] Tomahawk) and as existential ("if you don't have silicon, you're gonna struggle to be relevant to the hyperscalers"); ~half of CSCO's $1.9B Q3 hyperscaler AI orders are Silicon One systems. Distinction vs the table above: CSCO competes on switching/networking silicon (the fabric *around* XPU clusters) rather than the XPU accelerators themselves, and via a captive-system revenue model (Layer 3 capability / Layer 5 revenue — see [[CSCO]] straddling tension).

## Customer-owned tooling (COT) risk

Both Layer 1 and Layer 3 custom ASIC designers flag the same structural risk in their Tier 1 filings:

- **AVGO 10-K FY2025:** Customers may "proceed with customer-owned tooling" and "develop these products themselves." New risk: "AI-driven design tools may lower traditional barriers to entry in the semiconductor industry."
- **MRVL 10-K FY2026:** "Some large customers may begin developing and making their own semiconductor solutions."

Both CEOs dismiss the risk on earnings calls — a parallel Tier 1/Tier 2 framing gap:
- Hock Tan: "Very modestly, I would say we are by far way out there, and we will not see competition in COT for many years to come" (AVGO Q1 FY2026 call).
- Murphy does not address COT directly but characterizes custom ASIC as requiring "thousands of people" and "extraordinary depth" (MRVL Q4 FY2026 call).

The AI-driven design tool risk vector is new (flagged in both AVGO and MRVL FY2025/FY2026 10-Ks) and structurally different from traditional COT — it lowers the barrier for hyperscalers to design their own silicon without the traditional multi-thousand-person team investment. Whether this accelerates the COT timeline from "many years" to "several years" is a thesis-relevant monitoring question.

## Platform-integrative absorption

[[NVDA]]'s GTC March 16, 2026 disclosures introduce a mechanism that structurally counteracts the custom ASIC competitive thesis: platform-integrative absorption. Rather than competing head-on with custom ASIC programs, NVDA absorbs the technological advantages that make custom silicon attractive — incorporating them into the platform stack where they benefit all customers rather than one.

**Observed absorption vectors (from GTC):**

- **Team acquisition:** Groq's engineering team joined NVIDIA; LP30 (third-generation LPU) integrated into NVIDIA Dynamo as a disaggregated inference accelerator, not an independent product (NVDA GTC March 16, 2026).
- **Foundry diversification:** Samsung manufactures the LP30, breaking NVDA's [[TSM]] concentration for inference-specific silicon — a capability previously available only to hyperscalers designing their own chips.
- **Software integration:** Dynamo's disaggregated inference architecture (decode on LP30, prefill/attention on Blackwell GPUs) replicates at the platform level what hyperscaler custom ASIC programs build bespoke — workload-specific silicon orchestrated through software.

**Implications for the custom ASIC thesis.** If NVDA can absorb the performance advantages of specialized architectures (low-latency decode, disaggregated inference) into the platform, the economic case for hyperscaler custom ASICs weakens. Custom programs offer workload-specific optimization; platform-integrative absorption provides similar optimization without the multi-year design cycle, thousand-person teams, or single-customer economics. This does not eliminate the custom ASIC business — hyperscalers with sufficient scale (Google TPU, Amazon Trainium) may still prefer captive silicon — but it narrows the performance gap that justifies the investment. Monitor whether Dynamo's disaggregated inference architecture gains hyperscaler adoption as a test case. See [[NVDA-platform-integration]] for the broader pattern.

### Vera CPU competitive entry vs Arm hyperscaler custom silicon

NVDA Q1 FY2027 introduced Vera CPU as a new product line with standalone economics:

- "$200 billion TAM... a market we have never addressed before" (Jensen closing remarks)
- "$20 billion in total CPU revenue this year" — standalone Vera CPU (excludes Vera-as-part-of-Rubin pairing per Jensen Q&A response to Vivek Arya)
- "Built on custom Arm cores" (Colette prepared remarks)
- "Setting us up to become the world-leading CPU supplier" (Colette)
- 1.5x faster perf/core + 2x perf/watt + 4x density/rack vs x86-based alternatives (Colette)

**Vera classification — both framings preserved per Section 2.1 heterogeneous-framing discipline (Vic Decision #9).** Vera is simultaneously:
- **NVIDIA's custom silicon for the AI host CPU role** — fits within the broader custom silicon trend (NVDA designs its own host CPU using Arm IP rather than buying Intel/AMD x86 host CPU). Platform-integrative absorption pattern extended to the host CPU layer.
- **NVIDIA competitive entry vs hyperscaler custom silicon** — Vera competes within the same Arm architectural family as the hyperscaler-internal Arm CPU silicon. AWS Graviton ($20B+ annual per ARM Q4 FY2026 letter) + Google Axion + Microsoft Cobalt all use Arm IP; Vera does too. Hyperscalers can now choose NVIDIA's Arm CPU rack-scale integrated with Rubin GPUs OR build their own Arm CPU in-house OR mix.

Both framings are honest; neither is wrong. Reader can hold both rather than collapse to one.

### Implications for the custom ASIC thesis (refined post-S84)

The existing baseline framing — hyperscalers may eventually build their own NVIDIA-replacement silicon (COT risk) — is refined by Q1 FY2027 primary substantiation. The empirical pattern is multi-modal rather than zero-sum substitution:

- **Hyperscalers ARE building their own Arm CPU silicon at scale** (Graviton $20B+ annual; Axion + Cobalt at production deployment)
- **Hyperscalers ARE deploying NVIDIA at massive scale concurrently** (Microsoft hundreds of thousands Blackwell + AWS 1M+ Blackwell+Rubin + Google A5X 960k Rubin = the largest single-customer GPU count milestones in NVDA primary substantiation history)
- **Hyperscalers ARE collaborating on networking** (AWS + Spectrum networking)
- **NVDA is entering the CPU layer competitively** (Vera $200B TAM + $20B standalone) — competing both with x86 (AMD EPYC + INTC Xeon) AND with hyperscaler internal Arm silicon (Graviton + Axion + Cobalt)

The multi-modal relationship structure is more complex than the COT risk framing alone captures. Custom ASIC programs are real and growing AT THE SAME TIME as NVIDIA infrastructure scales. The competitive dynamic is not "either NVDA wins or hyperscaler custom silicon wins" — it is "both grow concurrently with overlapping architectural choices at each layer."

### Marvell–NVIDIA NVLink Fusion — the "bridge between custom and merchant" (MRVL Q1 FY2027)

The MRVL Q1 FY2027 NVIDIA partnership adds a new wrinkle to the custom-vs-merchant dynamic. Via **NVLink Fusion**, [[MRVL]] builds custom chips and networking silicon that interface directly with NVIDIA infrastructure — management frames this as letting hyperscalers "mix and match custom and merchant capabilities," with Marvell "uniquely providing the bridge between these two architectures." NVIDIA also took a **$2.0B convertible-preferred stake** in Marvell (issued March 31, 2026; MRVL 10-Q Q1 FY2027).

This refines the custom-ASIC thesis from the opposite side of Vera: rather than custom and merchant being a zero-sum substitution, the architectures interoperate. A custom-ASIC designer (MRVL) is now both a competitor to NVIDIA (named in the MRVL 10-K) and a partner whose custom silicon plugs into the NVIDIA fabric — with NVIDIA holding equity. MRVL guides custom revenue to "more than double" in FY2028 toward a >$10B FY2029 target (~$55B TAM × ~20% share; MRVL Q1 FY2027 call), so the custom franchise grows concurrently with deepening NVIDIA integration — consistent with the "both grow concurrently" refinement above. See [[NVDA-platform-integration]] Mode 4 for the bilateral-relationship documentation and [[MRVL]] for the partnership detail.

### Microsoft Maia + Cobalt — primary-source confirmation (S112)

This reciprocally confirms the [[MRVL]] Microsoft custom-silicon partnership and substantiates the "hyperscalers ARE building their own Arm CPU silicon at scale" refinement above (Cobalt now primary, alongside Graviton + Axion). ## Signals to track

1. **Hyperscaler in-house silicon announcements.** Google already designs TPUs internally; watch for Meta, Amazon, or Microsoft announcements of fully in-house ASIC programs that bypass AVGO/MRVL.
2. **Warrant vesting milestones.** ALAB's $6.5B and AAOI's $4B thresholds are falsifiable — revenue accumulation toward these milestones will be visible in future filings.
3. **AVGO custom ASIC margin trajectory.** Rack leasing compresses gross margin (Tier 1 disclosure); monitor whether custom ASIC mix shift improves or dilutes overall semiconductor segment margins.
4. **MRVL custom ASIC margin trajectory.** Custom is lower-margin while attached products (NIC, CXL, retimer) are "more margin-rich" (MRVL Q4 FY2026 call). Monitor the ratio.
5. **AI design tool maturation.** Both companies flag this as a new risk factor. Watch for concrete hyperscaler investments in AI-assisted chip design capabilities.
6. **Warrant holder diversification.** Currently Amazon is the only named warrant holder across ALAB and AAOI. Watch for Google, Meta, or Microsoft warrant disclosures at other suppliers.
