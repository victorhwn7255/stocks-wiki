# AI datacenter photonics supply chain

This page maps the AI datacenter photonics supply chain across ten supporting infrastructure layers underneath two product architectures (near-term 800G/1.6T pluggable transceivers and next-architecture co-packaged optics). It is organized as a third analytical cut on the supply chain — complementing `frameworks.md` Framework 1 (supply chain flow) and Framework 5 (photonics exposure tier) by combining architecture-level organization with explicit investability framing.

*Anchor sources.* Three Tier 3 synthesis reports (Vic-authored, April 2026): the supply-chain taxonomy (structural backbone, footnoted with primary-source URLs), the architecture primer (investability framing and bottleneck-hierarchy analysis), and the supply-chain narrative (supplementary enrichment). Vault company pages provide primary-source cross-validation per layer where coverage exists.

*Living document.* Vault company coverage across the ten layers is asymmetric. As future primary-source ingests deepen coverage, layer treatments expand. See Future ingest candidates for the priority-ranked list of non-vault companies surfaced by systematic supply-chain mapping.

## Architecture overview

### Near-term: 800G / 1.6T pluggable transceivers

Front-panel pluggable optical modules remain the volume center of gravity. OSFP and QSFP-DD form factors plug into switch faceplates and connect GPU racks, leaf-spine fabrics, and datacenter clusters via short-reach or medium-reach fiber. The technical transition is from 100G/lane to 200G/lane PAM4: OSFP scales 400G/800G/1.6T as 8 × 50G / 8 × 100G / 8 × 200G; IEEE 802.3dj targets 200G/lane electrical and optical building blocks. Pluggables retain one operational advantage that matters at scale — serviceability. A failed module is replaced at the front panel without disturbing the switch, the optical engine, or the package.

The near-term battle is bill-of-materials competition underneath the module shell. The BOM control points are the optical DSP, the modulator/PIC choice, the laser source, the connector, and the manufacturing/test flow — each treated as a separate layer below.

### Next: CPO optical engines

Co-packaged optics moves optics close to or onto the switch ASIC package. Shorter electrical paths reduce loss and improve bandwidth density at high lane rates; the architecture exists because long PCB traces become expensive in power, equalization, and signal integrity at 200G/lane and beyond. CPO does not eliminate the supply chain — it shifts the bottleneck toward optical engines, external laser sources, detachable fiber attach, package-substrate integration, advanced thermal control, and wafer-level photonic test.

The CPO platform competition is fully covered at [[CPO-platform-battle]] (39 data points across 14 vault companies, five-way executive comparison: NVDA Spectrum-X CPO + Kyber CPO scale-up + Spectrum-6, AVGO Bailly + Davisson 102.4T, MRVL Photonic Fabric, ALAB Scorpio + aiXscale, CSCO acknowledged deferral). Murphy's bifurcation — scale-out CPO "relatively limited" while scale-up CPO "inflects in a pretty big way" — is the most consequential structural framing.

The COUPE silicon photonics packaging technology is detailed in Section 2.6 below, including the vault-conflict reconciliation on the production-status claim.

See `frameworks.md` Framework 1 for the top-down supply-chain flow view that complements this architecture-organized cut.

## Supporting photonics infrastructure

The ten layers below underpin both pluggable and CPO architectures. Architectural change reweights value across these layers rather than collapsing the chain. The investable bottlenecks sit below the architecture label, not at the label itself.

### 2.1 Lasers / external laser sources

Lasers are the most basic chokepoint: no laser, no optical link. In pluggables, lasers sit inside the module or photonic integrated circuit. In CPO, lasers may move outside the optical engine as External Laser Source (ELS) modules feeding multiple silicon photonic engines from a shared continuous-wave source. ELS architectures provide approximately 2-2.5x content gain versus standalone lasers per [[LITE]] commentary.

*Vault.* [[LITE]] (three-inch InP, 40% capacity expansion front-loaded, EML focus, 400mW UHP CW for CPO), [[COHR]] (six-inch InP device-fabrication advantage at Sherman, Järfälla, Zürich; "exceptionally large CPO purchase order" disclosure), [[AAOI]] (in-house InP/GaAs MBE+MOCVD at Sugar Land — Layer 4/5 straddling), [[AVGO]] (Breinigsville Pennsylvania InP wafer fab, sole source for fiber-optics products — narrow internal use, not merchant). [[NVDA]]'s March 2026 $2B equity in each of LITE and COHR is the platform-definer signal.

*Non-vault.* Per the taxonomy report: MACOM (CW lasers, photodiodes, TIAs, drivers for 1.6T silicon-photonics modules; 200G/lane PMDs and 448G drivers); Furukawa Electric (commercialized 100mW × 16-channel blind-mate ELS connector for CPO, March 2024); Sumitomo Electric (InP substrates and EML); Mitsubishi Electric (EML); NTT Innovative Devices (EML); Sivers Semiconductors (laser modules).

### 2.2 Modulators (EML, silicon photonics, TFLN, BTO, polymers)

Modulators convert electrical data onto light. The technology choice determines lane speed, drive voltage, optical reach, and thermal envelope. The market is bifurcating between mature platforms (EML and silicon photonics) and emerging materials (TFLN, BTO, electro-optic polymers).

EML is the incumbent for 800G and early 1.6T pluggables — compact, mature, strong for DR/FR reach. Silicon photonics is the natural CPO platform because it integrates waveguides, splitters, modulators, photodiodes, and WDM structures on a wafer-scale process. TFLN, BTO, and polymers are second-wave optionality past 3.2T per the architecture primer; commercial maturity is meaningful but earlier than the EML/SiPh ramp.

*Vault.* [[COHR]] (EML + silicon photonics; 400G/lane SiPh demo with Tower Semiconductor in production-ready process; 100G and 200G EML leadership claim, first 400G differential EML demonstrated for 3.2T-class), [[LITE]] (EML focus; coherent DSP exited as Layer 4 boundary marker), [[CSCO]] (Acacia coherent + Cisco silicon photonics in 1.6T OSFP and 800G LPO), [[AVGO]] (Taurus 400G/lane optical DSP paired with first 400G EML/photodiode parts at OFC 2026).

*Non-vault.* Per the taxonomy report and the architecture primer: HyperLight (TFLN PIC, 400G/lane chiplet platform — UMC manufacturing partnership; per architecture primer, no primary-source verification of the 400G/lane PIC announcement); OpenLight (heterogeneous III-V on silicon, 1.6T DR8 PIC integrating DFB lasers + 224G modulators + SOAs — Synopsys/Jabil heritage, private); Tower Semiconductor (PH18 SiPh platform, 400G/lane modulator demos with Coherent and OpenLight); Lumiphase (BTO emerging); Lightwave Logic and NLM Photonics (electro-optic polymers, earlier-stage); imec/Veeco (300mm-compatible BTO-on-silicon process, January 2026).

Per the architecture primer, the practical hierarchy is: EML and silicon photonics drive 2026 revenue; TFLN, BTO, and polymers remain option value on the 3.2T-and-beyond roadmap. Modulator innovation is investable only when attached to a manufacturable platform — standalone laboratory performance is insufficient.

### 2.3 Optical DSP / SerDes

Optical DSPs and SerDes are the hidden engines inside high-speed optics. They compensate for channel loss, perform equalization, support FEC, monitor link quality, and integrate drivers or gearbox functions. At 1.6T, the DSP becomes both performance enabler and power problem. The architectural partition between SerDes, retimer, and optical engine remains a core design choice rather than a solved problem; LPO (linear-drive pluggable optics) and LRO can shift equalization burden between module and host but require tighter co-design.

*Vault.* [[MRVL]] (Ara 3nm 1.6T PAM4 DSP integrating eight 200G electrical + eight 200G optical lanes; 400G/lane demonstrated; Interconnect Product of the Year February 2026), [[AVGO]] (Taurus first 400G/lane optical DSP, Tomahawk 6 / Davisson 102.4T switch ASIC, 200G SerDes leadership claim — "we are the only one out there at 1.6T DSP" per Hock Tan), [[CSCO]] (Acacia 3nm Kibo 1.6T PAM4 DSP — captive in Cisco-branded pluggables, not merchant), [[CRDO]] (Cardinal family Optical PAM4 DSPs with mature node n-1 cost advantage; AEC franchise leadership; Blue Heron 200G/lane retimer purpose-built for scale AI with UALink/Ethernet/ESUN protocol coverage; ZeroFlap optics with embedded telemetry — Session 27 paired ingest closes Chokepoint 2 third primary-source threshold per Session 25 hybrid architecture).

### 2.4 Fiber attach / connectors

In legacy pluggables, fiber connectivity is mostly a density and cleanliness problem. In CPO, it becomes a precision packaging and service problem — repeatable low-loss coupling, detachability, blind-mate alignment, and test access from wafer through final package. Per the architecture primer, detachable faceplate-to-chip fiber attach is "one of the least appreciated but most real bottlenecks in the CPO transition" — simultaneously a precision-mechanics problem, a loss-budget problem, and a field-service problem.

### 2.5 Optical circuit switches

Optical circuit switching reconfigures optical paths directly in the optical domain, reducing OEO conversions and electrical packet-switching layers. OCS is not a replacement for Ethernet switching everywhere; it is a fabric-level tool for workloads where traffic patterns can be scheduled or where topology reconfiguration improves utilization. Google Jupiter validation reports 5x higher speed and capacity, 30% lower capex, and 41% lower power, with OCS and software-defined networking as key enablers. OCP launched its OCS project in July 2025 per the taxonomy report.

*Vault.* [[COHR]] (digital liquid-crystal OCS, 64x64 and 320x320 systems, 10+ customer engagements, addressable market claim >$2B; broadening from spine/scale-out into scale-up and DCI applications), [[LITE]] (R300 300x300 MEMS OCS, backlog >$400M across three customers, OFC 2026 interoperability demo with Marvell DSPs).

*Non-vault.* Per the taxonomy report: HUBER+SUHNER POLATIS (MEMS OCS, products to 384x384, 2025 annual report cites major hyperscaler order); Calient (MEMS optical switching, private); Salience Labs (Tower Semiconductor manufacturing partnership for optical circuit switches at scale); Google (Jupiter / Apollo internal deployment validation).

OCS is a strategic layer with credible technical positioning but earlier merchant adoption than mainstream pluggables. Vault coverage is anchored at component-supplier vantage points without dedicated OCS-specialist company pages.

### 2.7 Assembly / packaging / testing

CPO is fundamentally an advanced-packaging problem disguised as an optical problem. Per OIF, microbump reliability is the primary yield challenge in advanced-package CPO. Optics is harder to manufacture than ordinary electronics because performance depends on sub-micron alignment, optical coupling loss, laser burn-in, thermal cycling, fiber cleanliness, and high-speed electrical/optical compliance. At 1.6T, a module that works on a bench is not enough; it must be manufactured at volume with acceptable yield and test-cell throughput.

### 2.8 Thermal / liquid cooling

Thermal is part of the photonics supply chain because optical performance is temperature-sensitive and AI switches are power-dense. CPO concentrates this challenge — putting optics next to hot ASICs — but the pluggable roadmap is also reshaped by thermal density. NVIDIA's Quantum-X and Spectrum-X photonics switches use liquid cooling for onboard silicon photonics. The XPO MSA targets 12.8 Tb/s liquid-cooled optics; per the architecture primer, [[COHR]]'s XPO concept supports front-panel density up to 204.8 Tb/s per OCP rack unit.

ELS architectures are partly a thermal response — moving the heat-generating laser into a more manageable thermal zone, allowing the photonic engine to operate within a stable envelope. Thermal stability is a reliability issue, not just an efficiency issue: laser efficiency and lifetime, modulator wavelength drift (~0.1 nm/°C in photonic systems), and connector contamination all interact.

## Investability framing

The architecture primer organizes the photonics supply chain into three investability buckets that crosscut the layer-organized infrastructure above. Each bucket represents where qualification spend, capex, and architectural change concentrate durable value capture. The buckets are not mutually exclusive — some companies appear at bucket boundaries — but each company has a primary canonical placement based on revenue concentration and strategic positioning.

Vault company placements are anchored on the value-capture-layer assignments codified in `frameworks.md` v7 Framework 2. Non-vault candidates appear with footnote attribution per Tier 3 source.

### How the buckets relate to layer organization

Section 2 organizes the supply chain by component layer (light → modulation → signal recovery → coupling → switching → manufacturing → thermal → materials → IP). - **Layer organization** answers: given a hyperscaler AI datacenter buildout, what physical components must be supplied?
- **Bucket organization** answers: given the supply chain, where does qualification spend and capex concentrate as the architecture evolves?

The buckets crosscut layers — Bucket A spans Sections 2.3 and 2.10; Bucket B spans Sections 2.1, 2.2, and 2.9; Bucket C spans Sections 2.4, 2.6, and 2.7.
