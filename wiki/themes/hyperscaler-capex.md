# Hyperscaler AI-datacenter CAPEX — the payer/spender tracker

*Forward edge: see [[forward-edge-tracker]] for where the vault's view diverges from what the market is pricing (power-infrastructure > compute; custom silicon → [[TSM]]) plus the catalysts and falsifiers.*

## Thesis relevance

The chokepoint thesis is downstream of one number: how much the hyperscalers spend building out AI datacenters. Their capex is the **demand source** at the top of `frameworks.md` Framework 10 (CAPEX flow) — the cash that ultimately funds every supply-side chokepoint the vault tracks (compute, memory, power, cooling, photonics, fiber). If that capex keeps growing, the constraints stay binding; if it plateaus or is cut, capacity catches up and structural positions erode. This page is the **demand-side PAYER/SPENDER tracker** — the hyperscalers' capex drawn into one comparison: what each is committing, how firm/comparable the guidance is, and the dynamics that cut across them (the four big hyperscalers + [[ORCL]] as the 5th, distinct-model payer).

As of Session 122, the program spans **six core payers + a two-neocloud rent-side pair**: the four big hyperscalers — [[MSFT]] (S112) + [[GOOGL]] (S115) + [[AMZN]] (S116) + [[META]] (S117) — plus **[[ORCL]]** (S119, the structural outlier), and the rent-side pair **[[CRWV]]** (S121) + **[[NBIS]]** (S122, Nebius — the more extreme neocloud). The ~$640-720B 2026 aggregate the thesis rests on is now anchored to the companies' own filings/calls rather than to NVDA's earlier analyst-estimate framing; the build-vs-rent split is primary-sourced on both sides; and the rent-side neocloud "canary" is now a *pair*, not a single point.

**Boundary vs [[AI-demand-durability]].** That page asks *"is AI demand durable?"* — broad, framed from the supply side (15+ companies across the chain reporting demand exceeding supply), with the hyperscaler-capex subsections as one input among many. This page asks the narrower *"what are the payers actually committing, and how firm is it?"* — the consolidated payer-side comparison + the cross-cutting capex dynamics. The per-hyperscaler figures appear on both pages by design; AI-demand-durability keeps them inside its demand-signal narrative, and this tracker is the side-by-side view they point to.

**Honest framing.** These are demand-side pages (`layer: outside`) — the hyperscalers *fund* the chokepoints; they capture no supply-side rent. This page describes the spending, not whether to buy the spenders; reported figures are paired with their caveats (guidance firmness, reported-net-income noise, the capex-ahead-of-revenue timing risk).

- **[[MSFT]]** — the largest single guide (~$190B CY2026), the deepest contracted backlog (RPO $633B), and the cleanest reported net income of the four. CFO Amy Hood addressed the capex-vs-revenue "disconnect" directly (MSFT Q3 FY2026 call).
- **[[GOOGL]]** — $180-190B (raised, partly for the Intersect Power *energy* acquisition), with 2027 capex guided to "significantly increase"; Cloud backlog $462.3B (nearly doubled QoQ). Reported net income inflated by a $36.9B equity-securities gain (GOOGL Q1 2026 10-Q).
- **[[AMZN]]** — the highest Q1 run-rate ($43.2B cash; TTM $151B) but the *only one without a clean annual dollar-guide* (Olsavsky framed 2026 qualitatively); the ~$200B-2026 figure circulating in the aggregate is a Tier-3/analyst number, not Amazon primary. AWS backlog $364B excludes a recent ">$100B" Anthropic deal.
- **[[META]]** — the smallest of the four ($125-145B) but the steepest *raise* rationale: Zuckerberg attributed it to "higher component costs, particularly memory pricing." No contract backlog (ad-driven). Reported net income inflated by a ~$5B discrete tax benefit.
- **[[ORCL]]** — the cohort's **structural outlier and percentage-growth leader**: capex $6.9B→$21.2B (+209%)→$39.2B in 9 months (+223%), AI-infrastructure revenue +243% YoY, and a **$552.6B RPO (+325%)** — the second-largest backlog (after MSFT) and the fastest-growing. Three things set it apart: it is **contracted-backlog-driven** (not ad/retail/subscription cash flow), it **buys merchant silicon** (no captive accelerator — the exception to dynamic #4), and it is **financing the build off its own balance sheet** ($50B envelope; "uncoupling CapEx from capital requirements"), so its reported capex *understates* the buildout. Net income is clean; the caveat is leverage, not an equity mark. FY2027 capex guidance deferred.

**The instructive counterpoint — Apple, the deliberate non-spender.** Not every mega-cap is racing to build. Apple guides to roughly **$14B of 2026 capex — essentially flat**, a fraction of the others (per the `ai-capex-map-2025-2027.md` Tier-3 synthesis). It runs a hybrid model — first-party "Private Cloud Compute" on its own silicon, renting third-party capacity, and pushing inference onto the device — betting that foundation models commoditize. It has no vault page (and isn't a chokepoint participant), but it is the useful negative case against the "everyone is spending, so the demand must be real" read: one of the most capable buyers looked at the same demand and chose *not* to build hyperscale.

## The aggregate

Summed, the four big hyperscalers guide to roughly **$640-720B of 2026 AI-infrastructure capex** (MSFT ~$190B + GOOGL ~$185B + AMZN ~$177-200B + META ~$135B). The milestone of this tracker: that aggregate is now **primary-sourced across all four**, where the vault previously carried only NVDA's analyst-estimate ~$700B (NVDA Q4 FY2026 call). **Honest firmness note:** three of the four are firm (a precise range or a point estimate); Amazon's component is qualitative-only, so the aggregate's softest input is the largest run-rate. The number is a guide, not a contract — the disconfirming-signals discipline below tracks whether it holds.

**[[ORCL]] *extends* the aggregate rather than cleanly summing into it** (~$50B+ of CY2026 AI-infra capex — TTM ~$48.3B and accelerating; 9-month FY2026 already $39.2B), for two honest reasons: its May-31 fiscal year means its periods don't align with the calendar-2026 four, and — uniquely — part of its build is **partner-/off-balance-sheet-financed** ("uncoupling"), so its *reported* capex understates the capacity rising in its name. Read it as pushing the cohort total toward ~$700-770B with a wider firmness band, not as a precise fifth addend.

### The wider picture (global + multi-year)

The vault's five US hyperscalers are the core, but not the whole, of global AI-datacenter capex. Tier-3 aggregators (per the `ai-capex-map-2025-2027.md` synthesis, 2026-06-03) place the wider frame as follows — useful context, treated as scenario-dependent estimates, not vault-verified figures:

- **Global 2026:** TrendForce (May 2026) puts the **top-nine cloud providers** (the US five + ByteDance, Tencent, Alibaba, Baidu) at **~$830B in 2026, +79% YoY** (with AWS alone forecast above $230B). The US-hyperscaler core is thus roughly the larger part of an ~$830B global figure.
- **Multi-year:** Goldman's "Tracking Trillions" models **~$7.6T cumulative AI capex 2026-31** (explicitly a sensitivity analysis, not a forecast — and note that shortening assumed chip life from 5 to 3 years adds ~$1T of annual depreciation); McKinsey estimates **~$6.7T of data-center capex by 2030**. The Big-Four US hyperscalers are expected to be **about half of global data-center capex by 2030**.
- **The next tier of payers** (mostly beyond this tracker's scope): Oracle's Stargate partner OpenAI (~$1.4T of multi-year compute commitments, reset toward ~$600B by 2030), the neoclouds ([[CRWV]] + [[NBIS]] — now both tracker rows), xAI, the Chinese hyperscalers, and Gulf sovereigns (HUMAIN, G42, MGX).

Honest caveat (a standing vault discipline): these aggregates use different baskets and assumptions and are not directly comparable — TrendForce's $830B (top-9 CSPs), Goldman's $765B (global 2026), and the multi-year trillions are different measurements, not one number.

## Cross-cutting dynamics

What is visible only when the four are read together:

1. **Guidance-style heterogeneity.** Four distinct disclosure styles across the seven payers: GOOGL, META, [[CRWV]] and [[NBIS]] give precise dollar ranges; MSFT gives a point estimate (~$190B CY); AMZN declines an annual figure entirely (qualitative-only); ORCL **defers** a FY2027 capex number and points instead to its $50B CY2026 *financing envelope* as the spend marker. The same "AI capex guidance" line means different things across the group — firmest for the precise-range names, softest for AMZN/ORCL.
2. **Memory-pricing cost-push.** META named memory pricing as the *dominant driver* of its capex raise ("most of that is due to higher component costs, particularly memory pricing," Zuckerberg). Some of the 2026 capex increases are cost inflation, not only incremental capacity — a cross-cutting tie to [[HBM-oligopoly]] (the same memory-pricing signal CSCO flagged as "unprecedented"). It also complicates read-through from capex dollars to physical capacity.
3. **Capex-ahead-of-revenue / ROIC framing.** All four address the timing risk in their own words: MSFT the capex-vs-revenue "disconnect" (Hood), GOOGL a "robust ROIC framework" for allocating constrained compute, AMZN "CapEx growth meaningfully outpaces revenue growth" with a 6–24-month monetization lag (Jassy), META a ~+87% capex step-up against operating income guided only "above 2025." The shared demand-durability risk is timing, not direction — capacity is being funded ahead of the revenue meant to justify it.
4. **In-house silicon as a capex lever — with three exceptions.** Four of the seven build captive accelerators to bend the capex/margin curve — AMZN Trainium ("save us tens of billions of dollars of CapEx each year"), GOOGL TPU 8t/8i, META MTIA (Broadcom), MSFT Maia. **[[ORCL]], [[CRWV]] and [[NBIS]] are the exceptions** — all merchant-NVIDIA buyers with no captive accelerator (ORCL competes on multicloud neutrality; the two neoclouds are GPU renters whose whole product *is* merchant NVIDIA capacity). The capex tracker and [[hyperscaler-custom-ASIC]] are two views of the same spend — and the three merchant-only payers route all of theirs to NVIDIA (which, per #9, part-funds two of them).
5. **Reported-net-income noise.** Three of the four had reported net income inflated by non-operating items this quarter — GOOGL (a $36.9B equity-securities gain), AMZN (a $15.6B Anthropic-stake mark-up), META (a ~$5B discrete tax benefit). Operating income is the clean read across the group; headline net income / EPS will swing with equity marks and tax discretes.
6. **Backlog as forward-demand proxy.** MSFT RPO $633B, ORCL RPO $552.6B (+325% — the fastest-growing), GOOGL Cloud backlog $462.3B, AMZN AWS backlog $364B (+>$100B Anthropic) give a contracted forward read; META has no comparable contract backlog (its demand is ad-driven, not committed cloud spend) — a structural difference in how forward demand is visible. **ORCL is the most backlog-dependent of all** — its entire AI-infra thesis rests on the RPO converting (and on its concentration; see [[ORCL]] on the OpenAI/Stargate disclosed-but-unnamed counterparty).
7. **Build vs rent.** AMZN and META both noted *renting* external cloud capacity alongside building their own (META "signing multi-year cloud deals … over 2026 and 2027"). Hyperscaler capex is not the whole picture — some demand routes to neoclouds, which are payers in their own right. The vault now has the rent side primary-sourced as a **pair**: [[CRWV]] ($31-35B 2026 capex; Microsoft ~67%) and [[NBIS]] ($20-25B; Microsoft + Meta), both spending their own capex to *provide* the capacity the hyperscalers and labs rent. They are structurally distinct, debt-and-equity-funded payers — and where Meta and Amazon *build*, the neoclouds are who they *rent from*.
8. **The financing shift — cash → debt → off-balance-sheet.** The most important dimension the company-row figures *don't* show: how the build is funded. Per the `ai-capex-map-2025-2027.md` Tier-3 synthesis, hyperscalers issued **~$121B of new bonds in 2025 (>4× the prior five-year average)**, and combined hyperscaler capex now consumes **~94% of operating cash flow** after dividends/buybacks — the self-funded model is stretching. More importantly, **off-balance-sheet structures are masking leverage**: Meta's ~$30B Hyperion data center (a special-purpose vehicle — Blue Owl owns 80%, Meta 20%), Alphabet's ~$40.7B of variable-interest-entity commitments, and Oracle's vendor financing + $72B+ of partner-debt packages. Morgan Stanley sized a **~$1.5T data-center financing gap for 2025-28**; the Bank for International Settlements flagged private-credit lending to AI growing from near zero to **>$200B**. This generalizes [[ORCL]]'s "uncoupling CapEx from capital requirements" across the cohort: **reported balance sheets increasingly understate the buildout's true leverage** — so the clean-net-income, cash-rich read of these names is becoming less complete each quarter.
9. **The circularity — suppliers funding their own demand.** A reflexive loop runs underneath the capex: **NVIDIA is investing in the customers who buy its chips** — [[CRWV]] (**$2B, primary-confirmed in CoreWeave's own 10-K** — Jan 2026, at $87.20/share) and [[NBIS]] (**$2B + a pre-funded warrant, primary-confirmed in Nebius's 20-F**), OpenAI (up to $100B), xAI — alongside Oracle/OpenAI vendor financing and chip-vendor equity stakes (the rest per the Tier-3 synthesis). Both vault neoclouds are NVIDIA-funded — the loop is now primary-confirmed on two of its legs. The loop is stable while capital is cheap and unwinds quickly if it isn't; it also means some of the "demand" validating the buildout is partly funded by the buildout's own suppliers. See [[nvidia-supply-chain-commitments]] for the bilateral-commitment detail.

## Where the capex flows (Framework 10)

The tracker is the top of the CAPEX-flow diagram; the cash routes down into the supply-side chokepoints the rest of the vault analyzes:

- **Compute** — merchant GPUs ([[NVDA]]) + captive accelerators (Trainium / TPU / MTIA / Maia; see [[hyperscaler-custom-ASIC]]).
- **Memory** — HBM into those accelerators ([[HBM-oligopoly]]) — and, per dynamic #2, the cost line currently pushing the capex *up*.
- **Power** — the real 2026 bottleneck (the binding constraint per `_thesis.md`): committed dollars are not the limit, energized megawatts are. Sightline Climate's 2026 Data Center Outlook (Tier-3, via `ai-capex-map-2025-2027.md`) finds that of **~16 GW of capacity slated for 2026 globally, only ~5 GW is actually under construction** — the rest gated by transformers, switchgear, batteries, and grid interconnection ([[transformer-supply]]); data-center cancellations quadrupled to ~25 in 2025; PJM peak demand is projected to rise from ~154 GW (2025) toward ~210 GW by 2036. This is fresh 2026 corroboration that a large share of the capex above will slip or cancel on power, not capital. The hyperscalers are responding by buying energy assets directly (GOOGL/Intersect).
- **Cooling** — direct-to-chip liquid cooling at rising rack densities ([[liquid-cooling]]).
- **Networking + fiber** — datacenter interconnect and the fiber feeding it ([[GLW]] — Meta's $6B Corning agreement is one hyperscaler-to-supplier example).

## The capex-revenue gap — the bull/bear debate

The central question the spending raises: is the demand real enough to justify it? The `ai-capex-map-2025-2027.md` Tier-3 synthesis frames both sides, and bubble warnings have moved mainstream:

- **The bear case.** Even generous AI-revenue estimates (~$50-60B/year) sit against $500B+ of annual capex — **roughly $8-10 of capex per $1 of current AI revenue**. The IMF (October 2025 Global Financial Stability Report) warned risk-asset prices are "well above fundamentals"; the Bank of England called AI-tech valuations the most stretched since the dot-com era; MIT's NANDA study ("The GenAI Divide") found **only ~5% of integrated AI pilots are extracting real value**, the rest with no measurable P&L impact. The sharpest, most falsifiable version is **Michael Burry's claim** (per the synthesis): hyperscalers are understating depreciation by extending the assumed useful life of chips/servers — he estimates ~$176B of understated depreciation 2026-28 and argues "**ORCL will overstate earnings ~26.9% and META ~20.8% by 2028**." This is attributed as Burry's claim (per the rhetorical-claims convention), not a vault finding — but it is dated, quantified, and lands on two vault primaries ([[ORCL]], [[META]]), so it is a clean falsification test to register against their future depreciation disclosures.
- **The bull case.** Every hyperscaler *reaffirmed or raised* guidance even after the January-2025 DeepSeek efficiency shock, citing Jevons-paradox demand elasticity (cheaper compute → more total compute), and all report capacity being absorbed as fast as it comes online. Goldman's house view: not yet a bubble, but profits may be only ~half of what is needed to justify the spend.

The honest read: the spending is real and primary-verified (the table above); whether the *return* shows up before the *debt* (dynamic #8) and the *power* (above) bind is the open question — tested by the disconfirming signals below. Cross-ref [[AI-demand-durability]] (is the demand durable?) and [[software-AI-moat-durability]] (is it monetizing at the application layer?).

## Disconfirming signals

What would weaken the demand-side thesis this page tracks:

1. **A capex guidance cut or plateau** — any of the four lowering or freezing its FY2026/FY2027 capex (vs the current raise/maintain pattern).
2. **Deceleration in the raises** — the QoQ/YoY capex growth rate rolling over.
3. **A guidance-firmness retreat** — e.g., another hyperscaler dropping from a precise range to qualitative language (the AMZN pattern spreading).
4. **Return failing to converge** — operating income / FCF not catching up to the capex step-up over the build-out window (the timing risk in dynamic #3 widening rather than closing).
5. **A neocloud refinancing failure or covenant breach** — the rent-side, debt-and-equity-funded layer breaks before the cash-funded center does. Now primary-sourced as a **pair**: [[CRWV]] (the more debt-stretched — $25.1B debt, $1.23B FY2025 interest, ~67% Microsoft) and [[NBIS]] (the more extreme *spender* — capex ~7-8× revenue — but better-funded today, with a ~$9.3B cash buffer + an adjusted-EBITDA-positive core). The canary, and now with two birds: a strain at *either* — a financing-market tightening, a covenant breach, a customer pullback — would show here first, quarters before it dented a cash-funded hyperscaler.
6. **An HBM / memory price collapse** — signaling overcapacity at the component the capex raises were partly *driven* by (dynamic #2; [[HBM-oligopoly]]). A reversal there would suggest the demand-pull on memory is softening.

The Tier-3 synthesis adds a useful aggregation rule: **any two of these together signal the cycle is turning** — a single soft data point is noise; a guidance cut *and* a neocloud breach (or a return-gap *and* a memory-price collapse) is the pattern to watch.

**Current status (as of the latest filings):** none observed. All seven payers either *raised* (GOOGL, META, CRWV, and NBIS — which lifted its FY2026 guide from $16-20B to $20-25B) or *maintained/grew* (MSFT ~$190B, AMZN run-rate, ORCL +209%/+223%) their 2026 capex; GOOGL guided 2027 "significantly higher." The unanimity is consistent with genuinely strong demand, and is also the expected output from companies with aligned incentives to project AI-spending durability — recorded with that calibration. (Signal #5 — a neocloud refinancing strain at CRWV or NBIS — remains the most sensitive tripwire.)

## Open questions

1. **Program complete (six payers).** [[ORCL]] (S119) and [[CRWV]] (S121) were the last two ingests; all six payers are now in the table — five hyperscalers + one rent-side neocloud. The open watch is no longer *who* to add but whether the disconfirming signals fire (especially #5, the CRWV-canary refinancing watch).
2. **Does the aggregate keep growing into 2027?** GOOGL has already guided 2027 capex "significantly higher" — whether the others follow is the forward test.
3. **How durable is the memory-pricing cost-push?** If memory pricing normalizes, do the capex raises partially reverse, or does volume sustain them?
4. **Capex-vs-return convergence.** Does operating income / FCF catch up to the capex across the build window, closing the timing gap the four all acknowledge?
5. **Captive-silicon share.** As Trainium / TPU / MTIA / Maia scale, how much of the capex routes away from merchant GPUs — the dynamic tracked on [[hyperscaler-custom-ASIC]]?
