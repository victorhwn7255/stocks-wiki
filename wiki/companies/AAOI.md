# AAOI — Applied Optoelectronics

## Thesis role

AAOI is the vault's first Layer 5 (integrated systems/subsystems) company page and the structural test case for CPO displacement dynamics. frameworks.md places AAOI at Layer 5, photonics_tier 5 — the weakest thesis fit in the seed set. The three-source evidence broadly confirms this positioning, with one tension that warrants human review.

**The honest verdict.** AAOI is a rapidly growing but structurally fragile pluggable transceiver assembler. Revenue grew 83% in FY2025, Q4 data center revenue inflected +70% QoQ, and management guides to >$1B in FY2026 — more than doubling FY2025. The growth is real. But the structural position is weak: products are "only minimally differentiated from those of our competitors" (AAOI 10-K FY2025), customer concentration is the most extreme in the vault (two customers = 81.9% of revenue), the company remains loss-making despite 83% growth, and CPO displacement risk — the most material structural threat — goes unnamed in filings and is reframed as opportunity on the earnings call. AAOI is included in the vault for what its trajectory reveals about CPO displacement dynamics and Layer 5 economics, not for structural thesis fit.

**Layer 4/5 straddling tension.** AAOI manufactures its own InP and GaAs laser chips at Sugar Land, TX using MBE and MOCVD epitaxial growth — a Layer 4 capability. Thompson Lin (CEO) describes this as strategically critical: external laser suppliers are quoting 1+ year lead times, and AAOI plans to triple its own InP laser production by mid-2027 (AAOI Q4 FY2025 call). The earnings call frames CPO as a demand driver for this laser business, not just a threat to the transceiver business. However, current revenue is ~100% assembled transceivers — the laser capability is vertically integrated into modules, not sold as standalone Layer 4 product. The straddling is real but does not yet warrant a tier upgrade. photonics_tier 5 is maintained with this tension noted.

## Product and technology

**Current products:** Pluggable optical transceivers for datacenter (100G, 400G), CATV transmitters, nodes, and amplifiers (branded "Quantum Bandwidth" for DOCSIS 4.0), telecom transceivers, FTTH transceivers (AAOI 10-K FY2025).

**Ramping:** 800G pluggable transceivers. Firmware qualification delays pushed the volume ramp from H2 2025 to Q2 2026 — management states "firmware that wasn't there yet... that's the fundamental reason" (AAOI Q4 FY2025 call). 800G is expected to dominate data center revenue starting Q2 2026, with demand exceeding capacity through mid-2027.

**Development:** 1.6T transceivers. The 10-K mentions 1.6T only as a market trend; the earnings call provides more specificity — 1.6T is expected to contribute revenue in H2 2026. Mid-2027 monthly revenue projection: ~$71M from 1.6T (AAOI Q4 FY2025 call).

**Vertical integration — laser chips:** AAOI fabricates InP and GaAs laser chips in-house at Sugar Land, TX using MBE (molecular beam epitaxy) and MOCVD (metalorganic chemical vapor deposition). Management claims this dual-process capability is "unique in our industry" (AAOI 10-K FY2025). Thompson Lin: "more than 95% of laser will be AI laser" by end of year; tripling InP laser production by mid-2027 (AAOI Q4 FY2025 call). The laser manufacturing capability, while vertically integrated into modules rather than sold separately, is AAOI's strongest structural differentiator and the basis of the Layer 4/5 straddling tension noted in Thesis role. See [[InP-supply]] for the upstream substrate supply dynamics that constrain InP laser capacity industry-wide.

**Absent from product discussion:** No silicon photonics products. No CPO modules. No LPO (linear-drive pluggable optics) products. No standalone laser component products. The product portfolio is entirely pluggable transceivers and CATV equipment.

## CPO displacement analysis

This is the central analytical section for a Layer 5 company and the defining contribution of AAOI.md to the vault. The prior wiki's CPO analysis spans six companies across four supply chain tiers — at every tier, CPO is neutral-to-positive. AAOI introduces the first company where CPO is structurally negative for the core business.

**The structural tension.** AAOI's revenue comes from pluggable transceivers — the form factor that CPO is designed to replace. If CPO displaces pluggable transceivers in hyperscale datacenters, AAOI's addressable market contracts. Simultaneously, AAOI manufactures InP laser chips internally, and CPO architectures require external laser sources (ELS). CPO therefore threatens AAOI's module business while potentially creating demand for AAOI's laser business. Management chose to highlight only the second dynamic.

**Management's reframing.** The sole CPO mention across all three sources comes from Stefan Murry (CFO) on the Q4 FY2025 call: "We believe that in the future, CPO will continue to drive increased demand for high-power lasers and plan to continue to expand our laser manufacturing capacity in Texas in order to accommodate these future growth drivers" (AAOI Q4 FY2025 call). CPO is framed exclusively as a laser demand driver. The displacement risk to the transceiver business — which generates ~100% of current revenue — goes unmentioned.

**Filing silence.** The 10-K and 10-Q contain zero mentions of "co-packaged optics," "CPO," "silicon photonics," "LPO," "linear-drive," or any technology that could displace pluggable transceivers (AAOI 10-K FY2025; AAOI 10-Q Q3 FY2025). The closest language is boilerplate: "new or enhanced products developed by us or by others could render our existing products obsolete or unmarketable." This is conspicuous — AAOI's most analytically significant structural threat goes unnamed in its regulatory disclosure, even while [[LITE]] and [[COHR]] (both Layer 4) extensively discuss CPO as a catalyst on their same-quarter calls.

**Analyst silence.** Five sell-side analysts participated in the Q4 FY2025 earnings call (Simon Leopold, Raymond James; Michael Genovese, Rosenblatt; George Notter, Wolfe; Ryan Koontz, Needham; Tim Savageaux, Northland). Zero asked about CPO displacement risk. This is the most conspicuous analyst absence in the vault. For comparison, seven analysts asked substantive CPO questions on the [[LITE]] call and multiple asked on the [[COHR]] call. The analyst community covering Layer 5 transceiver assemblers is not pricing CPO displacement risk into its questioning — a meta-observation about how the market is processing the CPO transition. Either the covering analysts do not view CPO as near-term material for AAOI, or the Layer 5 coverage universe is less photonics-specialized than the Layer 4 analyst community.

**Technology alternatives not discussed.** The silicon photonics technology landscape includes several intermediate architectures between pluggables and full CPO: **LPO** (linear-drive pluggable optics, retaining the pluggable form factor but reducing power by offloading signal processing), **NPO** (near-packaged optics, moving the optical engine closer to the ASIC without full co-packaging), and **CPC** (co-packaged copper, eliminating board-level electrical routing without introducing photonics). Each represents a potential bridge technology that could delay full CPO adoption and extend pluggable relevance (per /raw/research/silicon-photonics-primer.md, citing OIF COI white paper). AAOI discusses none of these alternatives in any source. The absence suggests either that AAOI is not participating in any bridge-technology program, or that management does not view technology-transition positioning as a discussion topic for its investor audience.

**Timing gap.** [[LITE]] targets scale-up CPO deployments late calendar 2027. [[COHR]] characterizes scale-up CPO as "not years out." Both disclose active CPO program participation with specific laser products and purchase orders. AAOI provides no comparable CPO program timeline, no CPO product development disclosure, and no CPO customer engagement. The only timing-relevant disclosure is the pluggable roadmap: 800G dominance Q2 2026, 1.6T contributions H2 2026, capacity build through mid-2027. AAOI's disclosed investment is entirely in the pluggable trajectory that CPO may displace.

**Displacement vulnerability.** The "minimally differentiated" admission (AAOI 10-K FY2025) compounds the CPO risk. In a technology transition, companies with strong differentiation can pivot to the new form factor; companies with commodity products face displacement by incumbents who bring differentiated capabilities to the new architecture. [[LITE]] and [[COHR]] supply the UHP lasers that CPO requires. [[NVDA]] invested $4B in those two suppliers. AAOI's laser manufacturing is a partial hedge, but its scale is smaller and its laser products serve internal module integration rather than the merchant CPO supply chain.

**Cross-reference: the tiered silence pattern.** AAOI adds three data points to the CPO tiered silence pattern tracked in [[CPO-platform-battle]]. The Layer 5 outcome is asymmetric — a distinct pattern variant from all other tiers. See [[CPO-platform-battle]] for the full eighteen-data-point evidence table.
