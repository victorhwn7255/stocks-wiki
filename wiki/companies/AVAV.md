# AVAV — AeroVironment, Inc.

## Thesis role

AeroVironment is the **most complete US-listed pure-play in the Defense & Drones thesis** — the flagship investable name spanning unmanned aircraft, loitering munitions, counter-UAS, autonomy software, and (post-BlueHalo) space / cyber / directed energy. It is the vault's **first Defense & Drones platform-company page** (the [[MP]] dual-thesis annotation, a rare-earth chokepoint owner, was the first defense-domain placement). Placement is **`defense_tier 1`** (prime with real programs of record) per `frameworks-defense-drones.md` Framework D2 value-capture tiers. AVAV carries no AI-datacenter supply-chain role; the AI `*_tier` fields are not applied (first pure-defense page; see Change log).

**Why it matters — and where the open question moved (FY2026 refresh).** The prior baseline (Q3 FY2026) framed AVAV's central tension as *"demand is real; margin, integration, and execution are the question."* The FY2026 full year answers the demand half decisively — and sharpens the execution half. **Demand is validated:** revenue grew to **~$2.0B (+30% organic; +141% reported with BlueHalo)** on a record Q4 (**$642M, +31% organic**), both beating the cut guidance, with total backlog at **$2.7B** and a deep new-award slate (AVAV Q4/FY2026 call, Jun 29 2026). **But the acquisition-and-execution question got harder, not easier:** the BlueHalo Space unit produced a **$240.7M full-year goodwill impairment** on the terminated SCAR program, AVAV **restated its Q3 FY2026 interim financials** and disclosed **three material weaknesses** in internal controls (disclosure controls concluded *not effective* at year-end), a **securities class action** followed, and the **CFO changed**. Per Section 2.1 host-tensions-honestly: the demand thesis is now well-proven; the live question is whether the transformational BlueHalo deal was well-executed — even as the legacy Autonomous Systems business runs a healthy **21% adjusted-EBITDA margin**.

**Honest-verdict framing.** AVAV is `defense_tier 1` for breadth and programs-of-record depth, but it is a **chokepoint consumer, not a chokepoint owner** (Framework D5) — its moat is scale, production capacity, and incumbency across many programs rather than a geology/physics chokepoint. Its closest-to-chokepoint position is AI-enabled counter-UAS (Titan). Placement is **unchanged** at the FY2026 refresh: the write-off, restatement, and litigation are **acquisition-quality flags (Framework D6), not a tier-changing structural break** — breadth and programs-of-record depth are intact and, if anything, deeper. Per the Section 2.1 honest-verdict-trigger discipline, no baseline reframing fires on the strong-revenue momentum either. Inclusion is for thesis centrality (the flagship platform), with the platform-commoditization, government-contingency, and now acquisition-quality tensions surfaced plainly.

## Defense & Drones thesis role — how AVAV benefits from the themes

AVAV maps onto `frameworks-defense-drones.md` across nearly the entire value chain, and is a direct, named beneficiary of the demand drivers the thesis tracks. Program facts below are primary (AVAV Q4 FY2026 call, Jun 29 2026, unless noted).

**Framework D1 (value-chain position) — AVAV spans four layers:**
- **Platform layer** — Group 1–3 UAS (Puma family, JUMP 20 / JUMP 20-X, P550, Raven, Vapor); loitering munitions (Switchblade 300/400/600, Blackwing); one-way-attack drones (Red Dragon).
- **Autonomy / software** — the **AV_Halo** software platform (two new FY2026 modules: **AV_Halo Instinct**, an autonomous framework, and **AV_Halo Detect**, autonomous RF detection in contested environments); Kinesis multi-UAS command-and-control; Puma's visual-navigation kit for GNSS-denied / communication-denied environments.
- **Counter-UAS** — Titan, an AI-enabled radio-frequency detect-and-defeat system (acquired via BlueHalo).
- **Space / cyber / directed energy** — BlueHalo-derived: BADGER / WASP phased-array antennas, LOCUST directed-energy counter-UAS, long-haul laser-communication terminals.

It is a **consumer** of the upstream chokepoints the thesis prizes (rare-earth magnets, secure microelectronics, high-energy batteries) — see Supply chain / chokepoint consumption.

**Framework D2 (tier):** `defense_tier 1` — prime with real programs of record; the most complete US-listed pure-play.

**Framework D3 (demand map / programs of record) — the specific "benefit" channels (FY2026 award slate):**
- **Loitering munitions (Switchblade):** the **$168M US Army task order** for Switchblade 300 Block 20 + Switchblade 600 Block 2, issued under the existing **$990M-ceiling 5-year Army lethal-unmanned-systems IDIQ**; Switchblade was selected under **Replicator**. **Switchblade 400** was awarded under the Army's **LASSO** (Low-Altitude Stalking and Strike Ordnance) program in FY2026; the Army also **funded increased production of Switchblade 600 Block 1** ("capacity to produce several thousands a year").
- **Foreign military sales:** the **$874M 5-year sole-source Army IDIQ** for UAS + counter-UAS for allied FMS demand (Vapor, JUMP 20, P550, Puma, Raven, Titan).
- **Counter-UAS (Titan):** Titan sales **more than doubled** in FY2026 (pro forma); production rates expanded in Q4, with demand expected to rise "through 2027 and beyond" — AVAV's most thesis-central position (the high-conviction, under-served C-UAS theme).
- **Kinetic interceptor:** **FE-1 (Freedom Eagle-1)**, the **$96M** US Army long-range kinetic-interceptor program, framed as a **~$1B market opportunity** over the next several years; a new **Huntsville, Alabama** facility is being built for its production.
- **Group 2–3 UAS:** **P550** selected for the Army's **Long Range Reconnaissance** program — a **$117M** award (post-quarter close); **JUMP 20 / JUMP 20-X** demonstrated at Yuma and deployed in Operation Epic Fury; **MAYHEM 10** launched for the Army's Launched Effects program; **VAPOR CLE** ~$15M (company-level UAS, Tranche 2).
- **Directed energy / space:** a **~$240M long-haul laser-communications award** ("one of the largest on record"); **LOCUST** directed-energy programs (incl. a **$43M** PANTHER phased-array telemetry award; LOCUST X3 introduced); a new **Albuquerque** ($30M) LOCUST manufacturing line + a Dayton, Ohio expansion.
- **One-way-attack:** **Red Dragon** — a **$17M** production contract in Q4; sales more than doubled in FY2026; positioned to "define the next category" as Switchblade did for loitering munitions.
- **International:** Switchblade family engaged across allied markets; FY2027 SG&A is being invested in international presence to convert DCS + FMS demand.

These map directly onto the enacted **FY2025 reconciliation** "low-cost-enabler" + counter-UAS pots and the recurring counter-UAS demand the thesis flags.

**Framework D4 (structural vs cyclical):** AVAV benefits from the structural China-pacing demand build — but the **contingent/cyclical side is the explicit story in the FY2027 guide**. Management guides FY2027 revenue to **$2.125–2.225B (+10%)** and states plainly that the number **assumes a continuing resolution**: it does not assume the full FY2027 defense budget passes on time, expecting approval "December or January" and cash reaching the services "until March timeframe, roughly" (CEO Nawabi). The +10% is therefore **deliberate budget-timing conservatism off a very strong backlog, not a demand-deceleration signal** — the live expression of the thesis's enacted-vs-requested / CR-timing discipline (SCAR alone leaves a ~$30M hole in Q1 FY2027).

**Framework D5 (chokepoint quality gradient):** AVAV is **not** a chokepoint owner — it sits at the commoditizing platform layer (the ~$2,000 OWA ceiling pressures Red Dragon / small-Switchblade economics over time). Its defensible edges are **production scale** (the Salt Lake City facility, on track to begin production **spring CY2027**, with capacity for >$2B/yr of Switchblades or other products; plus the new Huntsville / Albuquerque / Dayton lines), **breadth**, and **programs-of-record incumbency** — plus **Titan counter-UAS**, the one position adjacent to a genuine chokepoint. On margins, FY2026 showed a **partial recovery** (adjusted gross margin ~30% for the year / 34% in Q4, up from the ~22% Q2 low, vs a legacy ~40%), with **Autonomous Systems at a 21% adjusted-EBITDA margin** but **Space, Cyber & Directed Energy still at a ~$(3)M adjusted-EBITDA loss**.

## Company structure — the BlueHalo two-segment structure

**Section 2.1 mid-fiscal-year disclosure shift (established; now a full year of comparatives).** AVAV closed the all-stock acquisition of **BlueHalo on May 1, 2025** (the start of FY2026) and reorganized from three reportable segments to two. FY2026 is the **first full year** reported on the new structure; comparatives are restated.

| Period | Segment framework |
|---|---|
| **FY2025 and prior** (pre-BlueHalo) | **Uncrewed Systems (UxS)** + **Loitering Munition Systems (LMS)** + **MacCready Works (MW)** |

Preserve the segment-comparability boundary at the May 1, 2025 shift date per Section 2.1: do not blend pre- and post-BlueHalo segment trends. **BlueHalo purchase consideration + financing:** ~$844.6M cash + ~3.5M AVAV shares at close, plus subsequent financing — **$747.5M of zero-coupon convertible notes (due 2031)** and a ~$1.0B follow-on equity offering (4.06M shares at $248). ## Restatement, material weakness & securities litigation

**The FY2026 honest-verdict centerpiece (Section 2.1 + Framework D6 acquisition-quality).** Three governance developments landed together with the FY2026 10-K, all traceable to the BlueHalo acquisition and the SCAR write-off:

1. **Restatement of the Q3 FY2026 interim financials.** AVAV restated its unaudited condensed consolidated financials for the quarter ended January 31, 2026 in an **amendment (Form 10-Q/A) filed June 22, 2026**. The 10-K states the error "originated from a material weakness... relate[d] to the design of controls over the preparation and review of our goodwill impairment analysis" — specifically, the company "did not have a properly designed control requiring preparation and review of a reconciliation of goodwill by reporting unit."

2. **Three material weaknesses; disclosure controls not effective.** Item 9A discloses that the CEO and CFO **concluded disclosure controls were *not effective* as of April 30, 2026**, citing three material weaknesses in internal control over financial reporting: (a) **BlueHalo IT general controls** (user-access / segregation-of-duties for certain systems); (b) the **goodwill-impairment analysis** control above; and (c) the **BlueHalo financial-close and reporting process**. All three trace to the BlueHalo integration; remediation is "ongoing."

3. **Securities class action.** *Norrell v. AeroVironment, Inc.*, No. **1:26-cv-01429 (E.D. Va.)**, alleges violations of **Securities Exchange Act §§10(b) / 20(a) and Rule 10b-5** — that defendants made false or misleading statements about the company's work on the Space Force SCAR program. **Class period: June 25, 2025 – March 10, 2026** (i.e., from the FY2025 10-K filing to the SCAR-termination disclosure on the day of the Q3 call). Named defendants: the company, **CEO Wahid Nawabi**, **former CFO Kevin McDonnell**, and **SCDE President Mary Clum**. The complaint seeks unspecified compensatory damages; status is early / pre-trial.

**CFO transition.** The FY2026 10-K and Q4 call name **Sean Woodward** as CFO; **Kevin McDonnell** (the CFO through Q3) is identified as "former CFO." On the call, management disclosed the material weakness proactively and described enhanced controls — the tone was measured, not defensive (Source audit notes).

**Framework D6 read.** AVAV is *not* a distressed micro-cap (the going-concern / dilution screen for Tier-3 names does not apply — the balance sheet is well-funded, net leverage ~1.2×). The screen surfaces a different quality question: **acquisition execution.** A transformational deal that closed May 1, 2025 produced, inside its first year, a **$240.7M goodwill write-off**, a **restatement**, **three control material weaknesses**, and a **securities suit** — a concrete acquisition-quality flag to weigh against the genuinely strong operating results (record Q4; AxS 21% adjusted EBITDA).

## The SCAR / BADGER termination — resolved (fully terminated) + commercial pivot

The honest-verdict centerpiece from Q3 is now **resolved to a full termination**. AVAV held an Other Transaction Agreement (OTA) to deliver **BADGER phased-array antennas** for the **US Space Force SCAR** (Satellite Communication Augmentation Resource) program — a BlueHalo (SCDE/Space) program. A **January 2026 stop-work order** was the impairment trigger; in **March 2026 the Space Force terminated the agreement for convenience** (AVAV FY2026 10-K MD&A + subsequent events). The consequences booked in FY2026:
- A **$240.7M goodwill impairment** on the Space reporting unit for the year (of which the call characterized ~$89M as the incremental Q4 charge); **~$291M of Space goodwill remains** and is exposed to further impairment if forecasts weaken.
- **SCAR revenue: $121M for FY2026 / $31M in Q4**, leaving a **~$30M hole in Q1 FY2027**.
- No cost/fee recovery is quantified in the 10-K.

**The pivot (management framing).** AVAV is redirecting **BADGER / WASP phased-array technology to a commercialized product** to compete on the Space Force's *upcoming* program and to broaden to other commercial customers — the 10-K frames this as "expanded opportunities and improve[d] long-term product margins" via increased R&D + capex. This is a forward, unproven pivot, not a recompete win (attributed to management, per the rhetorical-claims convention).

## Competitive positioning

The most capable pure drone-autonomy companies — **Anduril** (Lattice) and **Shield AI** (Hivemind) — are **private and un-investable**, and capture many of the marquee programs. AVAV is the public-market pure-play: its edge is **production scale**, **breadth** across Group 1–3 UAS + loitering munitions + counter-UAS + space/cyber/DE, and **programs-of-record incumbency**, rather than a single defensible chokepoint — management's own framing that the "ability to quickly scale manufacturing... differentiates us from... new entrants." Among US-listed peers it sits alongside [[KTOS]] (Kratos — the other Defense & Drones platform prime, weighted to jet-powered drones / CCA-adjacent platforms, hypersonics, propulsion) and the diversified primes (RTX / LHX in counter-UAS; not yet vault pages). AVAV and [[KTOS]] are structural co-positioned peers (Section 3.14 parallel-but-independent) — overlapping most at the CCA-adjacent / affordable-mass layer, with Kratos sitting further upstream via in-house engines and solid rocket motors.

## Supply chain / chokepoint consumption

AVAV is a **downstream consumer** of the upstream chokepoints the Defense thesis prizes — and its own 10-K says so plainly. Item 1A flags that "our products, including **motors, batteries, and other advanced components, rely on rare earth metals**, of which a significant majority are sourced from China," plus semiconductor / component China-sourcing risk; and in **March 2025 China placed AeroVironment on its export-control list** (this language is **unchanged** in the FY2026 10-K — no escalation or relief disclosed this cycle). This links AVAV to the vault's chokepoint owners:
- **Rare-earth NdFeB magnets** → [[MP]] (the intra-defense supply-chain link: MP is the magnet chokepoint owner; AVAV is a motor/actuator consumer). First intra-defense supply-chain linkage in the vault.
- **Secure microelectronics / FPGAs**, **high-energy-density batteries**, and **EO/IR sensors** — the secure-chip ([[LSCC]] / [[MRCY]]), battery ([[AMPX]]), and sensor ([[TDY]]) chokepoints AVAV depends on but does not own. The documented intra-defense supply-chain links: AVAV→[[MP]] magnets (S124) + AVAV→[[LSCC]] secure chips (S125) + AVAV→[[TDY]] EO/IR sensors (S131) + AVAV→[[AMPX]] batteries (S132) — and **[[MRCY]] (S134) joins [[LSCC]] as the *trusted-domestic* second source** at the secure-microelectronics node (LSCC = the China/Taiwan-exposed designer; MRCY = the onshore-DMEA-trusted integrator). All tracked under `frameworks-defense-drones.md` Framework D5.

The structural read (Framework D5): the China rare-earth + secure-component dependence is a *shared* vulnerability across the whole platform tier — value and pricing power sit upstream with the chokepoint owners, not with AVAV. AVAV's disclosed magnet dependence anchors the consumer side of [[rare-earth-magnet-chokepoint]]; it sits at the platform-consumer end of the [[defense-drone-supply-chain]] map; and its OWA/affordable-mass exposure is the subject of [[drone-platform-commoditization]] (where the FY2026 gross-margin partial-recovery is now a live data point).
