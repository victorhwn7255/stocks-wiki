# Lumentum (LITE)

## Thesis role

Lumentum is a Layer 4 differentiated component supplier — the first Layer 4 company page in this vault. Its thesis relevance is defined primarily through its relationships to [[NVDA]] (Layer 1) and [[TSM]] (Layer 2) rather than through its own platform position. Three structural roles:

1. **Laser supply for CPO.** Lumentum manufactures ultra-high-power (UHP) CW lasers and EML chips for co-packaged optics and next-generation datacenter interconnect. The Q2 FY2026 earnings call is the first primary source in this vault to break the CPO silence documented in [[CPO-platform-battle]] — CPO is named as one of three primary growth catalysts (LITE Q2 FY2026 call).

2. **NVDA-invested company.** [[NVDA]]'s March 2026 $2B equity investment plus multi-year purchase commitments positions Lumentum within the NVDA platform integration pattern documented in [[NVDA-platform-integration]]. This page documents the invested company's perspective on that relationship.

3. **Capacity-constrained bottleneck.** InP wafer fab fully allocated, ~25-30% supply-demand imbalance, all EML capacity spoken for through calendar 2027 (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). These dynamics qualify as a potential [[datacenter-laser-supply]] chokepoint — provisionally documented from LITE sources alone pending [[COHR]] cross-validation.

This page is template-setting for future Layer 4 company pages (COHR, AEHR, ONTO). The structural difference from [[TSM]] and [[NVDA]] is significant: where those pages are organized around platform position and ecosystem control, this page is organized around product capability, capacity constraint, and relationship leverage.

## Product portfolio and technology

**EML (electro-absorption modulated lasers).** Record shipments at 100G lane speeds, ramping 200G. The 200G EML transition is accelerating: currently 5% of unit volume but approximately 10% of datacenter laser chip revenue due to higher ASPs; expected to reach 25% of mix by end of calendar year (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). All EML capacity is spoken for through calendar 2027 via long-term agreements (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). At 1.6T speeds, silicon photonics is expected to become the majority technology for transceivers, but EML absolute volumes continue growing because WDM (wavelength division multiplexing) applications remain EML-dominant (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). This is a coexistence dynamic, not a displacement threat.

**CW lasers (continuous wave).** Shipping to 800G manufacturers with increasing volumes for CPO applications (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). Vertical integration of CW lasers into Lumentum's own transceivers was pushed from Q2 to late Q2/early Q3 FY2026 (LITE Q2 FY2026 call).

**UHP lasers (ultra-high power).** 400mW UHP CW lasers for CPO are described as a competitive moat: "400 mW is not something that many people can do" — Hurlston (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). Multi-hundred-million-dollar purchase order for UHP lasers supporting optical scale-out/CPO applications. Material shipment inflection in H2 2026 (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). See [[datacenter-laser-supply]] for chokepoint implications.

**Optical circuit switches (OCS).** Backlog exceeds $400M across three customers, majority to ship H2 calendar 2026 (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). Hit $10M quarterly revenue three months ahead of schedule. Four application scenarios: spine switch replacement, scale-across, optical scale-up, protection/redundancy (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). OCS manufacturing expansion planned over coming quarters (LITE 10-Q Q2 FY2026).

**Cloud transceivers.** The 1.6T transition is "definitely stronger than 90 days ago" and accelerating (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). Systems revenue ($221.8M, +60% YoY) was driven nearly entirely by cloud transceiver volumes, partially offset by lower pricing across multiple transceiver lines (LITE 10-Q Q2 FY2026).

**ELS modules (external light source).** For CPO applications, providing approximately 2-2.5x content gain versus standalone lasers (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). This content multiplier means CPO adoption increases Lumentum's revenue per socket even as it changes the interconnect architecture.

**Coherent DSP — exited.** Lumentum discontinued in-house development of coherent DSPs and RFICs during FY2024, writing off $29.1M of IPR&D from the IPG Photonics acquisition (August 2022) and incurring $6.7M in contract exit costs (LITE 10-K FY2025). This represents a strategic narrowing: Lumentum acquired coherent DSP capability, spent two years attempting integration, and retreated to focus on the optical component/laser layer. This confirms Layer 4 positioning — the company tried to move up the value chain into Layer 3 system integration and pulled back.

## CPO positioning and trajectory

The trajectory of CPO discussion across three sources spanning June 2025 to February 2026 is the most thesis-significant finding from this ingest:

**10-K (FY2025, filed August 2025):** Exactly one generic mention — "co-packaged optics and integrated photonics assemblies" listed as a customized solution category (LITE 10-K FY2025). No strategic emphasis, no development timeline, no competitive positioning. The filing uses broad product descriptions ("high-speed laser transmitters") rather than specific product terminology (EML, CW laser). Zero discussion of silicon photonics as a technology platform.

**10-Q (Q2 FY2026, filed February 3, 2026):** Zero mentions of CPO, co-packaged optics, silicon photonics, or photonic integration across the entire filing (LITE 10-Q Q2 FY2026). The 200G lane speed migration — the current pluggable transceiver paradigm — is the stated revenue driver.

**Earnings call (Q2 FY2026, February 3, 2026):** CPO named as one of three primary growth catalysts alongside cloud transceivers and OCS: "cloud transceivers, optical circuit switches, or OCS, and co-packaged optics, or CPO" — Hurlston (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). Specific disclosures: multi-hundred-million-dollar purchase order for UHP lasers; material UHP chip shipment inflection H2 2026; first scale-up CPO deployments expected late calendar 2027; 400mW UHP moat; ELS modules at 2-2.5x content gain; CPO reliability "gaining real customer confidence" and "much more broad-based than people think" (LITE Q2 FY2026 call).

**The same-day divergence:** The 10-Q and earnings call were both dated February 3, 2026 and cover the same quarter. The 10-Q contains zero CPO mentions; the call names CPO as a primary growth catalyst. CPO has entered management's strategic messaging but not its regulatory disclosures — consistent with a technology that is commercially imminent (forward purchase orders, customer engagement) but not yet revenue-generating in the reported period.

**What this means for the CPO silence thesis:** This is the first primary source to break the CPO silence documented across three prior sources (NVDA Q4 call, TSM Q1 call, TSM 20-F) in [[CPO-platform-battle]]. The break comes from the component layer (Layer 4) rather than the platform (Layer 1) or manufacturing (Layer 2) layers — CPO is being discussed where the revenue depends on it, before it surfaces at the structural layers above. See [[CPO-platform-battle]] for the tiered silence pattern analysis.

**Three-inch InP in industry context (per /raw/research/silicon-photonics-primer.md, citing Sumitomo technical reviews and AXT 2020 results).** LITE's three-inch InP position reflects the industry baseline, not an inferior capability. InP is brittle, thermally sensitive, and less forgiving than silicon; scaling from three-inch to larger diameters raises stress, bow, warp, dislocation management, and equipment-automation challenges simultaneously. [[COHR]]'s six-inch position is the unusual manufacturing scale, not LITE's three-inch being substandard. AXT's own historical commentary on six-inch InP yield difficulties reinforces this: larger-diameter InP production does not automatically translate to better economics until yields are brought under control. See [[InP-supply]] for the upstream substrate-level supply dynamics.

## Competitive landscape

Lumentum's 10-K provides no specific competitor names: "We compete against various public and private companies providing optical communications components, modules, and systems. Some of these competitors are also our customers for certain of our products" (LITE 10-K FY2025). Two competitive dynamics are disclosed:

**Commoditization pressure.** "The market for optical communications products in particular has matured over time and these products have increasingly become subject to commoditization. Both legacy competitors as well as new entrants, predominantly Asia-based competitors, have intensified market competition in recent years leading to pricing pressure" (LITE 10-K FY2025). Transceiver pricing pressure is already visible: Systems revenue growth was "partially offset by lower pricing across multiple transceiver lines" (LITE 10-Q Q2 FY2026).

**Vertical integration risk.** "Our current or potential customers may also determine to develop and produce products for their own use which may be competitive to our products" (LITE 10-K FY2025). If hyperscalers in-house laser capability, Layer 4 suppliers lose their structural position.

**Competitive differentiation at the frontier.** Hurlston characterized 400mW UHP lasers as "not something that many people can do" (LITE Q2 FY2026 call), implying a narrow competitive field at the performance frontier. [[COHR]] (also NVDA-invested) is the primary competitor/coexistent in the datacenter laser market, with both companies reporting similar supply-demand dynamics across the same quarter.

**400mW in engineering terms (per /raw/research/silicon-photonics-primer.md, citing Lumentum CLEO 2022 and OIF Co-Packaging Framework).** 400mW equals approximately 26 dBm. Moving from 200mW (23 dBm) to 400mW buys approximately 3 dB of additional optical budget — equivalent to the penalty of one extra 1:2 split in an ideal system. The significance is not that 400mW is a magical threshold but that it enables either more sharing (more silicon photonic engines fed by one laser) or more margin (tolerance for splitter, coupling, and routing losses) in ELS/CPO architectures. Lumentum's own CLEO 2022 paper estimated CPO-based 51T switches require roughly 22-25 dBm per laser source, which directly aligns with the current 350-400+ mW product announcements from both LITE and [[COHR]].

**COHR competitive asymmetry (from COHR sources).** [[COHR]] claims to be "the world's only producer of six-inch indium phosphide" wafers, yielding >4x chips at <50% cost versus LITE's three-inch wafers (COHR Q2 FY2026 call). COHR is approximately 3.5x LITE's revenue ($5.8B vs $1.6B FY2025) and maintains broader vertical integration (wafer to module to system). However, both companies are fully allocation-constrained: COHR's six-inch cost advantage manifests in expansion economics rather than current competitive pricing. Thomas O'Malley (Barclays) directly cross-validated on COHR's call, referencing "your competitor talked about 40% increases" (matching LITE's InP expansion), and COHR's Anderson confirmed the industry remains supply-short (COHR Q2 FY2026 call). The two companies are competing for the same laser supply chokepoint position — see [[datacenter-laser-supply]].

**Technology transition dynamics.** At 1.6T speeds, silicon photonics is expected to become the majority technology for transceivers, but EML absolute volumes continue growing because WDM applications remain EML-dominant (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). The 200G EML transition is accelerating — currently 5% of unit volume but ~10% of datacenter laser chip revenue, expected to reach 25% of mix by end of calendar year (LITE Q2 FY2026 call).

## Source audit notes

### 10-Q Q2 FY2026 (Tier 1, filed February 3, 2026)

The 10-Q and the Q2 FY2026 earnings call were filed and held on the same day (February 3, 2026), covering the same quarter. Despite this, they diverge sharply on CPO: zero mentions in the filing, named as primary growth catalyst on the call. This is meta-signal about disclosure lifecycles — filings document what has happened; management messaging anticipates what will happen. The CPO purchase orders and customer engagements described on the call had not yet generated revenue in the reported period, so they do not appear in the filing's backward-looking financial statements or MD&A.

Segment elimination to a single reportable segment occurred in Q1 FY2026, precisely as Cloud & Networking exceeded 85% of revenue. The CODM now reviews consolidated net income/loss as the sole performance measure, reducing analytical visibility into datacenter vs. industrial revenue trends. Whether this reflects genuine organizational simplification or disclosure strategy is unknown.

Going concern language around convertible notes is procedurally required (stock price triggered conversion conditions across all tranches) but analytically misleading — convertible note fair values ($10.67B) are 3.4x carrying value ($3.18B), and total liquidity exceeds $1.55B. The disclosure framework forces alarming language for what is operationally a sign of stock price strength.

Geographic revenue shifts — Mexico nearly tripled ($37.4M → $102.6M), China tripled ($18.1M → $54.6M) — likely reflect contract manufacturer routing changes rather than end-market demand geography. Hong Kong's share decline (25% → 17.9%) despite absolute growth may reflect Cloud Light integration normalizing shipment patterns.

### 10-K FY2025 (Tier 1, filed August 19, 2025)

Only one generic CPO mention in a filing from a leading datacenter laser supplier confirms the CPO absence pattern from a component-layer Tier 1 source. The filing uses broad product descriptions ("high-speed laser transmitters," "higher speed and higher optical power laser chips") rather than the specific terminology (EML, DML, CW laser) used on earnings calls — possibly reflecting legal review that avoids forward-looking product category commitments, or simply a different audience and register than investor calls.

Revenue disaggregation is explicitly refused: "We do not present other levels of disaggregation, such as by type of products, customer, markets, contracts" (Note 18). This is unusual for a semiconductor component company and limits external analysis. Combined with the Q1 FY2026 segment elimination, Lumentum is trending toward less, not more, public revenue granularity as its datacenter business grows.

The coherent DSP discontinuation — writing off the IPG acquisition's IPR&D ($29.1M) within two years of acquisition — is embedded in restructuring disclosures without being framed as a strategic reversal. Lumentum acquired coherent DSP capability, attempted integration, and abandoned it. This pattern is analytically significant as a Layer 4 boundary marker: the company tried to move up the value chain and retreated.

SG&A of $348.2M on $1,645.0M revenue (21.2%) is elevated for a component supplier, partly driven by $28.2M in CEO departure equity acceleration. The Huawei loss receives extensive risk factor treatment but minimal financial quantification beyond a $3.2M bad debt reserve — the full revenue impact of losing "historically our largest networking customer in China" is not isolated.

This call provides the most thesis-relevant content of the three sources despite being the lowest tier — a strong example of the Tier 1/Tier 2 calibration principle. The 1.6T transition commentary provides nuance absent from other sources: silicon photonics gains transceiver share at 1.6T, but EML absolute volumes continue growing because WDM remains EML-dominant.
