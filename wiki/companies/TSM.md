# TSM — Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company

## Thesis role

TSM occupies Layer 2 (irreplaceable infrastructure) in the value capture framework and Photonics Tier 1 (structural chokepoint). TSMC is the single most central node in the AI supply chain dependency map — every downstream player, including Layer 1 platform definers like [[NVDA]] and Broadcom, depends on TSMC for leading-edge logic foundry capacity and advanced packaging ([[TSMC-CoWoS]], CoPoS, and future technologies). The dependency is unavoidable at the leading edge, which is what makes TSMC a structural chokepoint rather than merely a large supplier.

TSMC's Q1 2026 gross margin of 66.2% exceeds the Layer 2 framework range of 40-60% (TSM Q1 2026 call). This suggests TSMC may be capturing value above typical Layer 2 levels, approaching Layer 1-like economics, due to its effective monopoly position in advanced packaging and leading-edge logic. The framework range is approximate and individual margins vary, but the persistent outperformance is worth tracking as evidence that structural chokepoint status can translate into pricing power that exceeds the layer's typical margin capture.

**Compound bottleneck position (per /raw/research/ASML-EUV-foundry-dependency.md).** TSMC sits at the junction of two high-value constraints simultaneously: leadership-node wafer supply (ASML EUV-dependent) and advanced packaging capacity ([[TSMC-CoWoS]], CoPoS, and the COUPE roadmap). This compound position makes TSMC more than a foundry — frontier AI accelerators can be blocked either because the compute die cannot be produced at N5/N3/N2-class nodes without ASML EUV, or because the package/interposer/HBM stack cannot be assembled fast enough even after the die exists. The two constraints are parallel, not sequential: both must scale together for system output to grow. This is the structural reason TSMC's Layer 2 position is so durable — a competitor must simultaneously close the gap on both leading-edge logic and advanced packaging, not merely one.

## Customer concentration (Tier 1 disclosure)

### Disclosed data

TSMC's 20-F provides the most granular customer concentration data available from any ingested source (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 38 and risk factors):

**Major customers representing ≥10% of net revenue:**

| Customer | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|----------|------|------|------|
| Customer A | Below 10% | NT$352,271M (12%) | NT$726,974M (19%) |
| Customer B | NT$546,551M (25%) | NT$624,346M (22%) | NT$645,179M (17%) |
| Customer C | NT$241,152M (11%) | Below 10% | Below 10% |

Customer A surpassed Customer B in FY2025 to become TSMC's single largest customer. Customer A's revenue more than doubled in one year (NT$352B → NT$727B), while Customer B grew modestly (NT$624B → NT$645B) (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 38).

**Aggregate concentration:**
- Top 10 customers: 78% of net revenue in 2025, up from 76% in 2024 and 70% in 2023 (TSM 20-F FY2025, risk factors)
- Top 10 customers: 84% of accounts receivable as of December 2025, down from 93% in December 2024 (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 33d). The AR concentration declining while revenue concentration increased is a data point worth tracking — possible timing effects or structural changes in payment terms; insufficient context to interpret definitively.

**No material contracts:** "TSMC is not currently a party to any material contract, other than contracts entered into in the ordinary course of business" (TSM 20-F FY2025, p.61). This means no formal customer supply agreement rises to SEC materiality threshold, even for customers representing 19% of revenue.

### Inferable but not disclosed

The 20-F does not name any customer. Any specific attribution is interpretation, not disclosure.

Customer A's trajectory — below the 10% disclosure threshold in 2023, rising to 12% in 2024 and 19% in 2025, with revenue more than doubling in a single year — is consistent with a company undergoing rapid AI-era scaling. The growth pattern resembles that of a major AI platform customer whose demand for leading-edge logic and advanced packaging has expanded dramatically during the 2023-2025 period. Customer B's gradual decline (25% → 22% → 17%) despite absolute revenue growth (NT$547B → NT$645B) is consistent with a maturing consumer electronics customer whose share declines as AI-driven HPC demand grows faster.

These patterns match publicly known TSMC customer profiles, but the filing provides no confirmation of identity.

### What remains unknown

- Which specific companies correspond to Customer A, B, and C
- Whether Customer A's growth is concentrated in a single product line (e.g., AI accelerators) or reflects diversified demand across multiple chip types
- Whether the AR concentration decline (93% → 84%) while revenue concentration increased (76% → 78%) reflects payment timing, changes in credit terms, or structural shifts in the customer payment mix
- Whether Customer C's decline below 10% after 2023 represents reduced demand or reclassification

### Temporary receipts from customers

Temporary receipts (advance capacity reservation payments from customers): NT$189,858M total as of December 2025, down 35% from NT$291,102M in December 2024 (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 22c). Of the 2025 total, NT$146,559M is classified as current (<1 year) and NT$43,299M as noncurrent (1-3 years) (TSM 20-F FY2025, p.34).

Three possible explanations for the decline: capacity availability increasing (reducing the need for advance reservations), reservation contract terms changing, or reclassification between liability categories. Insufficient context to interpret; flagged as an open question for tracking across future filings.

## Technology roadmap

### N3 expansion

N3 contributed 25% of Q1 2026 wafer revenue (TSM Q1 2026 call). N3 is in "severe undersupply backed by strong AI demand" per analyst framing, and TSMC confirmed capacity expansion through greenfield and conversion through 2028.

Three new N3 fabs announced (TSM Q1 2026 call):

1. **Tainan (GIGAFAB cluster)** — volume production scheduled H1 2027
2. **Arizona (2nd fab)** — construction complete, 3nm volume production H2 2027
3. **Japan (2nd fab)** — 3nm, volume production scheduled 2028

Additionally, TSMC is converting 5nm tools to 3nm capacity in Tainan and leveraging flexible capacity support among N7, N5, and N3 nodes (TSM Q1 2026 call).

N3 gross margin is expected to cross the corporate average in H2 2026 (TSM Q1 2026 call). Equipment depreciation on original N3 tools (ramping since Q4 2022) will be fully depreciated by 2027, suggesting margin expansion potential beyond that point.

Applications driving N3 demand: smartphone, HPC/AI, HBM base dies, automotive, and IoT (TSM Q1 2026 call). C.C. Wei characterized the demand driver simply: "It's still the HPC AI applications."

### Node-level revenue (Tier 1)

Wafer revenue by process node for FY2025 (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 22a):

| Node | NT$ (millions) | % of wafer revenue | YoY change (from 2024) |
|------|---------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 3nm | 794,341 | 24% | up from 18% |
| 5nm | 1,179,907 | 36% | up from 34% |
| 7nm | 459,258 | 14% | down from 17% |
| Advanced (≤7nm) | — | 74% | up from 69% |

The 74% advanced node share matches the Q1 2026 quarterly figure (74%, TSM Q1 2026 call), confirming stability. The three-year trend (58% → 69% → 74%) demonstrates accelerating concentration at leading-edge nodes.

### N2 ramp and yield

N2 entered volume production in 2025 at Fab 20 (Hsinchu) and Fab 22 (Kaohsiung) (TSM 20-F FY2025). The Q1 2026 call confirmed "good yield" and strong demand from smartphone and HPC/AI applications (TSM Q1 2026 call). The 20-F additionally discloses that 16-angstrom risk production is expected in 2026 (TSM 20-F FY2025).

TSMC's practice is to prioritize the land in Taiwan for the fast ramp of newest nodes due to the need for tight integration with R&D operations (TSM Q1 2026 call).

N2P and A16 enhancements are planned. Management expects the N2 family to be "another large and long-lasting node for TSMC" (TSM Q1 2026 call).

N2 ramp will dilute gross margin by 2-3% for full-year 2026 (TSM Q1 2026 call).

### A14 outlook

A14 features a second-generation nanosheet transistor structure, delivering another full-node stride from N2 (TSM Q1 2026 call):

- 10-15% speed improvement at the same power vs. N2
- 25-30% power improvement at the same speed vs. N2
- Close to 20% chip density gain

A14 development is "on track and progressing well" with "a high level of customer interest and engagement from both smartphone and HPC applications" (TSM Q1 2026 call). Volume production is scheduled for 2028.

## Advanced packaging

### Quantification (Tier 1)

Non-wafer revenue (packaging, testing, mask making, and other services): 14% of net revenue = NT$536,501M in FY2025 (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 22a). This is the first quantification of the broader non-wafer revenue line from any ingested source. No CoWoS-specific breakout is provided.

TSMC operates 7 advanced backend fabs as of the filing date (TSM 20-F FY2025). FY2025 capex was allocated in part to "expanding capacity for specialty technologies and advanced packaging, including building/facility expansion for Fab 23 and Fab 24" (TSM 20-F FY2025, p.33) — confirming that advanced packaging is an identified capex category, not merely embedded in front-end wafer spending.

### CoWoS

See dedicated page: [[TSMC-CoWoS]].

Advanced packaging capacity is "very tight" (TSM Q1 2026 call). TSMC is currently supplying the largest reticle size CoWoS in the market. The main approach for AI chip packaging today remains large-sized CoWoS combined with system and wafer technology (TSM Q1 2026 call).

TSMC is working with OSAT partners to increase advanced packaging capacity and working internally to expand its own capacity (TSM Q1 2026 call).

Technical challenges in advanced packaging include mechanical stress, warpage, and thermal limitations at larger die sizes. C.C. Wei framed these challenges as moat reinforcement: "A good challenge, and we like it, because of TSMC's strength in technical engineering, and we have confidence that we can work with our customers to solve all the issues" (TSM Q1 2026 call).

### CoPoS and future packaging

C.C. Wei referenced CoPoS (likely Chip-on-Panel-on-Substrate) as a future packaging technology under development alongside SoIC. A pilot line is being built now, with production expected "a couple of years later" (TSM Q1 2026 call). Today, large-sized CoWoS remains the main production approach.

CoPoS was mentioned in the context of addressing larger reticle size demands from AI customers and as a complement to, not replacement for, CoWoS.

## Capacity and supply constraints

TSMC's installed manufacturing base as of the 20-F filing date: 1 × 150mm, 6 × 200mm, and 9 × 300mm wafer fabrication fabs, plus 7 advanced backend fabs. Annual capacity exceeds 17 million 12-inch equivalent wafers, with approximately 15 million shipped in FY2025 (up from approximately 13 million in FY2024) (TSM 20-F FY2025).

Supply constraints are expected to persist through at least 2027. C.C. Wei stated: "with the current schedule, we believe that '27, we will announce it anyway when we enter '27" regarding new fab plans to address continuing demand (TSM Q1 2026 call). This implies management does not foresee demand-supply balance arriving before 2027 at the earliest.

Demand continues to outstrip supply across both front-end wafer capacity and advanced packaging. Management is "pulling forward" construction and equipment procurement schedules. C.C. Wei: "the demand are very robust, especially from the HPC and AI applications... Still our supply is very tight. Demand continues to increase" (TSM Q1 2026 call).

TSMC emphasized that it does not "pick-and-choose or play favorites among our customers" in allocation decisions, supporting all customers across all platforms (TSM Q1 2026 call). This allocation discipline is relevant to the [[NVDA]]-TSMC relationship and to the broader question of how chokepoint capacity is distributed.

## Capital expenditure

FY2025 capex: NT$1,272,411M (US$40.9B) (TSM 20-F FY2025). Capex was primarily allocated to (TSM 20-F FY2025, p.33):
- Installing and expanding capacity for 2nm, 3nm, and 5nm nodes, including building/facility expansion for Fab 20, Fab 21, and Fab 22
- Expanding capacity for specialty technologies and advanced packaging, including building/facility expansion for Fab 23 and Fab 24
- R&D projects for new process technologies

Capital purchase obligations as of December 31, 2025: NT$1,534,575M total, of which NT$1,220,393M due within one year (TSM 20-F FY2025, p.34). The near-term concentration of commitments confirms the intensity of the current expansion cycle.

Q1 2026 capex was $11.1B (TSM Q1 2026 call). Full-year 2026 capex is guided toward the high end of the $52-56B range, raised from prior guidance due to robust HPC/AI demand and tight equipment supply (TSM Q1 2026 call).

For context, TSMC's total capex over the prior three years (2023-2025) was $101B. At $56B, 2026 alone represents over 50% of that three-year total (TSM Q1 2026 call, Wendell Huang). Management expects capex over the next three years to be "significantly higher than the past few years" (TSM Q1 2026 call).

Capital intensity has remained around 30%+ in recent years. Management does not target a specific capital intensity ratio but expects revenue growth to outpace capex growth, meaning capital intensity should not surge (TSM Q1 2026 call, Wendell Huang).

## Geopolitical and supply chain risks

### Trade and tariff environment (Tier 1)

- **CHIPS Act:** TSMC Arizona is eligible for US$6.6B in direct funding and up to US$5B in loans under the CHIPS and Science Act (TSM 20-F FY2025). TSMC Arizona is also eligible for a 25% advanced manufacturing investment credit on qualified investments (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 30).
- **US-Taiwan trade agreement (January 2026):** Established reciprocal tariff rate of no more than 15% (TSM 20-F FY2025).
- **Section 232 tariffs:** 25% ad valorem tariff on advanced computing chips effective April 2, 2025, with advanced computing chips subject to separate tariff treatment (TSM 20-F FY2025).
- **FX sensitivity:** Every 1% depreciation of the USD against the NT dollar results in approximately 0.3 percentage point decrease in operating margin (TSM 20-F FY2025).

### Export controls and compliance (Tier 1)

- **October 2024 incident:** TSMC notified U.S. and Taiwan authorities that one type of a customer's chip manufactured by TSMC might have been diverted to a restricted entity's product. TSMC has been cooperating with authorities' requests for additional information and documents (TSM 20-F FY2025).
- **TSMC Nanjing:** Validated End-User (VEU) authorization expired December 2025. Operations now under annual export license (TSM 20-F FY2025). China noncurrent assets declined to NT$65,020M from NT$82,406M (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 38), consistent with reduced investment in the Nanjing facility.

### Physical and operational risks (Tier 1)

- **Earthquake losses:** April 2024: ~NT$3B; January 2025: ~NT$5.3B — both net of insurance, primarily affecting inventories, plant facilities, and machinery/equipment (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 37). These are concrete costs of Taiwan production concentration, reinforcing the rationale for geographic diversification per [[overseas-fab-expansion]].
- **ITRI capacity agreement:** Under a standing technical cooperation agreement with the Industrial Technology Research Institute, the R.O.C. government or its designee can use up to 35% of TSMC's capacity, provided TSMC's outstanding customer commitments are not prejudiced. The agreement dates from January 1, 1987 and automatically renews every five years (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 36). As of the end of FY2025, the R.O.C. government had not invoked this right. This is a structural fact about TSMC's relationship with the Taiwan government — a standing legal provision granting government call rights on a significant share of capacity. It has never been exercised, but its existence is relevant to the thesis disconfirming signal "macro or geopolitics dominate structural analysis."
- **Government equity position:** The National Development Fund (Executive Yuan) holds 6.38% of TSMC's outstanding common shares as of February 28, 2026 (TSM 20-F FY2025, p.54).

### Revenue geography vs. production geography

North America accounts for 75% of TSMC's revenue but only 14% of its noncurrent assets (NT$540,057M of NT$3,876,340M) (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 38 and Note 22a). Taiwan holds 80% of noncurrent assets (NT$3,102,343M) while generating only 8% of revenue. This divergence — the overwhelming majority of revenue coming from a geography where very little production capacity sits — is the precise dimension on which geopolitical risk operates. Any disruption to Taiwan-based production directly impacts the revenue base of TSMC's largest market, with limited domestic manufacturing to absorb the shortfall. The CHIPS Act-funded Arizona expansion addresses this gap, but at current scale (14% of assets) the exposure remains structurally significant.

### Operational risks (Tier 2)

**Middle East situation (TSM Q1 2026 call):**

- Chemical and gas prices likely to increase; impact too early to quantify
- Material supply: multi-source strategy with diversified global supplier base and safety stock inventory
- Energy: Taiwan government secured LNG supply through at least May, diversifying sourcing and establishing backup plans
- Management does not expect near-term disruption to operations from either material or energy supply

**Equipment supply (TSM Q1 2026 call):**

- Equipment supply acknowledged as tight alongside wafer demand
- TSMC working with equipment suppliers to "speed it up" and pull forward procurement schedules
- Analyst noted ASML reported strong results; C.C. Wei confirmed positive working relationship with suppliers but did not elaborate on EUV-specific constraints

**Geographic diversification:** See [[overseas-fab-expansion]].

## Key relationships

[[NVDA]] is implicitly TSMC's most significant HPC/AI customer, though TSMC does not name specific customers in earnings calls. The non-naming pattern is reciprocal: NVDA's Q4 FY2026 call (February 25, 2026) never names TSMC either, despite clear dependency — references are limited to "longstanding partnerships," "supply tightness," and "suppliers" (NVDA Q4 FY2026 call). Both sides obscure the Layer 1–Layer 2 dependency in public communications, consistent with each maintaining leverage ambiguity in their most critical bilateral relationship.

Relevant signals from the TSM Q1 2026 call:

(The "LPU" terminology is used by the source; the specific NVIDIA product this refers to is unclear from this source — "LPU" is typically associated with Groq's inference architecture. **Update after NVDA Q4 FY2026 call:** The NVDA Q4 FY2026 call (February 25, 2026) does not use the term "LPU" at all. ### Reciprocal non-naming pattern

A consistent pattern has emerged across ingested sources: companies at multiple value capture layers avoid naming TSMC in public earnings calls despite clear manufacturing dependency, and TSMC reciprocally avoids naming its customers. The pattern spans four companies across three layers:

- **[[NVDA]] (Layer 1):** Does not name TSMC in Q4 FY2026 earnings call despite explicit manufacturing dependency; references limited to "longstanding partnerships" and "suppliers" (NVDA Q4 FY2026 call).
- **[[AVGO]] (Layer 1):** Does not name TSMC in Q1 FY2026 earnings call despite 95% wafer dependency disclosed in filings; references oblique — "leading-edge wafers" and "T-glass" without TSMC attribution (AVGO Q1 FY2026 call; AVGO 10-K FY2025).
- **[[MRVL]] (Layer 3):** Does not name TSMC in Q4 FY2026 earnings call despite CoWoS/InFo/EMIB packaging dependency and Taiwan ship-to surge from 3% to 20% of revenue (MRVL Q4 FY2026 call; MRVL 10-K FY2026).
- **TSM (Layer 2):** Does not name any customer in Q1 2026 earnings call or 20-F FY2025, using anonymous designators (Customer A, Customer B, Customer C) per SEC convention (TSM Q1 2026 call; TSM 20-F FY2025).

The reciprocal non-naming reveals supply chain leverage dynamics: both sides of the Layer 1–Layer 2 and Layer 3–Layer 2 dependencies obscure the bilateral relationship in public venues, consistent with each party maintaining leverage ambiguity in their most critical commercial relationships. The equipment tier, by contrast, names TSMC directly — [[ONTO]] identifies TSMC as a customer at 21% of revenue (ONTO 10-K FY2025) — suggesting that naming discipline correlates with competitive leverage sensitivity rather than value chain position per se.

**Pattern update (GTC March 16, 2026):** [[NVDA]] broke the earnings-call non-naming pattern by naming TSMC explicitly at GTC in the context of COUP/COUPE packaging co-development: "We invented the process technology with TSMC" (Jensen, NVDA GTC March 16, 2026 — attributed per rhetorical claims convention). This is the first primary-source naming of TSMC by a Layer 1 company in any ingested venue. The pattern narrows to three active non-namers ([[AVGO]], [[MRVL]], TSM) on earnings calls; the GTC venue exception suggests naming discipline is venue-specific rather than absolute.

An analyst referenced ASML's strong quarterly results and asked about EUV supply as a potential bottleneck. C.C. Wei characterized the relationship as a partnership: "we're always working with our supplier because we view them as partners... so far, we are very happy with their support" (TSM Q1 2026 call).

**TSMC's Layer 2 position is partly derivative of ASML's upstream monopoly (per /raw/research/ASML-EUV-foundry-dependency.md, citing ASML 2024 Annual Report and TSMC 2024 Annual Report).** ASML is the sole commercial supplier of EUV lithography systems. TSMC is the largest and most experienced downstream absorber of ASML EUV capacity, process learning, and service support — a TSMC statement indicated approximately 56% of the global EUV installed base as of 2023 (per external reconstruction in The Register November 2024, not a filing disclosure). The scanner monopoly and the foundry execution moat reinforce one another: TSMC's process learning is built atop ASML tools that no competitor can source from elsewhere, and ASML's commercial success depends disproportionately on TSMC's sustained demand. Samsung and Intel can access ASML, but they do not inherit TSMC's downstream advantages automatically — see [[foundry-competition]] for the tiered EUV dependency analysis.

**Zeiss SMT chokepoint under ASML (per /raw/research/ASML-EUV-foundry-dependency.md, citing ASML 2020 and 2022 Annual Reports).** ASML itself rests on a smaller optics chokepoint: Carl Zeiss SMT is the sole supplier of optical columns (lenses, mirrors, illuminators, collectors) for EUV systems, and ASML holds a 24.9% equity stake in Carl Zeiss SMT Holding. In practical terms, the TSMC → ASML → Zeiss SMT chain means Layer 2's chokepoint position has its own upstream chokepoint — a second-order structural insight that the foundry execution moat ultimately rests on an optics monopoly within an equipment monopoly.

### Equipment-tier supplier relationships

[[ONTO]] is the first equipment-tier company in the vault to explicitly name TSMC as a customer. ONTO's FY2025 10-K states: "we derive substantial revenue from sales to semiconductor device foundries in Taiwan such as Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company Ltd." (ONTO 10-K FY2025). XBRL-identified customer data shows TSMC at 21% of ONTO's revenue in Q3 FY2025 and remaining a top-three customer in the full-year 10-K (ONTO 10-Q Q3 FY2025; ONTO 10-K FY2025). This is the first confirmed equipment-tier explicit disclosure of TSMC dependency from any ingested source — all prior TSMC references in the vault came from the platform layer ([[NVDA]]) or component layer ([[LITE]], [[COHR]]), where TSMC is inferred rather than named. The equipment tier's willingness to name TSMC directly, while the component and platform tiers maintain anonymity, is itself a disclosure-pattern observation. See [[ONTO]] for full customer concentration analysis, including the customer rotation between ONTO's 10-Q and 10-K (YMTC replaced by Samsung as a >10% customer). See [[TSMC-CoWoS]] for the advanced packaging demand context that drives ONTO's metrology sales into TSMC.

## Open questions

1. **COUPE absence — three sources, zero mentions.** TSMC's COUPE (compact universal photonic engine) silicon photonics platform was not mentioned in the Q1 2026 call, was absent from the NVDA Q4 FY2026 call, and does not appear anywhere in the 244-page TSM 20-F FY2025. The 20-F's HPC technology section formally enumerates "TSMC 3DFabric advanced silicon stacking and packaging solutions, such as TSMC-SoIC manufacturing services and CoWoS advanced packaging services" — COUPE is conspicuously absent from this formal technology enumeration (TSM 20-F FY2025). CoPoS was discussed in the Q1 2026 call as a separate future packaging technology (pilot line now, production in a couple of years). The three-data-point threshold has been crossed for considering a frameworks.md revision to the "COUPE + CoWoS integration starting 2026" timeline. See [[CPO-platform-battle]] and Phase 4 discussion.

2. **LPU terminology — three sources, unresolved.** The Q1 2026 call referenced Samsung manufacturing "NVDA's LPU." Neither the NVDA Q4 FY2026 call nor the TSM 20-F FY2025 uses the term "LPU." Three independent sources are now silent on this terminology. The original reference may have been imprecise analyst/management shorthand, or the LPU may be an unannounced product. Defer to future product-focused sources (GTC, analyst day).

3. **AI accelerator revenue definition expansion.** Management acknowledged CPUs are becoming more important in AI infrastructure (agentic AI workflows) but has not yet included datacenter CPUs in the AI accelerator revenue definition. If/when the definition expands, the reported AI CAGR would change. Track this across future calls. Note: NVDA's Vera CPU (LPDDR5, data-processing focused) is specifically designed for agentic and post-training workloads (NVDA Q4 FY2026 call), reinforcing the growing CPU content in AI infrastructure that C.C. Wei identified.

4. **Long-term capex trajectory.** Analysts pressed for multi-year capex guidance (similar to the $100B three-year plan from 2021). Management declined to give a specific number but signaled "significantly higher" than the prior three years ($101B). The 20-F confirms FY2025 actual capex of NT$1,272,411M (US$40.9B) and discloses capital purchase obligations of NT$1,534,575M, predominantly due within one year (TSM 20-F FY2025). The absence of a multi-year forward commitment figure leaves uncertainty about capital intensity trajectory beyond FY2026.

5. **Pricing power sustainability.** The "grow together" framing suggests TSMC is not yet fully monetizing its structural monopoly position. Whether this is strategic restraint (maintaining customer relationships) or competitive caution (preventing customers from investing in alternatives) is an open question with implications for long-term margin trajectory.

6. **Temporary receipts decline.** Customer capacity reservation payments declined 35% from NT$291,102M (December 2024) to NT$189,858M (December 2025) (TSM 20-F FY2025, Note 22c). Possible explanations: capacity becoming available (reducing need for advance reservations), reservation contract terms changing, or balance sheet reclassification. Track across future filings.

## Source audit notes

### TSM 20-F FY2025 (Tier 1, filed April 2026)

**Filing tone:** Precise, heavily qualified language throughout. Forward-looking statements wrapped in extensive safe harbor boilerplate (pp.2-3). The conservative register contrasts sharply with the Q1 2026 call's confident, emphatic tone — "very tight" and "extremely robust" from the call become measured legal disclosures in the filing. This is expected and reflects the different audience and legal obligations of each format.

**Topics omitted relative to Q1 2026 call:** No discussion of COUPE, CPO, silicon photonics, or CoPoS — the 20-F's formal technology enumeration lists CoWoS and SoIC but not COUPE, a stronger absence than the call simply not discussing it. No specific competitor naming — the competition section (p.21) lists generic categories ("dedicated foundries, IDMs, system companies with foundry capabilities") without naming Samsung, Intel, or any individual competitor, making it less analytically useful than the Q1 call's candid Samsung/Intel/EMIB commentary. No advanced packaging pricing disclosure. No individual customer naming. No quarterly data — annual figures only.

**Content disclosed with heavy qualification:** Customer concentration uses anonymous labels (Customer A/B/C) per SEC requirements. Forward-looking statements limited to safe-harbor-qualified technology timelines (16-angstrom risk production in 2026). Revenue forecasts absent — the 20-F covers the historical period and does not provide the forward guidance that earnings calls offer.

**Thesis-significant footnote disclosure:** Note 38 customer concentration table with NT$ amounts — the most granular customer data from any ingested source. Note 22c temporary receipts (capacity reservation payments) — a signal not available from earnings call commentary. Note 36 ITRI 35% capacity agreement — a structural fact about the Taiwan government relationship that is not discussed in earnings calls. Note 37 earthquake losses — concrete quantification of Taiwan physical risk. Export control incident disclosure (October 2024 chip diversion) — a compliance risk not detailed in earnings calls.

**Tier 1/Tier 2 calibration observation:** For factual data (financials, customer concentration, capacity metrics, legal obligations), the 20-F provides authoritative Tier 1 ground truth that the earnings call does not. For competitive assessment, strategic intent, and forward-looking business conditions, the Q1 2026 call is more analytically informative than the 20-F's legally conservative disclosures. Both tiers are valuable for different purposes; the source hierarchy correctly prioritizes Tier 1 for factual disputes but does not imply Tier 1 is uniformly more useful.

### TSM Q1 2026 call (Tier 2, April 16 2026)

**Management tone (TSM Q1 2026 call):** Confident bordering on emphatic. C.C. Wei used "very robust," "very tight," and "very strong" repeatedly throughout. The phrase "no shortcuts" appeared multiple times in response to competition questions — a deliberate messaging strategy reinforcing TSMC's moat narrative. This confidence level is notable given the Middle East uncertainty acknowledged in the same call.

**Analyst framing:** Eight analysts participated, with heavy focus on competition (three separate lines of questioning on Intel, Samsung, EMIB), capex sustainability, and margin outlook. No analysts asked about silicon photonics, CPO, or COUPE — conspicuous given the thesis's emphasis on these topics. This may reflect that the sell-side views COUPE as too early-stage for earnings call discussion, or that the photonics narrative hasn't yet entered mainstream coverage of TSMC.

**Conspicuous absences:** No discussion of COUPE, CPO, or silicon photonics integration despite the frameworks treating COUPE as a forward-looking TSMC chokepoint. No mention of the March 2026 NVDA equity investments in COHR and LITE, which are relevant to TSMC's downstream supply chain. No discussion of advanced packaging pricing (all pricing discussion was about wafer pricing). No quantification of CoWoS capacity (tight but unquantified) or CoWoS revenue as a percentage of total.

**Questions not fully answered:** Analysts asked for multi-year capex guidance and were declined. Analysts asked about the specific timeline for supply-demand balance and received only "2027 will still be tight." Bruce Lu (Goldman) pushed on whether TSMC's profitability targets fully reflect its value; C.C. Wei deflected with the "grow together" partnership framing rather than engaging on the pricing power question directly.
