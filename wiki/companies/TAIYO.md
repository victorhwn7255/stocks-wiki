## Thesis role

Taiyo Yuden is a **genuine member of the MLCC oligopoly — not a fringe name** — the clear **#3** behind [[MURATA]] and Samsung Electro-Mechanics, and a **full-stack maker** (materials-up, the same vertical model as Murata at ~one-fifth the scale). It is the **purest MLCC-cycle bet in the vault**: capacitors were **¥251.8bn (+8.5%), 71% of revenue** (FY ended Mar 2026 results, May 8 2026) — far more concentrated than Murata's 51% — and it is the **embedded-MLCC first-mover** (two embeddable families in mass production for AI-server IC power lines; see [[MLCC-oligopoly]]). On the vault's chokepoint-quality gradient it sits at the same precision-manufacturing/yield node as Murata, and these primary sources do real work for [[MLCC-oligopoly]] — they upgrade its #3-maker evidence from Tier-3 to Tier-1/2 and corroborate its pricing correction at a second independent source.

But the honest verdict has a second half that is the brake, and it is sharper than Murata's. **Taiyo is the junior member of the oligopoly — lower-margin, more cyclical, weaker balance sheet, in an AI+auto mix-driven earnings *recovery* rather than a fortress.** Operating margin was **just 5.6%** (vs Murata's ~15%); operating profit **¥20.0bn (+91% YoY)** sounds explosive but is off a *depressed* 3.1% base — the jump is almost entirely a **capacity-utilization recovery** (a ¥30.7bn utilization swing is the whole story; FY ended Mar 2026 results). It **missed every economic target of its previous five-year plan** (operating margin 5.6% vs a 15% target; ROE 4.5% vs 15%; ROIC 3.0% vs 10%) and **re-uses the identical ¥480bn / 15% / 15% / 10% targets in Plan 2030**, pushed out five years (Plan 2030 deck, May 8 2026). Its balance sheet is ordinary, not a fortress (equity ratio 56% vs Murata's 85%; it **raised ¥97bn of external funds** in FY2021-2025 because operating cash flow didn't cover capex through the trough). And its AI-server MLCC ramp is **mostly ahead**: *"significant changes in the MLCCs used in AI servers are expected from the second half of the current fiscal year, with a considerable increase in volumes from next fiscal year onward"* (full-year Q&A, May 8 2026). So Taiyo is **a real oligopoly seat bought at a cyclical-recovery price** — the opposite end of the spectrum from Murata's diversified fortress, and a useful reminder that the oligopoly's members are *not* equal.

## The MLCC chokepoint — Taiyo Yuden's structural position

Taiyo is a **full-stack MLCC maker** (materials → process → mass production), the same vertical model as Murata, and one of the *"limited"* set of suppliers qualified for AI-server-grade parts. The chokepoint logic comes straight from management when asked whether AI-server MLCC could collapse into single-sourcing: *"High product performance is required for use in AI servers, and the number of companies that can supply products meeting these requirements is limited… since the production load is high and single sourcing is not realistic, we believe that the multiple sourcing business model will not fundamentally change"* (full-year Q&A A7, May 8 2026). That is the oligopoly confirmed from the #3's own mouth — **few qualified suppliers (the moat), but multi-sourcing persists (a tight oligopoly, not a winner-take-all monopoly).** Its distinctive edge is the **embedded-MLCC niche**: capacitors buried in the package substrate next to the die — two families in mass production for AI-server IC power lines (1005/0402 22μF, Aug 2025; 2012/0805 100μF, Nov 2025; primary-confirmed via [[MLCC-oligopoly]]) — the same "move the critical part next to the chip" logic that intersects [[pcb-interconnect-substrate-chokepoint]].

## AI-datacenter demand — the content-per-box engine, mostly ahead

The clearest primary grounding yet of *why* AI servers drive MLCC — it is **content per box, not box count** (Plan 2030 deck, May 8 2026): an AI server already uses **10,000–20,000 MLCCs, rising to "more than double" by 2030**, and Taiyo's own market model shows **AI-server MLCC demand growing +32% CAGR (to FY2030) against AI-server *unit* growth of only +5%** (power inductors +18%). That gap *is* the thesis — even flat box counts drive double-digit component growth. The pull is toward *"smaller, higher-capacity models (mainly for AI servers) and integrating these into motherboards for improved performance and reduced power loss"* (Plan 2030 deck), plus power inductors for the AI-server power-delivery shift.

**The honesty caveat (vs Murata).** Unlike [[MURATA]] — which now breaks out a disclosure-grade *data-center revenue line* — **Taiyo discloses no AI-server / data-center revenue split**; its capacitor guide is an aggregate **+12% FY2027**, with AI servers + autos named as drivers but not quantified, and AI-server demand still folded inside the 24% IT-infra bucket. So Taiyo is **less disclosure-granular on AI than Murata**, and the ramp is timed *forward*: *"significant changes… from the second half of the current fiscal year, with a considerable increase in volumes from next fiscal year onward"* (full-year Q&A A2). The Tier-3 "AI-server MLCC +~80%" figure carried on [[MLCC-oligopoly]] is **not separately confirmable at primary** — treat it as Tier-3.

## Capacity & pricing — the buyer dynamic, honestly framed

- **Capacity:** MLCC capacity expanded only **~5%** in the year ended Mar 2026, stepping to **~10%** in FY2027, focused on autos + IT-infra/AI servers (Q3 Q&A A4, Feb 3 2026). Utilization ran **~85% → ~90% → targeted ~95% from Q2 FY2027** on strong orders (full-year Q&A A1); back-end capacity lead time is only **~6 months** (full-year Q&A A4) — i.e. supply can answer fast, a structural-durability caveat. Capex held flat at ¥40bn for FY2027.
- **Pricing — the same restraint as Murata, double-confirming the [[MLCC-oligopoly]] correction.** Taiyo is **not pushing industry-wide hikes**: *"there are no signs of industry-wide price increases yet"* (Q3 Q&A A12, Feb 3 2026), and prices were still *falling* (a ¥20.2bn selling-price decline in the profit bridge). It is negotiating *selective* pass-through on a **silver / precious-metal cost squeeze** (factored as a high-single-digit-¥bn risk; covers inductors + *some* MLCCs) but *"not considering price increases that exceed the increase in raw material costs"* (full-year Q&A A3). The pricing-power kernel is the **top bin**: *"the outlook for stable prices for large-sized products such as those for use in AI servers and automotive has not changed,"* and more customers are signing **annual contracts to ensure stable MLCC procurement** (Q3 Q&A A1) — a demand-durability signal.

## Multi-segment honest-verdict — the dilution

Capacitors (71%) are the engine; **inductors are the deliberate second pillar** (CAGR ~9% over five years, faster than capacitors' ~5%; metal power inductors for AI-server power + autos). The drags are explicit: **integrated modules & devices −35.6%** (circuit-module withdrawals) and the **communication-device business**, in its second structural-reform round (voluntary-retirement program; targeting break-even "as early as possible next fiscal year"; full-year Q&A A6 / Q3 Q&A A8) after a multi-year slide on weak high-end Chinese smartphones — plus the ¥2.1bn aluminum-electrolytic impairment. So, like Murata, the AI story is one slice of a recovering electronics maker — but here the *whole* is thinner (5.6% margin), so the cyclical-recovery character dominates more.

## Cyclicality & the bear clock

Taiyo is the *most* cycle-levered MLCC name in the vault — 71% capacitors, thin margins, recovery-mode — so it amplifies the cycle both ways. Two honest brakes:
- **The recovery is off a trough, against its own missed plan.** The +91% operating-profit jump restates a depressed base; management's *own* five-year scorecard missed every economic target (5.6% vs 15% margin), and Plan 2030 simply re-files them. The same **2027-28 capacity-wave-vs-hyperscaler-capex-deceleration clock** that hangs over [[MURATA]] and [[MLCC-oligopoly]] applies here, amplified by the thinner margin.
- **Double-booking is present but quarantined.** Q4's order surge included **front-loaded / tariff-pull-forward demand** (full-year Q&A A4), and a DDR5-inductor "lull" (DDR4 replacement temporarily stronger; Q2 Q&A A4/A7) — the hoarding is in consumer / information-equipment, while the AI-server / large-case parts stay supply-tight and price-stable. The bear risk is the low end, not (yet) the top bin — the same split Murata draws.
