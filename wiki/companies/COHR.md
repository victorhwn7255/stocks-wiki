# Coherent Corp. (COHR)

## Thesis role

Coherent is a Layer 4 differentiated component supplier — the second Layer 4 company page in this vault, following [[LITE]]. Its thesis relevance is defined through three structural roles that parallel LITE's but with significant scale and manufacturing differences:

1. **Laser supply for CPO and datacenter interconnect.** Coherent manufactures InP-based EML chips, CW lasers, and VCSEL arrays for datacenter transceivers and co-packaged optics. The Q2 FY2026 earnings call disclosed an "exceptionally large purchase order from a market-leading AI data center customer for a CPO solution" (COHR Q2 FY2026 call). Coherent is the second Layer 4 source to break the CPO silence documented in [[CPO-platform-battle]], with even more extensive CPO discussion than [[LITE]].

2. **NVDA-invested company.** [[NVDA]]'s March 2026 $2B equity investment positions Coherent within the NVDA platform integration pattern documented in [[NVDA-platform-integration]]. CEO Jim Anderson described this as "an expansion of a more than 20-year relationship" (per March 2, 2026 NVDA/COHR press release — not from the three ingested sources, which all predate the announcement).

3. **Six-inch InP device-fabrication advantage.** Anderson claimed Coherent is "the world's only producer of six-inch indium phosphide" (COHR Q2 FY2026 call), yielding >4x chips at <50% cost versus three-inch wafers. The "monopoly" framing is not accurate at the substrate level (multiple firms supply six-inch InP substrates), but Coherent holds an unusually advanced multi-site six-inch InP device-fabrication position across Sherman, Järfälla, and Zürich. This structural manufacturing advantage differentiates Coherent from [[LITE]] (which operates three-inch InP fabs with 40% expansion) and strengthens the [[datacenter-laser-supply]] chokepoint — even the larger of the two suppliers sees supply-demand imbalance extending through at least calendar 2027.

**Structural differences from LITE.** Coherent is approximately 3.5x LITE's revenue ($5.8B vs $1.6B FY2025), maintains a substantial Industrial segment (~28% of Q2 FY2026 revenue vs LITE's eliminated Industrial Tech at <15%), and is actively divesting non-core businesses (33 sites exited in ~6 quarters, including A&D for ~$400M and Munich materials processing). Where LITE is a narrowing specialist, Coherent is a portfolio company concentrating toward datacenter while retaining industrial breadth. The page structure reflects this: portfolio optimization and capital structure are analytically significant for Coherent in ways they are not for LITE.

## Product portfolio and technology

### Datacenter components

**EML (electro-absorption modulated lasers).** Record volumes at 100G lane speeds, ramping 200G. Both InP-based EMLs internally produced and external EML supply from multiple vendors (COHR Q2 FY2026 call).

**CW lasers (continuous wave).** New high-power CW laser for CPO applications, subject to the "exceptionally large purchase order" disclosed on the call (COHR Q2 FY2026 call). Coherent's 2025 sampling announcement paired >400mW at 55°C with linewidth below 200 kHz and RIN below -145 dB/Hz.

**400mW in engineering terms (per /raw/research/silicon-photonics-primer.md, citing Lumentum CLEO 2022 and OIF Co-Packaging Framework).** 400mW equals approximately 26 dBm. Moving from 200mW (23 dBm) to 400mW buys approximately 3 dB of additional optical budget — equivalent to the penalty of one extra 1:2 split in an ideal system. The significance is not that 400mW is a magical threshold but that it enables either more sharing (more silicon photonic engines fed by one laser) or more margin (tolerance for splitter, coupling, and routing losses) in ELS/CPO architectures. Lumentum's CLEO 2022 paper estimated CPO-based 51T switches require roughly 22-25 dBm per laser source, which directly aligns with current 350-400+ mW product announcements from both COHR and [[LITE]].

**VCSELs.** 6-inch GaAs VCSEL fabs in US and Europe. Both datacom (short-reach) and 3D sensing applications. Apple partnership for 3D sensing using GaAs VCSELs at Sherman, Texas, volume production H2 2026 (COHR Q2 FY2026 call). VCSEL-based CPO/MPO solutions in development alongside InP-based CPO.

**Transceivers.** Protocol-agnostic datacenter transceivers supporting Ethernet, InfiniBand, and NVLink (COHR 10-K FY2025). 800G shipping, 1.6T in development. Coherent spans from laser chips to finished transceiver modules — broader vertical integration than [[LITE]].

**Optical circuit switches (OCS).** Based on digital liquid-crystal technology. Over 10 customer engagements (up from 7), including both 64x64 and 320x320 systems. Expected addressable market >$2B. Broadening from spine/scale-out into scale-up and DCI applications (COHR Q2 FY2026 call).

### Industrial and other

**Silicon carbide (SiC).** Substrates (100-200mm). Silicon Carbide LLC joint venture with Denso and Mitsubishi ($1B for 25% equity, December 2023). Restricted cash of $661M locked in this subsidiary (COHR 10-Q Q2 FY2026).

**Industrial lasers.** Excimer, solid-state, CO2, ultrafast fiber lasers for semiconductor manufacturing, display, and materials processing. Industrial segment revenue essentially flat YoY (COHR 10-K FY2025).

**Telecom.** Coherent transceivers (100G-800G), pump lasers, EDFAs, ROADMs, optical line systems. Multi-Rail telecom product discussed on call (COHR Q2 FY2026 call).

## InP manufacturing and the six-inch advantage

**Six-inch InP wafer production.** Anderson claimed "we are the world's only producer of six-inch indium phosphide" (COHR Q2 FY2026 call). The six-inch platform yields >4x chips at <50% cost versus the industry-standard three-inch wafer. Three qualified device-fabrication sites: Sherman, Texas; Järfälla, Sweden; and Zürich (COHR Q2 FY2026 call and 2024/2026 InP disclosures).

**Framing refinement (per /raw/research/silicon-photonics-primer.md, citing Sumitomo 2016 technical paper, AXT 2020 results, and IQE 2024 expansion release).** Anderson's "monopoly" framing is not accurate at the substrate level: Sumitomo, AXT, and IQE all have six-inch InP substrate or epitaxial capability, and JX Advanced Metals is a relevant InP supplier at four-inch scale. The defensible claim is narrower but still structurally significant: Coherent holds an unusually advanced multi-site six-inch InP **device-fabrication** position — the ability to process six-inch wafers through high-yield epitaxy, lithography, etch, metallization, passivation, facet preparation, device testing, and qualification for specific CPO-grade products. The distinction matters: substrate availability and device-fabrication qualification are different bottleneck layers (see [[datacenter-laser-supply]] decomposed chokepoint map), and Coherent's advantage sits at the latter.

**Capacity ramp trajectory:**
- Quadrupled InP wafer starts from September to December quarter (COHR Q2 FY2026 call)
- Wafer starts at 80% of target capacity, "ahead of schedule" (COHR Q2 FY2026 call)
- Plan to **double** total InP capacity by end of calendar 2026 (COHR Q2 FY2026 call) — **Q3 FY2026 update: on track + one quarter ahead of schedule**
- Multiple six-inch InP substrate suppliers secured (COHR Q2 FY2026 call)

**Q3 FY2026 ramp acceleration (Session 50 NEW):**
- **First transceivers with 6-inch InP components shipped Q3 FY2026** (Anderson Q3 call 0:06:14): *"During the quarter, we shipped our first transceivers containing components produced on our 6-inch lines, and those shipments contributed to both sequential revenue growth and gross margin improvement."*
- **6-inch InP capacity doubling target met one quarter early** (Anderson Q3 0:23:59): *"our target this year is to double our indium phosphide capacity. The great thing is it looks like based on the current execution, we'll achieve that goal next quarter, which is one quarter earlier than we thought"*
- **More than doubling again by end of calendar 2027** (Anderson Q3 0:23:59): *"When I look into next calendar year, we expect more than double indium phosphide capacity again. That's a quadrupling of capacity over a two-year period"* — quadrupling over 2 years
- **6-inch yields exceed 3-inch yields** across all 3 device categories (EMLs, CW lasers, photodiodes) (Anderson Q3 0:06:14): *"Our 6-inch platform is producing EMLs, CW lasers, and photodiodes, and the yields for each of the three device categories continues to exceed those of our 3-inch production lines."*
- **Zürich announced as third 6-inch site** (Anderson Q3 0:06:14): *"Given the success of the 6-inch ramp to date, we have also announced plans to begin 6-inch indium phosphide production at a third site in Zürich"*; Zürich production starts beginning calendar 2027 (Anderson 0:27:40); Sherman TX = 1st site; Sweden 2nd site; Zürich 3rd site
- **6-inch vs 3-inch economics confirmed at primary** (Anderson Q3 0:25:57): *"6-inch versus 3-inch is more than four times as many devices at less than half the cost"* — S46 baseline ">4x chips at <50% cost" verified
- Sherman TX role for NVIDIA partnership (Anderson Q3 0:06:14): *"Texas, which is the world's most advanced indium phosphide production site and will play an important role in ramping CW laser production for our CPO solutions, including those supporting our NVIDIA partnership"*

**Competitive asymmetry with LITE.** [[LITE]] operates three-inch InP fabs with a 40% capacity expansion target (LITE Q2 FY2026 call). Coherent's six-inch advantage, if the yield and cost claims hold, represents a structural manufacturing asymmetry: more chips per wafer start at lower unit cost. Both companies are fully allocation-constrained, meaning the cost advantage manifests in expansion economics rather than current competitive pricing.

**Significance for the chokepoint thesis.** The six-inch advantage might appear to weaken the [[datacenter-laser-supply]] chokepoint if Coherent can expand capacity faster. However, Anderson directly addresses this: "I don't foresee the supply-demand getting back in balance this calendar year. I don't think it happens next calendar year" (COHR Q2 FY2026 call). Even with the six-inch cost advantage and aggressive capacity doubling, demand absorbs all new capacity "and then some." The chokepoint persists because the demand growth rate exceeds even the faster capacity expansion rate. See [[InP-supply]] for the upstream substrate supply dynamics.
